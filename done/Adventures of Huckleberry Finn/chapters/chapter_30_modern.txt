When they got on board, the king rushed at me and grabbed my collar. He said, “Trying to run away, are you, you brat? Had enough of us?”  

I answered, “No, Your Majesty, we weren’t—please don’t, Your Majesty!”  

“Quick, then, tell us what your plan was, or I’ll shake the life out of you!”  

“Honestly, I’ll tell you everything exactly as it happened, Your Majesty. The man who caught me was very kind. He kept saying he had a son about my size who died last year, and he felt sorry to see a boy in such danger. When they all got surprised by the gold and rushed toward the coffin, he let me go and whispered, ‘Run now, or they’ll hang you, for sure!’ So I ran. It didn’t seem right for me to stay—I couldn’t do anything, and I didn’t want to be hanged if I could escape. I kept running until I found a canoe. When I got there I told Jim to hurry, or they would catch me and hang me again. I said I was scared you and the duke were dead, that I felt terrible, and that Jim felt the same. I was very glad when we saw you coming; you can ask Jim if I didn’t say that.”  

Jim said it was true, and the king told him to be quiet. He said, “Oh yes, that’s very likely!” and grabbed me again, saying he thought he would drown me. But the duke shouted, “Let the boy go, you old fool! Would you have done anything different? Did you look for him after you got away? I don’t remember.”

The king let go of me and started cursing the town and everyone in it. The duke replied:

“You’d better blame yourself, because you’re the one who deserves it most. You haven’t done anything sensible from the start, except for showing up bold and cheeky with that imagined blue‑arrow mark. That was clever—it hit the target, and it saved us. If it hadn’t been for that, they would have locked us up until the English soldiers arrived, and then we’d have ended up in prison, you can bet! But that trick sent them to the graveyard, and the gold helped us even more; if the excited fools hadn’t let go of everything and rushed to get a look, we would have stayed in our cravats tonight—cravats that we’re supposed to wear—longer than we needed to.”

He thought for a minute, then the king said, absent‑mindedly:

“Man! And we thought the thieves stole it!”

That made me squirm.

“Yes,” said the duke, slower and more sarcastic, “we did.”

After about half a minute, the king drawled:

“At least I did.”

The duke replied in the same tone:

“On the contrary, I did.”

The king got a little upset and asked:

“What are you talking about, Bilgewater?”

The duke answered briskly:

“When it comes to that, may I ask what you were referring to?”

“Shucks!” the king said sarcastically. “I don’t know—maybe you were asleep and didn’t know what was happening.”

The duke bristled and said:

"Oh, stop this nonsense. Do you think I'm a fool? Don't you know I know who hid the money in that coffin?"

"Yes, sir! I know you know, because you did it yourself!"

"It's a lie!" The duke lunged at him. The king shouted, "Take your hands off! Leave my throat alone! I take it all back!"

The duke said, "First, admit that you hid the money there. You planned to run away, then come back, dig it up, and keep it for yourself."

"Hold on a minute, duke—answer me honestly. If you didn't put the money there, say so and I'll believe you and take back everything I said."

"You old scoundrel, I didn't, and you know I didn't. There, now!"

"Alright, I believe you. But answer one more thing—don't get angry—didn't you intend to take the money and hide it?"

The duke was silent for a moment, then said, "I don't care if I did or didn't do it. You not only thought about it, you did it."

"I wish I never died if I did it, duke, and that's honest. I won't say I wasn't going to do it, because I was, but someone got there before me."

"It's a lie! You did it, and you have to admit it, or—"

The king began to choke, then gasped, "Enough! I admit it!"

I was very glad to hear him say that; it made me feel much more at ease than before. So the duke let go of his hands and said:

“If you ever deny it again I’ll drown you. It’s good for you to sit there and whine like a baby—it fits the way you’ve acted. I’ve never seen such an old fool trying to take everything, and I trusted you all the time, as if you were my own father. You should be ashamed to stand by and hear it forced on many poor black people, and never say a word for them. It makes me feel ridiculous thinking I was soft enough to believe that nonsense. Damn you, I can see now why you were so eager to make up the deficit—you wanted to take the money I got from the Nonesuch and everything else, and take it all!”

The king said timidly, still sniffing:

“Why, duke, it was you who said make up the deficit; it wasn’t me.”

“Shut up! I don’t want to hear any more from you!” said the duke. “And now you see what you got because of it. They’ve got all their money back, and we have only a shekel or two left. Go to bed, and don’t give me any more deficits, as long as you live!”

The king slipped into the wigwam and turned to his bottle for comfort. Soon the duke did the same with his own bottle. Within about half an hour they were as close as ever. The closer they became, the more affectionate they got, and they fell asleep snoring in each other's arms.

Both of them became very relaxed, but I saw that the king was not relaxed enough to forget about the hidden money bag. That made me feel calm and satisfied.

Of course, while they were snoring we had a long talk, and I told Jim everything.
