They swarmed toward Sherburn’s house, shouting and raging like a mob, and everyone had to move aside or be trampled. It was terrible to watch. Children ran ahead of the crowd, screaming and trying to get out of the way. Every window along the road was filled with women’s heads, and black boys were in every tree, while men and women peered over every fence. As soon as the mob got close, the boys would scatter and disappear out of reach. Many women and girls were crying, trembling with fear.

They pressed up against Sherburn’s fence, packed as tightly as they could be, and the noise was deafening. It was a small yard, about twenty feet wide. Some shouted, “Tear down the fence! Tear down the fence!” Then came the sound of ripping, tearing, and smashing. The fence fell, and the front of the crowd surged forward like a wave.

At that moment Sherburn stepped onto the roof of his small front porch, a double‑barrel gun in his hand, and stood there calmly and deliberately, without saying a word. The noise stopped, and the wave of people pulled back.

Sherburn didn’t speak—he just stood, looking down. The stillness was eerie and uncomfortable. He slowly surveyed the crowd with his eyes; wherever he looked, people tried to meet his gaze, but they couldn’t. They lowered their eyes and stared furtively. Then, soon after, Sherburn laughed—a dry, unpleasant laugh that felt like biting into bread with sand inside.

Then he says, slowly and scornfully:

“The idea that *you* would lynch anyone is funny. You think you have enough courage to lynch a *man* because you’re brave enough to tar and feather poor, friendless women who are driven out here. Does that make you think you have the grit to lay a hand on a *man*? A man is safe when he’s surrounded by thousands of people like you—as long as it’s daytime and you’re not behind him.

“Do I know you? I see right through you. I was born and raised in the South, and I’ve lived in the North, so I know what most people are like. The average person is a coward. In the North a man lets anyone walk over him, then goes home and prays for the strength to endure it. In the South one man, by himself, can stop a stagecoach full of men in daylight and rob everyone. Your newspapers call your people brave, so you think you are braver than any other folks, but you’re only as brave as anyone else—no more.

“Why don’t your juries hang murderers? Because they’re afraid the murder’s friends will shoot them in the back, in the dark—exactly what they would do.

“So the juries always acquit, and then a man goes out at night with a hundred masked cowards behind him and lynches the criminal. Your mistake is that you didn’t bring a real man with you—that’s one error. The other mistake is that you didn’t come at night and wear masks. You only brought part of a man—Buck Harkness—so if you hadn’t had him to start with, you would have been beaten.”

You didn't want to come. Most people don't like trouble or danger. You don't like trouble or danger either. But if even a half‑man like Buck Harkness shouts “Lynch him! Lynch him!” you are scared to back down. You are scared you'll be seen for what you are—cowards. So you shout, cling to that half‑man's coat‑tail, and come up here, swearing about all the big things you will do.

The most pathetic thing is a mob; that's what an army is—a mob. They don't fight with courage that comes from themselves, but with courage borrowed from the crowd and from their officers. A mob that has no real man leading it is even more pathetic. What you should do is lower your head, go home and hide. If any real lynching is going to happen, it will be done in the dark, Southern style; the men will wear masks and bring a victim with them. Now leave—and take your half‑man with you, he said, tossing his gun across his left arm and cocking it.

The crowd pulled back suddenly, then scattered in every direction. Buck Harkness ran after them, looking quite cheap. I could have stayed if I wanted, but I didn't.

I went to the circus and hung around the back until the guard passed. Then I slipped under the tent. I had my twenty‑dollar gold coin and some other cash, but I thought I should keep it safe because you never know when you’ll need it, far from home and among strangers. You can’t be too careful. I don’t mind spending money on a circus when there’s no other choice, but it isn’t worth wasting it there.

It was a huge circus. It was the most spectacular sight I have ever seen when they all entered, two by two, a man and a woman, side by side. The men wore only underwear and undershirts, no shoes or stirrups, and rested their hands comfortably on their thighs. There must have been about twenty of them. Every woman had a beautiful complexion, looked perfectly gorgeous, and seemed like a group of real queens. They wore clothes that cost a fortune and were covered with diamonds. It was a powerful, wonderful view; I have never seen anything so beautiful. Then, one by one, they stood up and moved around the ring, moving gently and gracefully. The men looked tall, light, and straight, their heads bobbing as they walked under the tent roof. Each woman’s rose‑leaf dress fluttered softly around her hips, looking like the loveliest parasol.

They went faster and faster, all of them dancing. First one foot went up, then the other. The horses leaned more and more. The ringmaster ran around the center pole, cracking his whip and shouting “Hi! Hi!” A clown told jokes behind him. Soon everyone dropped the reins. Every lady put her hands on her hips, and every gentleman crossed his arms. The horses then leaned over and reared up. One after another they jumped into the ring and made the sweetest bow I have ever seen. Then they ran away, and everybody clapped and went almost wild.

All through the circus they did the most amazing things, and the clown kept going on and on; it almost killed the audience with laughter. The ringmaster couldn’t say a word to him, but the clown answered as quickly as a blink with the funniest things anyone had ever heard. How he could think of so many jokes so fast was something I could not understand. I couldn’t even picture them for a whole year.  

Later, a drunk man tried to get into the ring. He said he wanted to ride and that he could ride as well as anyone. They tried to keep him out, but he wouldn’t listen, and the whole show stopped. The crowd began to shout at him and make fun of him. That made him angry, and he started to tear around. The people got excited, and many men climbed down from the benches and rushed toward the ring, shouting, “Knock him down! Throw him out!” A few women started to scream.  

The ringmaster then gave a short speech. He said he hoped there would be no disturbance, and if the drunk man promised not to cause any more trouble, he would let him ride if he could stay on the horse. Everyone laughed and agreed, and the man got on.  

The moment he was on, the horse began to jump, kick, and run wildly. Two circus workers held the bridle, trying to control it, while the drunk man clung to the horse’s neck and his heels flew up with every jump. The whole crowd stood up, shouting and laughing until tears rolled down their faces.  

Finally, the horse broke free and ran around the ring like a wild animal, with the drunk man hanging on to its neck, one leg dangling on each side. The people went wild. It wasn’t funny to me; I was trembling watching his danger.  

Soon, the man managed to get back on the horse, grabbed the bridle, and rocked back and forth. A moment later he stood up, dropped the bridle, and the horse kept running as fast as a fire. He stood there, riding as if he had never been drunk in his life, then started taking off his clothes. He threw away so many outfits that the air seemed clogged, shedding seventeen suits in total.  

When he was finally done, he was slim, handsome, and dressed in the most colorful and pretty clothes you could imagine. He whipped the horse, making it hum, then bowed, skipped away, and danced to the dressing room. Everyone howled with pleasure and amazement.

Then the ring‑master saw that he had been tricked, and he was the silliest ring‑master you ever saw, I think. It turned out to be one of his own men! He made up the whole joke himself and never told anyone. I felt embarrassed for being taken in, but I wouldn’t want to be in that ring‑master’s shoes, not even for a thousand dollars. I don’t know; there may be worse circuses than that one, but I haven’t met them yet. Anyway, it was good enough for me, and wherever I run into it, I can bring all my customers every time.

That night we had our show, but there were only about twelve people—just enough to cover the costs. They laughed all the time, and that made the duke angry. Everybody left before the show was over, except for one boy who was asleep. So the duke said these Arkansaw clowns couldn’t compare to Shakespeare; they only wanted low comedy—maybe even something worse, he thought. He said he could match their style. The next morning he got some large sheets of wrapping paper and black paint, made some handbills, and stuck them up all over the village. The bills said:

AT THE COURTHOUSE!  
FOR 3 NIGHTS ONLY!  

The World‑Renowned Tragedians  
DAVID GARRICK THE YOUNGER  
AND  
EDMUND KEAN THE ELDER  

Of the London and Continental Theatres,  

In their Thrilling Tragedy of  
THE KING’S CAMELOPARD  
OR  
THE ROYAL NONESUCH!!!  

Admission 50 cents.

At the bottom was the biggest line of all—LADIES AND CHILDREN NOT ADMITTED.

“There,” he said, “if that line doesn’t get them, I don’t know Arkansas!”
