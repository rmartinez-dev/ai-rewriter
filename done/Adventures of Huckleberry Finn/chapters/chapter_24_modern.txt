The next day, toward evening, we set up camp under a small willow at the river’s bend, where there was a village on each side of the water. The duke and the king began to make a plan for handling those towns.  

Jim spoke to the duke and said he hoped it wouldn’t take more than a few hours, because it was very heavy and tiring for him to stay all day in the wigwam tied with rope. You see, when we left him alone we had to tie him, because if anyone found him untied it wouldn’t look like he was a runaway slave, you know. So the duke said it was hard to be tied all day, and he would figure out a way around it.  

The duke was unusually clever, and he soon found a solution. He dressed Jim in a King‑Lear costume—a long calico gown, a white horse‑hair wig, and fake whiskers. Then he used theater paint to cover Jim’s face, hands, ears and neck with a dead, dull solid blue, like a man who had been drowned for nine days. It was the most horrible sight I have ever seen.  

Finally, the duke wrote a sign on a plank that read:  

*“Sick Arab—but harmless when not out of his mind.”*

He nailed the shingle to a lath and set the lath about four or five feet in front of the wigwam. Jim was pleased. He said it was far better than lying tied up for years every day and trembling whenever he heard a sound. The duke told him to make himself comfortable. If anyone came to bother him, he should hop out of the wigwam, act a little, and let out one or two howls like a wild animal. He thought they would run away and leave him alone. That was good advice, but most people wouldn’t wait for him to howl. In fact, he didn’t just look dead; he looked much worse than that.

These rascals wanted to try the Nonesuch again because it promised a lot of money, but they thought it wasn’t safe. Maybe the news would already have spread by now. They couldn’t find any project that fit just right, so finally the duke said he would take a short break, think for an hour or two, and see if he could come up with something for the Arkansas village. The king decided to go to the other village without any plan, just trusting Providence—meaning the devil, I think—to lead him to profit.

We had all bought new clothes at the last store, and now the king put his on and told me to put mine on. I did, of course. The king’s clothes were all black, and he looked very sharp and proper. I had never realized how much clothes could change a person. Before, he looked like the grumpiest old man you could imagine, but now, when he took off his new white beaver hat, bowed, and smiled, he looked so grand, good, and holy that you might think he had stepped out of the ark, maybe even like the old Leviticus himself.

Jim cleaned the canoe, and I got my paddle ready. A large steamboat was docked at the shore about three miles upstream from town; it had been there a couple of hours loading cargo. The king said:

“Since I’m dressed like this, I think I should come from St. Louis or Cincinnati, or some other big city. Go get the steamboat, Huckleberry; we’ll travel down to the village on it.”

I didn’t need to be told twice to go and take the steamboat. I landed half a mile upstream from the village and then paddled along the shallow bank in the calm water. Soon we saw a young country man sitting on a log, wiping the sweat from his face because it was very hot. He had a couple of large canvas bags with him.

“Pull her onto shore,” said the captain. I did it. “Where are you headed, young man?”

“I’m going to catch the steamboat to New Orleans.”

“Get aboard,” said the captain. “Wait a moment, my servant will help you with those bags. Jump out and help the gentleman, Adolphus,” he added, meaning me.

I did, and the three of us set off again. The young man was very grateful; he said it was hard work carrying his bags in such weather. He asked the captain where he was going. The captain said he had come down the river, landed in the next village this morning, and was now going a few miles upstream to visit an old friend on a farm. The young man said:

“When I first saw you I thought, ‘It must be Mr. Wilks, and he almost made it on time.’ Then I thought again, ‘No, it can’t be him, because he wouldn’t be paddling upstream.’ You’re not him, are you?”

“No, my name is Blodgett—Alexander Blodgett, Reverend Alexander Blodgett, I suppose, because I am one of the Lord’s humble servants. Still, I feel sorry for Mr. Wilks for not arriving on time, in case he missed anything because of it—I hope he didn’t.”

"Well, he doesn't lose any property because he'll get it all sorted, but he missed seeing his brother Peter die—he may not mind, nobody can say for sure. His brother would have given anything in the world to see him before he died. He hasn't talked about anything else for these three weeks. He hadn't seen him since they were boys together, and he had never even seen his brother William at all—that's the deaf and mute one. William is no older than thirty or thirty‑five. Peter and George were the only ones who came out here. George was the married brother; he and his wife both died last year. Harvey and William are the only ones left now, and, as I was saying, they haven't arrived in time."

"Did anybody send them a message?"

"Oh yes; a month or two ago, when Peter first passed away, because Peter said he sort of felt he wasn't going to get well this time. You see, he was pretty old, and George's girls were too young to keep him company, except for Mary Jane, the red‑haired one. So he was kind of lonely after George and his wife died, and didn't seem to care much about living. He desperately wanted to see Harvey—and William too—because he was the type of person who couldn't bear to make a will. He left a letter for Harvey and said where his money was hidden and how he wanted the rest of the property divided so George's girls would be alright, since George left nothing. That letter was all they could get him to sign."

"Why do you think Harvey didn't come? Where does he live?"

"Oh, he lives in England, in Sheffield. He preaches there and has never been in this country. He hasn't had much time, and maybe he never got the letter, you know."

"Too bad, too bad he couldn't live to see his brothers, poor soul. You're going to Orleans, you say?"

"Yes, but that's not the only reason. I'm taking a ship next Wednesday to Rio de Janeiro, where my uncle lives."

"It's a pretty long journey, but it will be wonderful; I wish I were going too. Is Mary Jane the oldest? How old are the others?"

"Mary Jane is nineteen, Susan is fifteen, and Joanna is about fourteen. She's the one who does good works and has a harelip."

"Poor things, left alone in the cold world."

"Well, they could be worse off. Old Peter had friends, and they won't let them be harmed. There's Hobson, the Baptist preacher; Deacon Lot Hovey; Ben Rucker; Abner Shackleford; Levi Bell, the lawyer; Dr. Robinson; their wives; the widow Bartley; and many others. These are the people Peter was closest to and wrote about in his letters home, so Harvey will know where to find friends when he arrives."

The old man kept asking questions until the young man was almost exhausted. He asked about everyone and everything in that town, about the Wilkses, about Peter's business—he was a tanner—about George's—he was a carpenter—about Harvey's—he was a dissenting minister—and so on. Then he said, "Why did you want to walk all the way to the steamboat?"

“Because it’s a big New Orleans boat, I was afraid it might not stop there. When they’re deep they don’t stop for a hail. A Cincinnati boat would, but this is a St. Louis one.”

“Was Peter Wilks well off?”

“Oh, yes, pretty well off. He owned houses and land, and it’s said he left three or four thousand dollars in cash hidden somewhere.”

“When did you say he died?”

“I didn’t say, but it was last night.”

“The funeral will probably be tomorrow?”

“Yes, around midday.”

“It’s all very sad, but we all have to die sometime. We should be prepared, then everything will be okay.”

“Yes, sir, that’s the best way. My mother always used to say that.”

When we reached the boat, she was almost finished loading and soon left. The king never mentioned boarding, so I missed my ride. After the boat went away, the king told me to paddle another mile to a lonely spot. Then he got ashore and said:

“Now hurry back right away and bring the duke here, along with the new carpet bags. If he’s gone to the other side, go there and get him. Tell him to get up anyway. Move quickly.”

I saw what he was doing, but I didn’t say anything, of course. When I returned with the duke, we hid the canoe. Then they got off onto a log, and the king told him everything, exactly as the young man had said it—every word. While he was doing that, he tried to speak like an Englishman, and he did it quite well for a clumsy person. I can’t copy him, so I’m not going to try, but he really did a good job. Then he said:

“How are you on the deaf and dumb, Bilgewater?”

The duke told him to leave that alone; he had played a deaf and dumb character on the stage before. So they waited for a steamboat.

Around mid‑afternoon, a couple of small boats came, but they hadn’t come far enough upriver. Finally a large boat arrived, and they hailed it. It sent out its yawl, and we boarded. The boat was from Cincinnati. When they found out we only wanted to go four or five miles, they got very angry, cursed us, and said they wouldn’t let us land. But the king stayed calm. He said:

“If gentlemen can afford to pay a dollar per mile each to be taken on and off in a yawl, a steamboat can afford to carry them, can’t it?”

So they softened their tone and said it was all right. When we got to the village, they took us ashore in the yawl. About two dozen men came down when they saw the yawl arriving, and when the king said:

“Can any of you gentlemen tell me where Mr. Peter Wilks lives?” They looked at each other and shook their heads, as if to say, “What can we tell you?” Then one of them answered softly:

“I’m sorry, sir, but the best we can do is tell you where he lived yesterday evening.”

Suddenly, the grumpy old man rushed forward, fell against the other man, rested his chin on his shoulder, and cried out, “Alas, our poor brother—gone, and we never got to see him; oh, it’s too, too hard!”

He turned around, sobbing, and made a lot of foolish gestures toward the duke, blaming him for dropping a carpet‑bag and starting to cry. If they weren’t the worst lot, those two swindlers, I would have struck them.

The men gathered around, sympathized with him, said kind words, carried his carpet‑bags up the hill, let him lean on them and cry, and told the king everything about his brother’s last moments. The king repeated the story to the duke, and both of them mourned the dead tanner as if they had lost twelve disciples. If I ever experienced anything like that, I’m a fool. It made me feel ashamed of humanity.
