I set off for town in a wagon. When I was halfway, I saw another wagon coming. It was Tom Sawyer. I stopped and waited for him. I shouted, “Hold on!” and his wagon stopped beside mine. His mouth opened wide, and he swallowed a few times, as if his throat were dry. Then he said:

“I have never done you any harm. You know that. So what do you want now?”

I replied, “I didn’t come back—I haven’t been away.”

When he heard my voice, he sat up a little, but he still wasn’t satisfied. He said, “Don’t try to trick me, because I wouldn’t trick you. Honestly, you’re not a ghost, are you?”

“I’m not,” I answered.

“Okay, that should settle it, but I still don’t understand. Tell me, were you ever murdered?”  

“No. I was never murdered—I played that story on them. Come here and test me if you don’t believe me.”

So he did it, and it satisfied him. He was so glad to see me again that he didn’t know what to do. He wanted to hear all the details right away because it was a great adventure, mysterious, and it affected him deeply.  

I told him to leave it for a while. I asked his driver to wait, and we drove a short distance. I explained the difficult situation I was in and asked what he thought we should do. He said we should leave the person alone for a minute and not disturb him. He thought about it, and soon he said:  

“It’s fine, I have a plan. Put my trunk in your wagon and pretend it’s yours. Then drive back slowly so you arrive at the house at the right time. I’ll go toward town for a while, start fresh, and arrive a quarter‑hour or half‑hour after you. You don’t have to reveal who I am at first.”  

I replied, “All right, but wait a minute. There’s one more thing—something only I know. There’s a man here that I’m trying to rescue from slavery, and his name is Jim—Miss Watson’s Jim.”  

He said, “What! Why, Jim is—” He paused and thought. I said, “I know what you’ll say. You’ll think it’s dirty, low‑level work. It is. I’m low, and I’m going to rescue him, and I need you to stay silent and not reveal anything. Will you?”  

His eyes lit up. He said, “I’ll help you rescue him!”

Well, I gave up all hope then, as if I had been shot. It was the most astonishing speech I ever heard, and I must admit that Tom Sawyer fell a lot in my opinion. I could not believe it. Tom Sawyer—a black‑man stealer!

“Oh, come on!” I said. “You’re joking.”

“I’m not joking either.”

“Well then,” I said, “joking or not, if you hear anything about a runaway black man, remember that you know nothing about him, and I know nothing about him either.”

Then we put the trunk in my wagon. He rode away on his, and I rode mine. Because I was so happy and busy thinking, I forgot to drive slowly. I arrived home far too quickly for such a long trip.

The old gentleman was at the door and said:

“Wow, this is wonderful! Who would have thought a mare could do that? I wish we had timed her. She didn’t sweat a single hair. It’s amazing. I wouldn’t sell her for a hundred dollars now, honestly; yet I once sold her for fifteen, thinking that was all she was worth.”

That was all he said. He was the most innocent, kind old soul I have ever seen. It was not surprising, because he was not only a farmer but also a preacher. He had a small one‑room log church behind the plantation, which he built himself at his own expense as a church and schoolhouse. He never charged for his preaching, and his work was worthwhile. There were many other farmer‑preachers like him in the South, doing the same thing.

In about half an hour Tom’s wagon pulled up to the front gate. Aunt Sally saw it through the window because it was only about fifty yards away, and she said:

“Why, there’s someone coming! I wonder who it is? I think it’s a stranger. Jimmy—one of the children—run and tell Lize to set another plate for dinner.”

Everyone rushed to the front door. A stranger didn’t come every year, and when one did, it was a big event. Tom was at the gate and started toward the house; the wagon rolled down the road toward the village, and we all gathered at the front door. Tom was wearing his Sunday clothes and felt like an audience was watching him—something that always made Tom Sawyer nervous. In that situation, it wasn’t hard for him to add a touch of style that fit. He didn’t walk meekly like a sheep; no, he came on strong and important, like a ram. When he reached us he lifted his hat very gently, as if it were the lid of a box with sleeping butterflies inside that he didn’t want to disturb, and said:

“Mr. Archibald Nichols, I presume?”

“No, my boy,” said the old gentleman, “I’m sorry to tell you your driver misled you; Nichols’s house is about three miles farther down the road. Come in, come in.”

Tom looked back over his shoulder and said, “Too late—he’s out of sight.”

“Yes, he’s gone, my son. You must come in and have dinner with us, and then we’ll hitch a horse and take you down to Nichols’s.”

"Oh, I can't cause you any trouble; I wouldn't think of it. I'll walk—I don't mind the distance."

"But we won't let you walk—it wouldn't be Southern hospitality. Come right in."

"Oh, do," says Aunt Sally, "it's not a problem for us at all. You must stay. It's a long, dusty three‑mile road and we can't let you walk. Besides, I've already told them to set another plate when I see you coming, so you shouldn't disappoint us. Come in and make yourself at home."

Tom thanked them warmly and let himself be persuaded to come in. Once inside he said he was a stranger from Hicksville, Ohio, and his name was William Thompson, and he bowed again.

He kept talking, making up stories about Hicksville and everyone there. I began to feel nervous and wondered how this would help me out of my trouble. Finally, while still talking, he reached over and kissed Aunt Sally on the mouth, then settled back comfortably in his chair and kept talking. She jumped up, wiped his lips with the back of her hand, and said, "You cheeky fool!"

He looked a little hurt and said, "I'm surprised, ma'am."

"You are! Why... what do you think I am? I had a good idea to—say, what do you mean by kissing me?"

He looked humbled and said, "I didn't mean anything, ma'am. I meant no harm. I... I thought you would like it."

“Why, you dumb fool!” She picked up the spinning stick and barely held herself back from hitting him with it.  
“What made you think I’d like it?”

“I don’t know. They— they— told me you would.”

“They told you I would? Whoever told you is another lunatic. I’ve never heard any of that before. Who are ‘they’?”

“Everyone. Everybody said it, ma’am.”

She struggled to stay calm. Her eyes flashed, and her fingers twitched as if she wanted to scratch him. She said:  
“Who is ‘everybody’? Give me their names, or I’ll call you an idiot.”

He stood up, looking upset, fumbled with his hat, and said:  
“I’m sorry. I didn’t expect this. They told me to. They all told me to kiss you, and they said you’d like it. Every single one of them. But I’m sorry, ma’am, and I won’t do it again—I promise.”

“You won’t, will you? I bet you won’t!”

“No, I’m serious. I’ll never do it again—unless you ask me.”

“Until I ask you! I’ll never understand that in my whole life! I bet you’ll be the oldest fool in creation before I ever ask you—or anyone like you.”

He replied, “It really surprises me. I can’t figure it out. They said you would, and I thought you would. But—” He stopped, looked around slowly, as if hoping to find a friendly face somewhere, then turned to the old gentleman and asked, “Didn’t you think she’d want me to kiss her, sir?”

“No, I… I don’t think so.”

He looks at me the same way and says,  

“Tom, didn’t you think Aunt Sally would open her arms and say, ‘Sid Sawyer—’?”  

“My land!” she interrupts, jumping toward him. “You impudent young rascal, to fool a person so—” She is about to hug him, but he pushes her away and says,  

“No, not until you ask me first.”  

She doesn’t waste any time. She asks him, then hugs him and kisses him again and again. After that she hands him over to the old man, and he takes what is left. When they quiet down, she says,  

“Goodness, I never see such a surprise. We weren’t looking for you at all, only for Tom. My sister never wrote to me about anyone coming except him.”  

“It wasn’t meant for any of us to come but Tom,” he answers. “I begged and begged, and at the last minute she let me come too. So, traveling down the river, Tom and I thought it would be a great surprise for him to arrive at the house first, and for me to follow later and pretend to be a stranger. But it was a mistake, Aunt Sally. This isn’t a good place for a stranger.”  

“No, not impudent whelps, Sid. You should have kept your mouth shut; I haven’t been this upset in a long time. I don’t mind the words—I would gladly endure a thousand jokes just to have you here. To think of that performance! I don’t deny it, I was shocked when you gave me that smack.”

We had dinner outside, in the wide passage between the house and the kitchen. There was enough food on the table for seven families, and it was all hot. It wasn’t the soft, tough meat that sits in a damp cellar all night and tastes like a cold piece of old meat in the morning. Uncle Silas said a long blessing over the food, but it was worth it, and it didn’t cool the food at all, unlike what I’ve seen happen with other interruptions.

We talked a lot that afternoon, and Tom and I kept watch the whole time, but it didn’t help; they didn’t say anything about any runaway Black man, and we were afraid to bring it up. Then at supper, one of the little boys asked:

“Dad, can Tom, Sid and I go to the show?”

“No,” the old man answered, “I don’t think there will be one, and even if there was you couldn’t go, because the runaway Black man told Burton and me about that scandalous show, and Burton said he would tell the others. So I think they have already driven the audacious trouble‑makers out of town.”

That was it. I couldn’t stay calm. Tom and I had to share the same room and bed, so, being tired, we said good‑night and went to bed right after supper. We climbed out the window, down the lightning rod, and headed for town, because I didn’t think anyone would warn the king and the duke. If I didn’t hurry and give them a warning, they would get into trouble for sure.

On the road Tom told me all about how people thought I had been murdered, how Pap disappeared soon after and never came back, and the excitement when Jim ran away. I told Tom about our Royal Nonesuch rascals and about as much of the raft trip as I could remember.

We entered the town and went through its centre. It was about half past eight. A huge rush of people with torches came running. They shouted, yelled, banged tin pans and blew horns. We stepped aside to let them pass.

As they went by, I saw the king and the duke on a rail. They were covered in tar and feathers and didn’t look human at all; they looked like huge, monstrous soldiers. It made me feel sick to see them. I felt sorry for those poor, pitiful rascals and thought I could never feel any hard feelings toward them again. It was a terrible sight. Humans can be terribly cruel to each other.

We realized we were too late and could do nothing. We asked some stragglers what had happened. They said everyone went to the show acting very innocent, stayed low and hidden until the old king was in the middle of his performance on stage. Then someone gave a signal, and the crowd rose up and attacked them.

So we walked back home, and I wasn’t feeling as bold as before, but rather grumpy, humble, and somehow guilty—though I hadn’t done anything. But that’s always how it is; it doesn’t matter whether you do right or wrong, a person’s conscience doesn’t make sense, and it just gets in the way anyway. If I had a yellow dog that knew as little as a person’s conscience, I would piss on it. It takes up more space than all the rest of a person’s insides, and yet it isn’t useful at all. Tom Sawyer says the same.
