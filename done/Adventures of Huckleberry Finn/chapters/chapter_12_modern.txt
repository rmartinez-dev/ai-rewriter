It must have been close to one o’clock when we finally reached the island, and the raft seemed to move very slowly. If a boat had come, we would have taken the canoe and headed for the Illinois shore. It was a good thing no boat came, because we had not thought to bring a gun, a fishing line, or any food in the canoe. We were sweating too much to think about all those things. It was not wise to put everything on the raft.

If the men went to the island, I expect they found the camp‑fire I built and watched it all night, waiting for Jim. In any case, they stayed away from us, and if my fire did not fool them, that was not my fault. I kept my actions as low‑key as possible.

When the first light of day appeared, we tied the raft to a tow‑head in a big bend on the Illinois side. We cut cottonwood branches with the hatchet and covered the raft with them so it looked like the bank had collapsed there. A tow‑head is a sandbar that has thick cottonwood trees on it, like the teeth of a harrow.

We had hills on the Missouri side and thick forest on the Illinois side, and the channel ran along the Missouri side there, so we weren’t scared of anyone coming across us.  

We stayed there all day and watched the rafts and steamboats move down the Missouri side, and we saw upstream steamboats fighting the wide river in the middle.  

I told Jim the whole story of my talk with that woman. Jim said she was clever, and if she tried to chase us herself she wouldn’t sit by a campfire—no, she would go get a dog.  

I asked why she couldn’t just tell her husband to get a dog. Jim guessed she probably thought of it only when the men were ready to leave, and that they must have gone into town to get a dog, which cost them time.  

If they had got a dog sooner, we would be back in that same old town instead of being on a tow‑head sixteen or seventeen miles downstream from the village.  

So I said I didn’t care why they didn’t catch us, as long as they didn’t.

It was getting dark, so we stuck our heads out of the cottonwood thicket and looked upstream, downstream, and across. We saw nothing. Jim took some of the top planks from the raft and built a snug wigwam where we could stay in bright sun or rain and keep our things dry. He made a floor for the wigwam and raised it a foot or more above the raft. This kept the blankets and traps out of reach of the steamboat waves. In the middle of the wigwam we put a layer of dirt about five or six inches deep, surrounded by a frame to hold it in place. We made a fire on this dirt when the weather was wet or cold, and the wigwam hid the fire from view. We also made an extra steering oar in case one of the others broke on a snag. We attached a short forked stick to hang the old lantern from, because we had to light the lantern whenever we saw a steamboat coming downstream to avoid being hit. We did not need to light it for upstream boats unless we were in a “crossing.” The river was still high, and the low banks were partly underwater, so upstream boats did not always stay in the main channel but looked for easy water.

This second night we rowed for seven or eight hours, with a current moving at more than four miles an hour. We caught fish and talked, and we went for a swim now and then to keep sleepiness away. It felt a little solemn, drifting down the big, still river, lying on our backs and looking up at the stars. We never felt like speaking loudly, and we didn’t often laugh—only a soft, low chuckle. The weather was very good, and nothing happened to us that night, nor the next night, nor the one after.

Every night we passed towns, some high up on black hillsides, just a bright carpet of lights; you could not see any houses. On the fifth night we passed St. Louis, and it was like the whole world was lit up. In St. Petersburg they used to say there were twenty or thirty thousand people in St. Louis, but I didn’t believe it until I saw that amazing spread of lights at two o’clock that night. It was completely silent; everyone was asleep.

Every night I would go ashore around ten o’clock at a small village and buy ten or fifteen cents worth of food—maybe some meal, bacon, or something else. Sometimes I took a chicken that wasn’t roosting comfortably and brought it with me. Papa always said, “Take a chicken when you can, because if you don’t want it yourself you can easily find someone who does, and a good deed is never forgotten.” I never saw Papa when he didn’t want the chicken himself, but that’s what he used to say.

Before sunrise I would sneak into the cornfields and take a watermelon, a muskmelon, a pumpkin, some fresh corn—things like that. Dad always said it wasn’t wrong to borrow things if you planned to give them back later. The widow, however, called it just a nice word for stealing and said no decent person would do it.  

Jim thought the widow was partly right and Dad was partly right. He suggested we choose two or three items to stop taking. Then we could take the rest without feeling guilty.  

We talked about it one night while we floated down the river. We tried to decide whether to stop taking watermelons, cantaloupes, muskmelons, or something else. By sunrise we had made a decision. We chose to stop taking crabapples and persimmons. We hadn’t felt comfortable before, but now everything felt right. I was glad, because crabapples never taste good and the persimmons wouldn’t be ripe for another two or three months.  

We sometimes shot a waterfowl. We got up early in the morning or stayed up late at night. All in all, we lived a pretty lively life.

The fifth night after we left St. Louis, a strong storm came after midnight. Thunder boomed and lightning flashed, and the rain fell in a solid sheet. We stayed in our shelter and let the raft drift on its own. When the lightning lit up the sky, we could see a wide, straight river ahead, with high, rocky bluffs on both sides. After a moment I shouted, “Hello, Jim, look over there!” It was a steamboat that had crashed onto a rock. We were drifting straight toward it. The lightning made her shape very clear. She was tilted, with part of her upper deck sticking out of the water. You could see every small detail, even a chair by the big bell with an old slouch hat hanging on its back, whenever the flashes came.  

Because it was night, stormy, and everything seemed mysterious, I felt the same way any boy would feel when he sees a wreck lying there, sad and lonely in the middle of the river. I wanted to climb aboard and sneak around a bit to see what was inside. So I said,  

“Let’s land on her, Jim.”  

But Jim was against the idea at first. He said,  

“I don’t want to go out onto that wreck. We’re doing fine and we should leave it alone, as the good book says. There might be a watchman on that wreck.”

“Watch out, your grandmother,” I said. “There’s nothing to watch except the Texas and the pilot house. Do you think anyone would risk his life for a Texas and a pilot house on a night like this, when the ship could break up and sink down the river any minute?” Jim had no answer, so he stayed silent.  

“And besides,” I continued, “we could take something valuable from the captain’s cabin. I bet the seagulls—five cents each, solid cash. Steamboat captains are always rich; they earn sixty dollars a month and they don’t care how much something costs, as long as they want it. Put a candle in your pocket; I can’t rest, Jim, until we search the wreck.”  

“Do you think Tom Sawyer would ever try this? He wouldn’t do it for money. He would call it an adventure, and he would jump onto that wreck if it were his last act. And wouldn’t he make a show of it? He would act like Christopher Columbus discovering a new world. I wish Tom Sawyer were here.”  

Jim muttered a little, but he gave in. He said we should not speak louder than necessary and keep our voices very low. The lightning flashed the wreck again just in time, and we took the board derrick and fastened it there.

The deck was high up here. We slipped down its slope in the dark, moving toward the area we called the “texas.” We felt our way slowly with our feet and stretched out our hands to keep the other men away, because it was so dark we could not see any sign of them. Soon we reached the front edge of the skylight and climbed onto it. The next step took us to the captain’s open door. Down the hallway we could see a light, and at the same moment we heard faint voices nearby.

Jim whispered that he felt very ill and told me to follow him. I answered, “Okay,” and was about to head for the raft. At that moment a voice cried out, “Please, don’t, boys; I swear I will never tell!” Another, louder voice replied, “That’s a lie, Jim Turner. You have acted like this before. You always want more than your share of the cargo, and you get it because you threaten to tell if you don’t get it. This time you’ve said it one time too many. You are the most selfish, treacherous person in this country.”

By this time Jim had gone for the raft. I was trembling with curiosity, and I told myself that Tom Sawyer wouldn’t back down now, so I wouldn’t either. I was going to see what was happening. I dropped to my hands and knees in the narrow passage and crept forward in the dark until there was only one small room between me and the main hallway of the house. Inside, I saw a man lying on the floor, tied up hand and foot, with two men standing over him. One held a dim lantern, the other held a pistol. The man with the pistol kept pointing it at the bound man’s head and saying, “I’d love to! And I’m a mean skunk!”

The man on the floor shrank back and said, “Please don’t, Bill; I’ll never tell.” Every time he said that, the man with the lantern laughed and said, “You’re not lying! You’ve never spoken a truer thing.” He added, “Listen to him beg! If we hadn’t overpowered him and tied him up, he would have killed us both. And why? Just for nothing. Just because we claimed we were in our right. But I’m telling you you’re not going to threaten anyone any more, Jim Turner. Put that pistol down, Bill.”

Bill replied, “I don’t want to, Jake Packard. I want to kill him—didn’t he kill old Hatfield the same way? Doesn’t he deserve it?”

“I don’t want him killed, and I have my reasons.”

“Thank you for those words, Jake Packard! I’ll never forget you as long as I live!” says the man on the floor, almost sobbing.

Packard ignored him. He hung his lantern on a nail, walked toward where I was in the dark, and signaled Bill to follow. I scrambled as fast as I could for about two yards, but the boat was tilted, so I couldn’t move quickly. To avoid being run over or caught, I slipped into a stateroom on the upper deck. The man shuffled along in the darkness, and when Packard reached my stateroom, he said:

“Come in here.”

He entered, and Bill followed. Before they got inside, I was already in the upper berth, cornered and feeling sorry for myself. They stood with their hands on the edge of the berth and talked. I couldn’t see them, but I could guess where they were by the smell of the whisky they had been drinking. I was glad I hadn’t drunk any whisky; it wouldn’t have mattered much anyway, because most of the time they couldn’t hear me since I was holding my breath. I was too scared. Besides, a person can’t breathe and hear such a conversation at the same time.

They spoke quietly and seriously. Bill wanted to kill Turner. He said:

“He said he’ll talk, and he will. If we gave both our shares to him now, it wouldn’t change anything after the fight and the way we’ve treated him. If you’re born here, he’ll become a state witness; listen to me. I’m ready to get rid of him.”

“I’m the same,” Packard replied, very quietly.

“Honestly, I was starting to think you weren’t. Well, that’s fine. Let’s go and do it.”

“Wait a minute; I haven’t had my say yet. Listen to me. Shooting works, but there are quieter ways if we have to get the job done. Here’s what I think: it isn’t sensible to chase a horse if you can achieve the same result in a way that’s just as good and doesn’t put you at risk. Right?”

“Exactly. But how are you going to handle it this time?”

“My plan is this: we’ll rummage around and collect any supplies we missed in the cabins, then head for shore and hide the truck. After that we’ll wait. I doubt it will be more than two hours before this wreck breaks up and washes down the river. See? He’ll drown and have nobody to blame but himself. I think that’s a lot better than killing him. I’m against killing a man if there’s another way; it’s not sensible, it’s not moral. Am I right?”

“Yes, I think you are. But what if she doesn’t break up and wash away?”

“Then we can still wait the two hours and see, can’t we?”

“Alright, then; let’s go.”

So they started, and I ran out, sweating and hurrying forward. It was as dark as night; I whispered roughly, “Jim!” and he answered right at my elbow with a sort of groan, and I said:

“Quick, Jim, there’s no time to waste or complain. A gang of killers is nearby. If we don’t find their boat and let it drift down the river, those men won’t be able to escape the wreck, and one of them will be in serious trouble. If we do find the boat, we can get all of them into trouble—the sheriff will catch them. Hurry! I’ll search the left side, you search the right. Start at the raft, and—”

“Oh my lord! ‘Raft’? There’s no raft left. It’s broken and gone. I— and here we are!”
