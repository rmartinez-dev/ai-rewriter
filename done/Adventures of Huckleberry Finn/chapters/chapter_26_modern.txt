When everyone had left, the king asked Mary Jane if she had any spare rooms. She said she had one spare room that could be used for Uncle William. She would give her own larger room to Uncle Harvey, and she would move into the room with her sisters and sleep on a cot. In the attic there was a small cubby with a pallet. The king said the cubby would be fine for his valet—that meant me.

So Mary Jane took us upstairs and showed us our rooms. They were simple but nice. She said she would take her dresses and many other things out of her room if they were in Uncle Harvey’s way, but he said they were not. The dresses were hung along the wall, and in front of them was a calico curtain that reached the floor. In one corner there was an old hair trunk, and in another a guitar case, together with all kinds of little knick‑knacks, the kind girls usually fill a room with. The king said the room felt cozier and nicer because of these items, so we should not move them. The duke’s room was quite small, but it was more than enough, and so was my cubby.

That night they had a big dinner, and all the men and women were there. I stood behind the king’s chair and the duke’s chair and served them, while the servants served the rest of the guests.  

Mary Jane sat at the head of the table with Susan beside her. She complained about the biscuits, the preserves, and the fried chicken—just the usual kind of gripe women make to sound polite. Everyone knew everything was fine and they said things like, “How do you get the biscuits to brown so nicely?” and “Where on earth did you get these amazing pickles?” It was the typical small talk people have at a dinner, you know.  

When the meal was over, the hare‑lip and I ate the leftovers in the kitchen while the others helped the servants clean up. The hare‑lip started asking me about England, and I felt the ice getting a little thin. She asked, “Did you ever see the king?”  

I said, “Who? William IV? Well, I think I have—he goes to our church.” I knew he had been dead for years, but I didn’t say that. She asked, “Is that regular?”  

“Yes, regular. His seat is right across from ours, on the other side of the pulpit.”  

“I thought he lived in London?”  

“Well, he does. Where else would he live?”  

“But I thought you lived in Sheffield?”  

I realized I was in trouble. I had to pretend to choke on a chicken bone so I could buy some time to think of a way out. Then I said:

“I mean, he goes to our church regularly when he’s in Sheffield. That’s only in the summer, when he comes to take sea baths.”

“Why, how can you say that—Sheffield isn’t on the sea.”

“Well, who said it was?”

“Why, you did.”

“I didn’t either.”

“You did!”

“I didn’t.”

“You did.”

“I never said anything like that.”

“Well, what did you say then?”

“I said he comes to take sea baths—that’s what I said.”

“Well, then how is he going to take sea baths if it isn’t on the sea?”

“Look,” I say, “have you ever seen any Congress water?”

“Yes.”

“Did you have to go to Congress to get it?”

“No.”

“Well, William the Fourth doesn’t have to go to the sea to get a sea bath either.”

“How does he get it then?”

“He gets it the same way people here get Congress water—in barrels. In the palace at Sheffield they have furnaces, and he wants his water hot. They can’t bring that much water from the sea. They don’t have any facilities for it.”

“Oh, I see now. You could have said that at the start and saved time.”

When she said that I realized I was out of trouble again, and I felt comfortable and glad. Then she asks:

“Do you go to church, too?”

“Yes—regularly.”

“Where do you sit?”

“In our pew.”

“Whose pew?”

“Your Uncle Harvey’s.”

“His? What does he want with a pew?”

“He wants to sit in it. What do you think he wants with it?”

“Why, I thought he’d be in the pulpit.”

Damn it, I forgot he was a preacher. I realized I was in trouble again, so I tried another trick and thought about it. Then I said:

“Do you think a church can have only one preacher?”

“Why would they want more?”

“What? To preach before a king? I've never seen anyone like you. They have at least seventeen.”

“Seventeen? My goodness! I wouldn’t even try something like that unless I was sure I’d succeed. It would take them a week.”

“Actually, they don’t all preach on the same day—only one does.”

“So what do the others do?”

“Not much. They lounge around, pass the plate, and generally do nothing.”

“Then why do they exist?”

“They’re just for show. Don’t you know that?”

“I don’t want to hear any of that nonsense. How are servants treated in England? Do they treat them better than we treat our black people?”

“No! A servant isn’t respected there. They’re treated worse than dogs.”

“Don’t they get holidays like we do—Christmas, New Year’s week, and the Fourth of July?”

“Listen, you could tell someone has never been to England by that. They never have holidays from the end of one year to the next; they never go to the circus, theater, or any shows, and they never have any celebrations.”

“Not even church?”

“Not even church.”

“But you always went to church.”

I went up again and forgot that I was the old man's servant. The next minute I started trying to explain how a valley was different from an ordinary servant and that he had to go to church whether he wanted to or not, because the law required it. I didn't explain it very well. When I finished, she was not satisfied. She said:

“Honest Indian, haven’t you been telling me a lot of lies?”

“Honest Indian,” I replied.

“None at all?”

“None at all. Not a single lie,” I said.

“Put your hand on this book and say it.”

I saw that it was only a dictionary, so I put my hand on it and said the words. She then looked a little more satisfied and said:

“Well, then I’ll believe some of it, but I hope you’ll be kind if I don’t believe the rest.”

“What don’t you believe, Joe?” asked Mary Jane, stepping in with Susan behind her. “It isn’t right or kind for you to speak to him that way, especially since he’s a stranger far from his own people. How would you like to be treated?”

“That’s always your way, ma'am—always stepping in to help someone before they’re hurt. I haven’t done anything to him. He’s told some stories, I think, and I said I wouldn’t accept them all; that’s exactly what I said. I think he can handle a little bit like that, can’t he?”

“I don’t care if it was small or big. He’s in our house and he’s a stranger, and it wasn’t right of you to say that. If you were in his place you would feel ashamed, so you shouldn’t say anything that makes them feel ashamed.

‘Why, Mom, he said—’

“It doesn’t matter what he said. That isn’t the point. The point is that you should treat him kindly and not say things that remind him he isn’t in his own country or with his own people.”

I thought to myself, this is a girl I’m letting that old crook steal her money!

Then Susan walked in, and if you believe me, she really gave Hare‑lip a hard time!

I told myself, this is another one I’m letting him take money from.

Then Mary Jane took another turn and went in sweet and lovely again—that’s how she does things. When she finished, there was almost nothing left of poor Hare‑lip. So she shouted.

“All right,” said the other girls, “just ask his forgiveness.”

She did it, too, and she did it beautifully. She did it so beautifully it sounded good, and I wish I could tell her a thousand lies so she could do it again.”

I said to myself, this is another case where I’m letting him steal her money. When she arrived, they all pretended to make me feel welcome, as if I were among friends. I felt angry, down, and miserable, so I told myself that my mind was made up. I would get that money for them, or I would die trying.

I set off, saying I was going to bed, meaning I’d be back sometime later. When I was alone, I started thinking it over. I asked myself, “Should I go to that doctor privately and expose these frauds?” No, that wouldn’t work. He might reveal who told him, and then the king and the duke would make things difficult for me. Should I go privately and tell Mary Jane? No, I can’t do that. Her reaction would give them a clue. They have the money, and they would just slip away and get away with it. If she called for help, I’d get tangled up in the mess before it was over, I think. No, there isn’t any good way except one. I have to steal that money somehow, and I have to do it without raising suspicion. They have a good thing going here, and they won’t leave until they’ve squeezed this family and this town for all they’re worth, so I’ll wait for the right moment. I’ll steal it and hide it, and later, when I’m away down the river, I’ll write a letter to Mary Jane telling her where it’s hidden. But I’d rather do it tonight if I can, because the doctor may not be as calm as he pretends; he might still be able to scare them away.

So I thought I’d go and search those rooms. Upstairs the hallway was dark, but I found the duke’s room and began feeling around with my hands. Then I remembered that the king would never let anyone else handle that money except himself, so I went to his room and started feeling around there. I soon realized I couldn’t do anything without a candle, and I wasn’t allowed to light one. So I decided I would just lie down and eavesdrop.

At that moment I heard their footsteps coming, and I was about to slip under the bed. I reached for it, but it wasn’t where I expected. Instead I touched the curtain that covered Mary Jane’s dresses, so I jumped behind it and hid among the gowns, standing perfectly still.

They came in and shut the door. The first thing the duke did was get down and look under the bed. I was glad I hadn’t found the bed when I wanted it. Still, it’s natural to hide under the bed when you’re up to something private. They sat down, and the king said:

“Well, what is it? Let’s get to the point, because it’s better for us to be down there shouting about the mourning than up here giving them a chance to talk us over.”

“Alright, Capet, I’m not comfortable. That doctor is on my mind. I want to know your plans. I have an idea, and I think it’s a good one.”

“What is it, duke?”

We should get out of this before three in the morning and take it down the river with what we have. Especially since we got it so easily—handed back to us, thrown in our faces, as you might say, when we were forced to take it back. I think we should leave right away.

That made me feel very bad. An hour or two earlier I would have felt differently, but now I feel sad and disappointed. The king shouted and said:

“What! And not sell the rest of the property? Run away like a bunch of fools and leave eight or nine thousand dollars' worth of property just lying there for someone to take? And it’s all good, sellable stuff too.”

The duke grumbled. He said the bag of gold was enough and he didn’t want to go any further. He didn’t want to take everything from a lot of orphans.

“What are you saying?” the king replied. “We are not going to take anything from them except this money. The people who buy the property will be the ones who suffer, because as soon as it is discovered that we don’t own it—which will happen soon after we finish—the sale will be invalid and everything will return to the estate. These orphans will get their house back again, and that is enough for them. They are young and healthy and can easily earn a living. They will not suffer. Think about it—there are thousands and thousands of people who are not as well off. Fortunately, they have nothing to complain about.”

Well, the king talked to him blindly, so finally he gave in and said it was all right. He also said he thought staying was foolish and that the doctor was hanging over them.

The king then said, “Curse the doctor! What do we care about him? Haven’t we got all the fools in town on our side? Isn’t that a big enough majority in any town?”

They got ready to go downstairs again. The duke said, “I don’t think we put that money in a good place.”

That made me feel better. I had started to think I wasn’t going to get any help. The king asked, “Why?”

“Because Mary Jane will be in mourning because of this, and first the Black man who cleans the rooms will get an order to box these clothes up and store them. Do you think a Black man can find money and not borrow some of it?”

“Your head’s level again, duke,” says the king. He fumbles under the curtain, only two or three feet from where I am. I press myself against the wall and stay very still, though I am shaking. I wonder what those men would say if they caught me, and I try to think what I should do if they do. But the king grabs the bag before I can finish even a half‑thought, and he never suspects I am there. They push the bag through a hole in the straw mattress under the feather‑bed and pack it in a foot or two of straw. They say it is all right now because only a servant uses the feather‑bed, and they only turn the straw mattress over twice a year, so it isn’t in danger of being stolen.

But I know better. I take it out before they are halfway down the stairs. I feel my way to my small cubby and hide it there until I can find a better place. I decide to hide it outside the house because, if they miss it, they will search the house thoroughly. I know that well. Then I go back inside, with all my clothes on, but I cannot go to sleep even if I want to. I am sweating and nervous about finishing the job. After a while I hear the king and the duke coming up. I roll off my pallet and lie with my chin on the top of the ladder, waiting to see what will happen. Nothing happens.

So I waited until the later sounds had stopped and the early sounds hadn’t started yet. Then I slid down the ladder.
