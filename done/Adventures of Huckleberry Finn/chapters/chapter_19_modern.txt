Two or three days and nights passed. I would say they went by smoothly and quietly, almost beautifully. This is how we spent the time. Down there was a huge river—sometimes a mile and a half wide. We travelled at night, rested and hid during the day. As soon as the night was almost over, we stopped moving and tied up, usually in the calm water near a tow‑head. We then cut down young cottonwoods and willows and used them to hide our raft. After that we set up our lines.

We slid into the river and swam to freshen up and cool down. Then we settled on the sandy bottom where the water reached our knees and waited for daylight. It was completely silent, as if the whole world were asleep, except for occasional croaking of bullfrogs.

When we looked across the water, the first thing we saw was a faint line of trees on the other side. Beyond that was a pale sky that grew lighter and lighter. The river farther away turned from black to gray. Small dark spots drifted far off—trading scows and similar vessels. Long black lines marked other rafts. Sometimes a sweep creaked, or distant voices could be heard because everything was so still. Eventually we could see a streak on the water that meant a snag was in a swift current, causing the water to flow around it. Mist rose from the surface, the east sky reddened, and the river reflected the color. In the woods on the far bank we could make out a log cabin, probably a woodshed, piled with timber so a dog could run through it.

A pleasant breeze then blew from that direction, cooling us and bringing fresh, sweet smells of woods and flowers. At times the smell was not pleasant because dead fish—gar and other kinds—were left to rot, making the air rank. Later the whole day would be bright, the sun smiling on everything, and the songbirds would sing.

A little smoke couldn't be seen now, so we took some fish off the lines and cooked a hot breakfast. Afterwards we watched the loneliness of the river, feeling lazy, and soon we fell asleep. We woke up a little later and looked to see what was happening. Maybe we saw a steamboat chugging upstream far away on the other side. We couldn't tell much about it except whether it had a stern wheel or a side wheel. Then for about an hour there was nothing to hear or see—just solid emptiness. After that a raft drifted by far away, and maybe a rough fellow was chopping wood on it, because they usually do that on rafts. You could see the axe flash and come down, but you didn't hear anything. When the axe rose again, by the time it was above the man's head you heard a thunk—it had taken all that time to hit the water. So we spent the day lazily around, listening to the stillness. Once there was a thick fog, and the rafts and other things passing by rang tin pans so the steamboats wouldn't run into them. A scow or a raft passed so close we could hear the people talking, cussing and laughing clearly, but we couldn't see any sign of them. It made you feel uneasy, like spirits were moving through the air. Jim said he thought they were spirits, but I said:

“No; spirits wouldn't say, ‘Darn the darn fog.’”

When night fell we pushed the raft. When we got it to about the middle of the river we left it alone and let it drift wherever the current wanted. Then we lit some pipes, dangled our legs in the water and talked about all kinds of things. We were always naked, day and night, whenever the mosquitoes would let us. The new clothes Buck’s family made for me were too fancy to be comfortable, and I didn’t like wearing clothes anyway.

Sometimes we had the whole river to ourselves for a long time. On the other side were banks and islands, and sometimes a candle glowed in a cabin window. Occasionally you could see a light or two on a raft or a scow, and you might hear a fiddle or a song coming from one of those boats. Living on a raft was lovely. We had the sky above us, full of stars. We would lie on our backs, look up and talk about whether the stars were made or just happened. Jim thought they were made; I thought they just happened because it would take too long to make so many. Jim said the moon could have laid them, and that sounded reasonable, so I didn’t argue. I had seen a frog lay many eggs, so maybe it could be done. We also watched shooting stars streak across the sky. Jim said they had been spoiled and were out of the nest.

Sometimes at night we would see a steamboat gliding through the darkness. Every now and then it would throw a whole cloud of sparks from its chimneys, and the sparks would fall into the river, looking very pretty. Then the boat would turn a corner, its lights would go out, and its steam would stop, leaving the river quiet again. A while later the waves from the boat would reach us, long after it had disappeared, and they would make the raft shake a little. After that, we would hear nothing for a long time, except maybe the croaking of frogs.

After midnight the people onshore went to bed, and for two or three hours the shore was completely dark—no more sparks shining from the cabin windows. Those sparks were our clock. When the first spark appeared again, it meant that morning was near, so we quickly found a place to hide and tie up.

One morning, just after sunrise, I found a canoe and crossed a channel to the main shore. It was only about two hundred yards away. I paddled roughly a mile up a creek through the cypress woods, hoping to find some berries.

As I passed a spot where a small footpath crossed the creek, a couple of men ran up the path as fast as they could. I thought I was doomed, because whenever someone chased someone else, I expected it to be me—or maybe Jim.

I was ready to run away quickly, but the men were close to me. They shouted and begged me to help them. They said they hadn’t done anything wrong, but men and dogs were coming after them. They wanted to jump into the water, but I told them:

“Don’t do that. I don’t hear any dogs or horses yet. You still have time to move through the brush a little farther up the creek, then wade down to me and get in. That will throw the dogs off your scent.”

They did it, and as soon as they were on board I headed for our towhead. After about five or ten minutes we heard dogs and men shouting in the distance. We heard them coming toward the creek, but we couldn't see them. They seemed to stop and play around for a while. Then, as we moved farther away, we could barely hear them. By the time we had left a mile of woods behind us and reached the river, everything was quiet. We paddled to the towhead, hid among the cottonwood trees, and were safe.

One of the men was about seventy years old, with a bald head and very gray whiskers. He wore a worn‑out slouch hat, a greasy blue wool shirt, and ragged blue jeans that were tucked into his boots. He had a single homemade sack. Over his arm he draped an old long‑tailed blue coat with shiny brass buttons. He and the other man each carried a large, shabby carpet bag.

The other man was about thirty years old and dressed almost as raggedly. After breakfast we all stopped working and talked. The first thing we learned was that these two men did not know each other.

“What got you into trouble?” the bald‑headed man asked the other.

“Well, I had been selling a product that removes tartar from teeth. It does that, but it usually takes some enamel away too. I stayed one night longer than I should have, and I was just about to leave when I ran into you on the trail outside town. You told me they were coming and begged me to help you get away. I told you I was expecting trouble myself, so I would run away with you. That’s the whole story—what’s yours?”

“Well, I had been running a small temperance revival there for about a week. The women, both big and small, liked me because I was making it very hot for the drinkers, I tell you. I was earning as much as five or six dollars a night—ten cents per person, children and Black people free—and the business kept growing. Then a rumor spread last night that I was secretly serving a private jug. A Black man woke me up this morning and said the people were gathering near the quiet with their dogs and horses. They would be there soon and give me about half an hour’s warning before they tried to catch me. If they caught me, they would tar and feather me and hang me on a rail, for sure. I didn’t wait for breakfast—I wasn’t hungry.”

“Old man,” said the young one, “I think we could work together. What do you think?”

“I’m not opposed. What’s your main line?”

“By trade I’m a journeyman printer, and I dabble in patent medicines. I act in tragedies at the theater, and I try mesmerism and phrenology whenever I get the chance. I also teach singing and geography for a change, and I give a lecture now and then. I do a lot of different things—anything that’s handy—so I’m not really settled. What about you?”

“I’ve done a lot of medical work in my time. Laying on hands is my specialty—cancer, paralysis, and things like that. I can read fortunes fairly well when someone helps me gather the facts. Preaching is also my line, as is running camp meetings and doing missionary work.”

No one said anything for a while. Then the young man sighed and said:

“Alas!”

“What are you crying about?” asked the bald man.

“I can’t believe I’ve ended up living this way and have fallen into this company.” He began to wipe the corner of his eye with a rag.

“Come on, isn’t this company good enough for you?” the bald man replied, sounding a bit sarcastic.

“Yes, it’s good enough for me; it’s exactly what I deserve. Who brought me down when I was high? I did it myself. I don’t blame you, gentlemen—far from it; I don’t blame anyone. I deserve all of this. Let the cold world do its worst. I know one thing: there’s a grave somewhere for me. The world can keep going as it always has, taking everything from me—loved ones, property, everything—but it can’t take that. One day I’ll lie in that grave, forget it all, and my poor, broken heart will finally rest.” He kept wiping.

"Drop your broken heart," says the bald man. "Why are you yelling your broken heart at us? We haven't done anything."

"No, I know you haven't. I'm not blaming you, gentlemen. I brought myself down—yes, I did it myself. It's right that I should suffer—perfectly right. I don't complain."

"Brought you down from where? Where were you brought down from?"

"Ah, you wouldn't believe me. The world never believes. Let it go—it's no matter. The secret of my birth—"

"The secret of your birth! Do you mean—"

"Gentlemen," says the young man, very solemn, "I will tell you, because I think I can trust you. By right I am a duke!"

Jim's eyes widened when he heard that; I think mine did too. Then the bald man says, "No! You can't be serious?"

"Yes. My great‑grandfather, the eldest son of the Duke of Bridgewater, fled to this country around the end of the last century to enjoy freedom. He married here and died, leaving a son. His own father died at about the same time. The second son of the late duke took the titles and estates. The infant, the real duke, was ignored. I am the direct descendant of that infant. I am the rightful Duke of Bridgewater. And here I am, miserable, torn from my high estate, hunted by men, despised by the cold world, ragged, worn, heart‑broken, and reduced to keeping company with thieves on a raft!"

Jim felt a lot of pity for him, and so did I. We tried to comfort him, but he said it wasn't very useful; he couldn't be comforted much. He said that if we were willing to acknowledge him, that would help him more than anything else. So we said we would, if he told us how. He said we should bow when we spoke to him and call him “Your Grace,” “My Lord,” or “Your Lordship.” He didn't mind if we called him simply “Bridgewater,” which he said was a title, not a name. He also wanted one of us to serve him at dinner and do any small task he asked for.

That was easy, so we did it. During dinner Jim stood around and waited on him, asking, “Will Your Grace have some of this or that?” It was clear that the duke liked it.

But the old man became very quiet after a while. He didn't say much and didn't seem comfortable with all the attention. He seemed to have something on his mind. In the afternoon he said:

“Listen, Bilgewater,” he said, “I'm really sorry for you, but you're not the only one who has had trouble like that.”

“Not?”

“No, you're not. You're not the only person who has been wrongly pushed out of a high position.”

“Alas!”

“No, you're not the only person who has a secret about his birth.” And, by God, he began to cry.

“Wait! What do you mean?”

“Bilgewater, can I trust you?” the old man said, still sobbing.

"To the bitter death!" He took the old man's hand and squeezed it. "Tell me your secret!"

"Bilgewater, I am the late Dauphin!"

"You guessed it, Jim and I stared. Then the duke said:

'What do you mean?'

'Yes, my friend, it's true—your eyes are looking at the former Dauphin, Looy the Seventeenth, son of Looy the Sixteenth and Mary Antonette.'

'You! At your age! No, you must be the late Charlemagne; you must be at least six or seven hundred years old.'

'Trouble caused it, Bilgewater. Trouble gave me these gray hairs and early baldness. Yes, gentlemen, you see before you, in blue jeans and misery, the wandering, exiled, oppressed, and suffering rightful King of France.'

Well, he cried and reacted so badly that Jim and I hardly knew what to do. We felt terrible, but we were also glad and proud that we had him with us. So we sat down, just like we had done before with the duke, and tried to comfort him. He said it was no use; nothing could help him except to be dead and done with it all. He added that it sometimes made him feel a little better when people treated him with respect. He wanted them to kneel before him, always call him “Your Majesty,” serve him first at meals, and not sit down until he asked them to. So Jim and I started treating him like a king. We did this and that for him and stayed standing until he told us we could sit down. That helped him a lot, and he became cheerful and comfortable. But the duke became a bit sour about him and didn’t seem satisfied with how things were going. Still, the king was very friendly toward him and said the duke’s great‑grandfather and all the other Dukes of Bilgewater were well regarded by his father and were allowed to come to the palace often. The duke stayed angry for a while until finally the king said:

It’s likely we’ve been together for a long time on this raft, Bilgewater, so why are you being so sour? It will only make things uncomfortable. It’s not my fault I wasn’t born a duke, and it’s not your fault you weren’t born a king—so there’s no point in worrying. My motto is to make the best of things the way you find them. This isn’t a bad situation at all—we have plenty of food and an easy life. Come, shake my hand, Duke, and let’s all be friends.

The duke did it, and Jim and I were really glad to see it. It removed all the awkwardness and made us feel very good, because having any unfriendliness on the raft would have been terrible. What you want most on a raft is for everyone to be satisfied, to feel right, and to be kind to each other.

It didn’t take me long to realize that these liars weren’t kings or dukes at all, just low‑down cheats. I never said anything; I kept it to myself because that’s the safest way. Then there are no arguments and no trouble. If they wanted us to call them kings and dukes, I had no objections as long as it kept peace in the group. I didn’t tell Jim either, because it wasn’t useful. The only thing I learned from my parents is that the best way to get along with people like them is to let them have their way.
