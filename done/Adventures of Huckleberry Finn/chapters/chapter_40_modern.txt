We felt pretty good after breakfast, so I took my canoe and went out on the river to fish, bringing a lunch. We had a good time, checked the raft and found it all right, and got home late for supper. When we arrived, they were sweating and worrying, not knowing what to do. They sent us straight to bed as soon as supper was over. They wouldn’t tell us what the trouble was, and they didn’t mention the new letter. That didn’t matter, because we knew as much about it as anyone else.

As soon as we were halfway up the stairs and her back was turned, we slipped into the cellar cupboard, packed a good lunch, took it up to our room, and went to bed. We got up about half‑past eleven. Tom put on Aunt Sally’s dress that he had stolen and was about to start lunch. He said:

“Where’s the butter?”

“I left a piece of it on a piece of cornbread,” I said.

“Well, you left it out, then—it isn’t here.”

“We can get along without it,” I said.

“We can get along with it, too,” he said. “Just go down to the cellar and fetch it. Then come back down the lightning‑rod and join me. I’ll put the straw into Jim’s clothes to pretend he’s his mother in disguise, and be ready to ba like a sheep and push as soon as you get there.”

He went out, and I went down into the cellar.  
The piece of butter, as big as a fist, was still where I had left it.  
I picked up the slab of cornpone with the butter on it, blew out my light, and quietly climbed the stairs.  
I reached the main floor safely, but then Aunt Sally came with a candle.  
I quickly put the bucket in my hat, then put the hat on my head.  
The next second she saw me and said:

“Did you go down to the cellar?”  

“Yes, I did.”  

“What were you doing down there?”  

“Nothing.”  

“Nothing!”  

“No, I wasn’t.”  

“Well, then why did you go down there at this time of night?”  

“I don’t know.”  

“You don’t know? Don’t answer me like that. Tom, I want to know what you were doing down there.”  

“I haven’t done a single thing, Aunt Sally. I would be grateful if I had.”  

I thought she might let me go, as she usually would. But there were so many strange things happening that she was nervous about everything that wasn’t exactly right. So she said firmly:

“Go straight into that sitting‑room and stay there until I come. You’ve been up to something that isn’t your business, and I promise I’ll find out what it is before I’m done with you.”

She left as I opened the door and walked into the sitting room.  
There was a crowd—fifteen farmers, and each one had a gun.  
I felt very sick, so I slumped into a chair and sat down.  

The men were sitting around, some speaking in low voices.  
They all looked nervous and fidgety, trying to seem calm.  
I could tell they were uneasy because they kept taking off and putting on their hats, scratching their heads, changing seats, and fiddling with their buttons.  
I wasn’t feeling well either, but I kept my hat on.  

I wished Aunt Sally would come, finish with me, and kiss me if she wanted to.  
Then I could go to Tom, tell him how we had messed up, and explain the huge trouble we’d gotten into.  
We could stop messing around right away and leave with Jim before the angry men lost patience and came after us.

Finally she came and began asking me questions, but I couldn’t answer right away. I didn’t know what to do because the men were so nervous. Some wanted to start immediately, acting like desperadoes, and claimed it was only a few minutes to midnight. Others tried to make them wait for the sheep signal.

Aunt kept firing off questions while I shook all over, ready to collapse. I was terrified. The room grew hotter and hotter, and the butter started to melt and run down my neck and behind my ears. 

Then one of them said, “I’m going to the cabin first, right now, and I’ll catch them when they come.” I froze. A streak of butter ran down my forehead. Aunt Sally saw it, turned pale as a sheet, and shouted:

“For heaven’s sake, what’s wrong with the child? He has a brain fever as soon as he’s born, and it’s spilling out!”

Everyone rushed over. She ripped off my hat, took out the bread and the remaining butter, grabbed me, hugged me, and said:

“Oh, what a twist you gave me! I’m glad and grateful it isn’t worse. Luck is against us, and when it rains it pours. When I saw that truck I thought we had lost you, because the colour reminded me of how your mind would be. Dear, dear, why didn’t you tell me that was why you went down there? I wouldn’t have cared. Now get out of bed, and don’t let me see you again until morning!”

I got upstairs in a moment, then down the lightning rod in another, and I shone a light through the darkness toward the lean‑to. I could barely speak because I was so nervous, but I told Tom as fast as I could that we had to jump now and not waste any time. The house was full of men with guns!

His eyes flared and he said, “No—really? Isn’t that great! Huck, if we could do it again, I think I could get two hundred! If we could wait until—”

“Hurry, hurry!” I said. “Where’s Jim?” “He’s right beside you; if you stretch out your arm you can touch him. He’s dressed and ready. Now we’ll slip out and give the sheep signal.”

Then we heard men walking toward the door. They started to struggle with the padlock. A man said, “I told you we were early; they haven’t arrived yet—the door is locked. I’ll lock some of you in the cabin, and you can wait in the dark and kill them when they come. The others should spread out and listen for any sounds of their approach.”

They came in, but they couldn’t see us in the dark. Most of them stepped on us while we were hurrying to get under the bed. We managed to get under, and then we slipped out through the hole. We moved quickly but quietly—Jim went first, I went second, and Tom went last, just as Tom had told us.

Now we were in the lean‑to and we could hear footsteps close by outside. We crept to the door, and Tom stopped us there. He put his eye to the crack, but it was too dark to see anything. He whispered that he would listen for the steps and tell us when it was safe. He told us that Jim should go out first and he would be the last.

Tom pressed his ear to the crack and listened, and listened, and listened. The steps kept scraping outside. Finally he gave us the signal. We slid out, crouched low, and didn’t breathe or make a sound. We moved silently toward the fence in a line, just like the Indians. Jim and I got over the fence without trouble.

Tom’s trousers snagged on a splinter on the top rail. He heard the footsteps coming, so he tried to pull free. The splinter snapped and made a noise. As he fell, someone shouted,

“Who’s that? Answer, or I’ll shoot!”

We didn’t answer. We just lifted our heels and pushed forward. A rush of gunfire erupted—bang, bang, bang! The bullets whizzed around us. The men shouted,

“Here they are! They’ve broken for the river! After them, boys, and let the dogs loose!”

Here they come, running at full speed. We could hear them because they wore boots and yelled, but we had no boots and we did not yell. We were on the road to the mill. When they got close, we slipped into the bushes and let them pass, then followed them from behind.

They had kept all the dogs quiet so they wouldn’t scare away the robbers, but by then someone had released the dogs. The dogs came running, barking loudly, but they were our dogs. We stopped until the dogs caught up with us. When the dogs realized it was only us and there was nothing interesting for them, they just said hello and ran toward the shouting and clattering.

We started moving again and raced after them until we were almost at the mill. Then we ran through the bushes to where my canoe was tied, jumped in, and paddled desperately toward the middle of the river, making only the noise we were allowed to make. After that we headed easily toward the island where my raft was.

We could hear the dogs barking and shouting at each other all along the bank until we were far enough away that the sounds faded. When I stepped onto the raft I said:

“Now, old Jim, you’re a free man again, and I bet you’ll never be a slave again.”

“You did a really good job, Huck. It was planned beautifully, and it was done beautifully; and no one can make a plan that’s more mixed‑up and splendid than that one.”

We were all as glad as we could be, but Tom was the happiest because he had a bullet in his calf.

When Jim and I heard that, we didn’t feel as bold as before. It hurt him a lot and he was bleeding, so we laid him in the wigwam and tore one of the duke’s shirts to bandage him. He said:

“Give me the rags; I can do it myself. Don’t stop now; don’t fool around here. The plan is working well; man the sweeps and set her free! Boys, we did it elegantly—indeed we did. I wish we had handled it like Louis XVI; then there wouldn’t be a line in his biography saying ‘Son of Saint Louis, ascend to heaven!’ No, sir, we would have beaten him over the border—that’s what we would have done with him—and we would have done it smoothly, without any trouble. Man the sweeps—man the sweeps!”

Jim and I talked and thought about it. After a minute I said, “Speak up, Jim.”

He answered, “Well, this is how I see it, Huck. If it were him who was so free, and one of the boys was about to be shot, would he say, ‘Go on and save me, never mind a doctor for this one?’ Is that like Tom Sawyer? Would he say that? You bet he wouldn’t! So, is Jim supposed to say it? No, sir—I wouldn’t take a step out of this place without a doctor, not even if it took forty years.”

I knew he was good at heart, and I thought he would say what he said—so it was all right now, and I told Tom I was going to get a doctor. He made a big fuss about it, but Jim and I stuck to our plan and wouldn’t change. He wanted to crawl out and set the raft loose himself, but we wouldn’t let him. Then he gave us a piece of his mind, but it didn’t help.

When he saw me getting the canoe ready, he said:

“Well, if you’re going to go, I’ll tell you what to do when you reach the village. Close the door and blindfold the doctor tightly, and make him swear to stay silent as a grave. Put a purse full of gold in his hand, then lead him through the back alleys and everywhere in the dark. Bring him back here in the canoe, taking a round route among the islands. Search him and take his chalk away, and don’t give it back until you get him back to the village, otherwise he will mark the raft so he can find it again. That’s how they all do it.”

I said I would, and left. Jim was to hide in the woods until the doctor arrived and then leave again.
