Well, I caught my breath and almost fainted. Stuck in a wreck with a gang like that! But there was no time to be sentimental. We had to find that boat right away – we needed it for ourselves. So we started shaking and trembling down the starboard side. It was slow work; it felt like a week before we reached the stern. There was no sign of a boat. Jim said he didn’t think he could go any farther – he was so scared he had almost no strength left. I told him, “Come on, if we stay on this wreck we’ll be in trouble.” So we kept moving. We headed for the stern of the texas, found it, and then scrambled forward on the deck, hanging from one shutter to another, because the edge of the deck was in the water. When we got close to the cross‑hall door, there was the skiff, just as we hoped! I could barely see it. I felt very grateful. In another moment I could have been on board, but then the door opened. One of the men stuck his head out only a couple of feet from me, and I thought I was finished; but he pulled his head back in and said:

“Put that damned lantern out of sight, Bill!”

He threw a bag of something into the boat, then got in himself and sat down. It was Packard. Then Bill came out and got in. Packard said quietly:

“All set – push off!”

I could barely hold onto the shutters; I was so weak. But Bill said:

“Hold on – did you get past him?”

“No. Didn’t you?”

“No. So he still has his share of the cash.”

“Well, then, come with me; there’s no point in taking the truck and leaving the money.”

“Do you think he’ll suspect what we’re doing?”

“Maybe he won’t. But we have to do it anyway. Come on.”

So they got out and went inside.

The door slammed shut because it was on the leaning side, and in a half‑second I was in the boat, with Jim tumbling after me. I pulled out my knife, cut the rope, and we were off!

We didn’t touch an oar, we didn’t speak or whisper, and we barely breathed. We glided quickly and silently past the tip of the paddle‑box and the stern. In another second or two we were a hundred yards below the wreck; the darkness swallowed it completely, and we knew we were safe.

When we were three or four hundred yards downstream we saw a lantern flare like a tiny spark at the Texas door for a moment. We realized from that that the crooks had missed their boat and were now in as much trouble as Jim Turner.

Then Jim took the oars, and we headed after our raft. This was the first time I began to worry about the men—I don’t think I’d ever been worried before. I started to think how terrible it was, even for murderers, to be in such a situation. I told myself there was no way to know if I might become a murderer someday, and wondered how I would feel about that. So I said to Jim:

When we see the first light, we’ll land about a hundred yards below or above it, in a place that’s a good hiding spot for you and the boat. Then I’ll make up a story and find someone to help that group get out of trouble, so they can be hanged when their time comes.  

But that plan failed. Soon a storm started again, worse than before. The rain fell hard and no light appeared; everyone was probably in bed. We kept moving down the river, watching for lights and for our raft. After a long time the rain stopped, but the clouds stayed and the lightning kept flashing. Finally a flash showed a black object ahead, floating, and we headed toward it.

It was the raft, and we were very glad to get back on it. We saw a light down on the right side of the shore, so I said I would go for it. The small boat was half full of the goods the gang had taken from the wreck. We hurried the boat onto the raft and piled the goods inside. I told Jim to drift downstream, show a light when he thought he had gone about two miles, and keep it burning until I returned. Then I took my oars and rowed toward the light.

As I got closer, three or four more lights appeared on a hillside. It was a village. I moved closer to the shore light, laid my oars down and floated. When I passed, I saw it was a lantern hanging on the pole of a double‑hull ferry‑boat. I looked for the watchman, wondering where he was sleeping, and soon found him sitting on the forward posts, his head down between his knees. I nudged his shoulder a few times and began to cry.

He woke up startled, but when he saw it was only me, he stretched out and said:

“Hello, what’s wrong? Don’t cry, kid. What’s the problem?”

I said:

“Dad, Mom, sister, and—”

Then I broke down. He replied:

“Oh, come on, don’t get so upset; we all have troubles, and this will be alright. What’s the matter with them?”

“They—are you the watchman of the boat?”

“Yes,” he says, sounding fairly satisfied. “I’m the captain, the owner, the mate, the pilot, the watchman, and the head deckhand. Sometimes I’m also the freight and the passengers. I’m not as rich as old Jim Hornback, and I can’t be as generous and generous to Tom, Dick, and Harry as he is, or spend money the way he does. But I’ve told him many times that I wouldn’t trade places with him. A sailor’s life is the life for me, and I’d rather not live two miles out of town where nothing ever happens, no matter how much money I’m offered. I say—”

I interrupt and say:

“They’re in a terrible mess, and—”

“Who is?”

“Your dad, your mom, your sister, and Miss Hooker. If you could take your ferry and go up there—”

“Up where? Where are they?”

“At the wreck.”

“What wreck?”

“There’s only one.”

“You don’t mean the Walter Scott?”

“Yes.”

“Goodness! What are they doing there, for heaven’s sake?”

“Well, they didn’t go there on purpose.”

“I bet they didn’t! Oh dear, they’ll be doomed if they don’t get off quickly! How on earth did they get into such trouble?”

“It’s simple. Miss Hooker was visiting up there in the town—”

“Yes, Booth’s Landing—go on.”

She was visiting Booth’s Landing, and just at dusk she set off with her Black servant in the horse‑ferry to spend the night at her friend's house—Miss What‑you‑may‑call‑her, I forget her name. They lost their steering oar, turned around and drifted stern‑first for about two miles. The wreck sank, and the ferryman, the Black woman and the horses were all lost, but Miss Hooker grabbed onto the wreck and got aboard.

About an hour after dark we came down in our trading scow. It was so dark we didn’t see the wreck until we were right on top of it, so we climbed onto it. Everyone was saved except Bill Whipple—and he was the best fellow! I wish it had been me, I do.

“My George! That’s the worst thing I’ve ever seen. And then what did you all do?”

“We shouted and tried to call for help, but the river was so wide we couldn’t be heard. So my dad said someone had to get ashore and find help. I was the only one who could swim, so I swam to shore. Miss Hooker told me that if I didn’t get help quickly, she would go after her uncle and he would fix the problem. I reached land about a mile downriver and have been wandering ever since, trying to get people to do something. They said, ‘What, at night with a strong current? It’s useless; take the steam ferry.’ Now if you’ll …

"By Jackson, I’d like to, and I’m sorry, I don’t know why, but I will. Who in the nation is going to pay for it? Do you think your pa—"

"That's all right. Miss Hooker told me specifically that her uncle Hornback—"

"Great guns! Is he her uncle? Listen, go toward that light over there, then head west when you get there. About a quarter of a mile out you’ll find the tavern. Tell them to send you to Jim Hornback’s, and he’ll pay the bill. Don’t mess around, because he’ll want to hear the news. Tell him I’ll have his niece safe before he even reaches town. Hurry up now; I’m going around the corner to wake my engineer."

I walked toward the light, but as soon as he turned the corner I went back, got into my skiff, bailed her out, and then pulled up on the shallow water about six hundred yards away. I hid among some wooden boats because I couldn’t relax until I could see the ferry start. In the end, I felt pretty good about all the trouble I took for that gang, since not many people would have done it. I hoped the widow knew about it. I thought she would be proud of me for helping those troublemakers, because the widow and good people are most interested in troublemakers and deadbeats.

Well, it wasn’t long before the wreck appeared, dim and dark, sliding down the river. A cold shiver ran through me, and I rushed toward it. The water was very deep, and I realized there was little chance anyone could still be alive in it. I pulled around it and shouted a little, but there was no answer; everything was still dead. I felt a little sad for the crew, but not much, because I thought that if they could survive it, I could too.

Then the ferry boat arrived, so I headed for the middle of the river on a long downstream stretch. When I thought I was out of sight, I lowered my oars, looked back and saw it go, and sniffed around the wreck for Miss Hooker’s belongings, because the captain would know her uncle Hornback wanted them. Soon the ferry boat turned away and went toward the shore, and I kept working and drifted down the river.

It seemed like a long time before Jim’s light appeared, and when it finally did, it looked as if it were a thousand miles away. By the time I got there, the sky was beginning to turn a little gray in the east. We went to an island, hid the raft, sank the small boat, turned in, and slept as if we were dead.
