I sneaked up to their doors and listened. They were snoring. I tiptoed down the stairs without making any noise. There was no sound anywhere. I peeked through the crack in the dining‑room door and saw the men who had been watching the corpse sound asleep in their chairs. The door opened onto the parlor where the corpse lay, and there was a candle in each room. I kept moving, and the parlor door was open, but there was nobody there except the remains of Peter, so I went on. The front door was locked, and the key was missing. 

Just then I heard someone coming down the stairs behind me. I ran into the parlor and looked around quickly. The only place I could hide the bag was in the coffin. The lid was lifted about a foot, showing the dead man's face down, covered with a wet cloth and his shroud. I slipped the money bag under the lid, just below where his crossed hands were. It made me shiver; it was so cold. Then I ran back across the room and hid behind the door.

The person coming in was Mary Jane. She went to the coffin, knelt down gently, and looked inside. She put her handkerchief to her face and began to cry, though I couldn't hear her. She had her back to me. I slipped out, and as I passed the dining‑room I checked that the watchers hadn't seen me. I looked through the crack, and everything was fine. They hadn't moved.

I went to bed feeling rather sad because of how everything turned out after all the trouble and risk I had taken. I thought to myself that if it could stay where it is, that would be fine, because when we travel a hundred or two miles down the river I could write to Mary Jane, and she could dig it up again and get it. But that isn’t what will happen. The money will be found when they tighten the lid. Then the king will get it again, and it will be a long day before he gives anyone another chance to take it from him. Of course I wanted to go down and take it out, but I didn’t try. Every minute it was getting nearer, and soon the guards would start moving, and I might be caught—caught with six thousand dollars in my hands that nobody hired me to look after. I don’t want to be involved in any business like that, I told myself.

When I went downstairs in the morning, the parlor was closed and the guards were gone. The only people there were the family, Widow Bartley, and our group. I watched their faces to see if anything had happened, but I couldn’t tell.

Towards the middle of the day the undertaker came with his assistant. They put the coffin in the middle of the room on a couple of chairs. Then they arranged all our chairs in rows and borrowed more from the neighbours until the hall, the parlor and the dining‑room were full. I saw that the coffin lid was the same as before, but I didn’t look underneath it because there were people around.

People began to come in, and the men and the girls took seats in the front row at the head of the coffin. For half an hour the visitors moved around slowly, one by one, and looked at the dead man’s face for a moment. Some shed a tear. It was very still and solemn. Only the girls and the men held handkerchiefs to their eyes, kept their heads bowed and sobbed a little. The only sounds were feet scraping on the floor and people blowing their noses—people always blow their noses more at a funeral than anywhere else, except church.

When the room was completely full, the undertaker moved around in his black gloves with his gentle, soothing ways. He added the final touches, made everything tidy and comfortable, and made no more sound than a cat. He never spoke. He moved people, squeezed in those who arrived late, opened passages, and did it with nods and hand signals. Then he took his place against the wall. He was the softest, most graceful, quietest man I have ever seen, and he never smiled at all.

They had borrowed a melodeon—a broken one. When everything was set up, a young woman sat down and played it. It sounded very shrill and out of tune, and everyone joined in and sang. Peter was the only one who seemed to enjoy it, at least in my opinion.

Then Reverend Hobson began his slow, solemn sermon. Suddenly a huge commotion erupted from the cellar—a noise louder than anything anyone had ever heard. It was just a dog, but it made an incredibly loud racket and kept it up the whole time. The preacher had to stand over the coffin and wait, while the noise made it impossible to hear anything else.

The situation was awkward, and no one seemed to know what to do. Soon, the tall undertaker signaled to the preacher, as if to say, “Don’t worry—just leave it to me.” He bent down and started sliding his shoulders along the wall, just above the heads of the people. He kept gliding while the uproar grew louder and louder. After he had walked around two sides of the room, he disappeared down into the cellar.

A couple of seconds later we heard a thump, and the dog let out an amazing howl, then everything fell silent. The preacher resumed his solemn speech where he had left off. A minute or two later the undertaker returned, his shoulders again sliding along the wall. He moved around three sides of the room, then rose, covered his mouth with his hands, stretched his neck toward the preacher over the crowd, and whispered roughly, “He had a rat!” He then dropped down and slid back to his place.

You could tell the people were very satisfied, because they naturally wanted to know what had happened. Small actions like this cost nothing, but they are the things that make a person respected and liked. There was no one more popular in town than that undertaker.

The funeral sermon was good, but it was long and boring. Then the king came in and said a few of his usual things, and finally the service ended. The undertaker started to close the coffin with his screwdriver. I was sweating and watching him closely. He didn’t interfere at all; he just slid the lid on gently, like soft mush, and screwed it shut tightly. So there I was. I didn’t know if the money was inside or not. I thought maybe someone had secretly taken the bag. How could I know whether to write to Mary Jane? If she dug him up and found nothing, what would she think of me? I thought I might get caught and go to jail. I should stay quiet and not write at all. The situation is a mess; trying to fix it only made it worse, and I wish I had just left it alone.

They buried him, and we went back home. I kept watching people’s faces—I couldn’t stop, and I couldn’t relax. But nothing came of it; the faces didn’t tell me anything.

The king visited in the evening, and he cheered everyone up. He acted very friendly. He said that his followers back in England would be worried about him, so he had to hurry, settle the estate, and go home. He was very sorry to be rushed, and everyone else was sorry too. They wished he could stay longer, but they knew it wasn’t possible. He said that he and William would take the girls home with them, and that pleased everyone. The girls would be with their own families, and that made them happy. They laughed so much they forgot any trouble they had had. They told him to sell everything as quickly as he wanted; they would be ready. Those poor girls were so glad that it made my heart ache to see them being fooled and misled, but I didn’t see a safe way to help or change things.

It would be a shame if the king didn’t put the house, the black servants, and all the property up for auction right away—a sale two days after the funeral. Anyone could buy privately beforehand if they wished.

The next day after the funeral, around noon, the girls’ happiness was disturbed. A couple of slave traders arrived, and the king sold the enslaved people at a reasonable price, on a three‑day payment plan. The two sons were taken up the river to Memphis, and their mother was taken down the river to New Orleans. I thought the poor girls and the enslaved people would be heart‑broken with grief; they cried together, and it made me feel sick to watch. The girls said they had never imagined seeing a family separated or sold away from the town. I will never forget the sight of the miserable girls and the enslaved people hugging each other and crying. I think I couldn’t have stood it, but I would have had to run away and tell on our group if I hadn’t known the sale was a trick and that the enslaved people would be back home in a week or two.

The incident caused a big stir in the town, and many people said it was scandalous to separate a mother from her children that way. It hurt some of the swindlers, but the old fool kept going, no matter what the duke said or did, and I can tell you the duke was very uneasy.

The next day was auction day. Early that morning, the king and the duke came up to the attic and woke me. From their looks I could tell something was wrong. The king asked, “Were you in my room the night before last?”

“No, your majesty”—that’s how I always called him when nobody but our gang was around.  

“Were you in there yesterday or last night?”  

“No, your majesty.”  

“Be honest now—no lies.”  

“Honestly, your majesty, I’m telling the truth. I haven’t been near your room since Miss Mary Jane took you and the duke and showed it to you.”  

The duke asked:  

“Have you seen anyone else go in there?”  

“No, your grace, not as I remember, I think.”  

“Stop and think.”  

I thought for a moment, saw my chance, and said:  

“Well, I saw the black men go in there several times.”  

Both of them jumped a little, looking surprised, then as if they had expected it. The duke then asked:  

“What, all of them?”  

“No—at least not all at once. I never saw them all come out together, only one at a time.”  

“Tell me! When was that?”  

“It was the day we had the funeral, in the morning. It wasn’t early because I overslept. I was just starting down the ladder when I saw them.”  

“Go on, go on! What did they do? How did they act?”  

“They didn’t do anything. They didn’t act much, as far as I could see. They tiptoed away, so I understood they had gone in to tidy your room or something, thinking you were up, and found you weren’t up. They were trying to leave without causing trouble or waking you, if they hadn’t already woken you.”

“Great guns, this is a go!” says the king, and both of them look a little sick and rather silly. They stand there thinking and scratching their heads for a minute. Then the duke bursts into a short, raspy chuckle and says:

“It’s amazing how cleverly the black men played their hand. They pretended to be sorry they were leaving this area. I believed they were sorry, and you did, and everybody did. Don’t ever tell me a black person lacks dramatic talent. The way they performed would fool anyone. In my opinion, there’s a fortune in them. If I had the money and a theater, I wouldn’t want a better set‑up than that—and we sold them for almost nothing. Yes, we’re not even allowed to sing the song yet. So, where is that song, the draft?”

“In the bank, waiting to be collected. Where would it be?”

“Well, that’s all right then, thank goodness.”

I say, a little timid:

“Has something gone wrong?”

The king turns to me and snaps:

“It’s none of your business! Keep your head down and mind your own affairs—if you have any. As long as you’re in this town, don’t forget that, understand?” Then he says to the duke, “We just have to swallow it and stay silent: mum’s the word for us.”

As they start down the ladder, the duke chuckles again and says:

“Quick sales and small profits! It’s a good business—yes.”

The king snarls at him and says:

I was trying to do my best in selling them quickly. If the profit turned out to be none, or very small, and there was nothing left to carry, is it my fault any more than yours?

Well, they'd still be in this house and we wouldn't be here if I could have gotten my advice listened to.

The king answered as much as was safe for him, then turned and shouted at me again. He scolded me for not coming and for telling him I saw the black men leave his room acting that way—any fool would have known something was wrong. Then he walked in, cursed himself for a while, and said it was all because he hadn't stayed up late and taken his normal rest that morning. He said he would be blamed if he ever did it again. So they started arguing, and I felt terribly glad I had taken it all out on the black men, and yet I hadn't harmed them by it.
