"Come in," the woman said, and I went inside. She added, "Have something to drink."

I did. She looked at me with her bright eyes and asked, "What is your name?"

"Sarah Williams."

"Where do you live? Are you from this neighborhood?"

"No, I'm from Hookerville, about seven miles away. I walked all the way here and I'm very tired."

"You must be hungry, too. I'll get you something."

"No, I'm not hungry. I was so hungry that I had to stop at a farm two miles back, so I'm not hungry now. That's why I'm late. My mother is sick, we have no money, and I came to see my uncle Abner Moore. He lives at the far end of town, she says. I've never been here before. Do you know him?"

"No, I don't, but I don't know everyone yet. I've only lived here for about two weeks. It's a long way to the far end of town. You should stay here for the night. Take off your hat."

"No," I said, "I'll rest a little and then keep going. I'm not afraid of the dark."

She said she wouldn’t let me go alone. Her husband would be back soon, maybe in an hour and a half, and she would send him with me. Then she started talking about her husband, about her relatives upriver and downriver, about how much better off they used to be, and how they thought it was a mistake to come to our town instead of leaving things alone. She kept going on and on until I began to feel afraid that I had made a mistake coming to her to find out what was happening in the town. Then she stopped talking about Pap and the murder, and I was ready to listen to her. She told me about me and Tom Sawyer finding the six thousand dollars (she only got ten), about Pap and how hard his life was, and how hard my life was. At last she got to the part about my murder. I said:

“Who did it? We’ve heard a lot about what’s going on down in Hookerville, but we don’t know who killed Huck Finn.”

“Well, I think many people here would like to know who killed him. Some think old Finn did it himself.”

“No—really?”

“Almost everyone thought that at first. He’ll never know how close he came to being lynched. But before nightfall they changed their minds and said it was done by a runaway black man named Jim.”

“Why him—”

I stopped. I thought it was better to stay quiet. She kept talking and never noticed that I had spoken at all.

The black man ran away the night Huck Finn was killed. There is a reward of three hundred dollars for him, and a reward of two hundred dollars for old Finn. He came to town the morning after the murder and told people about it. He joined the hunt on the ferry‑boat, but he left soon after. That night they tried to lynch him, but he was already gone.  

The next day they learned the black man had disappeared; no one had seen him since ten o’clock that night. So they blamed him. While they were still angry, old Finn came back the next day and begged Judge Thatcher for money to hunt the black man all over Illinois. The judge gave him some money.  

That evening he got drunk and stayed out past midnight with a couple of tough‑looking strangers, then left with them. He has not returned since, and they are not looking for him until this trouble calms down a bit.  

People now think he killed his own son and set things up so others would think robbers did it, so he could get Huck’s money without a long lawsuit. People say he wasn’t any good enough to do it. He is clever, I think. If he doesn’t come back within a year, he’ll be fine. You can’t prove anything against him; everything will be settled then, and he will take Huck’s money easily.

“Yes, I think so, ma’am. I don’t see any reason not to. Has everyone stopped thinking the black man did it?”  

“Oh, no, not everyone. Many people think he did it. But they’ll catch the black man soon, and maybe they can scare him out.”  

“Why, are they after him already?”  

“Well, you’re innocent, aren’t you! Does three hundred dollars lie around every day for people to pick up? Some folks think the black man isn’t far from here. I’m one of them—but I haven’t said it publicly. A few days ago I was talking with an old couple who live next door in the log shanty, and they mentioned that hardly anyone ever goes to that island over there that they call Jackson’s Island. ‘Does anyone live there?’ I asked. ‘No, nobody,’ they said. I didn’t say any more, but I thought about it. I was pretty sure I had seen smoke over the head of the island a day or two before, so I thought maybe the black man is hiding there. Anyway, I thought it was worth the trouble to search the place. I haven’t seen any smoke since, so I guess he might be gone, if it was him. But the husband is going over to see him and another man. He went up the river but came back today, and I told him as soon as he arrived two hours ago.”

I was so uneasy that I couldn’t sit still. I had to do something with my hands, so I picked up a needle from the table and tried to thread it. My hands shook, and I did a poor job.  

When the woman stopped talking, I looked up. She was looking at me with curiosity and a slight smile. I put the needle and thread down, pretended to be interested—and I really was—and said:

“Three hundred dollars is a lot of money. I wish my mother could get it. Is your husband going there tonight?”

“Oh, yes. He went downtown with the man I told you about to get a boat and see if they could borrow another gun. They’ll go over after midnight.”

“Couldn't they see better if they waited until daytime?”

“Yes. And couldn’t the Black man see better, too? After midnight he’ll probably be asleep, and they can move through the woods and find his campfire more easily in the dark, if he has one.”

“I hadn’t thought of that.”

The woman kept looking at me with curiosity, and I felt uncomfortable. Soon she asked,

“What did you say your name was, honey?”

“Mary Williams.”

It seemed to me that I hadn’t said Mary before; I thought I had said Sarah, so I felt trapped and a little scared. I wished the woman would say something else; the longer she stayed silent, the more uneasy I became. Then she said:

“Honey, I thought you said your name was Sarah when you first came in?”

"Oh, yeah, I did. Sarah Mary Williams. Sarah is my first name. Some call me Sarah, some call me Mary."

"Oh, is that how it works?"

"Yes."

I felt a bit better then, but I still wanted to get out of there. I couldn't look up yet.

The woman started talking about how hard times were. She said they were very poor and that the rats seemed to own the place. Then I felt relaxed again. She was right about the rats. Every now and then you could see one poke its nose out of a hole in the corner. She told me she kept something handy to throw at them when she was alone, or they would never leave her alone. She showed me a twisted piece of lead and said she was usually a good shot with it. But she had strained her arm a day or two ago and wasn’t sure if she could throw accurately now. She waited for a chance and then swung at a rat. She missed badly and said, “Ouch!” because it hurt her arm. She asked me to try the next one. I wanted to leave before the old man returned, but I didn’t show it. I took the lead and when the first rat showed its nose, I let it pass; if it had stayed, it would have been a sickly rat. She said that was great and thought I would get the next one. She went and got the lump of lead, brought it back, and also a piece of yarn that she wanted me to help her with. I held out my two hands and she wrapped the yarn around them while she kept talking about herself and her husband’s problems. Then she stopped and said:

“Keep your eyes on the rats. Keep the lead in your lap, close at hand.”

She dropped the lump into my lap at that moment. I squeezed it with my legs and she kept talking, but only for about a minute. Then she took off her hat, looked straight at my face, smiled, and said:

“Come on, what’s your real name?”

“What? Mom?”

“What’s your real name? Is it Bill, Tom, Bob, or something else?”

I think I was shaking like a leaf and I hardly knew what to do. I said:

“Please don’t make fun of a poor girl like me, Mom. If I’m in the way, I’ll—”

“No, you won’t. Sit down and stay where you are. I’m not going to hurt you, and I won’t tell on you either. Just tell me your secret and trust me. I’ll keep it, and I’ll help you. My old man will help you too if you want. You see, you’re just a runaway apprentice, that’s all. It’s nothing serious. There’s no harm in it. You’ve been treated badly and you decided to leave. Bless you, child, I won’t tell on you. Tell me everything now, you’re a good boy.”

I said it was useless to keep pretending, so I would be honest and tell her everything, but she must keep her promise. I told her my father and mother were dead, and the law had sent me to work for a cruel old farmer thirty miles inland from the river. He treated me so badly that I could not stand it any longer. He left for a few days, and I took the chance to steal some of his daughter’s old clothes and run away. I walked three nights to cover the thirty miles. I traveled at night, hid during the day, and slept wherever I could. The bag of bread and meat I carried from home lasted the whole journey, and I had plenty. I said I believed my uncle Abner Moore would take care of me, and that was why I headed for the town of Goshen.

“Goshen, child? This isn’t Goshen. This is St. Peterburg. Goshen is ten miles further up the river. Who told you this was Goshen?”

“A man I met at dawn this morning, just as I was about to go into the woods for my usual sleep. He said that when the road split I should take the right fork, and five miles would bring me to Goshen.”

“He was probably drunk. He gave you the wrong directions.”

“He did seem drunk, but it doesn’t matter now. I have to keep moving. I’ll reach Goshen before daylight.”

“Wait a moment. I’ll give you a snack to eat. You’ll need it.”

“Tell me, when a cow lies down, which end gets up first? Answer quickly—don’t think too much about it. Which end gets up first?”

“The back end, Mom.”

“Okay, what about a horse?”

“The front end, Mom.”

“On which side of a tree does moss grow?”

“The north side.”

“If fifteen cows are grazing on a hillside, how many of them are eating with their heads pointing the same way?”

“All fifteen of them, Mom.”

“Well, I guess you have lived in the country. I thought maybe you were trying to trick me again. What’s your real name now?”

“George Peters, Mom.”

“Remember, George. Don’t forget and tell me it’s Alexander before you go, and then say it’s George Alexander when I catch you. And don’t wear that old calico when you’re with women. You look okay with a girl, but you might fool a man, maybe. Bless you, child—when you thread a needle, don’t hold the thread still and bring the needle to it. Hold the needle still and push the thread into it. That’s how a woman usually does it, but a man does it the other way. When you throw at a rat or anything, stand on tiptoes, lift your hand high over your head, and try to miss the rat by six or seven feet. Throw with a stiff arm from the shoulder, as if there were a pivot there, like a girl would. Don’t throw from the wrist and elbow with your arm out to the side, like a boy. And remember, when a girl tries to catch something in her lap she spreads her knees apart. She doesn’t clap them together like you did when you caught the lump of lead. I recognized you as a boy when you were threading the needle, and I set up the other tests just to be sure. Now go to your uncle, Sarah Mary Williams George Alexander Peters. If you get into trouble, send a message to Mrs. Judith Loftus—that’s me—and I’ll try to help you out. Stay on the river road the whole way, and next time bring shoes and socks. The river road is rocky, and your feet will be in bad shape when you reach Goshen, I think.”

I walked about fifty yards up the riverbank, then turned around and went back to where my canoe was, a short way downstream from the house. I jumped in and paddled away quickly. I rowed upstream far enough to reach the tip of the island, and then I started crossing. I took off my hat because I didn’t want any shade. When I was near the middle of the crossing I heard a clock start to strike, so I stopped and listened. The sound came faintly over the water but was clear—it was eleven o’clock. When I reached the tip of the island I didn’t wait to rest, even though I was very tired. I pushed straight into the woods where my old camp had been and built a good fire on a high, dry spot.

Then I got back into the canoe and paddled as fast as I could toward our spot, a mile and a half downstream. I landed, ran through the woods, up the ridge, and into the cave. Inside, Jim was lying asleep on the ground. I woke him and said, “Get up and move, Jim! We don’t have a minute to lose. They’re after us!”

Jim didn’t ask any questions and didn’t say anything, but the way he worked for the next half hour showed how scared he was. By then everything we owned was on our raft, and it was ready to be taken out of the willow cove where it had been hidden. We put out the camp fire in the cave first thing and did not light any candles outside after that.

I pushed the canoe a short distance from the shore and looked around. If there was a boat nearby, I couldn't see it because the stars and shadows made it hard to see. Then we took out the raft and quietly glided down in the shade, passing the base of the island, which stood silent and still.
