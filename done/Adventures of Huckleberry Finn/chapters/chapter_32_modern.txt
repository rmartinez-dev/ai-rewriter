When I arrived, everything was quiet, like a lazy Sunday, hot and sunny. The people were out in the fields. A faint droning of bugs and flies filled the air, making it feel lonely, as if everyone were dead and gone. When a breeze blows and the leaves tremble, it feels mournful, like distant spirits whispering. These spirits have been dead for many years, and you can’t help but think they are talking about you. In general, it makes a person wish they were dead too, and be done with everything.

Phelps’ was a small one‑horse cotton plantation, and they all look the same. A rail fence surrounded a two‑acre yard. A stile was made from logs that had been cut off and turned upside‑down, like barrels of different lengths, so people could climb over the fence and women could stand on it when they were about to jump onto a horse. Some sickly patches of grass grew in the big yard, but most of it was bare and smooth, like an old hat with the nap rubbed off. There was a large double‑log house for the white family—hewn logs with the gaps filled with mud or mortar, and the mud strips had been white‑washed at some time. A round‑log kitchen stood next to it, with a wide, open but roofed passage joining it to the house. Behind the kitchen was a log smoke‑house. On the other side of the smoke‑house were three small log cabins for the Black workers, placed in a row. A little hut stood alone farther back against the back fence, and a few outbuildings were a short distance away on the other side. Near the hut there was an ash hopper and a large kettle used for making soap. By the kitchen door was a bench with a bucket of water and a gourd. A hound slept there in the sun, and more hounds slept nearby. About three shade trees grew in a corner, and a patch of currant and gooseberry bushes grew near the fence. Outside the fence there was a garden and a watermelon patch. Then the cotton fields began, and beyond them the woods.

I went around and climbed over the back stile by the ash hopper and started toward the kitchen. After a short walk I heard the faint hum of a spinning wheel going up and down. Then I knew for sure that I wished I were dead—for that is the loneliest sound in the whole world.

I walked on without any special plan, just trusting that Providence would give me the right words when the moment came. I had learned that Providence always put the right words in my mouth if I let it.

When I was halfway, one hound and then another got up and ran toward me. Of course I stopped, faced them, and stood still. What a scene they made! In about fifteen seconds I was like the hub of a wheel, with the dogs as spokes—about fifteen of them gathered around me, their necks and noses lifted toward me, barking and howling. More were coming; I could see them jumping over fences and around corners from everywhere.

A Black woman burst out of the kitchen holding a rolling pin, shouting, “Go away, you tiger! you spot! go away!” She grabbed one dog, then another, and sent them howling away, and the rest followed. In the next moment the other half of the dogs came back, wagging their tails around me, becoming friendly. There is no danger in a hound, after all.

Behind the woman walked a little Black girl and two little Black boys. They wore only plain shirts and clung to their mother’s gown, peeking out shyly from behind her, just as they always do. Then a white woman ran out of the house. She was about forty‑five or fifty years old, without a hat, and she held a spinning stick in her hand. Behind her followed her small white children, acting the same way the Black children were. She was smiling so widely that she could barely stand and said:

“It’s you, at last! Isn’t it?”

I answered “Yes, ma’am” before I even thought about it.

She grabbed me and hugged me tightly, then took my hands and shook them. Tears welled up in her eyes and ran down her cheeks. She kept hugging and shaking, saying, “You don’t look as much like your mother as I thought you would, but that doesn’t matter. I’m so glad to see you! My dear, my dear, I could just eat you up! Children, this is your cousin Tom—say hello.”

The children lowered their heads, put their fingers in their mouths, and hid behind her. She continued:

“Liza, hurry and get him a hot breakfast right away—or did you have breakfast on the boat?”

I told her I had eaten on the boat. Then she led me toward the house, holding my hand, with the children walking close behind. When we reached the house, she placed me in a low chair with a split back, then sat on a small stool in front of me. She took both of my hands and said:

Now I can get a good look at you, and I swear I’ve been waiting for this a long time—through all those years—and at last it’s happening! We’ve been expecting you for a couple of days or more. What kept you? Did the boat run aground?

“Yes—she—”

“Don’t say ‘yes’—say ‘Aunt Sally.’ Where did she run aground?”

I didn’t really know what to say because I didn’t know if the boat would be coming up the river or going down. I rely a lot on my instinct, and my instinct said she would be coming up—from downstream toward New Orleans. That didn’t help much, though, because I didn’t know the names of the bars that way. I realized I had to make up a bar, or forget the name of the one we ran aground on. Then an idea came to me and I said:

“It wasn’t the grounding that held us back, just a little. We blew out a cylinder head.”

“Good grief! Did anyone get hurt?”

“No, we killed a Black man.”

“Well, it’s lucky, because sometimes people get hurt. Two years ago, last Christmas, your Uncle Silas was coming up from New Orleans on the old Lally Rook. He blew out a cylinder head and crippled a man. I think that man died afterwards. He was a Baptist. Your Uncle Silas knew a family in Baton Rouge who knew his people very well. Yes, I remember now—he did die. An infection set in, and they had to amputate him, but it didn’t save him. He turned blue all over and died hoping for a glorious resurrection. They said he was a sight to see. Your uncle has been going into town every day to fetch you. He left again less than an hour ago; he’ll be back any minute now. You must have met him on the road, didn’t you?—an old man, with a—”

“No, I didn’t see anyone, Aunt Sally. The boat arrived at daylight, and I left my baggage on the wharf‑boat. I walked around the town and then out into the country to kill time so I wouldn’t get here too early. I came down the back way.”

“Who did you give the baggage to?”

“Nobody.”

“Why, child, it will be stolen!”

“It won’t be stolen where I hid it, I think,” I said.

“How did you get breakfast so early on the boat?”

“It was thin ice, but I said:”

“The captain saw me standing around and told me I should eat something before I went ashore. So he took me to the officers’ mess in the texas and gave me whatever I wanted.”

I felt so uneasy that I couldn’t listen properly. My thoughts kept drifting to the children. I wanted to move them aside, calm them a bit, and figure out who I was. But I couldn’t get anything right; Mrs. Phelps kept talking nonstop. Soon her words gave me a cold shiver down my back. She said:

“Here we are, moving forward, and you haven’t told me a single word about Sis or any of the others. I’ll pause my work for a moment and you can start yours. Just tell me everything—tell me about him, about each of them, how they are, what they’re doing, what they asked you to tell me, and every last thing you can think of.”

I realized I was stuck, and badly so. Providence had helped me up to this point, but now I felt trapped. It was useless to try to move forward; I had to raise my hand in surrender. I thought, here’s another chance to find the truth. I opened my mouth to speak, but she grabbed me and shoved me behind the bed, saying:

“Here he comes! Lower your head—there, that’ll do; you can’t be seen now. Don’t let anyone know you’re here. I’ll play a trick on him. Children, don’t say a word.”

I was in a dilemma. Still, there was no point in panicking; all I could do was stay still and be ready to stand up when the moment arrived.

I caught only a quick glimpse of the old gentleman as he entered, and then the bed hid him. Mrs. Phelps jumped up and asked, “Has he come?”

“No,” her husband replied.

“Goodness gracious!” she exclaimed. “What on earth could have happened to him?”

“I can’t imagine,” said the old gentleman. “It makes me feel very uneasy.”

“Uneasy!” she said. “I’m about to lose it! He must have come, and you missed him on the road. I’m sure of it—something tells me so.”

“Why, Sally, I couldn’t have missed him on the road—you know that.”

“But dear, what will Sis say? He must have come! You must have missed him. He—”

“Oh, don’t upset me any more. I’m already distressed. I don’t know what to make of this. I’m at my wit’s end, and I admit I’m scared. There’s no hope that he’s come, because he couldn’t have come and I missed him. Sally, it’s terrible—something must have happened to the boat!”

“Silas, look over there—up the road! Isn’t that someone coming?”

He jumped to the window at the head of the bed, giving Mrs. Phelps the chance she wanted. She quickly bent down at the foot of the bed, gave me a pull, and I got up. When he turned away from the window, there she stood, beaming and smiling like a house on fire, while I stood anxious and sweaty beside her. The old gentleman stared and asked, “Who’s that?”

“Who do you think it is?”

“I have no idea. Who is it?”

“It’s Tom Sawyer!”

Wow, I practically fell through the floor! But there was no time to change knives. The old man grabbed my hand and kept shaking it. Meanwhile, the woman was dancing, laughing and crying. Then they both started firing questions at me about Sid, Mary and the rest of the tribe.

Even though they were happy, it wasn’t the same feeling I had. It was like being born again. I was so glad to discover who I was. They stared at me for two hours. Finally, when my chin was so sore I could barely move it, I told them more about my family—the Sawyer family—than any of the six Sawyer families had ever done. I explained how we blew out a cylinder head at the mouth of the White River, and it took us three days to fix it. That was fine and it worked perfectly, because they didn’t know it would take three days. If I had called it a bolt head, it would have been just as good.

Now I felt quite comfortable on one side and quite uncomfortable on the other. Pretending to be Tom Sawyer was easy and comfortable, and it stayed that way until I heard a steamboat chugging down the river. Then I thought, what if Tom Sawyer comes down on that boat? What if he steps in here any minute and shouts my name before I can wink at him to stay quiet? I couldn't let that happen; it wouldn't be right. I had to go up the road and stop him. So I told the people that I planned to go to town and bring back my luggage. The old gentleman wanted to come with me, but I said no, I could drive the horse myself, and I would rather he didn't worry about me.
