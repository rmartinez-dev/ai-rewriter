Soon it was time to get up. I went down the ladder and started toward the stairway. When I reached the girls’ bedroom, the door was open. I saw Mary Jane sitting by her old hair trunk, which was open, and she was packing things into it because she was getting ready to go to England. She had stopped, a folded gown in her lap, her face in her hands, crying. I felt terrible to see it; anyone would. I went inside and said:

“Miss Mary Jane, you can’t stand to see people in trouble, and I can’t either. Tell me what’s wrong.”

She told me what had happened. It was the black people—I had expected it. She said the wonderful trip to England was now a disappointment for her. She didn’t know how she could ever be happy there, knowing that mother and children would never see each other again. She became even more bitter, threw up her hands, and said:

“Oh dear, to think they’ll never see each other again!”

“But they will,” I replied, “within two weeks, I know it!”

Before I could finish, she threw her arms around my neck and begged me to say it again, again, again.

I realized I had spoken too hastily and said too much, and I was in a tight spot. I asked her to give me a minute to think, and she sat there, impatient, excited, and attractive, but also somewhat happy and relieved, like someone who had just had a tooth pulled. So I started to think it over. I told myself that a person who tells the truth when he is in a difficult situation is taking a lot of risk, even though I have no experience and can’t be sure. It seemed that way to me, and in this case the truth actually appeared safer than a lie. I needed to keep it in mind and think about it later; it was strange and irregular. I had never seen anything like it.  

Finally I decided to take a chance and tell the truth, even though it felt like stepping onto a powder keg just to see what would happen. I said:

“Miss Mary Jane, is there a place outside town, a short distance away, where you could stay for three or four days?”

“Yes, Mr. Lothrop’s. Why?”

“Don’t worry about the reason yet. If I tell you how I know the Black men will meet again within two weeks—here in this house—and prove that I know it, will you go to Mr. Lothrop’s and stay for four days?”

“Four days!” she replied. “I’ll stay a year!”

"All right," I say, "I don't want anything more from you than your word—I’d rather have that than another man's kiss, I promise." She smiles and blushes sweetly, and I add, "If you don't mind, I'll close the door and lock it."

Then I come back, sit down again, and say, "Don't shout. Stay calm and be brave. I have to tell you the truth, Miss Mary, and you need to brace yourself because it's a hard truth to hear, but there's no avoiding it. Those men you call uncles aren't uncles at all; they're just frauds—real dead‑beats. Now the worst part is over, and you can handle the rest."

She jumps up, startled, as you would expect. I'm already past the shallow water, so I keep walking. Her eyes keep getting brighter, and I tell her everything—from the first time we met that foolish young man on the steamboat, all the way to when she threw herself onto the king's chest at the front door and he kissed her sixteen or seventeen times. Then she leaps up, her face blazing like a sunset, and says, "The brute! Come on, don't waste a minute—not a second—we'll tar and feather them and throw them in the river!"

I answer, "Sure. But do you mean before you go to Mr. Lothrop's, or—"

"Oh," she says, "what am I thinking about!" She sits down again. "Don't worry about what I said—please don't. You won't, will you?" She places her soft hand on mine in a way that makes me feel like I could die. "I never expected to feel so upset," she says. "Now go ahead, and I won't complain anymore. Tell me what to do, and I'll do it."

"Well," I say, "those two guys are a rough bunch, and I'm stuck with them for a while, whether I like it or not. I don't want to tell you why. If we expose them, this town will get me out of their grip, and I'll be fine. But there will be another person you don't know about who will be in big trouble. We have to save him, don't we? Of course. So we won't expose them."

Those words give me a good idea. I think maybe I could get Jim and myself away from those crooks, have them locked up here, and then leave. But I don't want to run the raft in the daytime with no one on board to answer questions except me. So I want the plan to start late at night. I say:

"Miss Mary Jane, here's what we'll do, and you won't have to stay at Mr. Lothrop's any longer. How far is it?"

"A little under four miles—out in the country, back here."

“Well, that’s enough. Now go out there and stay hidden until nine or half‑past nine tonight. Then ask them to bring you back home and tell them you’ve thought of something. If you get here before eleven, put a candle in the window. If I don’t show up, wait until eleven. If I still don’t show up, it means I’m gone, out of danger, and safe. Then you can come out, spread the news, and get these criminals jailed.”

“Good,” she says, “I’ll do it.”

“And if it turns out that I don’t escape but get caught with them, you must tell everyone that I warned you beforehand, and you must support me as much as you can.”

“I will stand by you! I promise. They won’t lay a finger on you!” she says, and I see her nostrils flare and her eyes widen when she says it.

“If I manage to get away, I won’t be here to prove those troublemakers aren’t your uncles, and I couldn’t do it even if I were present. I could swear they are losers and cheats, that’s all, but that still matters. There are other people who can do this better than I can, and they won’t be doubted as quickly as I might be. I’ll tell you how to find them. Give me a pencil and a piece of paper. Write down ‘Royal Nonesuch, Bricksville.’ Keep it safe and don’t lose it. When the court wants information about these two, they can send someone to Bricksville and say they have the men who performed the Royal Nonesuch, and ask for witnesses. That will bring the whole town here before you can even blink, Miss Mary. And they will arrive quickly, too.”

I thought we had everything settled at that moment. So I said:

“Just let the auction continue, and don’t worry. No one has to pay for what they buy until a full day after the auction because of the short notice, and they won’t leave until they receive the money. The way we arranged it, the sale won’t count, and they won’t get any money. It’s just like what happened with the slaves—it wasn’t a real sale, and the slaves will be back soon. They can’t collect money for the slaves yet—they’re in a very bad situation, Miss Mary.”

“Well,” she said, “I’ll go down to breakfast now, and then I’ll head straight to Mr. Lothrop’s.”

“No, that isn’t the right thing to do, Miss Mary Jane,” I said, “not at all; go before breakfast.”

“Why?”

“What do you think I wanted you to go for, Miss Mary?”

“I never really thought about it. Now that you ask, I don’t know. What is it?”

“It’s because you’re not like those rough people. I don’t need any fancy book, just your face. Someone can sit down and read it like plain text. Do you think you can go and face your uncles when they come to kiss you good‑morning, and never—”

“Stop, stop! Yes, I’ll go before breakfast—I’ll be glad to. And leave my sisters with them?”

“Yes, don’t worry about them. They’ll have to wait a while. They might suspect something if all of you left together. I don’t want you to see them, or anyone in this town. If a neighbour asked how your uncles are this morning, your face would say something. No, you go right away, Miss Mary Jane, and I’ll take care of everything. I’ll tell Miss Susan to send your love to your uncles and say you went away for a few hours to rest, change, or visit a friend, and that you’ll be back tonight or early in the morning.”

“Going to see a friend is fine, but I don’t want my love sent to them.”

“Then it won’t happen.”  

That was enough to tell her—no harm in it. It was only a small thing to do, no trouble, and small things smooth people’s roads the most, down here. It would make Mary Jane comfortable, and it wouldn’t cost anything. Then I said, “There’s one more thing—that bag of money.”

“Well, they have it, and it makes me feel pretty silly thinking how they got it.”

“No, you’re wrong. They don’t have it.”

“Then who does?”

“I wish I knew, but I don’t. I had it because I stole it from them. I stole it to give it to you, and I know where I hid it, but I’m afraid it’s not there anymore. I’m very sorry, Miss Mary Jane. I’m as sorry as I can be. I did the best I could, honestly. I almost got caught, so I had to push it into the first place I could reach and run away. It wasn’t a good place.”

“Oh, stop blaming yourself. It’s a mistake, and I won’t let you blame yourself. It wasn’t your fault. Where did you hide it?”

I didn’t want to make her think about her own troubles again, and I couldn’t find the words to show her the corpse lying in the coffin with a bag of money on its stomach. For a moment I stayed silent, then I said:

“I’d rather not tell you where I put it, Miss Mary Jane, if you’ll let me go. But I can write it down on a piece of paper, and you can read it on the way to Mr. Lothrop’s, if you want. Will that be okay?”

“Oh, yes.”

So I wrote: “I put it in the coffin. It was still there when you were crying at night. I was standing behind the door, and I felt very sorry for you, Miss Mary Jane.”

It made my eyes water a little when I remembered her crying there alone at night, and those devils standing right under her own roof, shaming her and robbing her. When I folded it up and gave it to her, I saw tears come to her eyes, too. She shook my hand hard and said:

“Good‑bye. I’ll do everything just as you told me. If I never see you again, I won’t forget you. I’ll think of you many, many times, and I’ll pray for you, too!”—and then she was gone.

Pray for me! I thought that if she knew me she would take a job that fit her better. But I bet she did it anyway—she was just that kind. She had the courage to pray for Judas if she wanted to; she never backs down, I think. You can say what you want, but in my opinion she had more grit than any girl I’ve ever seen; she was full of determination. It sounds like flattery, but it isn’t. And when it comes to beauty—and goodness, too—she surpasses them all. I haven’t seen her since that day she left through that door. No, I haven’t seen her since, but I think of her many, many times, and I remember her saying she would pray for me. If I ever thought it would help me to pray for her, I would have done it, no doubt.

Well, Mary Jane ran away through the back, I think, because nobody saw her leave. When I talked to Susan and the hare‑lip, I asked:

“What’s the name of those people on the other side of the river that you all sometimes go to see?”

They answered:

“There are several, but mostly they’re the Proctors.”

“That’s the name,” I said. “I had almost forgotten it. Miss Mary Jane told me to tell you she’s gone over there in a terrible hurry—one of them is sick.”

“Which one?”

“I don’t know, really. I’m a little fuzzy, but I think it’s—”

“Oh my, I hope it isn’t Hanner?”

“I’m sorry to say it,” I replied, “but Hanner is the one.”

“My goodness, and she was fine just last week! Is she getting worse?”

“There’s no other word for it. They stayed with her all night, Miss Mary Jane said, and they don’t think she’ll last many more hours.”

“Just think about that! What’s wrong with her?”

I couldn’t think of a sensible answer right away, so I said:

“Mumps.”

“Mumps, your granny! They don’t stay with people who have mumps.”

“They don’t, do they? You’re sure they do with *these* mumps. These mumps are different. It’s a new kind, Miss Mary Jane said.”

“How is it a new kind?”

“Because it’s mixed with other things.”

“What other things?”

“Measles, whooping‑cough, erysipelas, consumption, yellow jaundice, brain fever, and I don’t know what else.”

“My goodness! And they call it the mumps?”

“That’s what Miss Mary Jane said.”

“Well, why do they call it the mumps?”

“Because it *is* the mumps. That’s how it starts.”

“Well, there’s no point in it. Someone could stub his toe, drink poison, fall down a well, break his neck, smash his brain, and then someone would ask what killed him. A fool might answer, ‘He stubbed his toe.’ Does that make any sense? No. There’s no sense in this either. Is it catching?”

“Is it catching? The way you talk. Is a harrow catching in the dark? If you don’t cling to one tooth, you’ll end up on another, right? And you can’t get rid of that tooth without pulling the whole harrow with it, can you? These kinds of problems are like a harrow, you could say. It’s not a weak harrow either; you have to attach it properly.”

“Well, it’s terrible, I think,” says the hare‑lip. “I’ll go to Uncle Harvey and—”

“Oh, yes,” I say, “I would. Of course I would. I wouldn’t waste any time.”

“Well, why wouldn’t you?”

“Just look at it for a minute; maybe you’ll understand. Haven’t your uncles promised to get back home to England as quickly as possible? Do you think they would be so cruel as to leave you to make the whole journey by yourselves? You know they’ll wait for you. So far, so good. Your Uncle Harvey is a preacher, isn’t he? Very well. Would a preacher try to trick a steamboat clerk, or a ship clerk, just to get them to let Miss Mary Jane board? You know he wouldn’t. What will he do then? He’ll probably say, ‘It’s a great pity, but my church matters have to be dealt with the best way they can. My niece has been exposed to a terrible disease, so it is my duty to stay here and wait the three months it takes to see if she has it.’ But never mind, if you think it’s best to tell Uncle Harvey—”

“Come on, stop wasting time here when we could be enjoying ourselves in England while we wait to find out whether Mary Jane has it or not. You sound like a fool.”

“Well, maybe you should tell some of the neighbours.”

“Listen, you’re the most foolish of all. Can’t you see they would just tell everyone? There’s no reason not to tell anyone at all.”

“Okay, maybe you’re right – I think you’re right.”

“But I think we should tell Uncle Harvey that she’s been away for a while, so he won’t worry about her.”

“Yes, Miss Mary Jane wanted you to do that. She said, ‘Tell them to give Uncle Harvey and William my love and a kiss, and tell them I’ve crossed the river to see Mr.—Mr.—what’s the name of that rich family your Uncle Peter admired so much? I mean the one that—’

“Why, you must mean the Apthorps, right?”

“Exactly. Those names are hard to remember, aren’t they? She said she has gone over to ask the Apthorps to come to the auction and buy this house, because she thought Uncle Peter would rather they had it than anyone else. She’ll keep asking them until they say they’ll come. If she isn’t too tired, she’ll go home afterwards; if she is, she’ll be home in the morning anyway. She told me not to mention the Proctors, only the Apthorps— that’s true because she’s going there to talk about them buying the house. I know this because she told me herself.”

“Alright,” they said, and set off to deliver the message to their uncles, sending love, kisses, and the instructions.

Everything was fine now. The girls wouldn’t protest because they wanted to go to England, and the king and the duke would rather have Mary Jane busy working for the auction than near Doctor Robinson. I felt very good; I thought I had done it quite neatly—I guessed Tom Sawyer couldn’t have done it any neater himself. He would have added more style, but I can’t do that very well because I wasn’t brought up to it.

The auction was held in the public square toward the end of the afternoon. It went on and on, and the old man was there, looking his most honest, standing next to the auctioneer. He would quote a bit of Scripture now and then, or say a moral saying. The duke was groveling for sympathy as best he could, and just spreading himself around.

Soon the auction finished, and everything was sold—everything except a small, insignificant lot in the graveyard. So they had to deal with that. I have never seen such greed as the king had, wanting to take everything. While they were doing that, a steamboat arrived, and in about two minutes a crowd came out shouting, laughing, making noise, and singing:

“Here’s your opposition line! Here are your two sets of heirs to old Peter Wilks—and you pay your money and you take your choice!”
