The news spread through the town in two minutes, and you could see people running away from every direction. Some of them put on their coats as they came. Soon we were in the middle of a crowd, and the sound of the marching was like a soldier’s march. The windows and courtyards were full, and every minute someone would shout over a fence:

“Is it them?”

Someone walking with the group would answer:

“You bet it is.”

When we reached the house, the street in front of it was packed, and three girls stood in the doorway. Mary Jane had red hair, but that didn’t matter. She was very beautiful, and her face and eyes shone with joy because her uncle had come. The king spread his arms, and Mary Jane jumped for him, and the hare‑lip jumped for the duke, and there they were! Everyone, especially the women, cried with joy to see them meet again at last and have such good times.

Then the king bowed to the duke privately—I saw it happen. He looked around and saw the coffin in the corner on two chairs. The king and the duke put a hand on each other’s shoulders and covered their eyes with the other hand. They walked slowly and solemnly toward the coffin. Everyone stepped back to give them room, and the chatter stopped. People whispered “Sh!” and the men took off their hats and lowered their heads, so you could hear a pin drop.

When they reached the coffin they leaned over, looked inside, and began to cry loudly. They put their arms around each other’s necks and rested their chins on each other’s shoulders. For three or four minutes I had never seen two men weep like that. Everyone else was doing the same. The room was so damp I had never seen anything like it.

One of them stood on one side of the coffin, the other on the opposite side. They knelt and rested their foreheads on the coffin, praying silently. At that moment the crowd broke down like never before. People started sobbing openly, even the poor girls. Almost every woman went to the girls without saying a word, kissed them solemnly on the forehead, placed a hand on their heads, looked up to the sky with tears streaming down, then broke into more sobs and wept, showing the next woman how to cry. I had never seen anything so disturbing.

Eventually the king stands up and steps forward a little. He gets emotional and delivers a speech full of tears and nonsense. He talks about how hard it is for him and his poor brother to lose the sick person and to miss seeing the sick man alive after a four‑thousand‑mile journey. He says this hardship is made bearable for us by the shared sympathy and holy tears. He thanks them from his heart and from his brother’s heart, because they can’t say the words themselves; the words feel weak and cold, full of nonsense, and it becomes almost sickening. Then he sighs a pious “Amen,” lets himself go, and starts crying loudly.

As soon as he finishes speaking, someone in the crowd starts a hymn, and everyone joins in with all their might. It warms you up and feels as good as a church service. Music really helps; after all that emotional talk and nonsense, I’ve never seen anything lift spirits like that, sounding so honest and strong.

The king started talking again. He said that he and his nieces would be happy if a few close family friends could join them for dinner this evening and help with the funeral arrangements. He added that if his poor brother, who lay there, could speak, he would know the names he would mention, because those names were very dear to him and appeared often in his letters. So he listed them: Reverend Mr. Hobson, Deacon Lot Hovey, Mr. Ben Rucker, Abner Shackleford, Levi Bell, Dr. Robinson, their wives, and the widow Bartley.  

Reverend Hobson and Dr. Robinson were out at the edge of town together. Actually, the doctor was sending a sick man to the afterlife, and the preacher was praying for him. Lawyer Bell was away in Louisville on business. The others were present, so they all came, shook hands with the king, thanked him, and talked with him. Then they shook hands with the duke but said nothing. They only smiled and nodded like a group of simple people while the duke made many hand gestures and kept saying “goo‑goo—goo‑goo‑goo,” like a baby who cannot speak.

The king kept moving around, asking about almost everyone and every dog in town by name. He mentioned all sorts of small events that had happened at some point in the town, to George’s family, or to Peter. He always pretended that Peter wrote the notes, but that was a lie. He actually got every single one of them from the young man we canoed up to the steamboat.

Then Mary Jane brought the letter her father had left behind. The king read it out loud and began to cry. The letter gave the house and three thousand dollars in gold to the girls. It gave the tanyard—which was doing good business—along with some other houses and land worth about seven thousand dollars, and three thousand dollars in gold to Harvey and William. It also told where six thousand dollars in cash was hidden in the cellar.

So the two swindlers said they would go and fetch the money and make everything clear. They told me to bring a candle. We shut the cellar door behind us. When they found the bag, they poured its contents onto the floor. It was a wonderful sight, all the gold coins shining. The king’s eyes glittered. He slapped the duke on the shoulder and said,

“Wow, this isn’t a joke at all! No, I don’t think so. Bilji, it’s even better than the Nonesuch, isn’t it?”

The duke agreed. They handled the gold coins, let them fall through their fingers, and heard them jingle on the floor, and the king said:

There's no point in talking. Being brothers of a rich dead man and representing the foreign heirs who are left is our job, Bilge. This is your trust in Providence. It's the best way in the long run. I've tried every option and none is better.

Most people would have been satisfied with the pile and accepted it on trust. But no, they had to count it. They counted it and found it $415 short. The king said, “Darn him, I wonder what he did with that $415?”

They worried about it for a while and searched everywhere for it. Then the duke said, “He was very sick, so he probably made a mistake. I think that's what happened. The best thing is to let it go and not worry about it. We can let it be.”

“Sure, we can let it be. I'm not worried about that—I'm thinking about the count. We want to be completely honest and transparent. We want to bring this money upstairs and count it in front of everyone. That way nothing looks suspicious. But when the dead man says there are six thousand dollars, we don’t want to—”

“Hold on,” said the duke. “Let’s cover the shortfall,” and he started taking out some yellow bills from his pocket.

“That’s a great idea, duke—you have a very clever mind,” said the king. “Good thing the old Nonesuch is helping us again,” and he began pulling out more yellow notes and stacking them.

It was a lot of work, but they covered the whole six thousand dollars perfectly.

“Listen,” says the duke, “I have another idea. Let’s go upstairs, count the money, and then give it to the girls.”

“Great idea, duke, let me hug you! It’s the most brilliant plan anyone has ever had. You really have the most impressive mind I’ve ever seen. Oh, this is the perfect trick, there’s no doubt about it. Let them follow their doubts if they want—this will knock them down.”

When we went upstairs, everyone gathered around the table. The king counted the money and piled it up: three hundred dollars in a stack, divided into twenty neat little piles. Everyone stared at it hungrily and licked their lips. Then they put the money back into the bag, and I saw the king puff himself up for another speech. He said:

Friends, my poor brother who lies over there has been generous to those left behind in the valley of sorrow. He has cared for those poor little lambs he loved and protected, even though they are now fatherless and motherless. We who knew him also know that he would have been even more generous if he hadn’t been afraid of hurting his dear William and me. Wouldn’t he? There is no doubt in my mind.

So what kind of brothers would stand in his way at a time like this? And what kind of uncles would steal—yes, steal—such sweet lambs that he loved? If I know William—and I think I do—I’ll just ask him.

He turns around and makes many gestures to the duke with his hands. The duke watches him blankly for a while, then suddenly seems to understand. He rushes to the king, shouting with joy, and hugs him about fifteen times before letting go. 

The king says, “I knew it. I think this will convince anyone how he feels. Here, Mary Jane, Susan, Joanner, take the money—take it all. It is the gift of the one who lies over there, cold but happy.”

Mary Jane goes to him, Susan and the hare‑lip go to the duke, and then there is more hugging and kissing than I have ever seen. Everyone gathers with tears in their eyes, shaking hands with the others, and keeps saying:

“You dear good souls! How lovely! How could you!”

Very soon everyone started talking about the sick man again. They praised how good he was and said his death was a great loss. Soon a large, iron‑jawed man entered from outside. He stood there listening and watching, but he said nothing. No one spoke to him either, because the king was talking and everyone was busy listening.

The king was in the middle of a speech when he said:

“…they are particular friends of the sick man. That is why they are invited here this evening; but tomorrow we want everyone to come—everybody. He respected everyone, he liked everyone, so his funeral should be public.”

He kept talking, enjoying the sound of his own voice. Every now and then he mentioned the funeral again. The duke could no longer stay quiet. He wrote on a small piece of paper, “Obsequies, you old fool,” folded it and slipped it over the heads of the people to the king. The king read it, put it in his pocket, and said:

“Poor William, even though he is ill, his heart is still right. He asks me to invite everyone to the funeral—he wants us all to feel welcome. But he need not worry—it was just what I was planning.”

Then the king continued calmly, mentioning the funeral again from time to time, just as before. When he did it for the third time, he said:

I use the word “orgies” not because it’s the usual word—we actually use “obsequies” for that—but because “orgies” is the correct term. “Obsequies” isn’t used in England any more; it’s outdated. People now say “orgies” in England. “Orgies” is better because it describes exactly what you mean. The word comes from the Greek *orgo*, meaning “outside, open, abroad,” and the Hebrew *jeesum*, meaning “to plant, cover up.” So a funeral orgy is an open or public funeral.  

He was the worst person I ever met. The iron‑jawed man laughed right in his face, and everyone was shocked. Everyone shouted, “Doctor!” and Abner Shackleford replied, “Robinson, haven’t you heard the news? This is Harvey Wilks.”  

The king smiled eagerly, pushed his hat back, and said, “Is this my poor brother’s dear friend and physician? I—”  

“Keep your hands off me!” the doctor shouted. “You sound like an Englishman, don’t you? That’s the worst imitation I’ve ever heard. You’re Peter Wilks’s brother! You’re a fraud, that’s what you are.”

Well, they all tried hard! They gathered around the doctor and tried to calm him down. They tried to explain to him that Harvey had shown in many ways that he really was Harvey. They said he knew everyone’s name, even the dogs’ names. They begged him not to hurt Harvey’s feelings or the poor girl’s feelings, and so on.  

But it didn’t help. He kept going and said that any man who pretended to be an Englishman and could not speak the language any better than he did was a fraud and a liar.  

The poor girl clung to the king and started crying. Suddenly the doctor turned on them. He said:

“I was your father’s friend, and I am your friend. I warn you, as a honest friend who wants to protect you and keep you safe, to turn your backs on that scoundrel and stay away from him. He is an ignorant tramp who calls his language ‘idiotic Greek and Hebrew’. He is the lowest kind of impostor. He has come here with many empty names and facts that he picked up somewhere, and you take them as proof. You are being fooled by these foolish friends, who should know better. Mary Jane Wilks, you know me as your friend, and as an unselfish friend too. Now listen to me: turn this pitiful rascal out—I beg you to do it. Will you?”

Mary Jane straightened herself up, and my, but she was handsome! She says:

“Here is my answer.” She lifted the bag of money and placed it in the king’s hands. “Take this six thousand dollars and invest it for me and my sisters however you like, and don’t give us a receipt.”

Then she put her arm around the king on one side, and Susan and the hare‑lip did the same on the other. Everyone clapped their hands and stomped on the floor like a perfect storm, while the king held his head up and smiled proudly. The doctor said:

“All right; I wash my hands of this matter. But I warn you that a time is coming when you will feel sick whenever you think of this day.” And he left.

“All right, doctor,” the king said, mocking him gently, “we’ll try to get them to send for you.” That made everyone laugh, and they said it was a great success.
