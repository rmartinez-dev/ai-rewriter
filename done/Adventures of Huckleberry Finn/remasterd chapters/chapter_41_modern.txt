The doctor was an old man, a very nice, kind‑looking old man when I met him. I told him that my brother and I had been on Spanish Island hunting yesterday afternoon. We camped on a piece of a raft we had found. About midnight he must have dreamed about kicking his gun, because it went off and shot him in the leg. We wanted him to go there and fix it without saying anything or letting anyone know, because we wanted to come home this evening and surprise the people.

“Who are your people?” he asked.

“The Phelpses, down yonder,” I said.

“Oh,” he replied. After a minute he asked, “How did he get shot?”

“He had a dream,” I said, “and the dream shot him.”

“A strange dream,” he said.

He lit his lantern, got his saddle‑bags, and we set off. When he saw the canoe he didn’t like its look. He said it was big enough for one person but didn’t seem safe for two. I said, “Don’t worry, sir, it can carry the three of us easily.”

“What three?” he asked.

“Me, Sid, and the guns—that’s what I mean,” I replied.

“Oh,” he said.

He put his foot on the gunwale, rocked the canoe, shook his head, and said he thought he would look for a bigger one. But all the larger canoes were locked and chained. So he took my canoe and told me to wait until he came back, or I could hunt around more, or maybe I should go home and get things ready for the surprise if I wanted to. I said I didn’t want to. I told him how to find the raft, and then he started away.

I got an idea quickly. I said to myself, “Supposedly he can’t fix that leg in a flash, like the saying goes, right? Supposedly it’ll take him three or four days? What are we going to do—just wait there until he finally tells us everything? No, I know what I’ll do. I’ll wait, and when he comes back, if he says he still has to go, I’ll go down there too, even if I have to swim. We’ll grab him, tie him up, keep him, and push him down the river. When Tom is finished with him we’ll give him whatever he’s worth, or all we have, and then let him get ashore.”

So I slipped into a pile of firewood to get some sleep. When I woke up, the sun was already high above me. I ran straight to the doctor’s house, but they told me he had left during the night and hadn’t returned yet. “That looks really bad for Tom,” I thought. “I’ll go straight to the island.” I hurried away, turned the corner, and almost bumped into Uncle Silas. He asked, “Tom! Where have you been all this time, you rascal?”

“I haven’t been anywhere,” I said, “just looking for the runaway Black man—Sid and I.”

“Where did you go?” he asked. “Your aunt has been very uneasy.”

"She didn't need to," I said, "because we were fine. We followed the men and the dogs, but they ran ahead of us and we lost them. We thought we heard them on the water, so we got a canoe and went after them. We crossed over but couldn't find any sign of them. So we drifted along the shore until we got tired, tied up the canoe and slept. I didn't wake up until about an hour ago. Then we paddled here to get the news. Sid is at the post office listening for information, and I'm going to get something to eat for us, and then we'll go home."

So we went to the post office to find Sid, but, just as I suspected, he wasn't there. The old man got a letter from the office, and we waited a little longer, but Sid still didn't arrive. Then the old man said, "Come with me. Let Sid walk home or canoe back when he stops messing around, but we will travel by vehicle." I couldn't convince him to let me stay and wait for Sid. He said there was no point and that I had to come with him so Aunt Sally could see that we were okay.

When we got home, Aunt Sally was so happy to see me that she laughed and cried at the same time. She hugged me and gave me a quick kiss, saying she would do the same for Sid when he arrives.

The house was full of farmers and their wives at dinner, a noise I had never heard before. Mrs. Hotchkiss was the loudest; she kept talking nonstop. She said:

“Well, Sister Phelps, I’ve searched that cabin and I think the man was crazy. I told Sister Damrell—didn’t I, Sister Damrell?—that he’s crazy, that’s what I said. You all heard me: he’s crazy, it’s clear. Look at that grindstone. No one in his right mind would want to scratch all those crazy things onto a grindstone, right? Here a person broke his heart; there he’s been stuck for thirty‑seven years, and all that—Louis’s natural son, some endless junk. He’s completely crazy, that’s what I said at the beginning, in the middle, at the end, and all the time—the man is crazy, as crazy as Nebokoodneezer, that’s what I mean.”

“And look at that ladder made of rags, Sister Hotchkiss,” says old Mrs. Damrell. “What on earth could he ever want from—”

“The same words I was saying just a moment ago to Sister Utterback, and she’ll tell you herself. She looked at that rag ladder, and I said, ‘Look at it—what could he want from it?’”

“But how on earth did they ever get that grindstone in there, anyway? And who dug that hole? Who—”

My very words, Brer Penrod! I was saying—pass that air sailor of mine, won’t you?—I was just telling Sister Dunlap how they got that grindstone in there, without any help, mind you. That’s where it is. Don’t tell me there was no help; there was help, and there was plenty of it. A dozen people have been helping that black man, and I swear I would skin every black person in this place if I could find out who did it. Moreover, I—

A dozen of you say that! Forty people couldn’t have done everything that’s been done. Look at those case‑knife saws and all the tools, how neatly they were made. Look at that bed‑leg that was sawed off; it took six men a week to do. Look at that black man made out of straw on the bed, and look at—

You can say that, Brer Hightower. It’s just what I told Brer Phelps himself. So, what do you think, Sister Hotchkiss? What are you thinking about, Brer Phelps? The bed‑leg that was sawed off? Think about it. I don’t think it cut itself; someone cut it. That’s my opinion. Take it or leave it. It may not count, but it’s my opinion. If anyone can do better, let them. That’s all. I told Sister Dunlap—

Oh my goodness, they must have a house full of Black people in there every night for four weeks to do all that work, Sister Phelps. Look at that shirt—every inch of it is covered with secret African writing done in blood! I have to stay on it almost all the time. I’d give two dollars to have it read to me, and as for the people who wrote it, I know I’d take them and beat them.

“People to help him, Brother Marples! Well, I think you’d feel the same if you’d been in this house for a while. They stole everything they could get their hands on while we were watching the whole time, mind you. They took that shirt right off the line! As for that sheet they turned into a rag ladder, there’s no way to say how many times they didn’t steal that, and they also took flour, candles, candlesticks, spoons, the old warming‑pan, and dozens of other things I can’t even remember now, including my new calico dress. Me, Silas, Sid and Tom kept watch day and night, as I told you, and none of us saw any sign of them—no hide, no hair, no sound. Then, at the last minute, they slipped right in under our noses and fooled us, and not only fooled us but also the Indian Territory robbers. They actually got away with that black man safe and sound, even though sixteen men and twenty‑two dogs were right on their heels at that exact moment! I tell you, it’s the strangest thing I’ve ever heard. Even spirits couldn’t have done it better or been smarter. I’m sure they must have been spirits—because, you know, our dogs are the best, and they never even got a scent on him! Explain that to me if you can—any of you!

“Well, it does beat—”

“Laws alive, I never—”

“So help me, I wouldn’t have—”

“House‑thieves as well as—”

“Goodness gracious, I’d be afraid to live in such a—”

“Afraid to live! I was so scared that I could barely go to bed, get up, lie down, or sit down, Sister Ridgeway. They might even steal something—good heavens, you can imagine how upset I was when midnight came last night. I would have been grateful if I weren’t afraid they would take something from the family! I was at a point where I couldn’t think clearly at all. It looks foolish now, in daylight, but I told myself that my two poor boys were asleep far up the stairs in that lonely room, and I was so uneasy that I went up there and locked them in! I did. Anyone would have done the same. When you are frightened like that, and the fear keeps growing, your mind starts to run wild. You begin to do all sorts of strange things, and eventually you think, maybe I was a boy once, maybe I’m up there, and the door isn’t locked, and you—”

She stopped, looking a little confused, then turned her head slowly. When her eyes met mine—I got up and went for a walk.

I tell myself that I can explain better how we didn’t end up in that room this morning if I step aside and think about it for a moment. So I do that. I don’t go any further, because she would have sent for me.  

When it got late, everyone left. I went back inside and told her that the noise and gunshots woke me and Sid, and that the door was locked. We wanted to see what was happening, so we went down the lightning‑rod. Both of us got a little hurt, and we never wanted to try that again.  

I then told her everything I had told Uncle Silas before. She said she would forgive us and that maybe it was okay after all. She thought boys were a wild lot, but as long as nobody was hurt, she decided it was better to be grateful that we were alive and still together, instead of worrying about the past. She kissed me, patted my head, and fell into a quiet mood. Soon she jumped up and said:  

“Goodness, it’s very late and Sid hasn’t come yet! What has happened to that boy?”  

I saw my chance, so I jumped up and said:  

“I’ll run straight to town and get him.”  

“No, you won’t,” she replied. “You’ll stay right where you are. One person is enough to lose at a time. If he isn’t here for supper, your uncle will go.”

Well, he wasn’t there for dinner, so Uncle left right after the meal.  

He came back about ten o’clock, a little uneasy. He hadn’t run into Tom’s path. Aunt Sally was quite uneasy, but Uncle Silas said there was no reason to worry—boys will be boys, and you’ll see this one turn up in the morning all fine. So she had to accept that. Still, she said she would stay up for a while and keep a light on so he could see it.  

When I went to bed, she came with me, got a candle, and tucked me in. She cared for me so much that I felt embarrassed and couldn’t look her in the eye. She sat on the bed and talked with me for a long time, saying how wonderful Sid was. She kept mentioning him and never seemed to stop talking about him. Every now and then she asked me if I thought he might be lost, hurt, or even drowned—maybe he was suffering somewhere or dead, and she couldn’t help him. Tears would fall silently. I told her that Sid was all right and would be home in the morning, for sure. She would squeeze my hand, sometimes kiss me, and ask me to repeat it, because it helped her feel better. When she was leaving, she looked into my eyes, steady and gentle, and said:

"The door won't be locked, Tom, and there's the window and the rod; you'll be fine, won't you? And you won't go? For my sake."

Laws knows I wanted to leave badly enough to think about Tom, and I was planning to go. But after that I wouldn't have gone, not even for kingdoms.

She was on my mind, and Tom was on my mind, so I slept very restlessly. Twice I went down the rod at night, slipped around the front, and saw her sitting by the candle in the window. Her eyes were looking toward the road, and tears were in them. I wished I could help her, but I couldn't. I only swore that I would never do anything to hurt her again.

The third time I woke at dawn, slid down, and she was still there. Her candle was almost out, and her old gray head rested on her hand. She was asleep.
