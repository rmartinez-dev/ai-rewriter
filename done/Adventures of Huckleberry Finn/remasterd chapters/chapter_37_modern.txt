Everything was settled. Then we went to the rubbish pile in the back yard, where they keep old boots, rags, broken bottles, and worn‑out tin objects. We rummaged around and found an old tin washpan. We patched the holes as best we could and used it to bake the pie. We took it down to the cellar, filled it with flour, and started making breakfast.

We also found a couple of small nails that Tom said would be useful for a prisoner to scratch his name and sorrows on the dungeon walls. We dropped one nail into Aunt Sally’s apron pocket that was hanging on a chair, and we stuck the other into the band of Uncle Silas’s hat, which was on the dresser. We had heard the children say that their mother and father were going to the runaway’s house that morning. After that we went to breakfast. Tom slipped a pewter spoon into Uncle Silas’s coat pocket. Aunt Sally hadn’t arrived yet, so we had to wait a little while.

When she finally came, she was hot, red, and angry. She could barely wait for the blessing. She poured coffee with one hand and tapped the child’s head with her thimble with the other. Then she said:

“I’ve searched high and low, and it beats everything that has become of your other shirt.”

My heart dropped down into my lungs, liver and everything else. A hard piece of corn crust went down my throat after it. It met a cough on the way, got spat across the table, hit one of the children in the eye, curled him up like a fishing worm and made him scream a war‑like cry. Tom turned a little blue around his throat, and the whole scene lasted about fifteen seconds. I would have sold it for half price if anyone wanted to bid.

After that we were all fine again. The sudden surprise had made us feel very cold. Uncle Silas said:

“It’s very strange, I can’t understand it. I know perfectly well I took it off, because—”

“Because you only have one on. Just listen to me! I know you took it off, and I remember it better than you do, because it was on the clothesline yesterday—I saw it there myself. But it’s gone, that’s the whole story, and you’ll have to wear a red flannel one until I can find time to make a new one. This will be the third I’ve made in two years. It keeps a person on their toes to keep you in shirts; and whatever you manage to do with it is more than I can figure out. A person would think you would learn to take some care of them at your age.”

“I know, Sally, and I try my best. But it shouldn’t be all my fault, because, you know, I don’t see them and I have nothing to do with them except when they’re on me. I don’t think I’ve ever lost any of them.”

“Well, it’s not your fault if you haven’t, Silas; you would have done it if you could, I think. And the shirt isn’t the only thing that’s gone. A spoon is missing, and that’s not all. There were ten spoons, and now there are only nine. I think the calf got the shirt, but the calf never took the spoon. That’s certain.”

“What else is missing, Sally?”

“There are six candles gone—that’s it. The rats could have taken the candles, and I think they did. I wonder why they don’t take the whole house, since you always try to block their holes. If they weren’t foolish they’d even sleep in your hair, Silas—you’d never notice. But you can’t blame the spoon on the rats, and I know that.”

“Well, Sally, I’m at fault and I admit it. I’ve been careless, but I won’t let tomorrow pass without sealing those holes.”

“Oh, I wouldn’t hurry; next year will be fine. Matilda Angelina Araminta Phelps!”

A thimble falls, and the child grabs her claws from the sugar bowl without hesitating. Just then the Black woman steps into the hallway and says:

“Missus, a sheet is missing.”

“A sheet missing! Well, for heaven’s sake!”

“I’ll stop those holes today,” says Uncle Silas, looking sorrowful.

“Oh, do shut it! Suppose the rats took the sheet? Where is it, Lize?”

"I'll be honest, I have no idea, Miss Sally. She was on the clothesline yesterday, but she's gone now; she's not here any more."

"I think the world is ending. I have never felt its rhythm in all my life. A shirt, a sheet, a spoon, and six cans—"

"Miss," says a young yellow girl, "they're missing a brass candlestick."

"Clear out of here, you hussy, or I'll hit you with a skillet!"

Well, she was just babbling. I started to look for an opportunity. I thought I would sneak out and walk into the woods until the weather calmed. She kept shouting, fighting on her own, while everyone else stayed quiet and calm. Everyone else was very meek and quiet. Finally, Uncle Silas, looking a bit foolish, pulled that spoon out of his pocket.

She stopped, her mouth open and her hands raised. As for me, I wished I were in Jerusalem or somewhere else.

But not for long, because she said, "It's just as I expected. So you had it in your pocket all the time, and probably you have the other things there too. How did it get there?"

"I really don't know, Sally," he answered, sounding apologetic. "If I knew, I would tell you. I was studying my text in Acts 17 before breakfast, and I think I put it in my pocket without noticing. I meant to put my Testament in, and it must be there because my Testament isn't. I’ll go check; if the Testament is where I left it, I’ll know I didn’t put it in, and that will show that I put the Testament down and took the spoon, and—"

“Oh, for the country’s sake! Give someone a break! Go away now, take all your gear with you, and don’t come near me again until I’ve found my peace of mind.”

I would have heard her even if she had only thought it, let alone said it out loud; and I would have gotten up and obeyed her even if I were dead. As we passed through the sitting room, the old man took off his hat. A shingle nail fell onto the floor. He simply picked it up, put it on the mantelpiece, said nothing, and left. Tom saw him do that and remembered the spoon, and said, “Well, there’s no point sending things with him any more; he isn’t reliable.” Then he added, “He did us a favor with the spoon, even though he didn’t know it. So we’ll do him a favor too, without him noticing—by blocking his rat holes.”

There were many rat holes in the cellar, and it took us a whole hour, but we did the job cleanly and well. Then we heard steps on the stairs, we blew out our light and hid. The old man came in, holding a candle in one hand and a bundle of things in the other, looking as absent‑minded as ever. He walked around, examining one rat hole and then another, until he had checked them all. He stood for about five minutes, picking tallow drops from his candle and thinking. Then he turned slowly and dreamily toward the stairs, saying:

“Honestly, I can’t remember when I did it. I could show her now that I wasn’t to blame because of the rats. But never mind—let it go. I don’t think it would help.”

He kept mumbling as he went up the stairs, and then we left. He was a very nice old man. He still is.

Tom was quite worried about what to do with a spoon, but he said we had to have it, so he thought about it. When he figured it out, he told me what we should do. Then we waited by the spoon basket until Aunt Sally came. Tom started counting the spoons and putting them aside, and I slipped one into my sleeve. Tom said:

“Why, Aunt Sally, there aren’t even nine spoons yet.”

She replied:

“Go back to your play and don’t bother me. I know better; I counted them myself.”

“Well, I’ve counted them twice, Auntie, and I still only get nine.”

She grew impatient, but of course she counted—anyone would.

“I declare there aren’t just nine!” she said. “What in the world—curse these things, I’ll count them again.”

I slipped the spoon back, and when she finished counting she said:

“Darn the troublesome rubbish, there are ten now!” She looked annoyed.

Tom said:

“Auntie, I don’t think there are ten.”

“You fool, didn’t you see me count them?”

“I know, but—”

“Well, I’ll count them again.”

I touched one, and nine appeared, just like the other time. She was shaking all over, really angry. She kept counting until she got confused and even started counting a spoon that was in the basket. Three times the numbers were right, three times they were wrong. Then she grabbed the basket and slammed it across the house, hitting the cat in the kitchen. She shouted that she was done and wanted some peace, and warned that if we bothered her again before dinner she would beat us.

We took an odd spoon and slipped it into her apron pocket while she was giving us our sailing orders. Jim got it right, along with her shingle nail, before noon. We were very satisfied with this, and Tom said it was worth twice the trouble because now she could never count the spoons correctly again. He said she wouldn’t even believe she had counted them right, and after three days of counting she would probably give up and threaten anyone who tried to make her count again.

That night we put the sheet back on the line and took one from her closet. We kept putting it back and taking it again for a few days until she didn’t know how many sheets she had left. She didn’t care and wasn’t going to waste her energy on it. She wouldn’t count them again even to save her life; she’d rather die first.

We were all right now with the shirt, the sheet, the spoon and the candles. The calf, the rats and the confused counting helped us. The candlestick wasn’t a problem; it would fall over later.

But that pie was a lot of work. We had many troubles with it. We prepared it deep in the woods and cooked it there. In the end we finished it, and it turned out quite well, but not in one day. We used three wash‑pans of flour before we finished, and we got burnt in many places. The smoke hurt our eyes. We only wanted a crust, but we couldn’t support it properly, so it kept collapsing. Finally we thought of the right solution – we would also cook the ladder inside the pie.

The next night we stayed with Jim. We tore the sheet into small strips and twisted them together. Before sunrise we had a strong rope that could hold a person. We pretended it took nine months to make.

In the morning we took the rope down to the woods, but it wouldn’t fit into the pie. Because it was made from a whole sheet, the rope was long enough for forty pies, with plenty left over for soup, sausage or anything else. We could have made a whole dinner.

We didn’t need it. All we needed was just enough for the pie, so we threw the rest away. We didn’t bake any of the pies in the wash‑pan because we were afraid the solder would melt. Uncle Silas had a nice brass warming pan that he thought was special. It had a long wooden handle and was said to have come from England on the Mayflower or another early ship. He kept it in the attic with other old pots and things that were valuable, not because they were useful, but because they were relics. We took it out quietly and brought it down, but it failed on the first pies because we didn’t know how to use it. It worked on the last one. We lined it with dough, placed it in the coals, tied it with rag rope, put a dough lid on, closed the lid, and put hot embers on top. We stood five feet away, holding the long handle, cool and comfortable. In fifteen minutes it produced a pie that was a pleasure to see. But whoever ate it would want to bring a couple of kegs of toothpicks, because if that rope ladder didn’t keep him busy I don’t know what I’m talking about, and it would give him enough stomach‑ache to last until next time.

Nat didn’t look when we put the witch pie in Jim’s pan. We placed the three tin plates at the bottom of the pan under the food. Jim got everything ready. As soon as he was alone, he broke into the pie, hid the rope ladder inside his straw hat, scratched some marks on a tin plate, and threw it out of the window.
