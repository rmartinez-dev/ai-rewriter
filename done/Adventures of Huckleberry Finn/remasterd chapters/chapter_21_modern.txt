It was after sunrise, but we kept going and didn’t stop. The king and the duke soon looked very tired. After they jumped overboard and swam, they felt a lot better.  

After breakfast the king sat on the corner of the raft. He took off his boots, rolled up his trousers, and let his legs dangle in the water so he could be comfortable. He lit his pipe and began reciting *Romeo and Juliet* from memory.  

When he had learned it well, he and the duke started practicing together. The duke had to teach him repeatedly how to say each line. He made the king sigh and put his hand on his heart. After a while the king said he could do it quite well.  

“Only,” the duke said, “you must not shout ‘Romeo!’ like a bull. Say it softly, almost like ‘R‑o‑o‑meo.’ Juliet is a sweet young girl, and she doesn’t sound like a donkey.”  

Next they took out two long swords that the duke had made from oak strips and began a sword‑fight practice. The duke called himself Richard III. The way they moved and pranced around the raft was great to watch.  

After a while the king slipped and fell overboard. They rested and talked about the many adventures they had had on the river in earlier times.  

After dinner the duke said:

“Well, Capet, we want this to be a top‑class show. So we’ll add a little more to it. We also need something to use for encores.

“What’s an encore, Bilgewater?”

The duke answered, “I’ll perform the Highland fling or the sailor’s hornpipe. And you—let me think—yes, you can recite Hamlet’s soliloquy.”

“Which part of Hamlet?”

“Hamlet’s soliloquy, you know, the most famous speech in Shakespeare. It’s wonderful, wonderful! It always makes the audience clap. I don’t have it in a script—I only have one book—but I think I can remember it. I’ll walk around for a minute and see if I can bring it back from memory.”

He started pacing back and forth, thinking. He frowned sometimes, raised his eyebrows, put a hand to his forehead, staggered back and moaned. He sighed, then pretended to shed a tear. It was beautiful to watch. After a while he remembered the lines. He asked us to pay attention. He took a noble pose: one leg forward, arms stretched upward, head tilted back toward the sky. Then he began to speak with passion, grinding his teeth. Throughout the speech he shouted, spread his arms, puffed out his chest, and gave a performance that surprised me more than any acting I had ever seen. This is the speech—I learned it easily while he was practicing it for the king:

To exist or not to exist—that is the simple dagger that turns a long life into misery. Who would bear such burdens until the forest of Birnam reaches Dunsinane? Only the fear of something after death stops us, stealing the peace of innocent sleep, the second act of nature, and making us prefer to launch the arrows of cruel fate rather than run toward unknown paths. This thought should make us pause. Wake Duncan with your knock! I wish you could. Who would endure the blows and insults of time, the oppressor’s injustice, the proud man’s contempt, the law’s delay, and the final release that his pain might bring? In the dead quiet of night, when cemeteries open their doors in black robes, the unknown country beyond whose border no traveler returns spreads disease over the world. Thus the natural resolve, like the poor cat in the proverb, becomes sick with worry. All the clouds that hang over our roofs turn their currents upside down and lose the name of action. It is an ending we would truly like to see. But wait, fair Ophelia: do not open your heavy, stone‑cold mouth. Instead, go to a convent—leave!

Well, the old man liked that speech, and he quickly learned it well enough to perform it perfectly. It seemed as if he was born for it. When he was involved and excited, it was impressive how he would tear and rip everything behind him as he finished.

The first opportunity we had, the duke printed some show posters. For the next two or three days while we drifted along, the raft was a lively place; there was nothing but sword fighting and rehearsals—what the duke called “practice”—happening all the time.

One morning, when we were well down the state of Arkansas, we spotted a small one‑horse town in a big bend of the river. We tied up about three‑quarters of a mile upstream, at the mouth of a creek that was hidden like a tunnel by cypress trees. Everyone except Jim took the canoe and went down to see if there was any chance for our show there.

We were very lucky. A circus was scheduled to be in town that afternoon, and the country people were already arriving in all kinds of old, rickety wagons and on horses. The circus would leave before night, so our show had a good chance. The duke hired the courthouse, and we went around posting our flyers. They read:

Shakespeare Revival!!!
Wonderful Attraction!
For One Night Only!
The world‑renowned tragedians,
David Garrick the younger, of Drury Lane Theatre, London,
and
Edmund Kean the elder, of the Royal Haymarket Theatre,
Whitechapel, Pudding Lane, Piccadilly, London, and the
Royal Continental Theatres,
in their sublime Shakespearean Spectacle entitled
The Balcony Scene
from
Romeo and Juliet!!!

Romeo...................................... Mr. Garrick.
Juliet..................................... Mr. Kean.

Supported by the entire company!
New costumes, new scenery, new lighting!

Also:
The thrilling, masterful, and blood‑curdling
Broad‑sword conflict
in Richard III!!!

Richard III................................ Mr. Garrick.
Richmond.................................. Mr. Kean.

Also (by special request):
Hamlet’s Immortal Soliloquy!!
By the famous Kean!
He performed it for 300 straight nights in Paris.
Now it will be shown for one night only, because of urgent European duties.
Admission: 25 cents; children and servants: 10 cents.

Then we spent the day wandering around the town. The shops and houses were mostly old, shabby, and never painted. They were built three or four feet above the ground on stilts so they would stay dry when the river flooded. The houses had small gardens, but they hardly grew anything except weeds, sunflowers, piles of ash, old twisted boots and shoes, broken bottle pieces, rags, and rusty tin items. The fences were made of different boards nailed together at different times; they leaned in all directions and often had gates that hung on a single leather hinge. Some fences had been whitewashed at various times, but the duke said they dated back to the time of Clumbus, more or less. There were usually pigs in the gardens, and people were driving them away.

All the stores were on the same street. They had white canvas awnings in front, and the country people tied their horses to the awning posts. Empty dry‑goods boxes sat under the awnings, and loafers lounged on them all day, carving them with their Barlow knives, chewing tobacco, yawning, and stretching—a very unruly bunch. They usually wore wide yellow straw hats, as big as umbrellas, but no coats or waistcoats. They called each other Bill, Buck, Hank, Joe, Andy, and talked slowly and lazily, using many curse words. There was about one loafer leaning against every awning post, and he almost always kept his hands in his trouser pockets, except when he took them out to offer a chew of tobacco or scratch an itch. What you would hear among them all the time was:

“Give me a chew, Tobacker, Hank.”

“Can’t; I only have one chew left. Ask Bill.”

Maybe Bill gives him a chew; maybe he lies and says he has none. Some of these loafers never have a cent, nor a chew of tobacco of their own. They get all their chewing by borrowing; they say to a friend, “I wish you’d lend me a chew, Jack; I’ll just give Ben Thompson the last chew I had.” That is a lie almost every time; it doesn’t fool anyone but a stranger. But Jack isn’t a stranger, so he says:

“You gave him a chew, didn’t you? So did your sister’s cat’s grandmother. Pay me back the chews you already borrowed from me, Lafe Buckner, then I’ll lend you one or two more, and I won’t charge any interest.”

“Well, I really did pay you back a little bit back then.”

“Yes, you did—about six chews. You borrowed a store plug and paid the guy back.”

Store tobacco is a flat black plug, but most people chew the natural leaf that’s twisted. When they borrow a chew, they don’t usually cut it with a knife. Instead, they put the plug between their teeth, bite it, and pull at it with their hands until it breaks in two. Then sometimes the owner looks sad when it’s handed back and says, sarcastically:

“Here, give me the chew and you keep the plug.”

All the streets and lanes were just mud; there was nothing but mud—mud as black as tar, about a foot deep in some places and two or three inches deep everywhere else. The pigs loitered and grunted all around. You could see a muddy sow with a litter of piglets strolling lazily down the street and plopping herself right in the middle of the road. People had to walk around her. She would stretch out, close her eyes, and wiggle her ears while the piglets nuzzled her, looking as happy as if she were getting paid. Soon an idle fellow would shout, “Hey, you! Get him, Tiger!” and the sow would run away, squealing loudly, with a dog or two tugging at her ears, and three or four dozen more coming after her. Then all the idle folk would get up, watch the scene disappear, laugh at the fun, and feel grateful for the noise. They would settle back down until a dog fight started. Nothing could wake them up and make them as happy as a dog fight—unless someone poured turpentine on a stray dog and set it on fire, or tied a tin pan to its tail and watched it run itself to death.

On the riverbank some houses stretched out over the edge. They were bowed and bent, almost ready to fall in. Their owners had moved out. The bank had collapsed under a corner of other houses, leaving that corner hanging over the water. Some people still lived there, but it was dangerous. Sometimes a strip of ground as wide as a house suddenly gives way. At other times a stretch of land a quarter of a mile long starts to collapse and keeps collapsing until it all slides into the river in one summer. A town like this has to keep moving farther away because the river is constantly eroding it.

As noon approached, more and more wagons and horses filled the streets. New ones kept arriving. Families brought dinner from the countryside and ate it inside the wagons. Lots of whisky was being drunk, and I saw three fights. Soon someone shouted:

“Here comes old Boggs! He’s back from the country for his little monthly drink. Here he comes, boys!”

All the idle men smiled. I thought they enjoyed Boggs’s visits. One of them said:

“Wonder what he’s going to chew up this time. If he had chewed up all the men he’s been chewing on for the last twenty years, he’d have a big reputation now.”

Another said, “I wish old Boggs would threaten me, because then I’d know I’m not going to die for a thousand years.”

Boggs rode in quickly on his horse, shouting and yelling like a native, and sang:

“Clear the track, there. I’m on the road, and the price of coffins is about to go up.”  

He was drunk, swaying in his saddle. He was over fifty years old and his face was very red. Everyone shouted at him, laughed at him, and teased him. He teased them back and said he would deal with them in his own time, but he couldn’t wait now because he had come to town to kill old Colonel Sherburn. His motto was, “First the meat, then a little extra food.”  

He saw me, rode up and said:  

“Where are you coming from, boy? Are you ready to die?”  

Then he rode on. I was scared, but another man said:  

“He doesn’t mean any harm; he always acts like that when he’s drunk. He’s the most harmless old fool in Arkansas—never hurts anyone, drunk or sober.”  

Boggs rode up to the biggest store in town, lowered his head to look under the awning and shouted:  

“Come out here, Sherburn! Come out and meet the man you’ve swindled. You’re the one I’m after, and I’m going to get you, too!”  

He kept calling Sherburn every insult he could think of, and the whole street filled with people listening, laughing, and watching. Soon a proud‑looking man about fifty‑five, the best‑dressed man in town, stepped out of the store. The crowd moved aside to let him pass. He said to Boggs, calmly and slowly—he said:

“I’m tired of this, but I’ll put up with it until one o’clock. Until one o’clock—mind you, no more. If you say anything to me after that, you won’t get very far, but I’ll find you.”

Then he turned and went inside. The crowd looked very serious; nobody moved, and the laughing stopped. Boggs rode away, shouting insults at Sherburn as loudly as he could, down the street. Soon he came back and stopped in front of the store, still shouting. A group of men gathered around him and tried to make him be quiet, but he wouldn’t. They told him it would be one o’clock in about fifteen minutes, so he had to go home—he had to leave right away. It didn’t help. He cursed with all his strength, threw his hat into the mud and rode over it, and soon he was raging down the street again, his gray hair flying. Everyone who could reach him tried to pull him off his horse so they could lock him up and make him sober, but it was no use. He kept tearing up the street, shouting at Sherburn again. After a while someone said:

“Go for his daughter! Quickly, go for his daughter; sometimes he’ll listen to her. If anyone can persuade him, it’s her.”

Someone started running. I walked down the street for a while and stopped.

After five or ten minutes Boggs appeared again, but this time he wasn’t on his horse. He stumbled across the street toward me, bare‑headed, with a friend on each side holding his arms and hurrying him along. He was quiet and looked uneasy. He wasn’t holding back; he was also trying to hurry.

Someone shouted, “Boggs!” I turned to see who it was, and it was Colonel Sherburn. He was standing perfectly still in the street, a pistol raised in his right hand. He wasn’t aiming it; he just held it out with the barrel pointed upward toward the sky.

At the same moment I saw a young girl running toward us, followed by two men. Boggs and the men turned to see who had called him. When they saw the pistol, the men stepped aside, and the barrel slowly lowered to a level position, both barrels cocked. Boggs raised both of his hands and said, “O Lord, don’t shoot!”

The first shot rang out. Boggs staggered back, flailing his arms. A second shot followed, and he fell backward onto the ground, heavy and solid, his arms outstretched.

The young girl screamed, rushed forward, and threw herself onto her father, crying, “Oh, he’s killed him, he’s killed him!” The crowd gathered around them, pushing and jostling each other, stretching their necks to see. People inside tried to shove the crowd back, shouting, “Back, back! Give him air, give him air!”

Colonel Sherburn tossed his pistol onto the ground, turned on his heels, and walked away.

They took Boggs to a small drugstore. The crowd pressed in, and the whole town followed. I rushed to the window and got a good spot. I was close enough to see inside.

They laid him on the floor, placed a large Bible under his head, and opened another Bible and spread it over his chest. First they tore open his shirt, and I saw where a bullet had entered.

He took about a dozen long gasps. His chest lifted the Bible as he inhaled and lowered it as he exhalated. Then he stopped moving; he was dead.

Then they pulled his daughter away from him. She was screaming and crying. She was about sixteen, sweet and gentle‑looking, but very pale and scared.

Soon the whole town was there, squirming, pushing, and shoving to see through the window. The people who had good spots would not give them up. Those behind them kept saying, “Enough, you have looked enough. It isn’t right or fair for you to stay there all the time and block everyone else. Others have the same right as you.”

There was a lot of arguing, so I stepped aside, thinking trouble might start. The streets were crowded and everyone was excited. Everyone who had seen the shooting was describing how it happened, and a big crowd gathered around each of the men, craning their necks to listen.

A tall, thin man with long hair, a big white fur stovepipe hat pulled back on his head, and a crooked‑handled cane marked on the ground where Boggs had stood and where Sherburn had stood. People followed him from place to place, watching everything he did, nodding to show they understood, bending a little and resting their hands on their thighs while he marked the spots with his cane. Then he stood straight and stiff where Sherburn had stood, frowning, his hat brim pulled down over his eyes, and shouted, “Boggs!” He lowered his cane slowly to the ground, said “Bang!”, staggered backward, said “Bang!” again, and fell flat on his back. The witnesses said he did it perfectly; they said it was exactly how it had happened. About a dozen people then took out their bottles and toasted him.

Soon someone said Sherburn should be lynched. Within a minute everyone was saying it; they rushed off, angry and shouting, grabbing any clothesline they could find to use for the hanging.
