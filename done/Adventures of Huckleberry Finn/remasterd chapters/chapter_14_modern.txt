Soon after we got up, we turned over the truck the gang had taken from the wreck. Inside we found boots, blankets, clothes, lots of other things, many books, a spyglass, and three boxes of seegars. We had never been this rich before in either of our lives. The seegars were top‑quality.  

We spent the whole afternoon in the woods. We talked, I read the books, and we just had a good time. I told Jim everything that had happened inside the wreck and at the ferry‑boat. I called those events adventures, but he said he didn’t want any more adventures. He told me that when I went into the water and he crawled back onto the raft and found her gone, he almost died. He thought it was all over for him, but he believed something could be fixed. If he wasn’t saved, he would drown; if he was saved, whoever rescued him would likely send him back home to claim a reward, and then Miss Watson would sell him South for sure. He was right. He was almost always right. He had an unusually clear head for a black man.  

I read to Jim a lot about kings, dukes, earls and similar nobles—how they dressed in bright colors, how formal they were, and how they called each other “Your Majesty,” “Your Grace,” “Your Lordship,” instead of “Mister.” Jim’s eyes widened, and he was interested. He says:

“I didn’t know there were so many of them. I haven’t heard about any of them, except old King Solomon, unless you count the other kings that live in a group of courtyards. How much does a king get?”

“Get?” I say. “They get a thousand dollars a month if they want it. They can have as much as they like; everything belongs to them.”

“Isn’t that gay? And what do they have to do, Huck?”

“They don’t do anything! Why, how do you talk! They just sit around.”

“No, is that so?”

“Of course it is. They just sit around—except maybe when there’s a war; then they go to war. At other times they are lazy, or they go hawking—just hawking and—sh!—did you hear a noise?”

We went out and looked, but it was only the flutter of a steamboat’s wheel far away, turning around the point, so we went back.

“Yes,” I say, “and other times, when things are dull, they argue with the parliament; and if everyone doesn’t behave, he hits them on the head. But mostly they hang around the harem.”

“Around which?”

“The harem.”

“What’s the harem?”

“The place where he keeps his wives. Don’t you know about the harem? Solomon had one; he had about a million wives.”

“Yeah, that’s right; I had completely forgotten it. I think a harem is a boarding house. They probably have noisy times in the nursery. I also think the wives argue a lot, and that adds to the noise. But they say Sollermun is the wisest man who ever lived. I don’t believe that. Why would a wise man want to live in the middle of such constant noise all the time? He wouldn’t. A wise man would build a boiler factory, and then he could shut the factory down whenever he wants to rest.

Well, he was the wisest man, anyway, because the widow herself told me so.

I don’t know what the widow said; he wasn’t a wise man either. He had some of his father’s foolish habits I’ve never seen before. Do you know about that child he used to try to cut in half?”

Well then! Do you want to see the beaten idea in the world? Just take a look at it for a minute. There's the stump – that's one of the women. Here you are – that's the other one. I'm Solomon, and this dollar bill is the child. You claim it and everything around it. What should I do? Should I wander among the neighbors, find out which one the bill belongs to, and hand it over to the right person, all safe and sound, like anyone with any courage would? No. I split the bill in two, give half to you, and the other half to the other woman. That's the way Solomon was going to handle the child. Now I want to ask you: what's the use of that half a bill? You can't buy anything with it. And what's the use of half a child? I wouldn't give a darn for a million of them.

But hang on, Jim, you've completely missed the point—no, you've missed it by a thousand miles.

Who? Me? Go away. Don't talk to me about your drinks. I think I recognize sense when I see it, and there's no sense in doing things like that. The dispute wasn't about half a child; the dispute was about a whole child. And a man who thinks he can settle a dispute about a whole child with half a child doesn't know enough to get out of the rain. Don't talk to me about Solomon, Huck; I know him by heart.

But I tell you, you don't get the point.

Blame the point! I think I know what I know. In my opinion, the real point is deeper—it’s further down. It lies in the way Solomon was raised. If you take a man who has only one or two children, is that man going to waste his children? No, he isn’t. He can’t afford to. He knows how to value them. But if you take a man who has about five million children running around the house, it’s different. He will cut a child in two like a cat. There are many more. One child here, another there—more or less—doesn’t matter to Solomon. Dad, fetch him!

I never see a man like that. If he gets an idea in his head once, there is no getting it out again. He was the most down on Solomon of any man I’ve ever seen. So I started talking about other kings and let Solomon slide. I talked about Louis Sixteenth, who had his head cut off in France a long time ago, and about his little boy, the dolphin, who would have been a king. They locked him up in jail, and some say he died there.

“Poor little chap.”

“But some say he got out, escaped, and came to America.”

“That’s good! But he’ll be terribly lonely—there are no kings here, are there, Huck?”

“No.”

“Then he can’t find a place. What will he do?”

“Well, I don’t know. Some of them go to the police, and some teach people how to speak French.”

“Why, Huck, don’t the French people talk the same way we do?”

“No, Jim; you couldn’t understand a single word they said.”

“Well now, I’m busted! How did that happen?”

“I don’t know, but that’s how it is. I read some of their talk in a book. Suppose a man came to you and said ‘Polly‑voo‑franzy’—what would you think?”

“I wouldn’t think anything; I’d hit him over the head— that is, if he wasn’t white. I wouldn’t let a black person call me that.”

“Come on, it’s not calling you anything. It’s only asking, do you know how to speak French?”

“Well then, why couldn’t he just say it?”

“He is saying it. That’s a Frenchman’s way of saying it.”

“Well, it’s a ridiculous way, and I don’t want to hear any more about it. There’s no sense in it.”

“Look here, Jim; does a cat talk like we do?”

“No, a cat doesn’t.”

“Well, does a cow?”

“No, a cow doesn’t either.”

“Does a cat talk like a cow, or does a cow talk like a cat?”

“No, they don’t.”

“It’s natural and right for them to talk differently from each other, isn’t it?”

“Of course.”

“And isn’t it natural and right for a cat and a cow to talk differently from us?”

“Yes, of course it is.”

“Well then, why isn’t it natural and right for a Frenchman to talk differently from us? Answer me that.”

“Is a cat a man, Huck?”

“No.”

“Well then, there’s no sense in a cat talking like a man. Is a cow a man? Or is a cow a cat?”

“No, it’s neither.”

“Well then, it has no business talking like either of them. Is a Frenchman a man?”

“Yes.”

“Well then! If that’s the case, why doesn’t he talk like a man? Answer that!”

I realized it was useless to keep talking—you can't teach someone to argue. So I gave up.
