All day he and the king worked hard, setting up a stage, a curtain, and a row of candles for footlights. That night the theater filled quickly with men. When it was full, the duke stopped standing at the door, went around the back, came onto the stage, stood in front of the curtain and gave a short speech. He praised the tragedy, saying it was the most thrilling one ever. He kept bragging about the play and about Edmund Kean the Elder, who was playing the main role. When he had built everyone's expectations high enough, he pulled back the curtain. The next moment the king came prancing on all fours, naked, painted with rings, stripes and all kinds of colors, looking like a rainbow. Don't worry about the rest of his costume; it was wild but terribly funny. The audience laughed so hard they almost died. When the king finished his antics and disappeared behind the scenes, they cheered, clapped, shouted, and begged him to come back and do it again. He did it another time. It would make a cow laugh to see the jokes that old fool cut.

The duke lowered the curtain, bowed to the audience, and announced that the great tragedy would be performed only two more nights because of busy engagements in London, where the seats at Drury Lane were already sold out. He bowed again and asked the audience to tell their friends about the play and invite them to come.

Twenty people shouted:

“What, is it over? Is that all?”

The duke answered yes. Then there was a lively applause. Everyone shouted, “Sold!” and rushed toward the stage and the actors. A tall, well‑dressed man jumped onto a bench and shouted:

“Hold on! Just a moment, gentlemen.” They stopped to listen. “We’re sold—very badly sold. But we don’t want to become the laughing‑stock of the whole town, and we don’t want to hear about this forever. No. What we want is to leave quietly, promote this show, and sell it to the rest of the town! Then we’ll all be in the same boat. Isn’t that sensible?” 

“Exactly!” everyone shouted. “Alright then—no more talk about selling. Go home and tell everyone to come and see the tragedy.”

The next day you couldn't hear anything in that town except how wonderful the show was. The house was packed again that night, and we sold to the crowd the same way. When the king, the duke, and I got back to the raft we all had supper. A little later, around midnight, Jim and I took her out, floated her down the middle of the river, then pulled her in and hid her about two miles downstream from town.

The third night the house was crowded again, and they weren't newcomers this time. They were people who had been at the shows the previous two nights. I stood by the duke at the door and saw that every man who went in had bulging pockets or something hidden under his coat. It wasn't perfume, not even close. I smelled sickly eggs near the barrel, rotten cabbage, and similar things. If I know the smell of a dead cat, there were sixty‑four of them that went in. I pushed inside for a minute, but it was too much for me; I couldn't stand it.

When the place couldn't hold any more people, the duke gave a man a quarter and told him to watch the door for a minute. Then he started toward the stage door, and I followed him. The moment we turned the corner and were in the dark, he said:

“Walk fast now until you get away from the houses, and then run for the raft like the devil is after you!”

I did it, and he did the same. We hit the raft at the same time, and in less than two seconds we were gliding downstream, dark and still, moving toward the middle of the river. Nobody said a word. I thought the poor king was going to have a wild time with the audience, but that didn’t happen. Soon he crawled out from under the wigwam and said,  

“​Well, how did the old thing turn out this time, duke?”  

He hadn’t been in town at all.  

We didn’t show a light until we were about ten miles downstream from the village. Then we lit one, had dinner, and the king and the duke laughed loudly at how they had served the people. The duke said:  

“​Greenhorns, flatheads! I knew the first house would stay quiet and let the rest of the town get involved. I knew they’d set us up for the third night and think it was their turn now. Well, it is their turn, and I’d love to know how much they’d charge for it. I want to see how they use their chance. They could turn it into a picnic if they want—they brought plenty of provisions.”  

Those scoundrels took $465 in those three nights. I had never seen that much money carried in a wagon before. Later, while they were asleep and snoring, Jim said:  

“​Doesn’t it surprise you how those kings behave, Huck?”  

“No,” I said, “it doesn’t.”  

“Why not, Huck?”  

“Because it’s just their nature. I think they’re all the same.”  

“But, Huck, these kings of ours are regular scoundrels; that’s exactly what they are; they’re regular scoundrels.”

Well, that's what I'm saying: most kings are basically scoundrels, as far as I can tell.

Is that so?

You read about them once and you’ll understand. Look at Henry VIII; he is like a Sunday‑school superintendent to himself. Also look at Charles II, Louis XIV, Louis XV, James II, Edward II, Richard III, and many more—forty other kings. Think of all those old Saxon kingdoms that used to fight and cause trouble in ancient times.

You should have seen Henry VIII when he was young. He was a real flower. He would marry a new wife every day and have her head cut off the next morning. He did it as easily as ordering eggs. “Bring me Nell Gwyn,” he would say. They brought her. The next morning he said, “Cut off her head!” and they did. Then he said, “Bring me Jane Shore.” She came. The next morning he said, “Cut off her head!” and they did. He kept doing the same with many women.

He made each of them tell him a story every night. He collected a thousand and one tales, put them in a book, and called it the Domesday Book. The name fit well.

You don’t know kings, Jim, but I do. This old story is one of the clearest I have found in history. Henry thought of causing trouble for the country. How did he do it? Not by giving notice or putting on a show. Suddenly he threw all the tea out of Boston Harbor, wrote a declaration of independence, and dared them to act. That was his style—he never gave anyone a chance.

He mistrusted his father, the Duke of Wellington. What did he do? He didn’t ask him to appear; he drowned him in a barrel, like a cat. If people left money where he was, he took it. If he promised to do something and got paid, he would do the opposite. If he opened his mouth, he would quickly shut it, otherwise he would lose a lie every time. That’s the kind of bug Henry was. If we had him instead of our kings, he would have fooled that town even more than we did.

I don’t say that our town is innocent, because it isn’t, when you look at the cold facts; but it is nothing compared to that old ram. All I say is, kings are kings, and you have to make allowances. All of them are a very stubborn lot. It’s the way they were raised.

"But this one smells so much like the nation, Huck."

"Well, they all do, Jim. We can't help the way a king smells; history doesn't explain it."

"The duke, he's a fairly likely man in some ways."

"Yes, a duke is different, but not very different. This one is a pretty tough guy for a duke. When he's drunk, no short‑sighted person could tell him apart from a king."

"Anyway, I don't want any more of that, Huck. This is all I can stand."

"I feel the same, Jim. But we have them with us, and we have to remember what they are and make allowances. Sometimes I wish we could hear about a country without kings."

What was the point of telling Jim these weren't real kings and dukes? It wouldn't have helped; besides, as I said, you couldn't tell them from the real ones.

I went to sleep, and Jim didn’t call me when it was my turn. He often did that. When I woke up at daybreak, he was sitting with his head in his knees, moaning and mourning to himself. I didn’t notice him or pretend anything. I knew why he was upset. He was thinking about his wife and his children, who were far away. He felt low and homesick because he had never been away from home before in his life. I believe he cared for his family as much as white people care for theirs. It may not seem natural, but I think it is true. He often moaned at night, when he thought I was asleep, saying, “Poor little Elizabeth! Poor little Johnny! It’s so hard; I don’t think I’ll ever see you again!” Jim was a very good man.

This time I managed to talk to him about his wife and children. After a while he said:

“What makes me feel so bad now is that I hear someone over there on the bank, a slap or a slam, like the one from a while ago. It reminds me of the time I treated my little Elizabeth badly. She was only about four years old and she had scarlet fever. She had a very rough spell, but she got better. One day she was standing around and I said to her, ‘She’ll do it.’ She never did it; she just stood there, smiling at me. It made me angry, and I said again, louder, ‘Don’t you hear me? She’ll do it!’”

She just stood there, smiling a little. I was shocked! I said, “I’m going to make you mine!” Then I gave her a slap on the side of the head, and she fell down.  

I went into the other room and was gone for about ten minutes. When I came back, a door was open and the girl was standing right in the doorway. She was looking down, crying, with tears running down her face.  

I was angry! I wanted to help her, but a door inside opened and a gust of wind slammed it shut behind the girl—bang! She didn’t move at all. My breath almost left me. I didn’t know how I felt.  

I crouched, shaking, and opened the door slowly. I peeked my head behind her, soft and still, and then I shouted “pow!” as loudly as I could. She didn’t move.  

Oh, Huck, I started crying, lifted her into my arms, and said, “Oh, poor little thing! May the Lord God Almighty forgive poor old Jim, because he never forgives himself as long as he lives!” She was completely deaf and mute, Huck, completely deaf and mute—and I had treated her that way.
