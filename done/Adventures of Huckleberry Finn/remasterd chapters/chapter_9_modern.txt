I wanted to visit a spot on the island that I had discovered while exploring. It was roughly halfway across the island, so we set off and soon arrived there.

The island was only three miles long and a quarter of a mile wide. Our destination was a decent-sized hill or ridge about forty feet high. We found it challenging to reach the top because the slope was steep and the undergrowth was thick. We climbed all over the hill, and eventually we stumbled upon a large cavern in the rock face.

The cavern was enormous – as big as three rooms stacked on top of each other – and Jim could stand upright inside it. The air inside was cool and refreshing. Jim suggested that we store our traps inside the cavern, so we wouldn't have to keep climbing up and down all day.

He said if we hid the canoe in a good spot and stored all the traps in the cavern, we could quickly escape if anyone came to the island. And with the rain forecasted by some birds – I didn't want our gear to get wet. So, we headed back to fetch the canoe and paddled up to the cavern. We then lugged the traps up there and found a nearby spot to conceal the canoe amongst the thick willows.

We took some fish off the lines and put them back in, and started preparing for dinner.

The door to our cave was huge enough for a big barrel to fit through it, and on one side, there was a small hill sticking out that made a perfect spot to build a fire.

We spread out the blankets inside as a floor and sat down to eat dinner in there. We put all our other stuff at the back of the cave.

As time went by, it started getting dark and thunderstorm warnings began to sound true. It wasn't long before it started pouring rain like crazy. The wind was blowing so hard that I'd never seen anything like it before. This summer storm was intense.

The darkness made everything look a deep blue-black color that actually felt beautiful. The thick rain would lash around, making the trees just a few feet away look all spindly and thin. Then, a strong gust of wind would blow by, bending the branches down and revealing their pale undersides. Next, another powerful blast would come through, causing the branches to wave wildly as if they were alive. Finally, when it was almost completely dark - suddenly the sky lit up like it was shining with glory.

For an instant, I could see tree-tops swaying in the distance far beyond what my eyes could see. But then the darkness came rushing back and I heard the thunder boom loudly through the air, rolling down towards the earth like a long staircase filled with empty barrels bouncing down the steps.

"Jim, this is great," I said. "I wouldn't want to be anywhere else right now. Can you pass me some more fish and hot cornbread?"

"Well, you're lucky Jim didn't get washed away if it hadn't been for him," she replied. "You would've ended up in the woods with no food, drowning too - that's for sure. Animals know when it's going to rain, just like birds do."

The river kept rising and rising for ten or twelve days until it finally broke over its banks. The water was three to four feet deep on the island in the low places and along the Illinois shoreline. On the Missouri side, it was still only half a mile across - the shore was just a wall of high bluffs.

We spent our days paddling all around the island in the canoe. It was really cool and shady under the trees, even when the sun was beating down outside. We wandered through the woods, winding in and out of the trees, and sometimes the vines were so thick we had to back away to find another path. Every old broken-down tree had rabbits and snakes and other creatures living in them. When the island was flooded for a couple of days, they got so hungry that you could paddle up and touch them if you wanted to - but not the snakes and turtles; they would swim away into the water. The ridge our cave was on was full of those slippery critters. If we'd wanted pets, we could've had all we wanted."

One night we stumbled upon a small section of a lumber raft - nice pine planks. It was 12 feet wide and about 15 or 16 feet long, with the top above water just six or seven inches high. The floor was solid and level.

We could see saw-logs floating by during the day sometimes, but we let them pass us by. We didn't want to show ourselves in daylight.

Another night, when we were at the head of the island, just before sunrise, a frame-house came into view on the west side. It was two-story and tilted quite a bit. We paddled out and got aboard - climbing in through an upstairs window. But it was still too dark to see yet, so we secured the canoe and waited for daylight.

As the light began to come, we looked inside through the window. We could make out a bed, a table, two old chairs, and lots of stuff scattered around on the floor. There were clothes hanging against the wall, and something lying in the far corner that looked like a person. Huck said:

"Hello!"

But it didn't move. So I shouted again, and then Huck said:

"This guy's not asleep - he's dead. You hold still - I'll go take a look."

He went down and took a closer look, and then came back to say:

"It's a dead man. Yes, that's true. He was naked, too. Someone shot him in the back. I reckon he's been dead for two or three days. Come on inside, Huck, but don't even look at his face - it's too ugly."

I didn't look at him at all. When Jim threw some old rags over him, I knew he wasn't worth my attention.

The floor was littered with old cards and greasy whisky bottles. There were also two dirty calico dresses, a sun-hat, women's underclothes, men's clothes, and more hanging against the walls. The walls were covered in childish scribbles made with charcoal.

On the floor, I spotted a boy's old straw hat. I picked it up too. Near the canoe, there was an empty milk bottle with a broken rag stopper. We might have taken it if it wasn't broken.

There was also a seedy old chest and an old hair trunk that stood open, but they were empty except for some useless things.

The way everything was scattered around made us think the people had left in a hurry, without taking most of their belongings with them.

We had an old tin lantern, a knife without a handle, and a brand new Barlow knife worth two dollars in any store. We also had tallow candles, a tin candlestick, a gourd, a tin cup, an old bedquilt from the bed, a small bag with needles, pins, beeswax, buttons, thread, and other stuff in it, a hatchet, some nails, a thick fishline with huge hooks on it, a roll of buckskin, a leather dog-collar, a horseshoe, and some unlabelled medicine bottles. Just as we were leaving, I found a good curry-comb, and Jim found an old fiddle-bow. We also found a wooden leg, although the strap had broken off.

We made a good haul overall. When we were ready to leave, we paddled about a quarter of a mile below the island on a sunny day. So, I told Jim to lie down in the canoe and cover himself with the quilt, because if he stood up, people could see that he was black from far away. I paddled over to the Illinois shore and drifted down most of the half a mile while doing it. I crept along the calm water under the bank without any problems or encounters. We got home safely.
