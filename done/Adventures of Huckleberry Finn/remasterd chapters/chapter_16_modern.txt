We slept most of the day and set off at night, a little behind a huge long raft that moved like a procession. It had four long oars at each end, so we guessed it could hold about thirty men. On board were five big wigwams spaced far apart, a camp fire in the middle, and a tall flagpole at each end. It had a distinct style about it. It felt like being a raftsman on such a craft.

We drifted down into a big bend, and the night grew cloudy and hot. The river was very wide and bordered by solid timber on both sides; you could hardly ever see a break or a light. We talked about Cairo and wondered if we would recognize it when we arrived. I thought we probably wouldn’t, because I had heard there were only about a dozen houses there, and if they weren’t lit up, how would we know we were passing a town? Jim said that if the two big rivers joined there, that would be a sign. But I said we might think we were passing the foot of an island and re‑entering the same river again. That worried both Jim and me. So we asked ourselves what to do. I suggested we row ashore the first time we saw a light, tell them that a father was behind us, coming with a trading scow, that we were new to the business and wanted to know how far it was to Cairo. Jim liked the idea, so we smoked a pipe and waited.

There was nothing left to do but keep a sharp eye out for the town and not pass it without seeing it. He said he would be absolutely sure to see it, because he would be a free man the moment he saw it. But if he missed it, he would be back in a slave country again and would never get freedom. Every now and then he jumps up and says: “Is that her?”

But it wasn't. It was just jack‑o‑lanterns, or lightning bugs, so he sat down again and kept watching, just like before. Jim said it made him feel all shaky and feverish to be so close to freedom. 

Well, I can tell you it made me feel the same way—shaky and feverish—when I heard him. I began to realize that he was truly free, and I wondered who was to blame for that. The answer was me. I couldn't get that feeling out of my conscience, no matter how hard I tried. It bothered me so much that I couldn't rest or stay still in one place.

I had never understood before what I was doing, but now it made sense, and the feeling stayed with me and grew stronger. I tried to convince myself that I wasn't to blame because I hadn't chased Jim away from his rightful owner. That didn't help; my conscience kept saying, “You knew he was running for his freedom, and you could have gone ashore and told someone.” I couldn't ignore that any longer. 

That was the real sting. My conscience asked, “What did poor Miss Watson do to you that you could watch her black girl leave right in front of you and say nothing? What did that old woman do to deserve such treatment? She tried to teach you to read, to teach you good manners, she tried to be kind to you in every way she knew. That’s what she did.”

I felt so mean and miserable that I almost wished I were dead. I paced back and forth on the raft, cursing myself, and Jim paced back and forth beside me. Neither of us could stay still. Every time he danced around and shouted, “There’s Cairo!” it hit me like a shot, and I thought that if it really were Cairo I would die of misery.  

Jim kept talking out loud while I talked to myself. He said the first thing he would do when he reached a free state was to save money and never spend a single cent. When he had enough, he would buy his wife, who was owned on a farm near where Miss Watson lived. Then they would both work to buy their two children, and if their master wouldn’t sell them, they would find an abolitionist to help steal them.  

It shocked me to hear him say that. He had never spoken like that before. It showed how much he had changed the moment he thought he was near freedom. It reminded me of the old saying, “Give a black man an inch and he’ll take a yard.” I thought, this is what happens when I don’t think. Here was this black man, whom I had almost helped to escape, standing there bluntly and saying he would steal his children—children that belonged to a man I didn’t even know, a man who had never harmed me.

I felt sad when Jim said that; it made him look so low. My conscience kept nagging me more and more. Finally I said to it, “Give me a break—it isn’t too late yet. I’ll row ashore at first light and tell the truth.” Right away I felt light, happy, and as free as a feather. All my worries vanished. I began to look eagerly for a light, humming to myself. Soon a light appeared, and Jim shouted:

“We’re safe, Huck, we’re safe! Jump up and tap your heels! That’s the good old Cairo at last, I just know it!”

I replied, “Let me take the canoe and see, Jim. It might not be what we think.”

He leapt, got the canoe ready, and placed his old coat at the bottom for me to sit on. He handed me the paddle. As I pushed off, he said:

“Soon I’ll be shouting for joy, and I’ll say it’s all thanks to you, Huck. I’m a free man, and I could never have been free if it weren’t for you. You did this. I’ll never forget you, Huck; you’re the best friend I’ve ever had, and the only friend I have now.”

I started paddling away, sweating, ready to tell on him. But when he said those words, they seemed to take the wind out of my sails. I went slower, unsure whether I was glad I had started or not. When I was about fifty yards away, Jim called out:

“There you go, old faithful Huck, the only white gentleman who ever kept his promise to old Jim.”

I felt sick, but I said I had to do it—I couldn’t get out of it. Then a small boat with two men who had guns came along and stopped, and I stopped too. One of them asked:

“What’s that over there?”

“A piece of a raft,” I said.

“Do you belong on it?”

“Yes, sir.”

“Anyone else on it?”

“Only one, sir.”

“Well, five Black men ran off tonight up there, above the bend. Is your man white or Black?”

I didn’t answer right away. I tried, but the words wouldn’t come. I tried for a second or two to pull myself together, but I wasn’t strong enough—I didn’t have the courage of a rabbit. I saw I was weakening, so I gave up trying and said:

“He’s white.”

“I think we’ll go and see for ourselves.”

“I wish you would,” I said, “because there’s paper there, and maybe you could help me pull the raft ashore where the light is. He’s sick—and so are Mom and Mary Ann.”

“Oh, damn! We’re in a hurry, boy. But I guess we have to. Come, grab your paddle, and let’s go.”

I grabbed my paddle and they started rowing. After we made a stroke or two, I said:

“Dad would be very grateful to you, I can tell you that. Everyone leaves when I need help pulling the raft ashore, and I can’t do it alone.”

“That’s terrible. Strange, too. Hey, boy, what’s wrong with your father?”

“It’s… it’s… well, it’s not a big deal.”

They stopped rowing. The raft was only a short distance away now. One of them said:

“That's a lie, kid. What's wrong with your dad? Answer me straight now, and it will be better for you.”

“I will, sir, I promise—but please don’t leave us. It’s the gentlemen; if you just go ahead and let me give you the headline, you won’t have to get near the raft—please do.”

“Set her back, John, set her back!” one man shouted. They turned back toward the water. “Stay away, boy—keep your distance. Damn it, I just think the wind has blown it toward us. Your dad has smallpox, and you know it well. Why didn’t you say something? Do you want to spread it everywhere?”

“Well,” I said, crying, “I told everyone before, and they just walked away and left us.”

Poor fellow, there’s something in that. We’re really sorry for you, but we—well, damn it, we don’t want smallpox, you see. Listen, I’ll tell you what to do. Don’t try to land by yourself, or you’ll break everything. Drift about twenty miles and you’ll reach a town on the left side of the river. It will be well after sunrise then, and when you ask for help, tell them your family has chills and fever. Don’t be foolish and let people wonder what’s wrong. We’re trying to do you a favor, so keep at least twenty miles between us—that’s good. It wouldn’t help to land where the light is; that place is only a woodshed. I’m guessing your father is poor, and I have to say he’s in hard luck. Here, I’ll put a twenty‑dollar gold piece on this board, and you can take it when it floats by. I feel terrible leaving you, but my kingdom! It won’t do to mess with smallpox, understand?

“Hold on, Parker,” says the other man, “here’s a twenty to put on the board for me. Goodbye, boy; do as Mr. Parker told you and you’ll be all right.”

“That’s right, my boy—good‑bye, good‑bye. If you see any runaway slaves, help catch them and you can earn some money.”

“Good‑bye, sir,” I say, “I won’t let any runaway slaves get past me if I can help it.”

They left and I got on the raft, feeling bad and low because I knew I had done something wrong. I realized it wasn’t worth trying to learn to do the right thing. A person who doesn’t start out right when he’s a child has no chance—when trouble comes there’s nothing to support him, so he gets beaten.  

I thought for a minute and said to myself, “Hold on. Suppose you had done the right thing and given Jim up. Would you feel better than you do now?” No, I thought. I would feel bad—I would feel exactly the same as now.  

Then I asked, “What’s the use of learning to do right when it’s hard and doing wrong is easy, and the reward is the same?” I was stuck. I couldn’t answer that. So I decided I wouldn’t worry about it any more. After that I just did whatever seemed easiest at the moment.  

I went into the wigwam; Jim wasn’t there. I looked around but couldn’t find him. I called,  

“Jim!”  

He answered, “Here I am, Huck. Are they still out of sight? Don’t talk loudly.”  

He was in the river under the stern oar, with only his nose sticking out. I told him they were out of sight, so he came aboard. He said:

I was listening to all the talk, and I slipped into the river. I was ready to push my way in if they came aboard. Then I planned to swim back to the raft after they left. But wow, how you fooled them, Huck! That was the cleverest trick! I tell you, child, I promised to save old Jim—old Jim won’t forget you for that, dear.

We then talked about the money. It was a good raise—twenty dollars each. Jim said we could travel on a steamboat now, and the money would be enough to go wherever we wanted in the free states. He said another twenty miles wasn’t far for the raft, but he wished we were already there.

At daybreak we tied up, and Jim was very careful about hiding the raft well. He spent the whole day repairing things and getting ready to stop rafting.

That night, around ten o’clock, we saw the lights of a town down the river in a left‑hand bend.

I took the canoe to ask about it. Soon I saw a man in the river with a small boat, setting a trot‑line. I rowed up and said, “Mister, is that town Cairo?”

He answered, “Cairo? No. You must be a fool.”

I asked, “What town is it, mister?”

He said, “If you want to know, go find out yourself. If you stay here bothering me for another half minute, you’ll get something you don’t want.”

I paddled back to the raft. Jim was very disappointed, but I said it didn’t matter; Cairo would be the next place, I thought.

We passed another town before daylight, and I was ready to set out again, but the land was high, so I didn’t go. “There is no high ground near Cairo,” Jim said. I had forgotten that. We stopped for the day on a tow‑head that was close to the left bank. I began to suspect something, and Jim did too. I said, “Maybe we passed Cairo in the fog that night.” He answered, “Let’s not talk about it, Huck. Poor people never have any luck. I always thought that rattlesnake skin wasn’t finished with its work.”  

“I wish I’d never seen that snake skin, Jim—I really wish I’d never looked at it.”  

“It’s not your fault, Huck; you didn’t know. Don’t blame yourself.”  

When daylight came, we could see the clear Ohio water near the shore, and beyond it the usual Muddy River. So everything was still linked to Cairo.  

We talked about it all night. It wouldn’t be safe to go to the shore, and we couldn’t carry the raft up the stream. The only option was to wait until dark, then go back in the canoe and take the risk. So we slept all day among the cottonwood thicket, so we would be refreshed for the work. When we returned to the raft at night, the canoe was gone!  

We didn’t say a word for a long time. There was nothing to say. Both of us knew it was another trick of the rattlesnake skin, so talking about it would only seem like we were looking for blame. That would only bring more bad luck, and keep bringing it, until we learned to stay quiet.

Soon we talked about what we should do, and we realized there was no choice but to keep going downstream on the raft until we could get a canoe to go back upstream. We weren’t going to borrow one when no one was around, like Dad would do, because that could get us into trouble.

So we pushed off after dark on the raft.

Anyone who still thinks it’s foolish to handle a snake‑skin, after everything that snake‑skin has done for us, will believe us now if they keep reading and see what else it has done for us.

The place to buy canoes is near rafts that are tied up on the shore. But we didn’t see any rafts tied up, so we kept going for three more hours. The night became dark and very thick, almost like fog. You couldn’t see the shape of the river or any distance. It was very late and still, when a steamboat appeared moving up the river. We lit the lantern, hoping she would see us. Boats that travel upstream usually stay away from us; they go out to follow the sandbars and look for easy water under the reefs. But on nights like this, they come straight up the channel, against the whole river.

We could hear her pounding, but we didn’t really see her until she was close. She headed straight for us. Often they do that, trying to get as close as possible without touching. Sometimes the wheel snaps off a sweep, and the pilot sticks his head out, laughs, and thinks he’s very clever. This time she came toward us. We thought she would try to shave us, but she didn’t look like she was pulling back at all. She was a huge ship and she was moving fast. She looked like a black cloud with rows of glowing lights around it. Suddenly she swelled up, huge and frightening, with a long line of wide‑open furnace doors that shone like red‑hot teeth. Her massive bows and guards hung low over us. There was shouting, the clatter of bells to stop the engines, curses, and the whistle of steam. As Jim fell overboard on one side and I on the other, she crashed straight through the raft.

I dived, trying to reach the bottom because a thirty‑foot wheel had to pass over me, and I wanted it to have plenty of space. I could usually stay underwater for a minute. This time I think I stayed down for about one and a half minutes. Then I quickly swam to the surface because I was almost out of breath. I pushed the water out of my nose and took a short breath. A strong current was rushing past, and the boat started its engine again just ten seconds after it had stopped. The crew never cared much for people on rafts, so the boat moved up the river, out of sight in the heavy weather, but I could still hear it.

I called out for Jim about a dozen times, but I got no reply. I grabbed a plank that brushed me while I was treading water and started heading for shore, pushing the plank ahead of me. I realized the current was pulling me toward the left bank, which meant I was in a crossing, so I turned and went that way.

It was a long, slanted crossing of about two miles, so it took a while to get across. I finally reached the shore safely and climbed up the bank. I could only see a short distance ahead, so I kept walking over rough ground for a quarter of a mile or more. Then I ran into a big old‑style double log house without noticing it at first. I tried to rush past, but a bunch of dogs jumped out, howling and barking at me, and I knew better than to keep moving forward.
