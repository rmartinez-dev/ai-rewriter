We didn't stop at any town for many days; we just kept going down the river. We were far down south now, in warm weather, and a very long way from home. We started to see trees covered with Spanish moss, hanging from the branches like long grey beards. It was the first time I had ever seen it growing, and it made the forest look solemn and gloomy. The swindlers thought they were safe now, so they began to work in the villages again.

First they gave a lecture on temperance, but they didn't raise enough money for themselves to get drunk. Then, in another village, they opened a dancing school, but they knew how to dance no better than a kangaroo. On their first performance the audience laughed and chased them out of town. Another time they tried public speaking, but they stopped before the audience got up and shouted at them, forcing them to leave. They tried missionary work, hypnosis, medicine, fortune‑telling, and a little of everything, but they never had any luck. Finally they were almost completely broke. They lay on the raft as it drifted, thinking and thinking, saying nothing for half a day at a time, feeling terribly sad and desperate.

Finally they decided to meet and sit together in the wigwam, talking quietly for two or three hours at a time. Jim and I felt uneasy. We didn’t like the look of it. We thought they were planning some kind of serious trouble. We kept turning it over in our minds, and finally we decided they were going to break into someone’s house or store, or get involved in counterfeit money, or something like that. So we were pretty scared and we made a pact that we would have nothing to do with those actions. If we ever got even a hint of it, we would give them a cold shoulder, leave, and go our own way.

Early one morning we hid the raft in a safe place about two miles downstream from a shabby village called Pikesville. The king went ashore and told us all to stay hidden while he walked into town to see if anyone had heard about the Royal Nonesuch yet. (“A house to rob, you mean,” I thought to myself, “and after you finish robbing it you’ll come back here and wonder what happened to me, Jim, and the raft—and you’ll be left wondering.”) He said that if he wasn’t back by noon, the duke and I would know everything was fine, and we should come along.

We stayed where we were. The duke was nervous and sweating, and he was in a very bad mood. He scolded us for everything, and we couldn’t seem to do anything right. He found fault with every little thing. Something was definitely brewing. I was glad when noon came and there was no king; we could have a change, anyway—and maybe a chance for a real change on top of that.

So the duke and I went up to the village and looked for the king. Eventually we found him in the back room of a small, cramped tavern. A lot of thugs were bullying him for sport. He was shouting and threatening with all his might, so cramped he couldn’t walk or do anything to them. The duke began to insult him as an old fool, and the king started to sass back. The moment they really started fighting, I ran out, spurred my horse, and galloped down the river road like a deer because I saw our chance. I decided it would be a long day before they ever saw me and Jim again.

I got there out of breath but full of joy, and shouted:

“Set her loose, Jim! We’re all right now!”

But there was no answer, and no one came out of the wigwam. Jim was gone! I shouted, then shouted again, and shouted once more, running back and forth through the woods, yelling and screaming. It was no use—old Jim was gone. Then I sat down and cried; I couldn’t stop it. I couldn’t stay still for long. Soon I walked onto the road, trying to decide what to do, and I met a boy walking. I asked him if he had seen a strange black man dressed in a certain way, and he said:

“Yes.”

“Where?” I asked.

“Down at Silas Phelps’ place, two miles below here. He’s a runaway black man, and they have him. Were you looking for him?”

“Of course not! I ran into him in the woods an hour or two ago. He said if I shouted he would cut my liver out, and he told me to lie down and stay where I was. I did it and have stayed there since, too scared to come out.”

“Well,” he said, “you don’t have to be scared any more because they have him. He ran away from down South, some time ago.”

“It’s good that they caught him.”

“Yes, I think so! There’s a $200 reward for him. It’s like finding money on the road.”

“Yes, it is—and I could have taken it if I had been strong enough; I saw him first. Who caught him?”

“It was an old man, a stranger. He sold his chance at catching him for forty dollars because he had to go up the river and couldn’t wait. Think about that now! I would wait if it were seven years.”

"That's me, every time," I say. "But maybe his chance isn't worth any more than that if he'll sell it so cheaply. Maybe something isn't right about it."

"It is right, though—straight as a line. I see the flyer myself. It tells everything about him, down to the smallest detail. It paints him like a picture and says he's from the plantation below Newrleans. No, sir, they aren't worried about that speculation, you bet. So, give me a chew, will you?"

I didn't have any, so he left. I went to the raft and sat in the wigwam to think. I couldn't come up with anything. I thought until my head hurt, but I couldn't see a way out of the trouble. After all this long journey and everything we had done for those scoundrels, it all turned out to be nothing. Everything was broken and ruined because they were willing to play such a trick on Jim and make him a slave again for the rest of his life, among strangers, all for forty dirty dollars.

I once thought it would be a thousand times better if Jim stayed as a slave at his family's home. As long as he remained a slave, I thought I should write a letter to Tom Sawyer and ask him to tell Miss Watson where Jim was.  

But I quickly gave up that idea for two reasons. First, she would be angry and disgusted by his misbehavior and his ungratefulness for leaving her, and she would sell him down the river again. Second, even if she didn’t, everyone naturally despises an ungrateful black man, and they would treat Jim badly all the time. That would make him feel angry and ashamed.  

And then think of me! People would hear that Huck Finn helped a black man gain his freedom. If I ever saw anyone from that town again, I would have to humiliate myself.  

That’s how it works: a person does something low and then doesn’t want to face the consequences. He thinks that as long as he can hide it, it isn’t disgraceful. That was exactly my problem.  

The more I thought about it, the more my conscience hurt me, and the more wicked and angry I felt. Finally, it suddenly struck me that Providence was slapping me in the face, letting me know my wickedness was being watched from heaven while I was stealing a poor old woman’s slave who had never harmed me. I realized there is someone always watching and will not let such miserable deeds go unpunished. I stopped dead in my tracks because I was so scared.  

I tried to make myself feel better by saying I was brought up in a wicked environment, so I wasn’t entirely to blame. But something inside me kept saying, “You went to Sunday school; if you had listened, you would have learned that people who act like I did toward that black man go to everlasting fire.”

It made me shiver. I decided to pray and see if I could stop being the kind of boy I was and become better. So I knelt down, but the words wouldn’t come. Why wouldn’t they? It wasn’t useful to hide it from Him, or from myself, either.

I knew exactly why the words wouldn’t come. My heart wasn’t right. I wasn’t honest. I was being two‑faced. I pretended to give up sin, but inside I was holding on to the biggest sin of all. I tried to make my mouth say I would do the right and clean thing and tell that black boy’s owner where he was. Deep down I knew it was a lie, and He knew it. You can’t pray a lie—I found that out.

So I was full of trouble, as full as I could be, and I didn’t know what to do. Finally I had an idea. I said, “I’ll go and write the letter—and then see if I can pray.” It was astonishing how light I felt, like a feather, and all my troubles disappeared.

I took a piece of paper and a pencil, feeling glad and excited. I sat down and wrote:

“Miss Watson, your runaway black boy Jim is down here two miles below Pikesville. Mr. Phelps has caught him and will turn him in for the reward if you send it.

—Huck Finn.”

I felt good and completely free of sin for the first time in my life. I knew I could pray now. But I didn’t pray right away. I put the paper down and started thinking—thinking how good it was that all this had happened, and how close I had come to being lost and going to hell.  

I kept thinking about our trip down the river. I see Jim in my mind all the time, both day and night, sometimes in moonlight, sometimes in storms. We floated together, talking, singing, laughing. I couldn’t find any reason to be hard against him; I felt the opposite. I picture him standing watch on his boat instead of calling me, so I could keep sleeping. I remember how happy he was when I came out of the fog, and when I met him again in the swamp at the place where the feud started. He always called me “honey,” patted me, and tried to do everything he could for me. He was always so kind.  

Finally, I remembered the time I saved him by telling the crew that we had smallpox on board. He was very grateful and said I was the best friend he ever had, the only one he had now. Then I looked around and saw the paper.

It was a tight place. I took it up and held it in my hand. I was trembling because I had to decide forever between two things, and I knew it. I thought for a minute, holding my breath, and then I said to myself:

“All right, then, I’ll go to hell,” and I tore it up.

The thoughts and words were terrible, but they were said. I let them stay said and never thought about changing. I pushed the whole thing out of my mind and decided I would return to wickedness, which felt natural to me because I had been raised that way, unlike the other. To start, I would go to work and steal Jim out of slavery again. If I could think of anything worse, I would do that too, because as long as I was in, and for good, I might as well go all the way.

Then I started thinking about how to get it and considered many possible ways. Finally I came up with a plan that suited me. I took the bearings of a wooded island downstream, and as soon as it got dark I slipped out with my raft, went to the island and hid it there, then turned back. I slept through the night, got up before sunrise, ate breakfast, put on my work clothes, tied up some tools and other things in a bundle, took the canoe and headed for shore. I landed a little downstream from what I thought was Phelps’s place, hid the bundle in the woods, then filled the canoe with water, loaded it with rocks and sank it where I could find it later, about a quarter of a mile downstream from a small steam sawmill on the bank.

Then I set out on the road. When I passed the mill I saw a sign that said “Phelps’s Sawmill.” A few hundred yards later I reached the farm houses. I looked around carefully, but I didn’t see anyone, even though it was bright daylight. I didn’t mind because I didn’t want to meet anyone yet. I just wanted to get a feel for the area. My plan was to arrive there from the village, not from the lower road. So I took a quick look and kept moving straight toward town.  

The first person I met when I reached town was the duke. He was posting a notice for the Royal Nonesuch, a three‑night performance, just like the last time. Those swindlers had a lot of nerve! I stepped up to him before I could avoid it. He seemed surprised and said, “Hello! Where did you come from?” Then, with a friendly tone, he added, “Where is the raft? Have you got it in a good place?”  

I replied, “That’s exactly what I wanted to ask you, Your Grace.”  

His expression changed, and he asked, “What made you want to ask me?”

“​Well,” I say, “when I saw the king in that mess yesterday I thought to myself that we couldn’t bring him home for a few hours, until he was sober. So I walked around town to kill time and wait. A man came up and offered me ten cents to help pull a small boat across the river and then go back to fetch a sheep, so I went with him. While we were dragging the boat, the man left me holding the rope and went behind it to push it. The boat was too strong for me; it slipped loose and ran away, and we chased after it. We didn’t have a dog, so we had to run after it all over the countryside until we wore it out. We didn’t catch it until dark; then we brought it back, and I went down to the raft. When I got there and saw it was gone, I thought, ‘They got into trouble and had to leave; they took my only slave, the only one I have, and now I’m in a strange place with no property, nothing, and no way to earn a living.’ So I sat down and cried. I slept in the woods all night. But what happened to the raft then? And poor Jim!”

I don’t know what happened to the raft. That stupid man made a deal and got forty dollars. When we found him in the gambling house, the gamblers gave him half‑dollars and took every cent except what he spent on whisky. I brought him home late last night and saw the raft was missing. We said, “That little thief stole our raft, cheated us, and ran down the river.”

I wouldn’t betray my nigger, would I? He was the only black man I owned, the only property I had.

We never thought about that. The truth is, I think we started to think of him as our nigger; we really did. God knows we already had enough problems for him. So when we saw the raft was gone and we were completely broke, there was nothing to do except try the Royal Nonesuch again. I have been wandering since, as dry as a powder horn. Where is that ten cents? Give it to me.

I had some money, so I gave him ten cents. I begged him to use it for food and give me a share, because it was all I had and I hadn’t eaten since yesterday. He didn’t answer. The next moment he turned on me and said:

“Do you think that nigger would betray us? We’d skin him if he did!”

“How could he betray us? Hasn’t he run away?”

“No! That stupid man sold him and never shared with me, and the money is gone.”

“Sold him?” I said, starting to cry. “He was my nigger, and that was my money. Where is he? I want my nigger.”

“Well, you can’t get your slave, that’s all—so stop crying. Listen, do you think you would dare to help us? I doubt I would trust you. Why, if you were to help us—”

He stopped, but I had never seen the duke look so ugly before. I started whimpering and said:

“I don’t want to help anyone, and I have no time to help, anyway. I have to go and find my slave.”

He looked more troubled, standing there with his bills fluttering on his arm, thinking, his forehead creased. Finally he said:

“I’ll tell you something. We have to stay here three days. If you promise you won’t help, and you won’t let the slave help, I’ll tell you where to find him.”

So I promised, and he said:

“A farmer named Silas Ph—” then he stopped. You see, he began to tell me the truth; but when he stopped like that and started thinking again, I thought he was changing his mind. And he was. He didn’t trust me; he wanted to keep me away for the whole three days. Soon he said:

“The man who bought him is named Abram Foster—Abram G. Foster—and he lives forty miles away, on the road to Lafayette.”

“All right,” I said, “I can walk there in three days. I’ll start this afternoon.”

“No, you won’t. You’ll start now, and don’t waste any time. Don’t talk nonsense either. Keep quiet in your head and keep moving, and you won’t get into trouble with us, understand?”

That was the order I wanted, and that’s what I followed. I wanted to be free to carry out my plans.

“Get out of here,” he says. “You can tell Mr. Foster whatever you want. Maybe you can convince him that Jim is your servant—some people don’t need any papers. I’ve heard that happens down South. And if you tell him the flyer and the reward are false, maybe he’ll believe you when you explain why you wanted to get them out. Go now and say whatever you need, but don’t waste time talking between here and there.”

So I left and headed for the back country. I didn’t look around, but I felt like someone was watching me. I knew I could tire him out. I walked straight into the countryside for about a mile before stopping, then turned back through the woods toward Phelps’. I decided I should start my plan right away without delaying, because I wanted to keep Jim quiet until the others could escape. I didn’t want any trouble with them. I had seen enough of them and wanted to stay far away.
