The old man went uptown again before breakfast, but he couldn't find Tom. Both of them sat at the table thinking in silence. They looked sad, their coffee grew cold, and they ate nothing.  

After a while, the old man asked, “Did I give you the letter?”  

“What letter?” Tom replied.  

“The one I got yesterday from the post office.”  

“No, you didn’t give me any letter.”  

“Then I must have forgotten it.”  

He searched his pockets, then went to the place where he had put it down, got it, and handed it to her. She said, “Why, it’s from St. Petersburg—it's from Sis.”  

I thought a walk would help, but I couldn't move. Before she could open the letter, she dropped it and ran because she saw something. I saw it too. It was Tom Sawyer on a mattress, an old doctor, and Jim in a calico dress with his hands tied behind his back, plus a lot of other people. I hid the letter behind the first thing I could find and rushed away.  

She threw herself at Tom, crying, “Oh, he's dead, he's dead, I know he's dead!”  

Tom turned his head a little and muttered something, showing he wasn't thinking clearly. Then she raised her hands and said, “He's alive, thank God! And that's enough!” She kissed him, ran to the house to make the bed, and gave orders to the servants and everyone else as fast as she could, shouting all the way.

I followed the men to see what they would do with Jim, and the old doctor and Uncle Silas followed Tom into the house. The men were very angry. Some of them wanted to hang Jim as an example to the other Black people nearby, so they wouldn’t try to run away like Jim did, causing a lot of trouble and frightening an entire family for days and nights. But the others said, “Don’t do it; it won’t help. He isn’t our slave, and his owner will come back and make us pay for him.” That calmed them down a little, because the people who are most eager to hang a Black man who hasn’t behaved “properly” are usually the ones who are least willing to pay for his loss.

They cursed Jim constantly and sometimes hit him on the head. Jim never said anything and he never told me anything. They took him back to the same cabin, put his own clothes on him, and chained him again. This time they didn’t use a bed‑leg; they fastened him to a large iron staple driven into the bottom log. They also chained his hands and both of his legs. They told him he would only get bread and water to eat until his owner came, or he would be sold at auction because he hadn’t returned in a certain time. They filled our hole and said that a couple of farmers with guns had to stand watch around the cabin each night, and a bulldog was tied to the door during the day. By then they had finished the work and were saying a sort of general goodbye with curses. Then the old doctor came, looked, and said:

Don’t be any rougher on him than you have to, because he isn’t a bad black man.  

When I got to where the boy was, I saw I couldn’t pull the bullet out without help. He wasn’t in any condition for me to leave and get help. He got a little worse and worse. After a long time he went out of his mind and wouldn’t let me come near him any more. He said that if I touched his raft he would kill me, and he kept saying all kinds of wild foolish things.  

I realized I couldn’t do anything with him, so I said I needed help somehow. The minute I said that, a black man appeared and offered to help. He did help, and he did it very well.  

Of course I guessed he must be a runaway slave, and there I was, stuck there all day and all night. It was a mess, I tell you! I had a couple of patients with chills, and I would have liked to run to town to see them, but I couldn’t because the black man might run away and I would be blamed. Yet no boat came close enough for me to hail.  

So I had to stay there until daylight this morning. I have never seen a black man who was more faithful or reliable. He was risking his freedom to help, and he was exhausted too. I could see he had been working very hard lately.  

I liked him for that. I tell you, gentlemen, a man like that is worth a thousand dollars and deserves kind treatment.  

I had everything I needed, and the boy was doing as well there as he would at home—maybe better, because it was so quiet. But there I was, with both of them in my care, and I had to stay until about dawn. Then some men in a boat came by, and as luck would have it the black man was sitting on the pallet with his head on his knees, sound asleep. I signaled them quietly, and they slipped up on him, grabbed him and tied him before he knew what was happening. We never had any trouble.  

The boy was also in a light sleep, so we muffled the oars, tied the raft and towed it over very nicely and quietly. The black man never rowed or said a word from the start. He isn’t a bad black man, gentlemen; that’s what I think about him.

Someone says, “Well, that sounds very good, doctor, I’m obliged to say.”

The others softened a little, too, and I was very grateful to that old doctor for helping Jim. I was glad it matched my opinion of him, because I thought he had a good heart and was a good man the first time I saw him. Then they all agreed that Jim had acted very well and deserved some recognition and a reward. So each of them promised, honestly and openly, that they would not blame him any more.

After that, they came out and locked him up. I hoped they would let him take off one or two of the heavy chains, or at least give him meat and vegetables with his bread and water, but they didn’t think of it. I decided it wasn’t right for me to get involved, but I figured I would tell Aunt Sally the doctor’s story as soon as I got past the obstacles ahead of me—explaining how I forgot to mention Sid being shot when I was telling her about how he and I spent that damned night paddling around hunting the runaway slave.

I had plenty of time. Aunt Sally stayed in the sickroom all day and night, and every time I saw Uncle Silas wandering around, I avoided him.

The next morning I heard that Tom was feeling a lot better, and they said Aunt Sally had gone to take a nap. I slipped into the sickroom, thinking that if I found him awake we could tell the family a story that would work. But he was sleeping, and very peacefully, too; he was pale, not red‑faced like he had been when he arrived. I sat down and waited for him to wake.

About half an hour later Aunt Sally glided in, and there I was, up a stump again! She motioned for me to stay still, sat down beside me, and began to whisper. She said we could all be joyful now because all the signs were good, and he had been sleeping like that for a long time, looking better and more peaceful all the while. She added that there was a ten‑to‑one chance he would wake up with his mind clear.

We sat there watching, and after a while he stirred a little, opened his eyes naturally, looked around, and said:

“Hello!—why, I’m home! How’s that? Where’s the raft?”

“It’s all right,” I said.

“And Jim?”

“The same,” I replied, though I didn’t sound very confident. He didn’t notice and said:

“Good! Splendid! Now we’re all right and safe! Did you tell Aunt?”

I was about to say yes, but she interrupted and asked, “About what, Sid?”

“Why, about the way the whole thing was done.”

“What whole thing?”

“Why, the whole thing. There’s only one: how we freed the runaway black man—me and Tom.”

“Good grief! Free the—What is the child talking about! Dear, dear, out of his head again!”

“No, I’m not crazy; I know what I’m saying. Tom and I set him free. We planned it and we did it. We even did it elegantly.” He flinched, and she never stopped him. She just stood and stared, letting him continue, and I saw there was no point for me to intervene. “Aunt, it took a lot of work—weeks of it, hours every night while you slept. We had to steal candles, sheets, shirts, your dress, spoons, tin plates, pocket knives, the warming‑pan, the grindstone, flour, and many other things. You can’t imagine how hard it was to make saws, pens, inscriptions, and other things, and you can’t imagine half the fun it was. We also had to draw pictures of coffins, write anonymous letters from the robbers, climb the lightning‑rod, dig a hole in the cabin, make a rope ladder, hide it inside a pie, and put spoons and other tools in your apron pocket—”

“Good heavens!”

—We filled the cabin with rats, snakes and other creatures to keep Jim company. Then you kept Tom here so long with butter in his hat that you almost ruined everything. The men left the cabin before we got out, so we had to hurry. They heard us and started shooting at us. I got my share, we dodged out of the way and let them pass. When the dogs came they weren’t interested in us; they went after the noise. We got our canoe, headed for the raft, and we were all safe. Jim was a free man, and we did it all ourselves. Wasn’t that great, Aunty?

“Well, I’ve never heard anything like that in all my life! So it was you, you little trouble‑makers, who caused all this mess, turned everyone’s heads upside down and scared us almost to death. I have as good an idea now as I ever had to deal with you right this minute. Think about it: night after night I’ve… you just get well once, you young scamp, and I’ll make Old Harry pay for both of you!”

But Tom was so proud and excited he couldn’t keep quiet, and his tongue just ran off—she kept chiming in, shouting all the time, and both of them started arguing at once, like a catfight; and she said:

“Enjoy it while you can, because I’ll tell you, if I catch you messing with him again—”

“Messing with who?” Tom asked, dropping his smile and looking surprised.

“Who? Why, the runaway, of course. Who did you think?”

Tom looks at me seriously and says:

“Tom, didn’t you just tell me he was okay? Hasn’t he escaped?”

“Him?” says Aunt Sally. “The runaway? No, he hasn’t. They’ve got him back, safe and sound. He’s back in that cabin with only bread and water, and he’s chained until someone claims or sells him.”

Tom sits up straight in bed, his eyes blazing and his nostrils flaring, and shouts to me:

“They have no right to lock him up! Push them—don’t waste a minute. Set him free! He isn’t a slave; he is as free as any creature on earth!”

“What does the child mean?”

“I mean every word I’m saying, Aunt Sally, and if anyone doesn’t go, I will. I’ve known him all his life, and Tom has too. Miss Watson died two months ago. She was ashamed that she was going to sell him down the river, and she said so in her will. She freed him.”

“Then why did you want to set him free if he was already free?”

“That’s a good question, and just like women! I wanted the adventure of it. I’d waded waist‑deep in blood to—good heavens, Aunt Polly!”

If she weren’t standing right there, just inside the door, looking as sweet and content as an angel with a slice of pie, I wish I never had to see that.

Aunt Sally jumped up. Most of the people hugged her tightly and cried because of her. I found a decent place to hide under the bed because it was getting pretty hot for us, I thought.  

I peeked out. After a little while, Tom’s Aunt Polly shook herself loose and stood there, looking at Tom over her glasses, as if she wanted to press him flat to the ground.  

Then she said,  

“Yes, you’d better turn your head away—I would if I were you, Tom.”  

“Oh, dear me!” said Aunt Sally. “Has he really changed? That isn’t Tom, that’s Sid. Where is Tom? He was just here a minute ago.”  

“You mean where’s Huck Finn? That’s what you mean! I think I haven’t raised a rascal like my Tom all these years without recognizing him when I see him. That would be a real surprise. Come out from under that bed, Huck Finn.”  

So I got out. I wasn’t feeling brave, though.

Aunt Sally was one of the most mixed‑up people I ever saw—except for Uncle Silas when he came in and they told him everything. It made him feel drunk, you might say, and he didn’t understand anything for the rest of the day. That night he gave a prayer‑meeting sermon that ruined his reputation, because even the oldest man in the world couldn’t understand it.

So Tom’s Aunt Polly told everyone who I was and what had happened. I had to explain how I was in such a tight spot that, when Mrs. Phelps took me for Tom Sawyer, she added, “Oh, go on and call me Aunt Sally, I’m used to it now, and there’s no need to change.” When Aunt Sally thought I was Tom Sawyer I had to accept it; there was no other way. I knew he wouldn’t mind, because it would be crazy for him, being a mystery, and he would turn it into an adventure and be perfectly satisfied. So it turned out that he pretended to be Sid and made things as easy as possible for me.

Aunt Polly said Tom was right about old Miss Watson freeing Jim in her will; and, sure enough, Tom Sawyer went through all that trouble to set a slave free! I had never understood before, until that moment and that conversation, how he could help a person set a slave free by the way he was brought up.

Well, Aunt Polly said that when Aunt Sally wrote to her that Tom and Sid had arrived safely, she thought to herself:

“Look at that! I might have expected it, letting him go that way with no one watching him. So now I have to go all the way down the river—eleven hundred miles—to find out what that creature is doing this time, since I couldn’t get any answer from you about it.”

“I haven’t heard anything from you,” says Aunt Sally.

“Well, I wonder! I wrote to you twice asking what you meant by Sid being here.”

“I never got them, Sis.”

Aunt Polly turns around slowly and sternly and says, “You, Tom!”

“Well—what?” he says, sounding a little irritated.

“Don’t you talk to me, you impudent thing—hand over those letters.”

“What letters?”

“Those letters. I’m sure, if I have to grab you, I will—”

“They’re in the trunk. Here they are. They’re exactly the same as when I took them from the office. I haven’t read them, I haven’t touched them. But I knew they’d cause trouble, and I thought if you weren’t in a hurry, I would—”

“Well, you do need a beating, there’s no doubt about it. I wrote another one to tell you I was coming; and I suppose he—”

“No, it arrived yesterday; I haven’t read it yet, but it’s fine, I have that one.”

I wanted to bet two dollars she didn’t have, but I thought it was safer not to. So I never said anything.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  CHAPTER THE LAST
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The first time I caught Tom in private, I asked him what his plan was. I wanted to know what he would do if the escape worked and he managed to set a man free who was already free. He told me that from the beginning, if we got Jim out safely, his idea was to run him down the river on a raft, have an adventure to the mouth of the river, then tell him he was free, bring him back home on a steamboat in style, pay him for the time he lost, send a message ahead, gather all the other enslaved people, and lead them into town with a torchlight procession and a brass band. He thought that would make him a hero, and us too. I thought it was about as good as it could be.

We got Jim out of his chains quickly. When Aunt Polly, Uncle Silas, and Aunt Sally learned how well he helped the doctor nurse Tom, they made a big fuss over him. They dressed him nicely, gave him everything he wanted to eat, a good time, and nothing to worry about. We brought him to the sickroom and had a deep conversation. Tom gave Jim forty dollars for being such a patient prisoner and doing everything so well. Jim was thrilled beyond words, broke down in tears, and said:

“Now, Huck, what did I tell you? What I said up on Jackson Island? I told you I had a hairy chest and what that meant. I told you I was rich once and that I would be rich again. It’s happened, and here she is! Now, don’t talk to me—signs are signs, I told you. I knew right then that I would be rich again, as I stand here this minute.”

Then Tom kept talking. He said, “Let’s all three sneak out one night, get some gear, and have a wild adventure with the Indians out in the Territory for a week or two.” I said, “Okay, that sounds good, but I don’t have any money for the gear, and I don’t think I could get any from home. My dad probably went back already and spent it all on Judge Thatcher’s drinks.”

“No, he hasn’t,” Tom replied. “It’s still there—six thousand dollars or more. And your dad hasn’t been back since. He couldn’t have been here when I left, anyway.”

Jim said in a serious tone, “He isn’t coming back anymore, Huck.”

I asked, “Why, Jim?”

He answered, “There’s no reason, Huck—he just isn’t coming back.”

I kept pressing him, and finally he said, “Don’t you remember the house that was floating down the river, and there was a man inside, tied up? I went in, untied him, and didn’t let you come in. Well, you can get your money whenever you want, because that was him.”

Tom is fine now. He wears a bullet on a chain as a watch fob, so he always knows the time. There is nothing more to write about, and I am very glad of that. If I had known how hard it was to make a book, I wouldn’t have tried, and I won’t do any more.

But I think I have to leave for the Territory before anyone else. Aunt Sally is going to adopt me and try to civilize me, and I can’t stand that. I have been there before.

THE END. YOURS TRULY, HUCK FINN.
