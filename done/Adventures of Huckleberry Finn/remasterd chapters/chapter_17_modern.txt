In about a minute, someone spoke from a window without leaning out and said,  
“Hold on, boys! Who’s there?”

I answered,  
“It’s me.”

“Who are you?”

“George Jackson, sir.”

“What do you want?”

“I don’t want anything, sir. I just want to get away, but the dogs won’t let me.”

“Why are you wandering here so late—hey?”

“I wasn’t wandering, sir. I fell overboard from the steamboat.”

“Oh, you did? Someone, light a candle. What’s your name again?”

“George Jackson, sir. I’m just a boy.”

“Listen, if you’re telling the truth you don’t have to be afraid. No one will hurt you. But don’t try to move; stay where you are. Get Bob and Tom, some of you, and bring the guns. George Jackson, is anyone with you?”

“No, sir, nobody.”

I heard people moving around inside the house and saw a light. The man shouted,  
“Take that light away, Betsy, you old fool—don’t you have any sense? Put it on the floor behind the front door. Bob, if you and Tom are ready, take your positions.”

“All ready.”

“Now, George Jackson, do you know the Shepherdsons?”

“No, sir; I’ve never heard of them.”

“That may be true, or it may not. Anyway, everyone ready. Step forward, George Jackson. And don’t hurry—move very slowly. If anyone is with you, keep them back; if they step forward they’ll be shot. Come now. Move slowly; open the door just enough to squeeze in, understand?”

I didn't hurry; I couldn't even if I wanted to. I took one slow step at a time, and there was no sound, only the beat of my own heart. The dogs were as still as the people, but they stayed a little behind me. When I reached the three‑log doorway, I heard them unlocking, unbarring, and unbolting it. I put my hand on the door and pushed it a little, then a little more, until someone said, “That's enough—put your head in.” I did it, but I thought they would take it off.

The candle lay on the floor, and they were all there, looking at me and I at them for about fifteen seconds. Three big men with guns pointed at me, which made me wince, I tell you. The oldest was gray‑haired and about sixty; the other two were around thirty or older. All of them were fine and handsome. Beside them stood a sweet old gray‑haired lady, with two young women behind her that I couldn't see clearly. The old gentleman said:

“There, I think it's all right. Come in.”

As soon as I arrived, the old man locked the door, barred it, and bolted it. He told the young men to come in with their guns. They all entered a large parlor that had a new rag carpet on the floor. They gathered in a corner that was out of sight from the front windows—there were no side windows. They held a candle and looked closely at me. Everyone said, “He isn’t a Shepherdson—no, there isn’t any Shepherdson about him.” Then the old man said he hoped I wouldn’t mind being searched for weapons because he meant no harm; it was only to be sure. He didn’t look inside my pockets; he only felt the outside of my clothes with his hands and said it was fine. He told me to make myself comfortable, feel at home, and tell them everything about myself.  

The old lady said, “Bless you, Saul, the poor thing is soaked through; don’t you think he must be hungry?”  

I replied, “You’re right, Rachel—I forgot.”  

The old lady called, “Betsy,” referring to the Black woman, “go quickly and get him something to eat, poor thing. One of you girls, go wake Buck and tell him—here he is. Buck, take this stranger, take off his wet clothes and give him some dry ones of yours.”

Buck seemed about my age—maybe thirteen or fourteen—but he was a little bigger than me. He was only wearing a shirt and his hair was very messy. He came in with his eyes wide open, digging one fist into his face while dragging a gun with the other hand.

He asked, “Aren’t there any Shepherdsons around?”

They answered no, it was a false alarm.

“Well,” he said, “if there had been some, I think I would have gotten one.”

Everyone laughed, and Bob said, “Come on, Buck, they might have taken us all if you hadn’t been so slow.”

“Nobody’s after me,” Buck replied, “and it’s not fair that I’m always held back; I never get any chance.”

“Don’t worry, Buck,” the old man said, “you’ll have plenty of chances in time. Don’t worry about it. Go on now and do as your mother told you.”

When we went upstairs to his room, he gave me a rough shirt and a pair of his pants, and I put them on. While I was changing, he asked me my name, but before I could answer he started talking about a blue jay and a young rabbit he had caught in the woods the day before yesterday. He then asked me where Moses was when the candle went out. I said I didn’t know; I had never heard about it before.

“Guess,” he said.

“How can I guess,” I replied, “when I’ve never heard of it?”

“You can guess, can’t you? It’s easy.”

“Which candle?” I asked.

“Any candle,” he said.

“I don’t know where he was,” I said. “Where was he?”

“Why, he was in the dark! That’s where he was!”

“Well, if you knew where he was, why did you ask me?”

“Why, blame it, it’s a riddle, don’t you see? How long are you going to stay here? You have to stay forever. We can have great times—they don’t have any school now. Do you have a dog? I have a dog, and he will go into the river and bring back the chips you throw in. Do you like to comb your hair on Sundays and do that kind of silly stuff? I don’t, but my mother makes me. Damn these old pants! I think I should put them on, but I would rather not, it’s so warm. Are you ready? All right. Come on, old horse.”

Cold cornbread, cold beef, butter, and buttermilk—that’s what they gave me down there, and I’ve never found anything better. Buck, his mother, and everyone else smoked clay‑pipe cigarettes, except the Black woman, who didn’t, and the two young women. They all smoked and talked while I ate and talked. The young women had quilts over their laps and their hair hanging down their backs. They asked me questions, and I told them how my dad, my brother, and the whole family lived on a small farm at the bottom of Arkansas. My sister Mary Ann ran away, got married, and we never heard from her again. Bill went out to hunt and was never heard from again, either. Tom and Mort died. Then only my dad and I were left. He grew thin because of his troubles, and when he died I took what little was left, because the farm wasn’t ours. I went down the river, took a deck passage, and fell overboard—that’s how I ended up here. They told me I could have a home there as long as I wanted.

It was daylight, and everyone went to bed. I went to bed with Buck. When I woke up in the morning, I realized I had forgotten my own name. I lay there for about an hour trying to remember. When Buck woke up, I asked, “Can you spell, Buck?”

“Yes,” he said.

“I bet you can’t spell my name,” I said.

“I bet you that I can,” he replied.

“All right,” I said, “go ahead.”

“G‑E‑O‑R‑G‑E  J‑A‑X‑O‑N—there,” he said.

“Well,” I said, “you did it, but I didn’t think you could. It’s not an easy name to spell—especially without studying.”

I wrote it down privately because someone might ask me to spell it later. I wanted to have it ready and be able to recite it quickly, as if I already knew it.

It was a very nice family and a very nice house, too. I had never seen a house out in the countryside that was so nice and stylish. It didn’t have an iron latch on the front door, nor a wooden one with a leather strap. Instead it had a brass knob, just like houses in town.

There was no bed in the parlor, nor any sign of one; yet many parlors in towns have beds in them. There was a big fireplace with bricks at the bottom. The bricks were kept clean and red by pouring water on them and scrubbing with another brick. Sometimes they painted them with a red water‑paint called Spanish‑brown, just like in town.

They had large brass hooks that could hold a sawn log. A clock sat in the middle of the mantelpiece. The lower half of its glass face showed a painted picture of a town, and a round spot in the center let the sun shine through. You could see the pendulum swinging behind it.

It was lovely to hear the clock tick. Sometimes, when a traveling repairman cleaned and fixed it, the clock would start and strike up to one hundred and fifty times before it got tired. They wouldn’t charge any money for it.

Well, there were two huge, brightly painted parrots standing on either side of the clock. They looked as if they were made of chalk. Next to one parrot was a cat made of pottery, and next to the other was a pottery dog. When you pressed them, they squeaked, but their mouths didn’t open and they didn’t change expression or seem interested. The squeaking came from beneath them. Behind these objects were two large fan‑shaped things that looked like turkey wings. In the middle of the room, on a table, there was a pretty ceramic basket filled with apples, oranges, peaches, and grapes. The fruit looked much redder, yellower, and more beautiful than real fruit, but it wasn’t real. You could see where bits had chipped away, revealing the white chalk‑like material underneath.

The table had a cover made of a fine oilcloth. It was painted with a red and blue spread‑eagle and a border around the edge. They said the cover came all the way from Philadelphia. On each corner of the table were some books, stacked neatly. One was a large family Bible with many pictures. Another was *Pilgrim’s Progress*, a story about a man who left his family; it didn’t say why. I sometimes read it. The language was interesting but difficult. There was also *Friendship’s Offering*, full of beautiful items and poems, but I didn’t read the poems. Another book was *Henry Clay’s Speeches*. There was also Dr. Gunn’s *Family Medicine*, which explained what to do when someone was sick or dead. A hymn book and many other books were there, too. Beside the table were nice split‑bottom chairs that were solid and not sagging in the middle like an old basket.

They had pictures hung on the walls—mostly portraits of Washington, Lafayette, battle scenes, Highland Mary, and one called “Signing the Declaration.” Some of the pictures were what the family called crayons, made by one of the daughters who had died when she was only fifteen. These pictures were different from any I had ever seen before; they were much darker than usual.

One picture showed a woman in a tight black dress that was belted just under the armpits. The sleeves bulged in the middle like cabbage leaves. She wore a large black bonnet with a veil, white ankles wrapped in black tape, and tiny black shoes that looked like chisels. She leaned pensively on a tombstone with her right elbow, under a weeping willow. Her other hand hung at her side, holding a white handkerchief and a small purse. Below the picture was the caption “Shall I Never See Thee More Alas.”

Another picture showed a young lady with her hair pulled straight up to the top of her head and tied in a knot in front of a comb that looked like a chair back. She was crying into a handkerchief and held a dead bird on its back, its feet pointing upward, in her other hand. The caption read “I Shall Never Hear Thy Sweet Chirrup More Alas.”

There was also a picture of a young woman at a window, looking up at the moon with tears running down her cheeks. She held an open letter in one hand, the black sealing wax visible on the edge, and pressed a locket with a chain against her mouth. Below it the words said “And Art Thou Gone Yes Thou Art Gone Alas.”

These were all nice pictures, I thought, but I never felt attached to them. Whenever I felt a little sad, they always made me feel nervous. Everyone was sorry she had died because she had planned many more pictures, and people could see from her work what they had lost. I imagined that, with her gentle nature, she was now having a better time in the graveyard. She had been working on what they said was her greatest picture when she fell ill. Every day and night she prayed to live long enough to finish it, but she never got the chance.

The unfinished picture was of a young woman in a long white gown standing on the railing of a bridge, ready to jump. Her hair fell down her back, and she looked up at the moon with tears on her face. She had two arms crossed over her chest, two arms stretched out in front, and two more reaching up toward the moon. The idea was to decide which pair of arms looked best and then erase the others. But, as I said, she died before she could finish it. Now they keep this picture above the head of the bed in her room, and every year on her birthday they hang flowers on it. At other times it is hidden behind a small curtain. The young woman in the picture has a sweet, pleasant face, but the many arms make her look a little spider‑like, in my opinion.

This young girl kept a scrapbook while she was alive. She cut out obituaries, accident reports, and stories of people suffering from the *Presbyterian Observer* and pasted them into it. Then she wrote her own poems after them. The poems were very good. This is what she wrote about a boy named Stephen Dowling Bots who fell down a well and drowned:

**ODE TO STEPHEN DOWLING BOTS, DECEASED**

Did young Stephen get sick?  
Did young Stephen die?  
Did sad hearts grow heavy?  
Did the mourners cry?  

No, that was not Stephen’s fate.  
Young Stephen Dowling Bots—  
People felt sorrow around him,  
But it was not because of illness.  

He did not suffer from whooping cough,  
Nor from measles with spots;  
These did not tarnish the good name  
Of Stephen Dowling Bots.  

Unrequited love did not bring him grief,  
Even with his curly hair,  
And stomach problems did not bring him down.  

No. So I write with tearful eyes,  
While I tell his story.  
His soul left this cold world  
When he fell down a well.  

They pulled him out and emptied him,  
But it was too late;  
His spirit had already gone  
To the realms of the good and great.

If Emmeline Grangerford could write poetry like that before she was fourteen, nobody knows what she could have done later. Buck said she could recite poetry without any trouble. She never had to stop and think. He said she would write a line, and if she couldn’t find a rhyme she would simply cross it out and write another line, and keep going. She wasn’t picky; she could write about anything you gave her, as long as it was sad. Every time a man died, a woman died, or a child died, she would be ready with her “tribute” before the body got cold. She called them tributes. The neighbors said the doctor came first, then Emmeline, then the undertaker. The undertaker never arrived before Emmeline, except once, when she added a rhyme about the dead person’s name, Whistler. After that she was never the same. She never complained, but she grew weaker and died young. Poor thing. Many times I went up to the small room that used to be hers, took out her old scrapbook, and read it when her pictures annoyed me and I had started to feel a little bitter toward her. I liked that whole family, the living and the dead, and I didn’t want anything to come between us. Emmeline wrote poems about all the dead people while she was alive, and it didn’t seem right that no one wrote anything about her after she was gone. So I tried to write a verse or two myself, but I couldn’t make it work. They kept Emmeline’s room tidy and nice, with everything arranged just as she liked when she was alive, and nobody ever slept there. The old lady took care of the room herself, even though there were many black workers. She spent a lot of time sewing there and mostly read her Bible.

Well, as I was saying about the parlor, there were beautiful curtains on the windows. They were white and had pictures of castles with vines climbing the walls, and cattle coming down to drink. There was also a little old piano that had tin plates inside, I think. Nothing was ever as lovely as hearing the young ladies sing “The Last Link is Broken” and play “The Battle of Prague” on it. The walls of all the rooms were plastered, most floors had carpets, and the whole house was white‑washed on the outside.

It was a double house, and the large open space between the two parts was roofed and floored. Sometimes a table was set there in the middle of the day, and it was a cool, comfortable place. Nothing could be better. And wasn’t the cooking good, and there was plenty of it, too?
