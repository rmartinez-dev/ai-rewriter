We stopped talking and began to think. Soon Tom said:

“Look here, Huck, how foolish we were not to think of this earlier! I think I know where Jim is.”

“No! Where?”

“In that shed down by the ash hopper. When we were at dinner, didn’t you see a Black man go in there with some food?”

“Yes.”

“What did you think the food was for?”

“For a dog.”

“So did I. But it wasn’t for a dog.”

“Why?”

“Because part of it was watermelon.”

“So it was—I noticed it. It shows how a person can see something and still miss it at the same time.”

“The Black man unlocked the padlock when he went in, and he locked it again when he left. He gave Uncle a key about the time we got up from the table—probably the same key. Watermelon points to a man, the lock points to a prisoner; and there can’t be two prisoners on such a small plantation where everyone seems so kind. Jim must be the prisoner. All right—I’m glad we figured it out like detectives; I’d rather not have found out any other way. Now you think of a plan to free Jim, and I’ll think of one too; we’ll use the one we like best.”

What a mind a boy can have! If I had Tom Sawyer’s mind, I wouldn’t trade it for being a duke, a steamboat captain, a circus clown, or anything else I can imagine. I started thinking of a plan, just to have something to do. I already knew where the right plan would come from. Soon Tom said:

“Ready?”

“Yes,” I said.

“Okay—let’s hear it.”

“My plan is this,” I said. “We can easily find out if it’s Jim inside. Then I’ll get my canoe ready tomorrow night and bring my raft over from the island. The first dark night after the old man goes to bed, we’ll steal the key from his pants, push off down the river on the raft with Jim, hide during the day and travel at night, just like Jim and I used to do before. Would that plan work?”

“Work? Of course it would work, like rats fighting. But it’s way too simple; there’s nothing to it. What good is a plan that isn’t a challenge? It’s as gentle as goose‑milk. Huck, it wouldn’t even be as interesting as breaking into a soap factory.”

I didn’t say anything because I wasn’t expecting a different answer. But I knew very well that when he finally had his own plan, he wouldn’t have any of those objections.

It didn’t work. He told me what it was, and I realized right away that it was worth fifteen of my own for style. It would make Jim as free as I would be, and it might even get us all killed. So I was satisfied and said we would jump in on it. I don’t need to say what it was, because I knew it wouldn’t stay the same. I knew he would keep changing it in every direction as we went on, adding new bold ideas whenever he could. And that’s what he did.

One thing was certain: Tom Sawyer was serious and really planning to help free that slave. That was too much for me. Here was a boy who was respectable and well‑brought‑up, with a reputation to protect; his family also had good names. He was smart and not dull, educated and not ignorant, kind rather than cruel. Yet he was willing to lower himself to this business, bringing shame on himself and his family in front of everyone. I couldn’t understand it at all. It was outrageous, and I knew I should stand up and tell him so, be a true friend, and let him quit the plan and save himself. I began to tell him, but he stopped me and said:

“Don’t you think I know what I’m doing? Don’t I generally know what I’m about?”

“Yes.”

“Didn’t I say I was going to help free the slave?”

“Yes.”

“Well, then.”

That's all he said, and that's all I said. It wasn't useful to say anything more, because when he promised to do something, he always did it. I couldn't understand how he was willing to get involved in this matter, so I let it go and stopped thinking about it. If it had to happen that way, I couldn't change it.

When we got home, the house was dark and quiet. We walked down to the hut by the ash pit to examine it. We moved through the yard to see what the dogs would do. They recognized us and made no more noise than usual country dogs make at night. When we reached the cabin, we looked at the front and the two sides. On the side I hadn't seen before—the north side—we found a square window opening, fairly high up, with a single thick board nailed across it. I said:

"That's the problem. This hole is big enough for Jim to get through if we remove the board."

Tom replied:

"It's as simple as a game of tic‑tac‑toe, three in a row, and as easy as skipping school. I hope we can find a solution that's a little more complicated than that, Huck Finn."

"Well then," I said, "how about we cut it out, the way I did before I was killed that time?"

"That's more like it," he said. "It's mysterious, troublesome, and interesting, but I bet we can find a solution that's twice as long. There's no hurry; let's keep looking around."

Between the hut and the fence, at the back, there was a lean‑to attached to the hut’s eaves. It was made of planks. It was as long as the hut but only about six feet wide. The door was on the south side and it was padlocked. Tom went to the soap kettle, looked around, and brought back the iron tool they use to lift the lid. He used it to pry open one of the bolts. The chain fell down. We opened the door, went inside, closed it, and lit a match. The shed was only built against a cabin and had no connection to it. There was no floor inside, just a few old rusty hoes, spades, picks, and a broken plow. The match went out, and we went out too. We put the bolt back in, and the door was locked again. Tom was happy. He said, “Now we’re all right. We’ll dig him out. It will take about a week!”

Then we walked toward the house. I entered through the back door; you only have to pull a leather latch‑string because the doors are not fastened. That was not exciting enough for Tom Sawyer. He wanted to climb the lightning rod. He tried to climb it three times, but each time he slipped and fell. The last time he almost broke his head. He thought he would give up, but after resting he decided to try one more time for luck. This time he succeeded.

In the morning we got up at daybreak and went down to the Black men's cabins. We petted the dogs and tried to be friendly with the man who fed Jim—if it was Jim who was being fed. The Black men were just finishing breakfast and were about to go out to the fields. Jim’s Black man was piling a tin pan with bread, meat, and other food. While the others were leaving, the key came from the house.

This Black man had a good‑natured, smiling face, and his hair was tied in little bunches with thread. He said the thread was to keep witches away. He told us that witches had been bothering him a lot at night. They made him see strange things and hear strange words and noises. He didn’t think he had ever been bewitched before in his life. He got so upset and started talking about his troubles that he forgot what he had been going to do.

So Tom asked, “What’s the food for? Are we going to feed the dogs?”

The Black man smiled slowly, like when you throw a brick into a mud puddle, and answered, “Yes, Mars Sid, a dog. Curus’ dog, too. Do you want to go and look at him?”

“Yes,” I whispered to Tom.

“You’re going, right here at daybreak? That wasn’t the plan.”

“No, it wasn’t, but it’s the plan now.”

So, despite our reluctance, we went along. I didn’t like it much. When we got inside it was almost completely dark, and we could barely see anything. Jim was there, of course, and he could see us. He shouted, “Why, Huck! And good land! Isn’t that Mister Tom?”

I just knew how it would turn out; I expected it. I didn’t know what to do, and even if I had, I couldn’t have done anything because that black man burst in and said:

“Why, for heaven’s sake! Does he know you gentlemen?”

We could see quite clearly now. Tom looked at the black man, steady and a little puzzled, and said:

“Who knows us?”

“Why, this runaway black man.”

“I don’t think he does, but what made you think that?”

“What made you think so? Didn’t he just a moment ago shout as if he knew you?”

Tom said, sounding confused:

“Well, that’s very strange. Who shouted? When did he shout? What did he shout?” He turned to me, calm, and asked, “Did you hear anyone shout?”

There was nothing else to say, so I answered:

“No, I didn’t hear anyone say anything.”

Then he turned to Jim, looked at him as if he had never seen him before, and asked:

“Did you shout?”

“No, sir,” Jim replied, “I haven’t said anything, sir.”

“Not a word?”

“No, sir, I haven’t said a word.”

“Have you ever seen us before?”

“No, sir; not as far as I know.”

So Tom turned to the black man, who looked wild and upset, and said sternly:

“What do you think is wrong with you? What made you think someone shouted?”

"Oh, it's the dad‑blame witches, you know, and I wish I were dead. They're always at it, you know, and they almost kill me. They scare me so much. Please don't tell anyone about it, or old Mars Silas will scold me because he says they aren't witches. I just wish he were here now—then what would he say! I bet he couldn't find any way to get around it this time. 

But it's always like that. People who are sad stay sad. They won't look into anything and find out for themselves. When you find out and tell them, they don't believe you."

Tom gave him a dime and said we wouldn't tell anyone. He told him to buy more thread to tie his wool with, then looked at Jim and said:

"I wonder if Uncle Silas is going to hang this man. If I caught a man who was ungrateful enough to run away, I wouldn't turn him in; I would hang him."

While the man stepped to the door to look at the dime and bite it to see if it was good, he whispered to Jim:

"Don't ever let anyone know about us. And if you hear any digging at night, it's us; we're going to set you free."

Jim only had time to grab our hand and squeeze it. Then the man came back, and we said we would come again sometime if the man wanted us to. He said he would, especially if it was dark, because the witches went after him mostly in the dark, and it was good to have people around then.
