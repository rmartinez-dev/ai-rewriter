We thought that three more nights would take us to Cairo, at the southern tip of Illinois where the Ohio River joins. That was what we wanted. We planned to sell the raft, get on a steamboat, and travel far up the Ohio River into the free states. Then we would be out of trouble.

On the second night a fog started to roll in, so we tried to find a post to tie the raft to. It would be unsafe to keep moving in the fog. I paddled ahead in the canoe with the rope, but there were only small saplings to tie to. I wrapped the rope around one of them at the edge of the bank, but the current was strong. The raft surged forward, pulled the rope out of the roots, and floated away.

I saw the fog closing in. It made me feel sick and scared, and I could not move for what felt like half a minute. Then I realized the raft was no longer in sight; the fog was so thick I could not see more than twenty yards. I jumped into the canoe, went back to the stern, grabbed the paddle, and tried to row backward. The raft did not come back. In my hurry I had not untied it. I tried to untie it, but my hands were shaking so much that I could barely do anything.

As soon as I started, I went after the raft, fast and heavy, straight down the towhead. That was fine at first, but the towhead was not even sixty yards long. The moment I passed its end I entered a thick white fog. I had no idea which way I was going, just like a dead man.

I thought paddling would not help; I would only hit the bank or a towhead. I had to stay still and float, but it was very hard to keep my hands steady at that moment. I shouted and listened. Somewhere down there I heard a small shout, and my spirits lifted. I rushed after it, listening carefully for the sound again. The next time it sounded, I realized I was not moving toward it, but to the right of it. The time after that I was moving to the left of it, and I was not getting any closer. I was flying around in circles, while the sound kept going straight ahead.

I wished the foolish thing would make a constant noise, like a tin pan being beaten, but it never did. The silence between the shouts was what caused my trouble. I kept going, and then I heard the shout behind me. I was now tangled up. It was someone else’s shout, or maybe I had turned around.

I threw the paddle down. I heard the shout again. It was behind me at first, but then it moved to another spot. It kept coming and changing its position, and I kept answering. After a while it was in front of me again, and I knew the current had turned the canoe’s bow downstream. I would be okay if that was Jim and not some other raftsman shouting. I couldn’t make out any voices in the fog, because nothing looks or sounds natural in a fog.

The shouting continued, and after about a minute I crashed onto a steep bank covered with dark silhouettes of big trees. The current pushed me to the left and carried me past many snags that seemed to roar as the fast water tore past them.

In a second or two it became solid white and quiet again. I held completely still, listening to my heart beat. I think I didn’t take a breath while it beat a hundred times.

I just gave up then. I knew what the problem was. That steep bank was actually an island, and Jim had gone to the other side of it. It wasn’t a small hill you could float past in ten minutes. It had big trees like a normal island; it could be five or six miles long and more than half a mile wide.

I stayed silent and listened carefully for about fifteen minutes, I think. I was drifting at four or five miles an hour, but I didn’t think about my speed. Instead, I felt as if I were lying completely still on the water. When a small piece of snag passed by, I didn’t notice how fast I was moving; I just caught my breath and thought, “Wow, that snag is moving fast!” If you think being alone in a night fog isn’t scary or lonely, try it once—you’ll understand.

For the next half‑hour I shouted every now and then. Finally I heard a response far away and tried to follow it, but I couldn’t. I realized I was in a cluster of tow‑heads. I saw faint shapes on both sides, sometimes only a narrow channel between them. Some I couldn’t see, but I knew they were there because I heard the water rushing against old dead brush and trash hanging over the banks.

It didn’t take long before my shouts got lost among the tow‑heads. I only tried to chase them for a short time, because it was more confusing than chasing a Jack‑o‑lantern. You never notice a sound moving around like that, changing places so quickly and often.

I had to pull away from the bank energetically four or five times to avoid hitting the islands. I think the raft was bumping into the bank now and then; otherwise it would move ahead and be out of earshot. It was drifting a little faster than I was.

Soon I found myself back in the open river, but I couldn't hear anyone shouting. I guessed that Jim had gotten stuck on a snag and that it was over for him. I was very tired, so I lay down in the canoe and told myself I wouldn't try any more. I didn't want to fall asleep, but I was so sleepy that I couldn't help it, so I decided to take a short nap.

It turned out to be more than a short nap, because when I woke up the stars were shining brightly, the fog had disappeared, and I was moving down a big bend stern‑first. At first I didn't know where I was; I thought I was dreaming. Then the details began to come back to me, slowly, like memories from the previous week.

The river was huge, with the tallest, thickest trees on both banks, forming a solid wall as far as the stars could show. I looked downstream and saw a dark spot on the water. I followed it, but when I got there it was only a couple of bound‑together logs. Then I saw another spot and chased that, and another. This time I was right—it was the raft.

When I reached it, Jim was sitting there with his head down between his knees, asleep, his right arm hanging over the steering oar. The other oar was broken, and the raft was covered with leaves, branches, and dirt. It had clearly had a rough ride.

I held on tightly and laid down under Jim’s nose on the raft. I started to stretch my fists toward Jim and said:

“Hello, Jim, have I been asleep? Why didn’t you wake me up?”

“Goodness, is that you, Huck? You’re not dead—you didn’t drown—you’re back again? This is unbelievable! Let me look at you, let me touch you. No, you’re not dead! You’re back, alive and sound, just the same old Huck—thanks to goodness!”

“What’s wrong with you, Jim? Have you been drinking?”

“Drinking? Have I been drinking? Have I even had a chance to drink?”

“Well then, why are you talking so wildly?”

“How am I talking wildly?”

“Why? Haven’t you been talking about my coming back and all that, as if I’d been gone?”

“Huck—Huck Finn, look at me in the eye; look at me in the eye. Haven’t you been gone?”

“Gone? What do you mean? I haven’t gone anywhere. Where would I go?”

“Look here, boss, something’s wrong. Am I me, or who am I? Am I here, or what am I? That’s what I want to know.”

“I think you’re here, plain enough, but I think you’re a confused old fool, Jim.”

“Am I? Well, answer me this: didn’t you pull the line in the canoe to make it fast to the tow‑head?”

“No, I didn’t. What tow‑head? I haven’t seen any tow‑head.”

“You haven’t seen any tow‑head? Look, didn’t the line come loose and the raft start humming down the river, leaving you and the canoe behind in the fog?”

“What fog?”

“Why, the fog! The fog has been around all night. Didn't you shout? Didn't I shout? We got mixed up on the islands. One of us got lost and the other was just as lost because he didn't know who he was. And didn't I break out again on those islands, have a terrible time, and almost get drowned? Isn't that right, boss? Answer me.”

“Well, that's too much for me, Jim. I haven't seen any fog, any islands, any trouble, nothing at all. I've been sitting here talking with you all night until you fell asleep about ten minutes ago, and I think I did the same. You couldn't have gotten drunk in that time, so of course you've been dreaming.”

“Damn it, how could I dream all that in ten minutes?”

“Well, forget it, you did dream it, because none of it actually happened.”

“But, Huck, it's as clear to me as—”

“It doesn't matter how clear it is; there's nothing to it. I know because I've been here the whole time.”

Jim was silent for about five minutes, then said:

“Well then, I think I did dream it, Huck; but dear me, it's not the most powerful dream I've ever had. I've never had a dream before that has tired me like this one.”

“Oh, that's okay, because a dream can tire you out, just like anything else sometimes. But this one was a lingering dream; tell me everything about it, Jim.”

Jim went to work and told me the whole story exactly as it happened, though he exaggerated it a lot. Then he said he had to start “interpreting” it because it was meant as a warning. He explained that the first tow‑head represented a man who would try to help us, but the current stood for another man who would take us away from him. The “whoops” were warnings that would appear from time to time. If we didn’t try hard to understand them, they would bring us bad luck instead of protecting us. The many tow‑heads meant the troubles we would face with quarrelsome and mean people. But if we kept to ourselves, didn’t talk back, and didn’t aggravate them, we would get through, leave the fog, and reach the clear river – the free states – without more trouble.

It had gotten very dark just after I got on the raft, but now it was clearing again.

“Okay, that’s interpreted well enough as far as it goes, Jim,” I said, “but what do *these* things stand for?”

I was pointing at the leaves, rubbish on the raft, and the broken oar. You could see them clearly now.

Jim looked at the trash, then at me, then back at the trash again. He had the dream fixed so firmly in his mind that he couldn’t shake it loose and put the facts back in order right away. When he finally sorted it out, he stared at me steadily, without a smile, and said:

What are you waiting for? I’m going to tell you. When I was completely worn out from work and from calling for you, I went to sleep. My heart was almost broken because you were lost, and I didn’t know what had happened to me and the rest. When I woke up and found you back again, safe and sound, the tears came. I could have gotten down on my knees and kissed your foot because I was so thankful. All you were thinking about was how you could make a fool of old Jim with a lie. That truck is trash, and trash is what people are who put dirt on their friends’ heads and make them ashamed.  

Then he got up slowly, walked to the wigwam, and went inside without saying anything else. That was enough. It made me feel so humble I could almost kiss his foot to get him to take it back.  

It took me fifteen minutes to gather the courage to humble myself to a nigger, but I did it and I was never sorry afterward. I didn’t play any more mean tricks on him, and I wouldn’t have done that one if I had known it would make him feel that way.
