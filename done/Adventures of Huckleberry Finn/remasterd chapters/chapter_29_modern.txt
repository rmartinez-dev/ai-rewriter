They were bringing a very good‑looking old gentleman and a good‑looking younger man, whose right arm was in a sling. My goodness, how the people shouted and laughed, and kept doing so. I didn’t see any joke in it, and I thought it would embarrass the duke and the king to see any. I expected they would turn pale. But no, they didn’t turn pale at all.  

The duke never showed that he suspected anything; he just went about merrily, happy and satisfied, like a jug spilling buttermilk. As for the king, he stared sorrowfully at the newcomers, as if it gave him a painful feeling in his heart to think that such frauds and rascals existed in the world. He handled it admirably.  

Many important people gathered around the king to show him that they were on his side. The old gentleman who had just arrived looked completely bewildered. Soon he began to speak, and I could tell right away that he sounded like an Englishman—not exactly like the king, though the king’s imitation was pretty good. I can’t repeat the old man’s words, and I can’t imitate him, but he turned to the crowd and said something like this:

“This is a surprise I wasn’t expecting, and I have to admit, honestly, I’m not really ready to deal with it or answer it. My brother and I have had some bad luck. He broke his arm, and our luggage was left in a town above us last night by mistake. I’m Harvey, Peter Wilks’s brother, and this is his brother William. William can’t hear or speak, and he can’t even make clear signs because he only has one hand to use. We are who we say we are, and in a day or two, when I get the luggage, I can prove it. Until then I won’t say anything more; I’ll just go to the hotel and wait.”

So he and the new dummy left, and the king laughed and said:

“Broke his arm—very likely, isn’t it?—and that’s convenient for a fraud who has to make signs but hasn’t learned how. Lost their luggage! That’s really good—very clever—under the circumstances!”

He laughed again, and everyone else laughed too, except for three or four people, maybe half a dozen. One of them was the doctor; another was a sharp‑looking gentleman with an old‑style carpet bag who had just stepped off the steamboat. He was speaking quietly, glancing at the king now and then and nodding. He was Levi Bell, the lawyer who had gone up to Louisville. Another was a big, rough, husky fellow who had come along, listened to everything the old gentleman said, and was now listening to the king. When the king finished, the husky spoke up and said:

“Listen, if you are Harvey Wilks, when did you come to this town?”

“The day before the funeral, my friend,” says the king.

“But what time of day?”

“In the evening—about an hour or two before sunset.”

“How did you get here?”

“I came down on the Susan Powell from Cincinnati.”

“Alright, then how did you end up at the Pint in the morning—by canoe?”

“I wasn’t at the Pint in the morning.”

“That’s a lie.”

Several of them jumped up and begged him not to speak that way to an old man and a preacher.

“The preacher is a fraud and a liar. He was at the Pint that morning. I live up there, don’t I? Well, I was there, and he was there. I saw him there. He came in a canoe with Tim Collins and a boy.”

The doctor stood up and said:

“Would you recognize the boy again if you saw him, Hines?”

“I think I would, but I’m not sure. He’s over there now. I can tell who he is easily.”

He pointed at me. The doctor continued:

“Neighbors, I don’t know whether the new couple are frauds or not. But if these two aren’t frauds, then I’m an idiot, that’s all. I think it’s our duty to make sure they don’t leave until we’ve investigated this. Come along, Hines; come along, everyone else. We’ll take these fellows to the tavern and confront them with the other couple, and I think we’ll find out something before we’re done.”

It was chaotic for the crowd, though maybe not for the king’s friends, so we all began. It was around sunset. The doctor guided me by the hand and was very kind, but he never let go of it.

We all went into a large hotel room, lit some candles, and brought in the newly married couple. First, the doctor said:

“I don’t want to be too harsh on these two men, but I think they are frauds, and they may have accomplices we don’t know about. If they do, won’t those accomplices take the bag of gold that Peter Wilks left? That could happen. If these men are not frauds, they won’t mind sending the money and letting us keep it until they prove they are innocent—right?”

Everyone agreed. I thought this put our group in a very tight spot from the start. The king, however, only looked sad and said:

“Gentlemen, I wish the money were here, because I have no desire to start a thorough, open investigation into this messy affair; but, unfortunately, the money isn’t here. You can send someone to look, if you like.”

“Where is it, then?”

“Well, when my niece gave it to me to keep for her, I took it and hid it inside the straw mattress of my bed. I didn’t want to keep it for the few days we would be here, and I thought the bed was a safe place. We weren’t used to black men, and I assumed they were honest, like servants in England. The black men stole it the very next morning after I had gone downstairs. When I sold them, I hadn’t noticed the money missing yet, so they got away with it. My servant here can tell you about it, gentlemen.”

The doctor and several others said, “Shucks!” and I saw that nobody truly believed him. One man asked me if I had seen the black men steal it. I said no, but I saw them sneaking out of the room and hurrying away. I didn’t think much of it; I just guessed they were afraid they had woken my master and were trying to leave before he caused trouble for them. That was all they asked me. Then the doctor turned to me and said:

“Are you English, too?”

I said yes, and he and some others laughed and said, “Nonsense!”

Well, then they started the general investigation, and there it was, going on all day, hour after hour. No one ever mentioned dinner or seemed to think about it, and they kept it going. It was the most confusing mess you could ever see.

They made the king tell his story, and they made the old gentleman tell his. Anyone who wasn’t a prejudiced fool could see that the old gentleman was telling the truth while the other was lying.

Soon they asked me to speak about what I knew. The king gave me a sideways glance, so I knew I had to be careful with my words. I began to talk about Sheffield, how we lived there, and about the English Wilkses, and so on. I didn’t get very far until the doctor started to laugh, and Levi Bell, the lawyer, said:

“Sit down, boy; I wouldn’t push myself if I were you. I think you’re not used to lying; it doesn’t come naturally. You need practice. You do it very awkwardly.”

I didn’t care about the compliment, but I was glad to be let off anyway.

The doctor began to say something, then turned and said:

“If you had been in town first, Levi Bell—”

The king interrupted, reached out his hand, and asked:

“Is this the old friend of my poor dead brother that he wrote about so often?”

The lawyer shook hands with him. The lawyer smiled, looked pleased, and they talked for a while. Then they moved to a side of the room and spoke in low voices. Finally the lawyer said,

“That will settle it. I’ll take the order and send it, together with your brother’s, and then they’ll know everything is fine.”

They got some paper and a pen. The king set the paper down, tilted his head, bit his tongue, and scribbled something. He then handed the pen to the duke, who looked sick for the first time. The duke took the pen and wrote.

The lawyer turned to the new old gentleman and said,

“You and your brother, please write a line or two and sign your names.”

The old gentleman wrote, but nobody could read it. The lawyer looked surprised and said,

“Well, it beats me,” and pulled many old letters from his pocket. He examined them, then looked at the old man’s writing, and examined them again. He said,

“These letters are from Harvey Wilks, and here are these two handwritings; anyone can see they weren’t written by the king or the duke. The king and the duke looked foolish, thinking the lawyer had deceived them. And here is this old gentleman’s writing; anyone can tell, easily, that he didn’t write it. The marks he makes aren’t proper writing at all. Now, here are some letters from—”

The new old gentleman interrupted,

“Please let me explain. No one can read my handwriting except my brother, so he copies for me. The hand you have there is his, not mine.”

“​Well!” says the lawyer. “This is a mess. I have some of William’s letters, too. If you can get him to write a line or two, we can compare—”

“He can’t write with his left hand,” says the old gentleman. “If he could use his right hand, you would see that he wrote his own letters and mine too. Look at both, please—they’re written in the same hand.”

The lawyer agrees and says:

“I think that’s right. Even if I’m wrong, the similarity is stronger than I expected. Well, well, well! I thought we were close to a solution, but it’s slipped away a bit. One thing is clear—these two aren’t either of the Wilkes.” He shakes his head toward the king and the duke.

What do you think? That stubborn old fool wouldn’t give up. He said it wasn’t a fair test. He claimed his brother William was the biggest prankster in the world and hadn’t even tried to write—he could see William was about to play a joke the moment he picked up a pen. So he warmed up, kept babbling, and eventually began to believe his own words. Then the new gentleman interrupted and said:

“I’ve thought of something. Did anyone here help prepare the late Peter Wilks for burial?”

“Yes,” says a voice, “me and Ab Turner did. We’re both here.”

The old man turns to the king and asks:

“Perhaps this gentleman can tell me what was tattooed on his chest?”

If the king hadn’t had to brace himself so quickly, he would have been crushed like a riverbank that the water has worn away. It happened so suddenly. You see, it was meant to make anyone collapse and get a hard blow without any warning, because how could he know what was tattooed on the man? He turned a little pale; he couldn’t help it. The tattoo was still there, and everyone leaned forward a bit and stared at him. I thought to myself, “Now he’ll give up—there’s no point.” Did he? It’s hard to believe, but he didn’t. I think he hoped to keep the pressure on until the people got tired, so they would thin out, and he and the duke could break free and escape.  

He stayed there, and soon he began to smile. He said:

“Wow! That’s a very tough question, isn’t it? Yes, sir, I can tell you what’s tattooed on his chest. It’s just a small, thin, blue arrow. If you don’t look closely, you won’t see it. So, what do you think?”

I’ve never seen anything like that old scar on a clean cheek.  

The new gentleman turned quickly toward Ab Turner and his friend. His eyes lit up as if he thought he finally had the king’s attention. He said:

“There—you heard what he said! Was there any such mark on Peter Wilks’s chest?”

Both of them answered:

“We didn’t see any such mark.”

“Good!” says the old gentleman. “Now, what you saw on his chest was a small, dim P, a B (which is an initial he dropped when he was young), and a W, with dashes between them—so: P—B—W.” He wrote it down on a piece of paper. “Come on, isn’t that what you saw?”

Both of them answered again: “No, we didn’t. We never saw any marks at all.”

Everyone was excited now, and they shouted: “All those tricks of his! Let’s duck them! Let’s drown them! Let’s ride them on a rail!” Everyone cheered together, and there was a noisy commotion. Then the lawyer jumped onto the table and yelled: “Gentlemen! Listen to me, just one word, if you please! There is still one way—let’s dig up the corpse and look.”

That caught their attention.

“Hooray!” they all shouted, and they began to move, but the lawyer and the doctor called out: “Hold on, hold on! Arrest these four men and the boy, and bring them with us too!”

“We’ll do it!” they shouted. “And if we don’t find the marks, we’ll lynch the whole gang!”

I was scared, I tell you. There was no way to escape. They grabbed us all and marched us straight to the graveyard, which was a mile and a half down the river. The whole town was behind us, because we made a lot of noise, and it was only nine o’clock in the evening.

As we passed our house, I wished I hadn't sent Mary Jane away. If I could give her a signal now, she would rush back and help me, and deal with our enemies.

We hurried down the river road, running like wildcats. The sky grew darker, lightning flashed, and the wind rattled the leaves.

It was the worst trouble and the most dangerous situation I had ever faced, and I was almost stunned. Everything was happening very differently from what I had expected. Instead of being in control, taking my time, enjoying ourselves, and having Mary Jane there to protect me when danger came, there was nothing between me and sudden death except their marks. If they didn't find them—

I couldn't stand thinking about it, but I also couldn't think about anything else. It grew darker, and it seemed like a perfect moment to slip away from the crowd. But the big husky, Hines, grabbed my wrist. It was as if trying to escape Goliar. He pulled me along, very excited, and I had to run to keep up.

When they arrived, they rushed into the cemetery and flooded it like an overflow. When they reached the grave, they realized they had far more shovels than they needed, but nobody had thought to bring a lantern. They started digging anyway by the flash of lightning and sent a man to the nearest house, about a half‑mile away, to borrow one.

They kept digging. It became very dark. Rain began, the wind swished, lightning grew brighter, and thunder boomed. The people didn’t notice; they were too busy with the work. One moment you could see every face in the crowd and the shovelfuls of dirt flying out of the grave; the next moment the darkness covered everything and you could see nothing.

Finally they lifted the coffin and tried to open the lid. The crowd pushed and shoved even more as people tried to get a look. In the darkness it was terrible. He pulled my wrist hard and hurt it badly, and I think he completely forgot I was there because he was so excited and out of breath.

Suddenly a bright flash of lightning illuminated everything, and someone shouted:  

“By the living, there’s a bag of gold on his chest!”

Hines let out a whoop, just like everyone else. I let go of his wrist, gave a big push to break through, and raced down the dark road. No one could tell how fast I was going.  

I had the road all to myself and I flew along. It was just me, the deep darkness, occasional flashes of lightning, the sound of rain, the howling wind, and the cracking thunder. I rode as fast as I could.  

When I reached the town, I saw that nobody was out in the storm, so I didn’t look for side streets. I drove straight through the main street. As I got closer to our house, I looked at it. The house was completely dark, which made me feel sad and disappointed for some reason. Then, just as I was passing, a flash of light appeared in Mary Jane’s window. My heart suddenly swelled with joy. At the same moment the house and everything behind me faded into darkness, and I would never see it again. She was the best girl I had ever seen, and she had the greatest spirit.

The moment I was high enough above the town to see that I could reach the tow‑head, I started looking carefully for a boat to borrow. The first time the lightning showed me a boat that wasn’t chained, I grabbed it and pushed it away. It was a canoe tied only with a rope.

The tow‑head was far away, out in the middle of the river, but I didn’t waste any time. When I finally reached the raft I was so exhausted I could have just lain down and gasped, if I had been able to. I didn’t. As I jumped aboard I shouted:

“Out with you, Jim, and set her loose! Thank goodness, we’re away from them!”

Jim ran out, coming toward me with both arms spread, full of joy. But when I saw him in the lightning, my heart leapt to my throat and I fell overboard backwards. I had forgotten that he was like an old King Lear and a drowned Arab at the same time, and it terrified me.

Jim pulled me out and was about to hug me and bless me. He was so glad I was back and we were away from the king and the duke, but I said:

“Not now; have it for breakfast, have it for breakfast! Cut loose and let her slide!”

In just two seconds we were sliding down the river. It felt wonderful to be free again, alone on the big river, with no one bothering us.  

I had to hop a little and jump up, and my heels cracked a few times—I couldn't stop it. Around the third crack I heard a sound I knew very well. I held my breath, listened, and waited.  

Sure enough, when the next flash of light appeared over the water, they were coming—rowing with their oars, the boat humming. It was the king and the duke.  

I fell down onto the planks and gave up. I could barely stop myself from crying.
