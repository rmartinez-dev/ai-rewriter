Colonel Grangerford was a gentleman, you know. He behaved like a gentleman everywhere, and his family did too. He was well‑born, as they say, and that meant as much for a man as it did for a horse. Widow Douglas said that, and nobody ever denied that she came from the highest class in our town. His father always repeated the same thing, even though he was no better than a common mud‑cat.  

The colonel was very tall and very thin. His skin was a pale, grayish colour, with no hint of red anywhere. Every morning he shaved his thin face completely. He had very thin lips, tiny nostrils, a high nose, heavy eyebrows, and the darkest eyes. His eyes were set deep in his face, as if they were looking at you from a cavern.  

His forehead was high, his hair was black, straight, and fell to his shoulders. His hands were long and thin. Every day he wore a clean white shirt and a full linen suit that was so bright it hurt the eyes. On Sundays he wore a blue tailcoat with brass buttons. He carried a mahogany cane with a silver tip.  

There was nothing frivolous about him, not a bit, and he never raised his voice. He was as kind as he could be—you could feel that, and you felt confident around him. Sometimes he smiled, and it was pleasant to see. But when he straightened up like a pole and a flash of light seemed to flicker under his eyebrows, you wanted to climb a tree first and find out what was happening later.  

He never had to tell anyone to behave; everyone was always well‑mannered when he was there. Everybody liked having him around; he was like sunshine most of the time—you felt the weather was good. When he turned into a cloud, it became very dark for a minute, and that was enough. Nothing went wrong again for a whole week.

When he and the old lady came downstairs in the morning, everyone in the family got up from their chairs, greeted them, and didn’t sit down again until the guests were seated. Tom and Bob went to the sideboard where the decanter was, poured a glass of bitters, and handed it to him. He held it while they finished mixing their drinks. Then they all bowed and said, “Our duty to you, sir and madam.” They bowed only slightly, thanked them, and the three of them drank together. Bob and Tom added a spoonful of water to the sugar and a little whisky or apple brandy at the bottom of their glasses, gave the drinks to me and Buck, and we also toasted the older couple.

Bob was the oldest, and Tom was next. They were tall, handsome men with broad shoulders, brown faces, long black hair, and dark eyes. They wore white linen from head to toe, like the old gentleman, and broad Panama hats.

Then there was Miss Charlotte. She was twenty‑five, tall, proud, and dignified, and she behaved well when she wasn’t upset. When she was angry, her look could make anyone freeze, just like her father’s. She was beautiful.

Her sister, Miss Sophia, was also beautiful but in a different way. She was gentle and sweet like a dove, and she was only twenty.

Each person had their own servant, including Buck. My servant had an unusually easy time because I wasn’t used to having anyone do anything for me, but Buck’s servant was always ready to help.

Now this is all that is left of the family, but there used to be more—three sons and Emmeline, who died.

The old gentleman owned many farms and more than a hundred Black workers. Sometimes a group of people would come from ten or fifteen miles away on horseback. They stayed for five or six days. They enjoyed rides around the area and on the river, danced, had picnics in the woods during the day, and attended balls at the house at night. Most of these visitors were relatives of the family. The men brought their guns with them. It was a fine, respectable group, I tell you.

There was another group of aristocratic families nearby—about five or six families—mostly named Shepherdson. They were as high‑toned, well‑born, rich, and impressive as the Grangerford tribe. The Shepherdsons and the Grangerfords used the same steamboat landing, which was about two miles upstream from our house. So when I went up there with many of our people, I often saw the Shepherdsons on their fine horses.

One day Buck and I were out in the woods hunting and we heard a horse coming. We were crossing the road. Buck said, “Quick! Jump into the woods!”

We did it and then peeked down into the woods through the leaves. Soon a handsome young man came galloping down the road. He eased his horse and looked like a soldier. He had his gun across his saddle. I had seen him before. It was the young Harney Shepherdson. I heard Buck’s gun fire next to my ear, and Harney’s hat fell off his head. He grabbed his gun and rode straight to the place where we were hidden. But we didn’t wait. We ran through the woods. The woods weren’t thick, so I looked over my shoulder to avoid the bullet. Twice I saw Harney cover Buck with his gun, and then he rode away the way he had come—probably to get his hat, I guess, but I couldn’t see. We kept running until we got home. The old gentleman’s eyes flashed for a moment—probably with pleasure, I thought—then his face softened and he said gently:

“I don’t like shooting from behind a bush. Why didn’t you step onto the road, my boy?”

“The Shepherdsons never do, father. They always take advantage.”

Miss Charlotte held her head up like a queen while Buck told his story. Her nostrils flared and her eyes widened. The two young men looked angry but said nothing. Miss Sophia turned pale, but her color returned when she saw that the man wasn’t hurt.

As soon as I could get Buck down by the corn cribs under the trees, I asked:

“Did you want to kill him, Buck?”

“Well, I think I did.”

“What did he do to you?”

“Him? He never did anything to me.”

“Then why did you want to kill him?”

“Nothing, really—it's just because of the feud.”

“What’s a feud?”

“Where did you grow up? Don’t you know what a feud is?”

“I’ve never heard of it—tell me.”

“Well,” says Buck, “a feud works like this. One man fights another man and kills him. Then the dead man’s brother kills the first man. After that, the brothers on both sides fight each other. Then the cousins join in. Eventually everyone is dead, and the feud ends. It happens slowly and can last a long time.”

“Has this feud been going on a long time, Buck?”

“I think so. It started about thirty years ago, give or take. There was a disagreement about something, and a lawsuit was filed. The verdict went against one of the men, so he shot the man who won. He did that because he felt he had to. Anyone would have.”

“What was the dispute about, Buck? Land?”

“I guess maybe, but I’m not sure.”

“Who did the shooting? Was it a Grangerford or a Shepherdson?”

“I don’t know. It happened so long ago.”

“Does anyone know?”

“Maybe my dad and some of the older people remember, but they don’t know what the original argument was about.”

“Have many people been killed, Buck?”

“Yes, there have been a lot of funerals. But not everyone dies. My dad has a few buckshot wounds, but he doesn’t mind them because he’s light. Bob has been cut with a bowie knife, and Tom has been injured once or twice.”

“Has anyone been killed this year, Buck?”

“Yes; we got one and they got one. About three months ago my cousin Bud, who was fourteen, was riding through the woods on the other side of the river. He didn’t have any weapon with him, which was foolish. In a lonely spot he heard a horse coming behind him and saw old Baldy Shepherdson riding after him with a gun in his hand and his white hair blowing in the wind. Instead of jumping off and hiding in the brush, Bud thought he could outrun him, so they chased each other for five miles or more, the old man gaining ground the whole time. Finally Bud realized it was no use, so he stopped and turned around so the bullets would hit him from the front. The old man rode up and shot him down. Bud didn’t have much chance to enjoy his luck, because within a week our family buried him.

“I think that old man was a coward, Buck.”

I think he wasn’t a coward. Not by any measure. There isn’t a coward among the Shepherdsons—none at all. There are no cowards among the Grangerfords either. One day the old man fought for half an hour against three Grangerfords and won. They were all on horseback. He jumped off his horse and hid behind a small pile of wood, using his horse as a shield from the bullets. The Grangerfords stayed on their horses, rode around the old man, and fired at him, and he fired back. He and his horse both came home badly wounded, but the Grangerfords had to be brought home. One of them died, and another died the next day. No, sir; if someone is out hunting for cowards, he doesn’t want to waste any time among the Shepherdsons, because they don’t produce that kind of people.

The next Sunday we all went to church, about three miles away, each on horseback. The men took their guns with them, as did Buck, and kept them between their knees or leaned them against the wall. The Shepherdsons did the same. The preaching was stubborn—talking about brotherly love and other boring topics—but everyone said it was a good sermon. They talked about it on the way home and mentioned many things about faith, good works, free grace, and predestination. I don’t know all the details, but it seemed to me to be one of the roughest Sundays I have ever experienced.

About an hour after dinner everyone was dozing, some in their chairs and some in their rooms, and it became quite dull. Buck and a dog were stretched out on the grass in the sun, fast asleep. I went up to our room and decided I would take a nap myself. I saw sweet Miss Sophia standing in her doorway, which was next to ours. She took me into her room, closed the door quietly, and asked me if I liked her. I said I did. Then she asked if I would do something for her and not tell anyone. I said I would. She said she had forgotten her book and left it on the pew at church between two other books. She asked me to slip out quietly, go there, and bring it to her without telling anyone. I agreed. So I slipped out and walked up the road. There was nobody at the church except maybe a pig or two, because the door had no lock, and pigs like a wooden floor in summer because it is cool. Most people don’t go to church unless they have to, but a pig is different.

I said to myself that something was wrong; it isn’t normal for a girl to be so nervous about a Testament. So I shook it, and a little piece of paper fell out with “Half‑past two” written on it in pencil. I looked through the book but found nothing else. I couldn’t figure it out, so I put the paper back into the book. When I got home and went upstairs, Miss Sophia was waiting for me at her door. She pulled me in and shut the door, then searched the Testament until she found the paper. As soon as she read it, she looked happy. Before I could think, she grabbed me, gave me a hug, and said I was the best boy in the world and not to tell anyone. Her face turned bright red for a moment, her eyes lit up, and she looked strikingly pretty. I was very surprised, but when I caught my breath I asked what the paper meant. She asked if I had read it. I said no. She asked if I could read any writing, and I told her “no, only a rough hand.” Then she explained that the paper was just a bookmark to keep her place, and that I could go and play now.

I walked down to the river, thinking about it, and soon noticed that my friend was following me. When we were out of sight of the house, he looked back, then ran up to me and said:  

“Mars Jawge, if you come down into the swamp I’ll show you a whole bunch of water moccasins.”

I think it’s very curious; he said that yesterday. He should know that nobody loves water moccasins enough to go hunting for them. What is he up to, anyway? So I said:

“Alright, go ahead.”

I followed him for half a mile. Then he turned into the swamp and walked ankle‑deep in water for another half‑mile. We reached a small flat area that was dry and thick with trees, bushes and vines. He said:

“Just go a few steps in there, Mars Jawge; that’s where they are. I’ve seen them before; I don’t want to see them again.”

Then he slipped away, and the trees quickly hid him. I kept moving forward and came to a little open patch about the size of a bedroom, surrounded by vines. I found a man lying there asleep – and, by goodness, it was my old friend Jim!

I woke him up, thinking it would be a great surprise for him to see me again, but it wasn’t. He almost cried because he was so happy, but he wasn’t surprised. He said he swam behind me that night and heard me yell each time, but he didn’t answer because he didn’t want anyone to pick him up and take him into slavery again. He says:

I was hurt a little and I couldn’t swim fast, so I was far behind you toward the last part. When you landed I thought I could catch up with you on land without shouting, but when I saw that house I started to slow down. I was too far away to hear what they were saying to you. I was afraid of the dogs. When it got quiet again I knew you were in the house, so I went to the woods to wait for daylight.

Early in the morning some of the black men came, went to the fields, and they took me and showed me this place where the dogs can’t track me because of the water. They bring me food every night and tell me how you are doing.

“Why didn’t you tell my Jack to bring me here sooner, Jim?”

“Well, it wasn’t worth bothering you, Huck, to tell you we could do something—but we’re all right now. I was buying pots, pans and food whenever I could, and fixing the raft at night when—”

“What raft, Jim?”

“Our old raft.”

“You mean to say our old raft wasn’t smashed to pieces?”

“No, she wasn’t. She was torn up a lot—one end of her was damaged. But no serious harm was done; only our traps were almost all lost. If we hadn’t dived so deep, swum so far under water, and if the night hadn’t been so dark, we wouldn’t have been so scared. If we hadn’t been such pumpkin‑heads, as the saying goes, we would have seen the raft. But it’s just as well we didn’t, because now she’s all fixed up again, almost as good as new, and we have a new lot of stuff in place of what was lost.”

“Why, how did you get hold of the raft again, Jim—did you catch her?”

“How could I catch her in the woods? No. Some of the black men found her caught on a snag down there in the bend. They hid her in a creek among the willows. They talked so much about who she belonged to that I heard about it pretty soon. So I solved the problem by telling them she didn’t belong to any of them, but to you and me. I asked them if they were going to take a young white man’s property and hide it for them. Then I gave them ten cents each, and they were very satisfied. They wished more rafts would come along and make them rich again. Those black men are very good to me, and whatever I want them to do for me, I don’t have to ask them twice, honey. Jack is a good black man, and pretty smart.”

“Yes, he is. He never told me you were here. He told me to come, and he would show me a lot of water moccasins. If anything happens, he isn’t involved. He can say he never saw us together, and that would be true.”  

I don’t want to talk much about the next day, so I’ll keep it short. I woke up at dawn and was about to turn over and go back to sleep when I noticed how still everything was—no one seemed to be moving. That wasn’t usual. Then I saw that Buck was up and gone. I got up, puzzled, and went downstairs—no one around; everything was as quiet as a mouse. It was the same outside. I thought, what does this mean? By the wood‑pile I met my Jack and asked,  

“What’s it all about?”  

He said,  

“Don’t you know, Mars Jawge?”  

“No,” I replied, “I don’t.”  

“Well then, Miss Sophia has run off! She really has. She ran away during the night, and nobody knows exactly when. She ran off to marry that young Harney Shepherdson, at least that’s what they say. The family found out about half an hour ago, maybe a little more, and I’m telling you there was no time to lose. They’re rushing guns and horses you’ve never seen! The women have gone to stir up the relatives, and old Mars Saul and the boys have loaded their guns and rode up the river road to try to catch that young man and kill him before he can get across the river with Miss Sophia. I think they’re going to have very rough times.”  

“Buck left without waking me up.”

“Well, I think he did! They weren’t going to involve you in it. Mars Buck loaded his gun and said he was going to bring home a Shepherdson or nothing. Well, there will be plenty of them there, I think, and you bet he’ll bring one back if he gets a chance.”

I rode the river road as fast as I could. After a while I started hearing gunshots far away. When I saw the log store and the woodpile near the steamboat landing, I moved along under the trees and brush until I reached a good spot. Then I climbed into the branches of a cottonwood that was out of reach and watched. There was a four‑foot‑high wooden fence a short distance in front of the tree. At first I thought about hiding behind it, but maybe it was better that I didn’t.

Four or five men were riding their horses around the open area in front of the log store. They were swearing and shouting, trying to reach a couple of young men who were hiding behind the wooden fence near the steamboat landing. But they couldn’t get to them. Every time one of the men stepped onto the river side of the woodpile, he was fired at. The two boys crouched back‑to‑back behind the pile so they could see both directions.

After a while the men stopped playing around and shouting. They began riding toward the store. Then one of the boys rode up, aimed a steady shot at the wooden fence, and knocked a man off his saddle. All the men jumped off their horses, grabbed the injured man, and started carrying him to the store. At the same moment the two boys started to run. They got halfway to the tree I was standing near before the men noticed. Then the men saw them, jumped on their horses, and gave chase. They caught up with the boys, but it didn’t help; the boys had a good head start. They reached the woodpile in front of my tree, slipped behind it, and turned to face the men again. One of the boys was Buck, and the other was a thin young man about nineteen years old.

The men rode around for a while and then left. As soon as they were out of sight I shouted to Buck and told him. At first he didn’t know what to make of my voice coming from the tree. He was very surprised. He told me to be careful and to let him know when the men appeared again. He said they were up to some mischief and wouldn’t be gone long. I wanted to get out of the tree, but I didn’t come down. Buck began to cry and shout. He promised that he and his cousin Joe, the other young man, would get even later that day. He said his father and two brothers had been killed, and two or three of the enemy too. He said the Shepherdsons had set an ambush for them. Buck said his father and brothers should have waited for their relatives because the Shepherdsons were too strong for them. I asked him what had happened to young Harney and Miss Sophia. He said they had crossed the river and were safe. I was glad of that, but Buck was angry because he hadn’t managed to kill Harney that day. He had shot at him – I’ve never heard anything like it.

All of a sudden three or four guns went off—bang! bang! bang! The men had moved through the woods and came from behind, without their horses. The two boys jumped into the river, and both were hurt. While they were swimming downstream, the men ran along the bank, shooting at them and shouting, “Kill them, kill them!” It made me feel so sick that I almost fell out of the tree. I’m not going to tell everything that happened—it would make me sick again. I wish I had never come ashore that night to see those things. I will never get rid of them; I keep dreaming about them.

I stayed in the tree until it started to get dark, too afraid to come down. Sometimes I heard gunshots far away in the woods, and twice I saw small groups of men riding past the log store with guns. I thought the trouble was still going on. I felt very downhearted, so I decided I would never go near that house again because I felt somehow responsible. I believed that the piece of paper meant Miss Sophia was supposed to meet Harney somewhere at half‑past two and run away. I thought I should have told her father about that paper and her strange behavior; maybe he would have locked her up and this terrible mess would never have happened.

I climbed down from the tree and crept a short way along the riverbank. I saw two bodies lying at the edge of the water and dragged them to shore. I covered their faces and ran away as fast as I could. I cried a little while covering Buck’s face because he had been very good to me.

It was getting dark. I didn’t go near the house; instead I pushed through the woods toward the swamp. Jim wasn’t on his island, so I hurried toward the creek, squeezing through the willows, eager to jump on a raft and leave that terrible place. The raft was gone! I was terrified. I could hardly catch my breath for a minute. Then I shouted. A voice that was only about twenty‑five feet away said:

“Goodness! Is that you, honey? Don’t make any noise.”

It was Jim’s voice—nothing had ever sounded so good before. I ran a short way along the bank, got aboard, and Jim grabbed me and hugged me. He was so glad to see me. He said:

“Lord bless you, child. I was sure you were dead again. Jack has been here; he says he thinks you were shot because you didn’t come home any more. So I was just now starting the raft down toward the mouth of the creek, getting ready to push out and leave as soon as Jack comes back and tells me for sure you are dead. Thank goodness, I’m so glad to have you back, honey.”

I said:

“Alright, that’s great; they won’t find me, and they’ll think I’ve been killed and floated down the river. Something up there will make them believe that. So don’t waste any time, Jim—just push off into the big water as fast as you can.”

I didn’t feel relaxed until the raft was two miles downstream, in the middle of the Mississippi. Then we hung up our signal lantern and thought we were free and safe again. I hadn’t eaten since yesterday, so Jim got out some cornbread, buttermilk, pork, cabbage, and greens—nothing in the world is as good as food cooked right. While I ate my supper we talked and had a good time. I was really glad to get away from the fights, and Jim was glad to leave the swamp. We said there was no home like a raft after all. Other places seem cramped and smelly, but a raft does not. You feel very free, easy, and comfortable on a raft.
