As soon as we thought everyone was asleep that night, we went down the lightning rod, hid in the small shelter, took out our pile of glow‑sticks, and started working. We cleared everything away about four or five feet along the middle of the lower log. Tom said he was right behind Jim’s bed now, and we would dig under it. When we finished, no one in the cabin could see the hole because Jim’s counter‑pin hung down almost to the floor. You would have to lift it up and look underneath to notice anything. So we dug with our pocket knives until almost midnight. By then we were exhausted, our hands were blistered, but it looked like we hadn’t done much at all. Finally I said,  

“This isn’t a thirty‑seven‑year job; it’s a thirty‑eight‑year job, Tom Sawyer.”  

He didn’t answer. He sighed, then stopped digging. He thought for a while, and then he said:

“It isn’t going to work, Huck. If we were prisoners, it would, because then we’d have as many years as we wanted and no hurry. We would only get a few minutes each day to dig while they changed watches, so our hands wouldn’t get blistered. We could keep it up year after year and do it the right way. But we can’t take our time; we have to rush. We don’t have any time to spare. If we tried another night like this, we would have to stop for a week to let our hands heal—we couldn’t use a case‑knife with them any sooner.”

“Well, then, what are we going to do, Tom?”

“I’ll tell you. It isn’t right, it isn’t moral, and I don’t want it to get out, but there’s more than one way. We have to dig him out with picks and pretend they are case‑knives.”

“Now you’re talking!” I say. “Your head gets clearer and clearer all the time, Tom Sawyer,” I say. “Picks are the tool, moral or not; and as for me, I don’t care about the morality at all. When I start to steal a nigger, a watermelon, or a Sunday‑school book, I don’t worry about how it’s done. What I want is my nigger, or my watermelon, or my Sunday‑school book, and if a pick is the most useful thing, that’s what I’ll use to dig it out. I don’t care what the authorities think.”

“Well,” he said, “there’s a reason to use picks and to pretend in a case like this. If there wasn’t, I wouldn’t approve of it, and I wouldn’t stand by and watch the rules broken—because right is right and wrong is wrong. Nobody should do something wrong when they aren’t ignorant and know better. It might be okay for you to dig Jim out with a pick without pretending, because you don’t know any better; but not for me, because I do know better. Give me a case knife.”

He had his own, but I gave him mine. He threw it down and said, “Give me a case knife.”

I didn’t know what to do at first, but then I thought. I looked through the old tools, found a pickaxe, and handed it to him. He took it, started working, and didn’t say a word.

He was always very particular. Full of principle.

So I got a shovel, and we started picking and shoveling, turning around, and kicking up dust. We worked for about half an hour, which was as long as we could keep going, but we had a decent hole when we finished. When I went upstairs, I looked out the window and saw Tom trying his best with the lightning rod, but he couldn’t finish because his hands were sore. Finally he said, “It’s no use, it can’t be done. What do you think I should do? Can’t you think of any way?”

“Yes,” I said, “but I think it’s not proper. Come upstairs and pretend it’s a lightning rod.”

So he did it.

Next day Tom stole a pewter spoon and a brass candlestick from the house. He wanted to turn them into pens for Jim, and he also took six tallow candles. I hung around the cabins and waited for a chance, and I stole three tin plates. Tom said that wasn’t enough. I replied that no one would ever see the plates that Jim threw away because they would fall into the weeds under the window opening. Then we could bring them back and Jim could use them again. Tom was satisfied. Then he said:

“The next thing we need to figure out is how to get the items to Jim.”

“Put them through the hole,” I said, “once we’re ready.”

He looked scornful and said no one had ever heard of such a foolish idea, and then he went back to planning. After a while he said he had worked out two or three possible ways, but we didn’t need to choose any of them yet. He said we had to send Jim a message first.

That night, a little after ten, we went down the lightning‑rod. We took a candle with us and listened at the window. We could hear Jim snoring, so we put the candle in the room. It didn’t wake him.  

Then we started digging with a pick and a shovel. After about two and a half hours the work was finished. We slipped under Jim’s bed, entered the cabin, felt around, found the candle and lit it. We stood over Jim for a while. He looked healthy and strong, so we woke him up gently and slowly.  

He was so glad to see us that he almost cried. He called us “honey” and used every affectionate nickname he could think of. He asked us to bring a cold chisel right away to cut the chain off his leg and to get him out quickly, without wasting any time.  

But Tom showed him how uncomfortable that would be. He sat down and explained our plans. He said we could change the plans at any moment if there was an alarm, and that Jim didn’t need to be afraid because we would make sure he got away safely.  

Jim agreed that everything was fine. We all talked about old times for a while. Then Tom asked many questions. When Jim told him that Uncle Silas comes in every day or two to pray with him, and Aunt Sally comes to check that he is comfortable and has enough food, and that both of them are as kind as they can be, Tom replied:  

“Now I know what to do. We’ll send you some things through them.”

I told him, “Don’t do anything like that; it’s one of the stupidest ideas I’ve ever had.” He didn’t listen at all. He just kept going. That’s how he acted when he was determined.

Then he explained to Jim how we would have to smuggle the rope‑ladder, the pie, and other large items with Nat, the Black man who fed him. He said Jim had to stay alert, not be surprised, and make sure Nat didn’t see him open the packages. We would hide small items in Uncle’s coat pockets, and Jim would have to steal them out. If we got a chance, we would tie things to Aunt’s apron strings or put them in her apron pocket. He told Jim what the items were and what they were for. He also told him how to keep a journal on his shirt with his blood, and all that. He told him everything. Jim didn’t understand most of it, but he thought we were white people and knew better, so he agreed and said he would do everything just as Tom had said.

Jim had plenty of corn‑cob pipes and tobacco, so we had a very enjoyable social time. Then we crawled out through the hole and went home to bed, our hands looking as if they had been chewed. Tom was in high spirits. He said it was the best fun he had ever had, and the most intellectual. He added that if we could keep doing it for the rest of our lives, we would leave Jim for our children to take over, because he believed Jim would like it more and more as he got used to it. He thought it could last up to eighty years and would be the best record ever. He also said it would make all of us who were involved famous.

In the morning we went to the woodpile and cut the brass candlestick into small pieces. Tom put those pieces and a pewter spoon into his pocket. Then we went to the black cabins. While I was getting Nat’s notice off, Tom put a piece of the candlestick into the middle of a corn‑pone that was in Jim’s pan. We went with Nat to see how it would work, and it worked well. When Jim bit into it, it knocked all his teeth out. Nothing ever worked better than that. Tom agreed. Jim pretended it was just a piece of rock that sometimes gets into bread, but after that he never ate anything without first stabbing it with his fork in three or four places.

While we were standing in the dim light, a couple of dogs came out from under Jim’s bed. They kept coming until there were eleven of them, and there was hardly any room to breathe. Damn, we had forgotten to close the lean‑to door!  

The Black man, Nat, shouted “Witches!” once, then fell to the floor among the dogs and started groaning like he was dying. Tom swung the door open and threw a piece of Jim’s meat out. The dogs grabbed it. In two seconds Tom was out, back again, and shut the door. I knew he had also fixed the other door.  

Then he went over to Nat, soothing him, patting him, and asked if he was imagining something again. Nat sat up, blinked, and said:  

“Mars Sid, you’ll call me a fool, but if I didn’t think I saw a million dogs, devils, something, I wish I could die right here in these woods. I almost did. Mars Sid, I felt them—they were all over me. God help me, I just wish I could get my hands on one of those witches, just once—just once—it’s all I ever wanted. But mostly I wish they’d leave me alone, I do.”  

Tom replied:  

“Well, here’s what I think. Why do they come here just at this runaway’s breakfast time? Because they’re hungry; that’s the reason. Make them a witch pie; that’s what you should do.”

“But my love, Mars Sid, how am I supposed to make a witch pie? I don’t know how to make it. I’ve never heard of anything like it before.”

“Then I’ll have to make it myself.”

“Will you do it, honey? Will you? I’ll clean the ground under your foot, I promise!”

“All right, I’ll do it because it’s you, and you’ve been good to us and showed us the runaway black man. But you have to be very careful. When we come around, turn your back. Whatever we put in the pan, don’t let anyone see it. And don’t look when Jim unloads the pan—something might happen, I don’t know what. Most importantly, don’t touch the witch things.”

“Hannah, Mars Sid? What are you talking about? I wouldn’t put my finger on them, not even for a huge amount of money.”
