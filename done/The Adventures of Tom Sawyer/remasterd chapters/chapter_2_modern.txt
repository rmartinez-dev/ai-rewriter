Saturday morning arrived, and everything in summer was bright, fresh, and full of life. Every heart sang, and when someone was young the song came out of their mouth. Every face was cheerful and every step was lively. The locust trees were blooming, and the scent of the flowers filled the air. Cardiff Hill, beyond and above the village, was covered in green plants. It was just far enough away to look like a pleasant, dreamy, peaceful place.

Tom showed up on the sidewalk with a bucket of whitewash and a long brush. He looked at the fence, and all his happiness left him. A deep sadness settled over him. The fence was thirty yards long and nine feet high. Life felt empty to him, and being alive seemed like a burden. He sighed, dipped his brush, and painted the top plank. He did it again and again, comparing the tiny white stripe he made to the huge stretch of unfixed fence. Discouraged, he sat on a tree stump. Jim came out of the gate, skipping with a tin pail, singing “Buffalo Gals.” Getting water from the town pump had always seemed like hateful work to Tom, but now it didn’t bother him as much. He remembered that many children—white, mixed‑race, and black—always gathered at the pump, waiting their turn, resting, trading toys, arguing, fighting, and playing. He also remembered that, although the pump was only about 150 yards away, Jim never returned with a bucket of water in less than an hour, and someone usually had to go after him. Tom said, “Hey Jim, I’ll get the water if you whitewash some.” Jim shook his head and said:

“Can’t, Tom. The old lady told me I have to go get this water and stop fooling around with anyone. She said she expects Tom to ask me to whitewash, so she told me to go along and mind my own business—she promised she’d take care of the whitewashing herself.

‘Never mind what she said, Jim. That’s how she always talks. Give me the bucket—I’ll be gone only a minute. She’ll never know.’

‘I won’t, Tom. The old lady would take the hat off my head. She really would.

She never hurts anyone—she just taps them on the head with her thimble. Who cares about that? She talks badly, but words don’t hurt. It doesn’t matter if she doesn’t cry. Jim, I’ll give you something wonderful—a white lane!’

Jim started to hesitate.

‘White lane, Jim! And it’s a great one.’

‘Wow! That’s a wonderful gift, I tell you! But Tom, I’m scared of the old lady—’

‘And besides, if you want I’ll show you my sore toe.’

Jim was only human—this attraction was too much for him. He put down his bucket, took the white lane, and leaned over the toe with interest while the bandage was being unwound. In the next moment he was rushing down the street with his bucket and a tingling behind him, Tom was whitewashing with energy, and Aunt Polly was leaving the field with a slipper in her hand and a triumphant look in her eye.

Tom’s energy soon ran out. He started thinking about the fun he had planned for the day, and his worries grew. The other boys would soon be running around on all kinds of exciting adventures, and they would tease him for having to work. Just the thought of that made him feel like he was on fire. He looked at the money he had—some toys, marbles, and junk. It was enough to trade for a little work, but not even enough to buy a half‑hour of pure freedom. So he put his small amount of money back in his pocket and gave up the idea of buying the boys’ attention. At that dark, hopeless moment, a great idea suddenly came to him. It was a big, wonderful inspiration.

He picked up his brush and calmly began to work. Soon Ben Rogers appeared—exactly the boy whose teasing Tom had been dreading. Ben moved with a hop‑skip‑and‑jump, showing that he was happy and full of excitement. He was eating an apple and shouting a long, musical “whoop,” followed by a deep “ding‑dong‑ding‑dong,” because he was pretending to be a steamboat. As he got closer, he slowed down, moved to the middle of the street, leaned far to the right, and turned slowly with exaggerated ceremony, because he was pretending to be the big Missouri River and imagined that his boat was in nine feet of water. He acted as both the boat and its captain, making the engine bells sound, so he imagined himself standing on his own deck, giving orders and carrying them out:

“Stop, sir! Ting‑a‑ling‑ling!” The boat barely moved, and he slowly turned toward the sidewalk.

“Ship back! Ting‑a‑ling‑ling!” He straightened his arms and held them rigid by his sides.

Put her back on the board! Ting‑a‑ling‑ling! Chow! Ch‑chow‑wow! Chow! His right hand kept moving in big circles—it looked like a forty‑foot wheel.

Put her back on the board! Ting‑a‑ling‑ling! Chow‑ch‑chow‑chow! His left hand also drew circles.

Stop the board! Ting‑a‑ling‑ling! Stop the board! Come forward on the board! Stop her! Turn the outer part slowly! Ting‑a‑ling‑ling! Chow‑ow‑ow! Get that headline out! Quickly now! Come out with your spring line—what are you doing there! Turn around that stump with its bend! Stand by that stage now—let her go! The engines are finished, sir! Ting‑a‑ling‑ling! SH’T! S’H’T! SH’T! (testing the gauge‑cocks).

Tom kept whitewashing and ignored the steamboat. Ben stared for a moment and then said, “Hi‑Yi! You’re on a stump, aren’t you?”

There was no answer. Tom looked at his last brushstroke like an artist, then gave the brush another gentle sweep and looked at the result, just as before. Ben moved up beside him. Tom’s mouth watered at the thought of an apple, but he stayed focused on his work. Ben said, “Hello, old chap, you’ve got to work, right?”

Tom turned suddenly and said, “Oh, it’s you, Ben! I didn’t see you.”

“Listen—I’m going swimming. Don’t you wish you could? Of course you’d rather work, wouldn’t you? Of course you would!”

Tom thought about the boy for a moment and asked, “What do you call work?”

“Well, isn’t that work?”

Tom went back to whitewashing and replied carelessly, “Maybe it is, maybe it isn’t. All I know is that it suits Tom Sawyer.”

“Oh, come on, you don’t really mean you like it, do you?”

The brush kept moving.

“Like it? Well, I don’t see why I couldn’t like it. Does a boy get to whitewash a fence every day?”

That changed his view. Ben stopped nibbling his apple.

Tom brushed delicately back and forth. He stepped back to see the result, added a little more here and there, then criticized the result again. Ben watched every move. He became more interested and more absorbed. Finally he said:

“Hey, Tom, let me whitewash a little.”

Tom thought about it and was about to agree, but then changed his mind:

“No, I don’t think that would work, Ben. Aunt Polly is very particular about this fence—right here on the street, you know. If it were the back fence I wouldn’t mind, and she wouldn’t either. Yes, she’s very particular about this fence; it has to be done very carefully. I doubt there’s even one boy in a thousand, maybe two thousand, who could do it the way it needs to be done.”

“No, is that so? Come on, let me just try a little. I’ll only do a bit—I’d let you, if you were me, Tom.”

“Ben, I’d love to, honestly, but Aunt Polly—well, Jim wanted to do it, but she wouldn’t let him; Sid wanted to do it, and she wouldn’t let Sid. See how I’m stuck? If you take on this fence and something goes wrong—”

“Oh, I’ll be careful. Let me try. I’ll give you the core of my apple.”

“Here—no, Ben, don’t. I’m afraid—”

“I’ll give you all of it!”

Tom put down his brush with a reluctant look on his face, but with eagerness in his heart. While the old steamer *Big Missouri* toiled and sweated in the sun, the retired artist sat on a barrel in the shade nearby. He dangled his legs, ate an apple, and thought about killing more innocent people.

There was no shortage of material; boys appeared now and then. They came to tease, but stayed to help whitewash. By the time Ben was exhausted, Tom had traded his next chance to Billy Fisher for a good kite. When he finished playing, Johnny Miller bought in for a dead rat and a string to swing it with—and the trading went on hour after hour.

By the middle of the afternoon, Tom had gone from being a poor, destitute boy in the morning to literally rolling in wealth. In addition to the items already mentioned, he now owned twelve marbles, part of a Jew’s harp, a piece of blue bottle glass to look through, a small cannon made from a spool, a key that wouldn’t open anything, a fragment of chalk, a glass stopper from a decanter, a tin soldier, a couple of tadpoles, six fire‑crackers, a one‑eyed kitten, a brass door knob, a dog collar (but no dog), the handle of a knife, four pieces of orange peel, and a dilapidated old window sash.

He had enjoyed a nice, idle time all the while, with plenty of company, and the fence now had three coats of whitewash on it! If he hadn’t run out of whitewash, he would have bankrupted every boy in the village.

Tom thought to himself that the world wasn’t so empty after all. He had discovered a great rule about human behavior without realizing it: to make a man or a boy want something, you only need to make it hard to get. If he were a great, wise philosopher like the author of this book, he would now understand that work is whatever a person is required to do, and play is whatever a person is not required to do. This would help him see why making artificial flowers or working on a treadmill is work, while bowling ten‑pins or climbing Mont Blanc is only fun. In England there are wealthy gentlemen who drive four‑horse passenger coaches twenty or thirty miles each day in the summer because the privilege costs them a lot of money; but if they were paid a wage for the service, it would become work and they would quit.

The boy thought for a while about the big change in his circumstances, then went back to the headquarters to report.
