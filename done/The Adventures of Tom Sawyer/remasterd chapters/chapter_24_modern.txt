Tom was once again a shining hero. He was liked by the old people and envied by the young. His name even appeared in print forever because the village newspaper praised him. Some people thought he would become President, as long as he avoided being hanged.  

As usual, the unpredictable world took Muff Potter in its arms and treated him as badly as it had before. That is simply how the world works, so it is not useful to criticize it.  

Tom’s days were full of glory and joy, but his nights were full of fear. Injun Joe appeared in all his dreams, looking threatening. Almost nothing could make the boy go out after dark.  

Poor Huck felt the same misery and terror. Tom had told the whole story to a lawyer the night before the trial, and Huck was very scared that his part in the matter might become known. However, Injun Joe’s escape meant Huck did not have to testify.  

Huck had gotten the lawyer to promise to keep it secret, but that did not help. Tom’s guilty conscience forced him to go to the lawyer’s house at night and force a terrible story out of someone who had sworn a strong oath of silence. This almost destroyed Huck’s trust in people.  

Every day Muff Potter’s thank‑you made Tom glad he had spoken, but each night he wished he had kept his mouth shut.

Half the time Tom was scared that Indian Joe would never be caught; the other half he was scared that he would be caught. He was sure he could not breathe easy again until that man was dead and he had seen the body.

Rewards were offered and the whole countryside was searched, but Indian Joe was not found. A famous detective from St. Louis arrived, investigated, shook his head, seemed wise, and achieved the kind of remarkable success detectives usually have. In other words, he “found a clue.” But you can’t hang a “clue” for murder, and after the detective left, Tom felt just as uneasy as before.

The slow days passed, and each one reduced his fear a little.
