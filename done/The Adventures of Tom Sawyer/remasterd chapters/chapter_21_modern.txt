Vacation was coming soon. The schoolmaster, who was always strict, became even stricter and more demanding. He wanted the school to look good on “Examination” day. His rod and ruler were hardly ever idle now—at least with the younger pupils. Only the biggest boys and the young women aged eighteen to twenty escaped the punishments. Mr. Dobbins’s punishments were very harsh too; although he wore a wig over a completely bald, shiny head, he was only middle‑aged and showed no sign of weakness in his muscles.

As the big day drew near, all his anger seemed to surface. He took a spiteful pleasure in correcting even the smallest mistakes. Because of this, the younger boys lived in fear and suffering during the day and plotted revenge at night. They missed no chance to cause trouble for the master. But he always stayed one step ahead. Every time they succeeded in a small act of revenge, his retaliation was so swift and overwhelming that the boys always ended up badly beaten.

Finally, they banded together and came up with a plan that promised a brilliant victory. They confided in the sign‑painter’s apprentice, told him the scheme, and asked for his help. He had his own reasons to be pleased, because the master boarded in his father’s house and had given the boy plenty of cause to hate him.

The master’s wife would be away at a country visit in a few days, leaving nothing to interfere with the plan. The master always prepared himself for important occasions by getting very drunk. The sign‑painter’s apprentice said that when the schoolmaster reached the right condition on Examination Evening, he would “handle the situation” while the master napped in his chair. Then he would wake the master at the right moment and hurriedly take him away to the school.

At the right moment the special event arrived. At eight o’clock in the evening the schoolhouse was brightly lit and decorated with wreaths and garlands of leaves and flowers. The headmaster sat on his large chair on a raised platform, with his blackboard behind him. He looked fairly relaxed. Three rows of benches on each side and six rows in front of him were filled with town officials and the pupils’ parents.

To his left, behind the rows of adults, was a large temporary platform where the students who would perform that evening were seated. There were rows of tiny boys in fresh but uncomfortable clothes, rows of awkward older boys, and many girls and young women in light summer dresses. They were very aware of their bare arms, their grandmothers’ old jewelry, the pink and blue ribbons, and the flowers in their hair. The rest of the hall was full of students who were not performing.

The performances began. A very young boy stood up and shyly said, “You’d hardly expect someone my age to speak on a stage,” and so on. He used stiff, exact movements that looked like a malfunctioning robot. He managed to finish, even though he was very nervous, and received a warm round of applause when he took a practiced bow and left the stage.

A shy girl whispered, “Mary had a little lamb,” and gave a sweet curtsy. She received her share of applause and sat down, blushing and happy.

Tom Sawyer stepped forward with arrogant confidence and launched into the famous “Give me liberty or give me death” speech. He spoke with fierce anger and wild gestures, but he broke down in the middle. A terrible stage fright seized him. His legs shook and he seemed about to choke. He did have the audience’s obvious sympathy, but the audience was silent, and that was even worse than their sympathy. The teacher frowned, and that sealed the disaster. Tom struggled for a while and then left, completely defeated. A weak attempt at applause started, but it fell silent quickly.

Next came “The Boy Stood on the Burning Deck,” then “The Assyrian Came Down,” and other dramatic pieces. After that were reading exercises and a spelling contest. The small Latin class recited with pride. The main event of the evening was now the original “compositions” by the young ladies. Each girl stepped up to the edge of the platform, cleared her throat, held up her manuscript tied with a neat ribbon, and began to read, trying hard to use proper “expression” and punctuation. The topics were the same ones that had been used on many similar occasions by their mothers, grandmothers, and probably all their female ancestors back to the Crusades. Titles included “Friendship,” “Memories of Other Days,” “Religion in History,” “Dream Land,” “The Advantages of Culture,” “Forms of Political Government Compared and Contrasted,” “Melancholy,” “Filial Love,” “Heart Longings,” and many others.

A common feature in these essays was a carefully tended sadness. Another was an extravagant flow of fancy language. A third was a habit of stuffing in prized words and phrases until they were worn out. A clear flaw that marked and marred them was the constant, tiresome sermon that appeared at the end of each piece.

No matter what the subject was, great effort was made to force it into a moral or religious idea that the mind could use for instruction. The obvious insincerity of these sermons was not enough to stop the practice in schools, and it is not enough today; it never will be enough while the world still exists, perhaps. There is no school in our country where young girls do not feel they must finish their essays with a sermon. Even the most careless and least religious girl writes the longest and most forcefully pious sermon. But enough of that. Simple truth is hard to swallow.

Let us return to the “Examination.” The first essay that was read was titled “Is this, then, Life?” Perhaps the reader can tolerate an excerpt from it:

In everyday life, the young mind looks forward to a festive event with happy excitement. Imagination paints bright pictures of joy. In her daydream, the fashionable young woman sees herself in the crowd, the centre of attention. She imagines herself in white clothes, dancing gracefully through the lively party. Her eyes shine, her steps are light, and she feels part of the happy gathering.

In these pleasant fantasies, time seems to fly. Finally, the moment arrives for her to enter the heavenly world she has dreamed about. Everything looks magical to her enchanted vision. Each new scene seems more beautiful than the last. After a while, however, she realizes that beneath the pretty surface there is emptiness. The praise that once pleased her now feels harsh. The ballroom loses its charm, and with poor health and a bitter heart, she turns away, convinced that earthly pleasures cannot satisfy the soul.

While reading, there were occasional feelings of satisfaction, with whispered exclamations such as “How sweet!”, “How eloquent!”, “So true!”, and when the piece ended with a somewhat troubling sermon, the applause was enthusiastic.

Then a thin, sad girl with a pale face from pills and indigestion stood up and read a short poem. Two stanzas are enough:

“A Missouri maiden’s farewell to Alabama…”

Alabama, goodbye! I love you very much.  
But for now I have to leave you.  
My heart feels sad thoughts about you, and hot memories fill my mind.  
I have walked through your flower‑filled woods,  
I have roamed and read near the Tallapoosa River,  
I have listened to the noisy floods of Tallassee,  
and I have admired the sunrise on the Coosa side.

I am not ashamed to have a full heart,  
and I do not blush when my eyes fill with tears.  
I am not leaving because the land is strange,  
and I do not send these sighs to strangers.  
This state was welcome and home to me.  
I am leaving its valleys, and its towers fade quickly from my view.  
My eyes, heart, and mind will become cold,  
when, dear Alabama, they turn cold toward you.

There were only a few people who knew what “tête” meant, but the poem was still very good.

Next, a dark‑skinned, black‑eyed, black‑haired young lady paused for a moment, made a sad face, and began to read in a steady, solemn voice:

A VISION

The night was dark and stormy. No star moved around the high throne, but the deep rumble of thunder kept shaking my ears. Terrible lightning flashed angrily through the cloudy sky, as if it mocked the power of the famous Franklin. Even the loud winds came out of their hidden places and howled, making the scene even wilder.

In that dark, bleak moment, my spirit sighed for human kindness, but instead,

“My dearest friend, my counselor, my comfort and guide—  
my joy in grief, my second happiness in joy,” came to my side.

She moved like one of those bright figures described in dreamy, sunny walks of Eden by young romantics—a queen of beauty dressed only in her own extraordinary loveliness. Her step was so soft it made no sound. If not for the magical feeling her gentle touch gave, like other quiet beauties, she would have slipped away unnoticed and unwanted. A strange sadness rested on her face, like cold tears on a December coat. She pointed to the opposing forces outside and asked me to think about the two beings shown.

This nightmare filled about ten pages of manuscript and ended with a sermon that destroyed all hope for anyone who was not a Presbyterian, so it won first prize. The work was judged the best piece of the evening. When the village mayor gave the prize to the author, he gave a warm speech. He said it was the most eloquent thing he had ever heard and that even Daniel Webster would be proud of it.

It is worth noting that the number of pieces that overused the word “beauteous” and called human experience “life’s page” was about average.

The teacher, very calm and almost cheerful, pushed his chair aside, turned his back to the class and started drawing a map of America on the blackboard for the geography lesson. His hand shook, and a suppressed giggle spread through the room. He knew what was happening and tried to fix it. He erased the lines and drew them again, but he only made the map look worse, and the giggling got louder.

He focused all his attention on the work, determined not to be distracted by the laughter. He felt every eye on him. He thought he was improving, but the giggling kept going and even grew louder. That was understandable. A garret above the classroom had a small opening right over his head. Through that opening a cat was lowered on a string, its hind legs hanging. A rag was tied around its head and its mouth was closed so it couldn’t meow. As the cat slowly came down it arched upward and clawed at the string, then swung down and clawed at the empty air.

The giggling rose higher. The cat was only six inches from the teacher’s bald head. It reached down, grabbed his wig with its desperate claws, held on, and was pulled back up into the garret in an instant, still holding the wig as a trophy. Light shone from the teacher’s bald scalp because the sign‑painter’s boy had painted it gold!

The meeting broke up. The boys got their revenge. Vacation had arrived.

Without changing a passage from a book called *Prose and Poetry, by a Western Lady*, they follow the schoolgirl pattern exactly. That makes them much more successful than any simple imitation could be.
