“Tom!”

No answer.

“Tom!”

No answer.

“Where has that boy gone? Tom!”

No answer.

The old lady lowered her glasses and looked around the room. Then she raised them and looked out. She rarely used them to see something as small as a boy; they were her fancy pair, the pride of her heart, made for style, not for practical use—she could have seen as well through a pair of stove lids. She looked confused for a moment, then said, not angrily but loud enough for the furniture to hear:

“Well, I swear, if I catch you I’ll—”

She didn’t finish, because she was already bending down, sweeping under the bed with a broom, needing a breath between strokes. She only found the cat.

“I never saw that boy’s heartbeat!”

She went to the open door, stood in it, and looked out among the tomato vines and wild weeds that made up the garden. No Tom. So she raised her voice to reach far and shouted:

“You... Tom!”

There was a slight noise behind her and she turned just in time to grab a little boy by his shirt and stop him from running away.

“There! I should have thought about that closet. What have you been doing in there?”

“Nothing.”

“Nothing! Look at your hands. Look at your mouth. What is that sticky stuff?”

“I don’t know, aunt.”

“Well, I know. It’s jam—that’s what it is. I’ve told you forty times, if you don’t leave that jam alone I’ll give you a beating. Hand me that switch.”

The switch hovered in the air—the danger was serious.

“Whoa! Look behind you, aunt!”

The old lady spun around and quickly pulled her skirts away from danger. The boy ran away at once, scrambled up the tall wooden fence, and vanished over the top.

Aunt Polly was surprised for a moment, then she gave a gentle laugh.

Do I have to keep learning this lesson? He has played enough tricks on me for me to be careful now. But old fools are the biggest fools. You can’t teach an old dog new tricks, as the saying goes. Yet he never repeats the same trick two days in a row, and how can anyone know what he’ll do next? He seems to know exactly how long he can tease me before I lose my temper. He also knows that if he can make me laugh for a minute, everything goes back to normal and I can’t punish him. I am not doing my duty to that boy, and I know that is the truth. The Good Book says, “Spare the rod and spoil the child.” I am taking on sin and suffering for both of us, I realize. He is full of devilish ways, but he is also my dead sister’s son, a poor child, and I cannot bring myself to lash him. Every time I let him go, my conscience hurts; every time I hit him, my old heart breaks. Life, as the Scripture says, is short and full of trouble, and I think that is true. He will skip school this evening, and I will have to make him work tomorrow as a punishment. It is very hard to make him work on Saturdays when all the other boys are on holiday, but he hates work more than anything else. I must do my duty for him, or I will ruin the child.

Tom skipped school and had a great time. He got home just in time to help Jim, the little black boy, gather wood for the next day and split kindling before dinner. At least he arrived early enough to tell Jim about his adventures while Jim did most of the work.

Tom’s younger brother – actually his half‑brother – Sid had already finished his part of the work, which was picking up chips. He was a quiet boy and didn’t like trouble or adventure.

While Tom was eating his supper and stealing sugar whenever he could, Aunt Polly asked him sly, deep questions because she wanted to trap him into saying something harmful. Like many simple‑hearted people, she liked to think she was good at secret diplomacy. She loved to see her obvious tricks as clever plans. She said:

“Tom, it was pretty warm at school, wasn’t it?”

“Yes, ma’am.”

“Really warm, wasn’t it?”

“Yes, ma’am.”

“Didn’t you want to go swimming, Tom?”

A little fear ran through Tom – a touch of uneasy suspicion. He looked at Aunt Polly’s face, but it gave him no clue. So he answered:

“No, not really.”

The old lady reached out and felt Tom’s shirt and said:

“But you’re not too warm now, are you?” She liked to think she had noticed that his shirt was dry without anyone else knowing that was what she was thinking about. Despite her, Tom understood what she was trying to do. So he tried to stay ahead of her next move:

“Some of us have water on our heads – mine is still damp. See?”

Aunt Polly was annoyed that she had missed an important clue. Then she had a new idea.

“Tom, you didn’t have to take apart the shirt collar I sewed just to pull it over your head, did you? Take off your jacket!”

Tom’s expression changed instantly. He opened his jacket and the collar was still sewn on tightly.

“Darn it! I thought you’d been skipping school and swimming. But I forgive you, Tom. I think you’re a bit like a rescued cat—better than you look this time.”

She felt partly sorry that her clever plan had failed, but also pleased that Tom finally behaved.

Sidney said, “Well, I didn’t think you used white thread for his collar, but it’s black.”

Tom replied, “I did sew it with white!”

Tom didn’t wait for more. As he left, he said, “Sid, I’ll get you back for that.”

In a quiet place Tom examined two large needles stuck in the lapels of his jacket, each with thread wrapped around it—one with white thread, the other with black. He thought, “She would never have noticed if it weren’t for Sid. Sometimes she uses white thread, sometimes black. I wish she would stick to one color—I can’t keep track. I’ll definitely give Sid a hard time for this. I’ll teach him a lesson!”

He wasn’t the perfect model boy in the village. He knew the model boy very well, and he disliked him.

Within two minutes, or even less, he had forgotten all his troubles. It wasn’t because his troubles had become any lighter or less bitter, but because a new, strong interest pushed them aside and drove them out of his mind for a while—just as people’s misfortunes are forgotten in the excitement of new projects. This new interest was a useful novelty in whistling that he had just learned from a Black man. He was eager to practice it without interruption. It involved a peculiar, bird‑like turn—a smooth warble made by touching the tongue briefly to the roof of the mouth while whistling. The reader probably remembers how to do it, if he was ever a boy. With diligence and attention he soon mastered it. He walked down the street with his mouth full of melody and his heart full of gratitude. He felt much like an astronomer who has discovered a new planet. In terms of pure, deep pleasure, the boy had the advantage, not the astronomer.

The summer evenings were long. It was not yet dark. Tom checked his whistle. A stranger stood in front of him – a boy a little taller than he was. A newcomer of any age or gender was an amazing sight in the poor, shabby village of St. Petersburg. This boy was well dressed, even for a weekday. That was simply stunning. His cap was delicate, his button‑up blue coat was new and smart, and his trousers were new too. He even had shoes on – and it was only Friday. He wore a necktie, a bright ribbon. He had a city‑like air that made Tom feel uneasy. The more Tom stared at the splendid boy, the more he turned up his nose at his own clothes, and his own outfit seemed poorer and poorer. Neither boy spoke. If one moved, the other moved as well, but only sideways, in a circle; they stayed face to face and eye to eye the whole time. Finally Tom said:

“I can beat you!”

“I’d like to see you try.”

“Well, I can.”

“No, you can’t.”

“Yes, I can.”

“No, you can’t.”

“I can.”

“You can’t.”

“Can!”

“Can’t!”

An uncomfortable pause. Then Tom asked:

“What’s your name?”

“It’s not your business, maybe.”

“Well, I’ll make it my business.”

“Why don’t you?”

“If you talk a lot, I will.”

“Much—much—much. There now.”

“Oh, you think you’re very smart, don’t you? I could beat you with one hand tied behind my back, if I wanted to.”

“Well, why don’t you do it? You say you can.”

“I will, if you mess with me.”

“Oh yes—I’ve seen whole families in the same trouble.”

“Smart‑aleck! You think you’re something now, don’t you? What a hat!”

“If you don’t like that hat, you can throw it away. I dare you to knock it off – anyone who accepts a dare will end up eating eggs.”

“You’re lying!”

“You’re another liar.”

“You’re a stubborn liar and you won’t fight.”

“Aw—just go away!”

“Listen—if you keep talking back to me, I’ll knock a rock off your head.”

“Oh, of course you will.”

“Well, I will.”

“Then why don’t you do it? Why do you keep saying you’ll do it? It’s because you’re scared.”

“I’m not scared.”

“You are.”

“I’m not.”

“You are.”

They stared at each other, moving around a bit. Soon they were shoulder to shoulder. Tom said:

“Get away from here!”

“You go away yourself!”

“I won’t.”

“I won’t either.”

They each planted a foot as a brace and pushed hard, glaring at each other with anger. Neither could get the upper hand. After they were hot and flushed, they stopped and watched carefully. Tom said:

“You’re a coward and a kid. I’ll tell my big brother about you, and he’ll beat you with his little finger. I’ll make him do it.”

“What do I care about your big brother? I have a brother who’s bigger than yours, and he can throw him over that fence too.”  
[Both brothers are imaginary.]

“That’s a lie.”

“Just because you say it doesn’t make it true.”

Tom drew a line in the dust with his big toe and said:

“I dare you to step over that, and I’ll beat you until you can’t stand. Anyone who accepts a dare will steal a sheep.”

The new boy stepped over right away and said, “You said you’d do it, now show me.”

“Don’t crowd me now; you’d better watch out.”

“Well, you said you’d do it—why aren’t you doing it?”

“By Jingo! For two cents I will do it.”

The new boy took two big copper coins from his pocket and held them out mockingly. Tom knocked the coins to the ground. In an instant both boys were rolling in the dirt, tangled together like cats. For about a minute they pulled each other’s hair and clothes, punched and scratched each other’s noses, and covered themselves with dust. Then the chaos became clearer. Through the dust Tom appeared, sitting on the new boy and hitting him with his fists. “Enough!” he shouted.

The boy only tried to get free. He was crying, mostly from anger.

“Enough!” the pounding continued.

Finally the stranger managed a muffled “Enough!” and Tom let him go, saying, “Now you’ll learn. Next time be careful who you mess with.”

The new boy ran off, brushing dust from his clothes, crying and sniffing. He kept looking back, shaking his head and threatening what he would do to Tom the next time he caught him. Tom answered with taunts and walked away proudly. As soon as Tom turned his back, the new boy picked up a stone, threw it, and hit Tom on the shoulders. Then he turned and ran away quickly. Tom chased him home and learned where he lived. He stood at the gate for a while, daring the boy to come out, but the boy only made faces through the window and did not come. Finally the boy’s mother appeared, called Tom a bad, vicious, rude child, and sent him away. Tom left, but he said he would lie for that boy.

He got home very late that night. When he carefully climbed in through the window, he found a trap set by his aunt. Seeing how dirty his clothes were, his aunt decided firmly to turn his Saturday holiday into a period of hard work.
