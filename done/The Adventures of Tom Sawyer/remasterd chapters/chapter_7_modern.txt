The harder Tom tried to focus on his book, the more his thoughts drifted away. Finally, with a sigh and a yawn, he gave up. It felt as if the noon break would never arrive. The air was completely still. Not a single breath moved. It was the sleepiest of days.

The soft murmuring of the twenty‑five studying scholars soothed the soul, like the hum of bees. In the bright sunshine, Cardiff Hill raised its gentle green slopes through a shimmering heat haze, tinged with distant purple. A few birds drifted lazily on the wind; no other living thing could be seen except some sleeping cows. Tom’s heart longed to be free, or at least to have something interesting to do to pass the dull time.

His hand slipped into his pocket and his face brightened with a grateful feeling that was like a prayer, even though he didn’t realize it. Then, secretly, he took out the percussion‑cap box. He released the tick and placed it on the long, flat desk. The creature probably glowed with gratitude that seemed like a prayer, too, but it was too early: when he started to move away gratefully, Tom nudged it aside with a pin and made it go in a new direction.

Tom’s close friend sat next to him, feeling the same pain Tom felt. He was suddenly very interested in the game. The friend was Joe Harper. The two boys were best friends during the week, but they became rivals on Saturdays. Joe took a pin from his lapel and helped move the prisoner. The game became more exciting quickly. Then Tom said they were getting in each other's way and not getting the full benefit of the tick. He placed Joe’s slate on the desk and drew a line down the middle, from top to bottom.

“Now,” he said, “as long as he stays on your side you can stir him up and I’ll leave him alone; but if you let him escape and come to my side, you must leave him alone while I try to keep him from crossing over.”

“Okay, go ahead; start him up.”

The tick escaped from Tom quickly and crossed the line. Joe chased it for a while, then it got away and crossed back again. They kept moving back and forth often.  

While one boy watched the tick very closely, the other watched just as intently. Both boys leaned over the slate, completely focused and unaware of anything else.  

Finally, it seemed that luck favored Joe. The tick tried many different moves and became as excited and nervous as the boys. Each time it seemed about to win, Tom’s fingers would start to move, but Joe would quickly stop him and keep control.  

Eventually Tom could not wait any longer. The temptation was too strong, so he reached out with his pin. Joe got angry right away and said:  

“Tom, you have to leave it alone.”  

“I just want to move it a little, Joe.”  

“No, that’s not fair; you have to leave it alone.”  

“I promise I won’t move it much.”  

“Leave it alone, I’m telling you.”  

“I won’t!”  

“You will—it's on my side of the line.”  

“Hey, Joe Harper, whose tick is that?”  

“I don’t care whose it is—it's on my side, and you must not touch it.”  

“Well, I’m going to touch it anyway. It's my tick and I’ll do whatever I want with it, or die!”

A huge knock hit Tom’s shoulder, and the same knock hit Joe’s. For about two minutes dust kept flying from their jackets, and the whole school watched. The boys were so focused they didn’t notice the quiet that had fallen over the school earlier, when the teacher slipped into the room and stood over them. He had watched most of what they were doing before he added his own twist.

When school ended at noon, Tom ran to Becky Thatcher and whispered in her ear: “Put on your hat and pretend you’re going home. When you reach the corner, slip away from the others, go down the lane and come back. I’ll go the other way and meet you the same way.”

One of them went with one group of students, the other with another group. Soon they met at the end of the lane and, when they got back to the school, it was empty. They sat together with a slate in front of them. Tom gave Becky the pencil and held her hand, guiding it, and together they drew another surprising picture. When they lost interest in drawing, they started to talk. Tom felt very happy. He said, “Do you like rats?” “No! I hate them!” “I like them too—alive ones. But I mean dead ones you can swing on a string around your head.” “No, I don’t like rats much anyway. I like chewing gum.” “Me too… I wish I had some now.”

“Do you? I have some. I’ll let you think about it for a while, but you have to give it back to me.”

Becky agreed, so they talked and dangled their legs over the bench, feeling very happy.

“Have you ever been to a circus?” said Tom.

“Yes, and my dad will take me again sometime if I’m good.”

“I’ve been to the circus three or four times—many times. Church isn’t everything compared to a circus. There’s always something happening at a circus. I want to be a clown in a circus when I grow up.”

“Oh, really? That would be nice. They’re so lovely, all spotted up.”

“Yes, that’s true. And they get a lot of money—about a dollar a day, Ben Rogers says. Say, Becky, have you ever been engaged?”

“What’s that?”

“Engaged to be married.”

“No.”

“Would you like to be?”

“I think so. I don’t know. What is it like?”

“It’s not like anything else. You just tell a boy you’ll never have anyone else but him, forever, and then you kiss and that’s it. Anyone can do that.”

“Kiss? What do you kiss for?”

“Well, that’s how people do it.”

“Everyone?”

“Yes, everyone who loves each other. Do you remember what I wrote on the slate?”

“Yes.”

“What was it?”

“I won’t tell you.”

“Should I tell you?”

“Yes—but another time.”

“No, now.”

“No, not now—tomorrow.”

“Oh no, now. Please, Becky—I’ll whisper it, I’ll whisper it very gently.”

Becky hesitated. Tom took her silence as permission, put his arm around her waist and whispered the story softly, his mouth close to her ear. Then he added:

“Now you whisper it to me—just the same.”

She resisted for a while and then said:

“You look away so you can’t see, and then I will. But you must never tell anyone—will you, Tom? Now you won’t, will you?”

“No, I really won’t. Now, Becky.”

He turned his face away. She shyly moved close until her breath brushed his curls and whispered, “I love you!”

She then ran around the desks and benches, with Tom chasing her, and finally hid in a corner, pulling her white apron over her face. Tom put his arms around her neck and pleaded:

“Now, Becky, it’s all over except for the kiss. Don’t be afraid of that—it’s nothing. Please, Becky.” He tugged at her apron and her hands.

After a while, she gave up, let her hands fall, and lifted her face, still flushed from the struggle. She let herself be kissed. Tom kissed her red lips and said:

“It’s all done, Becky. From now on, you’ll never love anyone else but me, and you’ll never marry anyone else but me—forever. Will you?”

“No, I’ll never love anyone but you, Tom, and I’ll never marry anyone but you. And you won’t ever marry anyone else but me, either.”

“Exactly. That’s part of it. And whenever we’re at school or going home, you’ll walk with me when no one is watching. You’ll choose me and I’ll choose you at parties, because that’s what you do when you’re engaged.”

“It’s so nice. I’ve never heard of that before.”

“Oh, it’s so fun! Me and Amy Lawrence—”

His big eyes showed Tom his mistake, and he stopped, confused.

“Oh, Tom! Then I’m not the first you’ve ever been engaged to!”

The child began to cry. Tom said, “Don’t cry, Becky. I don’t care for her any more.”

“Yes you do, Tom—you know you do,” she answered.

Tom tried to put his arm around her neck, but she pushed him away. She turned her face to the wall and kept crying. He tried again, speaking soothingly, but she rejected him once more. His pride rose, and he walked away outside. He stood there, restless and uneasy, glancing at the door now and then, hoping she would change her mind and come to find him. She did not. Then he began to feel uneasy and feared that he was wrong. It was a hard struggle for him to make a new move, but he gathered his courage and went back inside. She was still standing in the corner, sobbing, her face against the wall. Tom’s heart ached. He went to her and stood for a moment, not knowing exactly what to do. Then he said hesitantly:

“Becky, I—I don’t care for anyone but you.”

She did not answer, only sobbed.

“Becky,” he pleaded. “Becky, won’t you say something?”

More sobs.

Tom took his most precious object, a brass knob from the top of an iron, and showed it to her. He said, “Please, Becky, will you take it?”

She threw it on the floor. Then Tom marched out of the house, over the hills, far away, and did not return to school that day. Soon after, Becky began to suspect. She ran to the door; he was not there. She went to the playground; he was not there. Then she called:

“Tom! Come back, Tom!”

She listened carefully, but there was no answer. She was alone with only silence and loneliness. She sat down to cry again and to scold herself. At the same time the students started to gather again. She had to hide her grief, calm her broken heart, and bear the weight of a long, dull, painful afternoon. There was no one among the strangers around her to share her sorrows.
