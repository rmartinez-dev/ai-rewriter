There was something about Aunt Polly’s way of kissing Tom that lifted his spirits and made him feel lighthearted and happy again. He went to school and luckily ran into Becky Thatcher at the end of Meadow Lane. His mood always affected how he acted. Without any hesitation, he ran up to her and said:

“I was very mean today, Becky, and I’m really sorry. I’ll never do that again, ever—please make up with me, okay?”

Becky stopped and looked at him with scorn:

“I’d rather you stay away from me, Mr. Thomas Sawyer. I’ll never speak to you again.”

She shook her head and walked away. Tom was so shocked that he didn’t even have the presence of mind to reply, “Who cares, Miss Smarty?” before the moment passed, so he stayed silent. Nevertheless, he was very angry. He sulked in the schoolyard wishing she were a boy, imagining how he could beat her up if she were. Soon after, he saw her again and threw a sharp comment at her as he passed. She shot one back, and the angry fight was finished. In her heated anger, Becky could hardly wait for school to start; she was impatient to see Tom punished for the damaged spelling book. Any lingering thought she had about exposing Alfred Temple was completely erased by Tom’s harsh words.

The poor girl didn’t realize how quickly she was getting into trouble.  

Mr. Dobbins, the schoolmaster, was middle‑aged and still had an unfulfilled ambition. He had always wanted to be a doctor, but poverty forced him to remain a village schoolmaster. Every day, when there were no classes, he took a mysterious book out of his desk and read it. He kept the book locked. Every pupil at the school longed to see it, but they never got the chance. Each boy and girl had their own guess about what the book contained, but the guesses were all different and nobody could find out the truth.  

One day, Becky walked past the desk near the door and saw that the key was still in the lock. It was a perfect chance. She looked around, saw she was alone, and quickly took the book. The title page said “Professor Somebody’s Anatomy,” but it meant nothing to her, so she opened the book. She immediately saw a beautifully printed colour front page showing a naked human figure. At that moment a shadow fell across the page as Tom Sawyer entered the room and caught a glimpse of the picture. Becky grabbed the book to close it, but she accidentally tore the picture page in half. She shoved the book back into the desk, locked it, and ran out crying, embarrassed and angry.  

“Tom Sawyer, you are just as mean as you can be, to sneak up on a person and look at what they’re looking at.”

“How could I know you were looking at anything?”

“You should be ashamed of yourself, Tom Sawyer; you know you’re going to tell on me. Oh, what will I do? I’ll be punished, and I’ve never been punished at school.”

She stamped her foot and said, “Be as mean as you like! I know something is going to happen. Just wait and you’ll see! Hateful, hateful, hateful!” Then she ran out of the house, crying loudly.

Tom stood still, feeling confused by her outburst. He thought to himself, “Girls can be such strange fools. I’ve never been punished at school, so I don’t even know what that feels like. Girls are so thin‑skinned and scared. I’m not going to tell old Dobbins about her; I can get back at her in a nicer way. Anyway, old Dobbins will ask who tore his book. No one will answer. He always asks each student one after another, and when he gets to the right girl he will know it without anyone saying anything. A girl’s face always gives her away. She has no courage. She’ll get punished. This is a tight spot for Becky Thatcher because there’s no way out.” Tom thought about it a little longer, then added, “All right, she’ll want to see me in this trouble—let her deal with it!”

Tom joined the group of joking scholars outside. A few minutes later the master arrived and school began. Tom didn’t feel a strong interest in his lessons. Whenever he glanced at the girls’ side of the room, Becky’s face bothered him. He didn’t want to feel sorry for her, yet that was all he could do to help. He could not feel any genuine pride.

Soon the spelling‑book incident happened, and Tom’s mind was full of his own thoughts for a while afterward. Becky woke from her anxious lethargy and showed interest in what was going on. She didn’t expect Tom to try to get out of trouble by denying that he had spilled the ink on the book himself, but he did. The denial only seemed to make things worse for Tom.

Becky thought she would be glad about that and tried to convince herself she was, but she wasn’t sure. When the situation got worse, she felt an urge to stand up and tell on Alfred Temple. She forced herself to stay quiet, telling herself, “He’ll say I tore the picture. I won’t say a word, not even to save his life.”

Tom received his whipping and went back to his seat, not completely heart‑broken. He thought it possible that he had accidentally knocked the ink onto the spelling‑book during a playful bout. He had denied it because it was the proper thing to do and because it was the custom, and he stuck to his denial on principle.

An hour passed while the master sat nodding in his chair. The room felt sleepy with the quiet hum of study. Finally, Mr. Dobbins straightened up, yawned, unlocked his desk, and reached for his book. He hesitated, unsure whether to take it out or leave it. Most of the pupils glanced up lazily, but two of them watched his movements closely. Mr. Dobbins fidgeted with the book for a moment, then finally took it out and settled into his chair to read.

Tom glanced at Becky. She looked like a scared rabbit with a gun pointed at its head. In an instant he forgot his argument with her. He thought, “Quick—something must be done, and fast!” But the urgency made him freeze. Then an idea came to him. He imagined running, snatching the book, bursting through the door, and escaping. His resolve wavered for just a split second, and the chance was gone—the master opened the book. “If only I could have that moment back,” Tom thought. It was too late. There was no help for Becky now, he realized.

The master then turned his attention to the whole school. Everyone’s eyes dropped under his stare. His gaze was powerful enough to scare even innocent people. A heavy silence fell over the room; you could almost count to ten as the master gathered his anger. Finally, he spoke, “Who tore this book?”

No one made a sound. The silence was as complete as a pin dropping. The master looked from face to face, searching for a guilty expression.

“Benjamin Rogers, did you tear this book?”

Benjamin shook his head. Another pause.

“Joseph Harper, did you?”

Another denial. Tom’s nervousness grew stronger as the hearing dragged on. The master looked over the rows of boys, paused for a moment, then turned to the girls.

“Amy Lawrence?”

She shook her head.

“Gracie Miller?”

The same answer.

“Susan Harper, did you do this?”

Another shake of the head. The next girl was Becky Thatcher. Tom trembled from head to toe, feeling both excitement and helplessness.

“Rebecca Thatcher—” Tom glanced at her face. It was white with fear. “Did you tear—no, look at me.” She raised her hands in pleading. “Did you tear this book?”

A sudden thought struck Tom like lightning. He jumped to his feet and shouted, “I did it!”

The school stared, confused by his sudden claim. Tom stood for a moment, gathering his thoughts. When he stepped forward to accept his punishment, the surprise, gratitude, and admiration in Becky’s eyes seemed worth more than a hundred floggings.

Encouraged by his own dramatic act, he took, without complaint, the harshest whipping Mr. Dobbins had ever given. He also accepted, without protest, the extra cruelty of having to stay two hours after school was over. He knew someone would be waiting for him outside, and he didn’t consider the extra time a loss.

That night Tom went to bed planning revenge against Alfred Temple. Becky, ashamed and repentant, had told him everything, even admitting her own betrayal. But even his desire for revenge soon gave way to softer thoughts, and he finally fell asleep with Becky’s last words echoing in his mind:

“Tom, how could you be so noble!”
