At about half past ten the cracked bell of the small church began to ring, and soon people started to gather for the morning sermon.  

The Sunday‑school children spread around the building and sat in the pews with their parents so they could be watched.  

Aunt Polly arrived, and Tom, Sid and Mary sat with her. Tom was placed next to the aisle so he would be far from the open window and the tempting summer view outside.  

The crowd filed into the aisles: the old and poor postmaster, who had seen better days; the mayor and his wife—there was a mayor here, among other unnecessary things; the justice of the peace; the widow Douglas, a fair, smart, forty‑year‑old woman, generous and well‑off, who owned the only big house in town and was known for the most lavish parties St. Petersburg could claim; the old and respected Major and Mrs. Ward; the lawyer Riverson, a new notable from out of town; then the village beauty, followed by a group of ribbon‑wearing young heart‑breakers; after that all the young clerks in town entered together—they had been standing in the vestibule, watching the ladies, until the last girl got through them; and finally the Model Boy, Willie Mufferson, who took careful care of his mother as if she were made of glass.  

He always brought his mother to church and was the pride of all the women. The other boys disliked him because he was so good. He also seemed to be “thrown up to them” a lot. His white handkerchief stuck out of his back pocket, as it always did on Sundays—by accident. Tom had no handkerchief, and he thought the other boys were snobs.

The congregation was fully assembled, and the bell rang again to warn latecomers and those who were still outside. Then a solemn silence fell over the church. It was broken only by the giggling and whispering of the choir in the gallery. The choir always giggled and whispered during the service. Once there was a church choir that behaved properly, but I have forgotten where it was. It was many years ago, and I can barely remember anything about it, except that it was in a foreign country.  

The minister gave the hymn and read it with great enjoyment, in a special way that many people in this region admired. His voice started at a medium pitch and rose steadily until it reached a high point. At that moment he gave strong emphasis to the last word, then dropped the tone quickly, as if from a springboard:  

Shall I be carried to the skies, on flowery beds of ease,  
while others fight to win the prize, and sail through bloody seas?  

He was considered a wonderful reader. At church social gatherings he was always asked to read poetry. When he finished, the women would raise their hands, let them fall helplessly in their laps, close their eyes, and shake their heads, as if to say, “Words cannot express it; it is too beautiful, too beautiful for this mortal earth.”

After the hymn was sung, Reverend Sprague turned into a sort of bulletin board. He read out all the notices of meetings, clubs, and other events. He kept going until it seemed the list would never end. This strange habit is still common in America, even in cities, today when we have many newspapers. Often the less reason there is for an old custom, the harder it is to stop it.

Then the minister prayed. It was a generous prayer and it covered many specific requests. He asked for the church and its young children. He asked for the other churches in the village. He asked for the village itself, the county, the state and its officials. He asked for the United States, its churches, Congress, the President and the government officers. He asked for poor sailors caught in stormy seas. He asked for the millions of people suffering under European monarchies and Asian dictatorships. He asked for those who have the truth and good news but cannot see or hear it. He asked for the non‑believers on distant islands. He finished by asking that the words he was about to speak would be received kindly, like seed planted in good soil, and would bring a grateful harvest of good. Amen.

The sound of dresses rustling filled the air, and the people who were standing sat down.  

The boy whose story is told in this book did not enjoy the prayer. He only tolerated it, if even that. He was restless the whole time. He counted the words of the prayer in his mind without realizing it. He wasn't really listening, but he knew the old parts and the way the clergyman usually said them. When a small piece of new material was added, his ear picked it up and he felt upset. He thought the additions were unfair and dishonest.  

In the middle of the prayer, a fly landed on the back of the pew in front of him. It annoyed him as it calmly rubbed its legs together, lifted its head with its forelegs, and cleaned it so hard that its body seemed to separate, showing a thin neck. It scraped its wings with its back legs and smoothed them against its body as if they were a coat. It went through its whole grooming routine very calmly, as if it knew it was safe.  

It really was safe. Tom's hands wanted to catch the fly, but he didn't dare. He thought his soul would be ruined if he tried while the prayer was still happening. But when the prayer reached its last sentence, his hand moved forward. The moment “Amen” was said, the fly was caught. His aunt saw what he was doing and forced him to release it.

The minister read his sermon in a flat, monotonous voice. His argument was so dull that many people began to nod off. Yet the argument talked about endless fire and judgment, and it reduced the chosen few to a group so tiny it seemed hardly worth saving. Tom counted the pages of the sermon; after church he always knew how many pages there were, but he rarely remembered anything else about the talk. This time, however, he was interested for a while. The minister painted a vivid picture of the world’s peoples gathering at the end of the thousand years, when the lion and the lamb will lie down together and a little child will lead them. The boy missed the feeling, the lesson, the moral of the scene. He only thought about how noticeable the main character would be before all the watching nations. His face lit up with the thought, and he whispered to himself that he wished he could be that child, if it were a tame lion.

Now he began to suffer again as the dull argument continued. Then he remembered a treasure he owned and took it out. It was a large black beetle with strong jaws, which he called a “pinchbug.” He kept it in a small percussion‑cap box. The beetle’s first move was to bite his finger. It lurched forward, fell onto its back in the aisle, and his hurt finger slipped into the boy’s mouth. The beetle lay there, moving its helpless legs but unable to turn over.

Tom watched it and wanted it, but it stayed out of his reach. Other people who were not interested in the sermon also looked at the beetle and found some relief. Soon a stray poodle wandered in, tired and lazy on the warm summer day, bored of being locked up and longing for something new. He saw the beetle; his tail drooped then wagged. He sniffed it from a safe distance, walked around it, sniffed again, and grew bolder. He tried to bite it, missed, tried again, and kept trying. He began to enjoy the game, held the beetle between his paws, and kept experimenting. Eventually he grew tired, then indifferent, and absent‑minded. His head lowered, his chin touched the beetle, and the beetle grabbed it. There was a sharp yelp, the poodle’s head jerked, and the beetle fell a few yards away, landing on its back again.

The onlookers felt a gentle, quiet joy; some covered their faces with fans and handkerchiefs, and Tom was completely happy. The dog looked foolish and probably felt that way, but he also felt angry and wanted revenge. He went back to the beetle and attacked it again, jumping from all sides, snapping with his front paws close to the creature, trying to bite it, and shaking his head until his ears flapped. After a while he grew tired again, tried to amuse himself with a fly but got no relief, followed an ant close to the ground, and quickly got bored. He yawned, sighed, forgot the beetle, and sat on it.

Then a wild yelp of pain erupted and the poodle shot up the aisle. The yelps continued as he ran across the church in front of the altar, down the other aisle, past the doors, and up the final stretch. His anguish grew as he ran, until he became like a fuzzy comet shooting through the air. Finally, the frantic dog broke off his run and leapt into his master’s lap; the master threw him out the window. The cries faded quickly and disappeared in the distance.

By now the whole church was flushed and choking on held‑back laughter, and the sermon had come to a complete stop. The preacher tried to continue a moment later, but his words were weak and hesitant, and any chance of being impressive was gone. Even the most serious remarks were met with a hidden burst of inappropriate mirth from somewhere in the back rows, as if the minister had said something unintentionally funny. The entire congregation felt genuine relief when the ordeal finally ended and the blessing was pronounced.

Tom Sawyer went home feeling quite cheerful, thinking that a little variety made the church service more enjoyable. He had only one lingering thought: he liked the idea of the dog playing with his pinching bug, but he didn’t think it was right for him to take it away.
