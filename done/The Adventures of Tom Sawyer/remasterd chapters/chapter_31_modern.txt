Now let’s go back to Tom and Becky’s part of the picnic. They walked along the dim aisles with the rest of the group, stopping at the familiar wonders of the cave—places that had been given long, descriptive names like “The Drawing‑Room,” “The Cathedral,” “Aladdin’s Palace,” and so on. Soon they started a game of hide‑and‑seek and played it eagerly, until the activity began to feel a little tiring. Then they wandered down a winding passage, holding their candles up and reading the tangled list of names, dates, post‑office addresses, and mottos that had been painted on the rocky walls in candle smoke. They kept drifting and talking, and barely noticed that they had entered a part of the cave where the walls were plain. They wrote their own names on an overhanging shelf and kept moving on. After a short while they reached a spot where a small stream of water slipped over a ledge, carrying limestone sediment with it. Over many slow years the water had carved a lacy, ruffled “Niagara” out of shining, enduring stone. Tom squeezed his small body behind the waterfall to light it up for Becky’s enjoyment. He saw that the water hid a steep natural stairway enclosed by narrow walls, and instantly he felt the urge to be a discoverer.

Becky answered his call. They left a smoke mark so they could find their way back later, and then began their quest. They twisted and turned far down into the hidden depths of the cave. There they made another mark and split off to look for new things to tell the world above.

In one spot they found a large cavern. From its ceiling hung many bright stalactites as long and as thick as a man's leg. They walked around, surprised and admiring the sight. Then they left through one of the many passages that opened into the cavern.

The passage led them soon to a magical spring. Its basin was covered with glittering crystal frost. The spring lay in the middle of a cavern whose walls were held up by huge pillars. These pillars were formed when big stalactites and stalagmites grew together over centuries from constant dripping water.

Below the ceiling, thousands of bats were crowded together in big clusters. The lights scared the bats, and they swooped down in hundreds, squeaking and darting at the candles. Tom knew how dangerous this could be. He grabbed Becky’s hand and hurried her into the first corridor they saw. It was just in time, because a bat knocked out Becky’s light with its wing as she was leaving the cavern.

The bats chased the children for a long way, but the pair kept slipping into new passages until they finally escaped the danger. Tom soon found a underground lake that stretched far into the darkness until its shape disappeared in the shadows. He wanted to explore its edges, but decided it was better to sit down and rest first. For the first time, the deep silence of the place pressed on the children’s spirits. Becky said:

“Why, I didn’t notice, but it feels like it’s been a very long time since I heard any of the others.”

“Come to think of it, Becky, we are far below them, and I don’t know if we’re north, south, east, or whatever direction. We couldn’t hear them here.”

Becky became worried.

“Tom, how long have we been down here? We should go back.”

“Yes, I think we should. Maybe we really should.”

“Can you find the way, Tom? Everything looks twisted to me.”

“I think I can find it, but there’s the bats. If they put out our candles it will be a big problem. Let’s try another route so we don’t have to go through there.”

“Okay, but I hope we don’t get lost. That would be terrible!” The girl shivered at the thought of the scary possibilities.

They walked down a corridor in silence for a long time, looking at each new opening to see if anything looked familiar, but everything was strange. Every time Tom checked, Becky watched his face for a reassuring sign, and he said cheerfully:

“It’s all right. This isn’t the right one, but we’ll find it soon!”

He became less hopeful after each failure. Soon he began wandering off in random directions, hoping to find what they needed. He still said, “It’s all right,” but a heavy dread weighed on his heart, and his words sounded like, “All is lost!” Becky clung to him, terrified, and tried hard not to cry, but the tears came. Finally she said:

“Oh, Tom, forget the bats. Let’s go back the way we came. We keep getting worse.”

“Listen!” he said.

A deep silence fell over them. Even their breathing seemed loud in the quiet. Tom shouted. His voice echoed down the empty aisles and faded into a faint sound that resembled mocking laughter.

“Don’t do that again, Tom. It’s too horrible,” Becky replied.

“It is horrible, but I have to, Becky. They might hear us,” he shouted again.

The word “might” was even scarier than the ghostly laughter; it showed how hopeless they felt. The children stood still and listened, but nothing happened. Tom turned back immediately and hurried his steps. After a short while his uncertain manner revealed another problem to Becky—he couldn’t find the way back.

“Oh, Tom, you didn’t leave any marks!”

“Becky, I was such a fool! I never thought we would need to return. I can’t find the way. It’s all mixed up.”

“Tom, Tom, we’re lost! We’re lost! We’ll never get out of this terrible place! Oh, why did we ever leave the others?”

She fell to the ground and began to cry uncontrollably. Tom was terrified that she might die or lose her mind. He sat down beside her and put his arms around her. She rested her face on his chest, clung to him, and poured out her fears and regrets. The empty surroundings turned her cries into mockery. Tom begged her to find hope again, but she said she couldn’t. He started blaming himself for bringing her into this miserable situation, and that only made things worse. She promised to try to hope again and to follow wherever he led, if he would stop speaking like that. She told him he was not more to blame than she was.

So they kept moving, aimlessly, without any direction—just walking because they had to keep moving. For a short time hope seemed to return, not because there was any reason, but because hope naturally revives when it has not been worn out by age or repeated failure.

Later Tom took Becky’s candle and blew it out. The gesture said everything. No words were needed. Becky understood, and her hope faded again. She knew Tom had a whole candle and three or four pieces in his pockets, but he chose to use only a little.

Soon, fatigue started to take over. The children tried to stay focused because it felt terrible to think about sitting down when time was so precious. Moving in any direction was at least progress and might bring results. Sitting still felt like inviting death and cutting short their journey.

Finally, Becky’s weak legs couldn’t carry her any farther, so she sat down. Tom sat with her, and they talked about home, their friends, comfortable beds, and especially the light. Becky began to cry, and Tom tried to comfort her, but his words sounded worn out and almost sarcastic. Becky was so tired that she fell asleep. Tom felt relieved. He watched her tired face relax as she dreamed, and soon a smile appeared.

Her peaceful expression gave him a feeling of calm and healing, and his thoughts drifted to past times and happy memories. While he was lost in his thoughts, Becky woke up with a small laugh, but it quickly faded and a groan followed.

“Oh, how could I sleep! I wish I had never woken up! No! No, I don’t, Tom! Don’t look at me like that! I won’t say it again.”

“I’m glad you’ve slept, Becky; you’ll feel rested now, and we’ll find a way out.”

“We can try, Tom; but I saw such a beautiful land in my dream. I think we’re going there.”

“Maybe not, maybe not. Cheer up, Becky, and let’s keep trying.”

They got up and walked on, hand in hand, feeling hopeless. They tried to guess how long they had been in the cave. All they could tell was that it felt like days or weeks, but that couldn't be true because their candles were still burning. After a long, uncertain time, Tom said they should move quietly and listen for dripping water. They needed to find a spring. They found one soon, and Tom said it was time to rest again. Both were very tired, but Becky thought she could go a little farther. She was surprised when Tom disagreed. She didn't understand why. They sat down, and Tom stuck his candle to the wall with some clay. Soon they were both lost in thought; no one spoke for a while. Then Becky broke the silence:

“Tom, I’m so hungry!”

Tom took something out of his pocket.

“Do you remember this?” he asked.

Becky almost smiled.

“It’s our wedding cake, Tom.”

“Yes—I wish it were as big as a barrel, because it’s all we have.”

“I saved it from the picnic so we could dream about it, like grown‑ups do with wedding cake—but it’ll be our—”

She stopped mid‑sentence. Tom cut the cake and Becky ate a big piece, while Tom took small bites. They had plenty of cold water to drink after the meal. After a while Becky suggested they keep moving. Tom was silent for a moment, then said:

“Becky, can you handle it if I tell you something?”

Becky’s face turned pale, but she thought she could.

“Well, Becky, we have to stay here, where there is water to drink. That little piece is our last candle!”

Becky burst into tears and wailing. Tom tried to comfort her, but it didn’t help much. Finally Becky said:

“Tom!”

“What is it, Becky?”

“They’ll miss us and will hunt for us!”

“Yes, they will. I’m sure of it!”

“Maybe they’re already hunting for us, Tom.”

“Maybe they are. I hope they are.”

“When will they miss us, Tom?”

“When they get back to the boat, I think.”

“Tom, it might be dark then—would they notice we didn’t come?”

“I don’t know. But anyway, your mother will miss you as soon as they get home.”

A frightened look on Becky’s face made Tom realize he had made a mistake. Becky was not supposed to go home that night! The children fell silent and thoughtful. In a moment another outburst of grief from Becky showed Tom that the same thought was in her mind too—that the Sabbath morning might be half over before Mrs. Thatcher discovered that Becky was not at Mrs. Harper’s.

The children stared at their small candle and watched it melt slowly and mercilessly away; they saw the half‑inch wick standing alone at last; they saw the weak flame rise and fall, climb the thin column of smoke, linger at its top for a moment, and then—darkness fell completely!

They couldn't tell how long it took before Becky slowly realized she was crying in Tom's arms. All they knew was that, after what felt like a very long time, they both woke up from a deep sleep and their troubles began again. Tom guessed it might be Sunday now, maybe Monday. He tried to get Becky to talk, but her sadness was overwhelming and all her hope was gone. Tom said they must have been missed a long time ago, and that the search was still going on. He thought that if he shouted, someone might hear. He tried, but the distant echoes in the darkness sounded so terrible that he stopped.

Hours passed, and hunger started to bother the captives again. A piece of Tom’s half of the cake was left; they divided it and ate it. Still, they felt hungrier than before. The small bite of food only increased their appetite.

Later Tom whispered, “Shh! Did you hear that?”

Both held their breath and listened. A faint, faraway shout could be heard. Tom answered it immediately and, taking Becky by the hand, felt his way down the corridor toward the sound. Soon he listened again; the sound came again, a little closer this time.

“It’s them!” Tom said. “They’re coming! Come on, Becky—we’re okay now!”

The prisoners felt an almost overwhelming joy. They moved slowly, though, because hidden pits were common and they had to watch out for them. Soon they reached a pit and had to stop. It might have been three feet deep or a hundred feet deep; either way they could not get across it. Tom lay on his stomach and reached down as far as he could, but there was no bottom. They had to stay there and wait for the searchers to arrive. They listened and could hear that the distant shouting was getting farther away. After a minute or two the shouting stopped completely. The thought made their hearts sink with misery! Tom shouted until his voice was hoarse, but it didn’t help. He spoke hopefully to Becky, but a long, anxious wait passed without any more sounds.

The children felt their way back to the spring. The tired time seemed to drag on; they slept again, then woke up hungry and full of sorrow. Tom thought it must be Tuesday now.

Now he got an idea. There were some side passages nearby. It would be better to explore them than to waste time standing still. He took a kite string from his pocket, tied it to a hook, and Tom and Becky began, Tom leading, letting the line out as he walked forward.

After about twenty steps the corridor opened onto a ledge. Tom knelt and felt around the edge, reaching as far as his hands could. He tried to stretch a little farther to the right. Suddenly, less than twenty yards away, a hand holding a candle appeared from behind a rock.

Tom let out a loud shout, and instantly the hand was followed by the rest of the body—it was Injun Joe. Tom was frozen; he couldn’t move. He felt a huge relief when the “Spaniard” ran away and disappeared.

Tom wondered why Joe hadn’t recognized his voice and come over to kill him for testifying in court. He thought the echoes must have hidden his voice. He was sure of that.

His fear made his whole body feel weak. He told himself that if he could make it back to the spring, he would stay there and not risk meeting Injun Joe again. He was careful not to tell Becky what he had seen. He said he had only shouted “for luck.”

Hunger and misery become stronger than fear over time.  
Another boring wait at the spring and another long sleep caused changes.  
The children woke up tormented by a raging hunger.  

Tom thought it must be Wednesday, Thursday, maybe Friday or Saturday now, and that the search had been given up. He suggested exploring another passage. He felt ready to risk Injun Joe and any other dangers.  

But Becky was very weak. She had sunk into a bleak apathy and would not stir. She said she would stay where she was and die—it would not be long. She told Tom to take the kite‑line and explore if he wanted, but she begged him to come back often and talk to her. She made him promise that when the terrible moment came, he would stay by her side and hold her hand until it was over.  

Tom kissed her, feeling a choking sensation in his throat, and pretended to be confident that he would find the searchers or an exit from the cave. Then he took the kite‑line, went crawling down one of the passages on his hands and knees, hungry and sick with a sense of looming doom.
