The sun rose over a calm world and shone on the peaceful village like a blessing. After breakfast, Aunt Polly led the family in worship. It began with a prayer made from many solid Scriptural quotations, linked together with a little original wording. From the height of this she delivered a grim...

The Mosaic Law, as from Sinai.

Then Tom got ready and went to work to “get his verses.” Sid had learned his lesson a few days earlier. Tom focused all his energy on memorizing five verses. He chose a part of the Sermon on the Mount because he couldn’t find any shorter verses. After half an hour, Tom only had a vague idea of the passage. His mind was wandering through many thoughts, and his hands were busy with other distractions. Mary took his book to hear him recite, and he tried to find his way through the fog:

“Blessed are the—a—a—”

“Poor—”

“Yes—poor; blessed are the poor—a—a—”

“In spirit—”

“In spirit; blessed are the poor in spirit, for they—they—”

“Theirs—”

“For theirs. Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven. Blessed are they who mourn, for they—they—”

“Sh—”

“For they—a—”

“S, H, A—”

“For they S, H—Oh, I don’t know what it is!”

“Shall—!”

“Oh, shall! for they shall—for they shall—a—a—shall mourn—a—a—blessed are they that shall—they that—a—they that shall mourn, for they shall—a—shall what? Why don’t you tell me, Mary?—what do you want to be so mean for?”

“Oh, Tom, you poor thick‑headed thing, I’m not teasing you. I wouldn’t do that. You must go and learn it again. Don’t be discouraged, Tom; you’ll manage it—and if you do, I’ll give you something very nice. There, now, that’s a good boy.”

“All right! What is it, Mary? Tell me what it is.”

“Never mind, Tom. If I say it’s nice, it’s nice.”

“You’re right, Mary. All right, I’ll try again.”

He tried again, driven by curiosity and the hope of a reward, and he did it with such enthusiasm that he succeeded brilliantly. Mary gave him a brand‑new Barlow knife that cost twelve and a half cents. The joy he felt shook him to his core. The knife couldn’t cut anything, but it was a genuine Barlow, and that was impressive. No one knows how the Western boys ever thought such a weapon could be faked; it may always stay a mystery. Tom tried to scratch the cupboard with it and was about to start on the bureau when he was called away to get ready for Sunday‑school.

Mary handed him a tin bowl of water and a piece of soap. He went outside, placed the bowl on a small bench, dipped the soap in the water, set the soap down, rolled up his sleeves, and gently poured the water onto the floor. Then he went back into the kitchen and carefully wiped his face with the towel behind the door. Mary took the towel away and said, “Aren’t you ashamed, Tom? You shouldn’t be so careless. Water won’t hurt you.”

Tom felt a little uneasy. The basin had been refilled, and this time he stood over it for a moment, gathering courage. He took a deep breath and began. He walked into the kitchen right away, closed his eyes and felt for the towel with his hands. Clean water and soap were dripping from his face. When he took the towel off, he was not completely clean. The clean area stopped at his chin and jaw, like a mask. Below that line a dark patch of dry skin spread around his neck. Mary took him by the hand. When she was done, he looked like a man and a brother, without any difference in colour. His wet hair was brushed neatly, and its short curls were arranged into a tidy, symmetrical shape. He carefully smoothed the curls, working hard, and pressed his hair flat against his head because he thought curls were feminine, and this made him bitter. Then Mary brought out a suit of his clothes that had only been worn on Sundays for two years – they were simply called his “other clothes” – so we know how many clothes he owned. The girl “put him to rights” after he had dressed himself; she buttoned his neat jacket up to his chin, turned his large shirt collar down over his shoulders, dusted him off and placed his speckled straw hat on his head. He now looked much better but also uncomfortable. He felt as uncomfortable as he looked, because the whole outfit and the cleanliness felt restrictive. He hoped Mary would forget his shoes, but that hope was ruined; she coated them thoroughly with tallow, as was the custom, and brought them out. He lost his temper and said he was always forced to do everything he didn’t want to do. But Mary said, persuasively:

“Please, Tom—good boy.”

He slipped on his shoes, growling. Mary was ready quickly, and the three children went to Sunday school—a place Tom hated with all his heart, but Sid and Mary liked.

Sunday school ran from nine to half past ten, then the church service began. Two of the children always stayed for the sermon by choice, and the third always stayed too—for stronger reasons. The church’s high, hard‑backed pews could seat about three hundred people. The building was small and plain, with a pine‑board box on top as a steeple. At the door Tom stepped back and spoke to a classmate dressed for Sunday:

“Hey, Billy, got a yellow ticket?”

“Yes.”

“What will you take for her?”

“What will you give?”

“A piece of licorice and a fish‑hook.”

“Okay, let’s see them.”

Tom showed his work. It was satisfactory, and the property was transferred.

Then Tom exchanged a couple of white tickets for three red ones, and some small trifle for two blue tickets. He stopped other boys as they arrived and kept buying tickets of different colors for ten or fifteen minutes longer.

He entered the church with a crowd of clean‑shaven, noisy boys and girls. He went to his seat and started arguing with the first boy he could find. The teacher, a serious older man, intervened. He turned his back for a moment, and Tom pulled a boy’s hair on the next bench. While the boy was turning around, Tom stuck a pin in another boy just to hear him say “Ouch!” and he received another reprimand from the teacher.

Tom’s whole class was restless, noisy, and troublesome. When it was time to recite their lessons, none of them knew the verses perfectly; they needed prompts the whole time. Still, they persisted, and each earned a reward—small blue tickets, each bearing a passage from Scripture. One blue ticket paid for two verses of recitation. Ten blue tickets could be exchanged for a red ticket; ten red tickets for a yellow ticket; and ten yellow tickets could be turned in for a plainly bound Bible (worth about forty cents then).

How many of my readers would have the diligence and focus to memorize two thousand verses, even for a Doré Bible? Yet Mary obtained two Bibles this way after two years of patient work, and a boy of German descent earned four or five. He once recited three thousand verses without stopping, but the strain on his mind was too great, and from that day he was barely more than an idiot—a tragic loss for the school. On important occasions, before an audience, the superintendent (as Tom described it) always made this boy come forward and “show off.”

Only the older pupils managed to keep their tickets and continue the tedious work long enough to receive a Bible. Therefore, delivering one of these prizes was a rare and noteworthy event. The successful student shone so brightly that day that every scholar’s heart was sparked with fresh ambition, which often lasted a couple of weeks.

It is possible that Tom’s mind never truly craved one of those prizes, but undeniably his whole being longed for the glory and fame that came with it.

In time, the superintendent rose to stand in front of the pulpit. He held a closed hymn book in his hand, with his forefinger tucked between the pages, and called for attention.

When a Sunday‑school superintendent gives his usual short speech, a hymn book is as necessary as a sheet of music is for a singer who steps forward on a stage to perform a solo. It’s a mystery why this is required, because neither the hymn book nor the music sheet is ever mentioned by the speaker.

The superintendent was a slim man of thirty‑five, with a light‑brown goatee and short light‑brown hair. He wore a stiff standing‑collar that rose almost to his ears, with sharp points that curved forward at the corners of his mouth. This collar forced him to look straight ahead and to turn his whole body when he needed to look to the side. His chin rested on a wide, long cravat that was as big as a banknote and had fringe at the ends. His boot toes were turned sharply upward, like sleigh runners—a style of the day that young men achieved by sitting with their toes pressed against a wall for hours.

Mr. Walters had a very serious expression and was truly sincere and honest. He treated sacred things and places with great respect, keeping them separate from everyday matters. Without realizing it, his Sunday‑school voice took on a special tone that never appeared on regular weekdays. He began in this way:

“Now, children, I want you to sit up straight, look nice, and give me your full attention for a minute or two. There, that’s it. That’s how good boys and girls should sit. I see a little girl looking out the window—maybe she thinks I’m up in the trees talking to the birds. I feel very happy to see so many bright, clean faces here, learning to do what’s right and be good.”  

There’s no need to write down the rest of the speech. It always followed the same pattern, so we all know it.  

The last part of the speech was disturbed by fights and other games among some of the bad boys, and by fidgeting and whispering that spread everywhere, even reaching the isolated, unchanging rocks like Sid and Mary. Then all the noise stopped suddenly when Mr. Walters lowered his voice, and the speech ended with a quiet feeling of gratitude.

Most of the whispering started because something unusual happened. Some visitors arrived. First came lawyer Thatcher with a very weak old man. Then a nicely dressed, middle‑aged man with iron‑gray hair. He was followed by a respectable lady, probably his wife, who was holding a child. Tom had been nervous and irritated. He felt guilty too. He could not look at Amy Lawrence or stand her caring stare. When he saw the little child, his heart filled with sudden joy. Right after that he began to show off. He pushed other boys, pulled hair, made funny faces – in short, he tried every trick that might impress a girl and earn her applause. His excitement was mixed with one feeling: the memory of his embarrassment in that garden. That memory was quickly fading, washed away by the flood of happiness he now felt.

The visitors were given the place of honor. When Mr. Walters finished his speech, he introduced them to the school. The middle‑aged man turned out to be an impressive figure—no less important than the county judge. He was the most respected person the children had ever seen. They wondered what he was made of. Some of them wanted to hear him speak loudly; others were a little scared. He came from Constantinople, about twelve miles away. He had travelled and seen the world. His eyes had already looked at the county courthouse, which was said to have a tin roof. The children were silent and stared in awe. This was Judge Thatcher, the brother of their own lawyer. Jeff Thatcher stepped forward to meet the important man and to make the school jealous. He would have loved to hear whispers like:

“Look at him, Jim! He’s going up there. Look, he’s shaking hands with him! Wow, don’t you wish you were Jeff?”

Mr. Walters began to “show off.” He ran around giving orders, making judgments, and sending directions wherever he could find someone to address. The librarian also “showed off.” He hurried back and forth with his arms full of books, creating the noisy fuss that people in authority seem to enjoy. The young women teachers “showed off” too. They bent over the pupils, lifted warning fingers at the naughty boys, and gently patted the good ones. The young men teachers “showed off” with short scoldings and other small displays of authority and strict discipline. Most of the teachers, both men and women, had work to do in the library and by the pulpit. Their tasks often had to be repeated two or three times, which made them look annoyed. The little girls “showed off” in several ways, and the little boys “showed off” with such energy that the air was full of paper pads and quiet shuffling sounds. Above all, the great man sat with a proud, judicial smile, basking in his own importance. He was “showing off” as well.

Only one thing was missing to complete Mr. Walters’ joy: a chance to award a Bible prize and display a prodigy. A few pupils had a few yellow tickets, but none had enough. He had visited the top students to ask. Now he would have given anything to see that German boy return, sound of mind.

At that moment, when hope seemed lost, Tom Sawyer stepped forward. He held nine yellow tickets, nine red tickets, and ten blue tickets, and he asked for a Bible. It was like a sudden thunderclap on a clear day. Walters did not expect such a request for ten years, but there was no way to ignore it. The tickets were official and they were valid. Because of this, Tom was raised to the same level as the Judge and the other chosen ones, and the news was announced from headquarters.

It was the biggest surprise of the decade. The excitement was so strong that it lifted the new hero to the same height as the judge. The school now had two wonders instead of one. All the boys felt deep jealousy. The ones who felt the worst were those who realized too late that they had helped create this hated glory by trading their tickets to Tom for the money he earned from selling white‑washing rights. They began to hate themselves, seeing themselves as fools duped by a clever cheat, a sly snake in the grass.

The prize was given to Tom with as much enthusiasm as the superintendent could manage. But the excitement was not completely genuine. The superintendent’s gut feeling told him that something mysterious was hidden, something that could not stand in the light. It was absurd to think that this boy could store two thousand bundles of biblical knowledge in his place—just a dozen would already be too much for him.

Amy Lawrence felt proud and happy, and she tried to show it to Tom with her face, but he wouldn’t look. She wondered why. Then she felt a little uneasy. A vague suspicion appeared, disappeared, and appeared again. She watched him. A secretive glance told her everything. Then her heart broke. She felt jealous and angry. Tears came, and she hated everyone—especially Tom.  

Tom was introduced to the Judge, but his mouth felt dry, his breathing was shallow, and his heart trembled. He felt this because the Judge seemed very important, and also because the Judge was his mother’s father. He wanted to kneel and worship him, if it were dark. The Judge placed his hand on Tom’s head, called him a fine boy, and asked his name. Tom stammered, took a breath, and said, “Tom.”  

The Judge said, “No, not Tom—what’s your full name?”  

Tom answered, “Thomas.”  

The Judge replied, “Ah, that’s it. I thought there might be more. That’s fine. But you have another name, don’t you? Tell me, please.”  

“Tell the gentleman your other name, Thomas, and call him sir. Don’t forget your manners,” said Walters.  

“Thomas Sawyer—sir.”

“That's it! Good boy. Fine boy, brave little fellow. Two thousand verses is a lot—really, really a lot. You should never feel bad about the effort you spent learning them; knowledge is more valuable than anything else in the world. It is what makes men great and good. One day, Thomas, you will become a great and good man, and you will look back and say, ‘It’s all because of my Sunday‑school lessons, because of my teachers, because of the superintendent who encouraged me, looked after me, and gave me a beautiful Bible to keep. It’s all because of the right upbringing!’ That’s what you will say, Thomas. And you wouldn’t ask for any money for those two thousand verses—no, you wouldn’t. Now you can tell me and this lady some of what you have learned—yes, you will—because we are proud of boys who study. You must know the names of the twelve disciples. Will you tell us the names of the first two who were chosen?”

Tom tugged at his buttonhole and looked embarrassed. He blushed, and his eyes dropped. Mr. Walters felt his heart sink. He thought, it seems impossible that the boy can answer such a simple question—why did the judge ask him? Yet he felt he had to speak up:

“Answer the gentleman, Thomas—don’t be afraid.”

Tom still hesitated.

“Now I know you’ll tell me,” said the lady. “The names of the first two disciples were—”

“David and Goliath!”

Let's end the rest of the scene with kindness.
