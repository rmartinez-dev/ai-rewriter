At 9:30 that night, Tom and Sid went to bed, as usual. They said their prayers, and Sid fell asleep quickly. Tom stayed awake, waiting anxiously. When it felt like it might be almost morning, he heard the clock strike ten. That made him feel hopeless. He wanted to toss and move, as his nerves told him, but he was scared of waking Sid. So he stayed still and looked up at the darkness. Everything was very quiet. After a while, small barely‑hearable sounds began to appear. The clock’s ticking became noticeable. Old beams creaked mysteriously. The stairs made a faint squeak. It seemed like spirits were moving around. A soft, muffled snore came from Aunt Polly’s room. Then the annoying chirp of a cricket, which no one could find, started. Next, the eerie ticking of a death‑watch beetle in the wall at the head of the bed made Tom shiver—it felt like someone’s life was ending. Then a distant dog howled, and a softer howl answered from farther away. Tom was in agony. Finally he felt that time had stopped and eternity began; he started to doze despite himself. The clock struck eleven, but he didn’t hear it. Then, mixed with his half‑formed dreams, a sad wailing sound came. The sound of a nearby window opening woke him. A shout of “Scat! you devil!” and the crash of an empty bottle against the back of his aunt’s woodshed snapped him fully awake. A minute later he was dressed, out of the window, and crawling on all fours along the roof of the “ell”. He whispered “meow” cautiously once or twice as he moved, then jumped onto the woodshed roof and down to the ground. Huckleberry Finn was there with his dead cat. The boys ran off and disappeared in the darkness. After about half an hour they were walking through the tall grass of the graveyard.

It was an old‑fashioned western cemetery. It stood on a hill about one and a half miles from the village. A broken wooden fence surrounded it. The fence leaned inward in some places and outward in others; it was never upright. Tall grass and weeds covered the whole area. The old graves were sunken, and there were no tombstones. Round, worm‑eaten wooden boards lay over the graves, leaning for support that never came. Once the boards had been painted with “Sacred to the memory of …”, but now the writing could not be read, even in daylight.  

A soft wind moaned through the trees, and Tom was afraid it might be the spirits of the dead complaining about being disturbed. The boys spoke only quietly, because the time, the place, and the heavy silence weighed on them. They found the fresh new mound they were looking for and settled under the shade of three large elms that grew close together near the grave.  

They waited in silence for what felt like a long time. The only sound was the distant hoot of an owl breaking the stillness. Tom felt uneasy and wanted to speak. He whispered, “Hucky, do you think the dead like us being here?”  

Huckleberry whispered back, “I wish I knew. It’s terribly solemn, isn’t it?”  

“I think so,” Tom replied.  

They were silent for a while, thinking. Then Tom whispered, “Do you think Hoss Williams can hear us?”  

“Of course he can. At least his spirit can,” Hucky answered.  

Tom, after a pause:

“I wish I’d said Mr. Williams. But I never meant any harm. Everybody calls him Hoss.”

“You can’t be too careful about how you talk about these dead people, Tom.”

That made things quiet, and the conversation stopped again.

Then Tom grabbed his friend’s arm and whispered, “Shh!”

“What is it, Tom?” The two clung together, their hearts pounding.

“Shh! It’s happening again! Didn’t you hear it?”

“I—”

“There! Now you hear it.”

“Lord, Tom, they’re coming! They’re definitely coming. What will we do?”

“I don’t know. Do you think they’ll see us?”

“Oh, Tom, they can see in the dark, just like cats. I wish I hadn’t come.”

“Oh, don’t be afraid. I don’t think they’ll bother us. We aren’t doing any harm. If we stay completely still, maybe they won’t notice us at all.”

“I’ll try, Tom, but I’m shaking all over.”

“Listen!”

The boys pressed their heads together and hardly breathed. A muffled sound of voices drifted from the far end of the graveyard.

“Look! See there!” whispered Tom. “What is that?”

“It’s like devil‑fire. Oh, Tom, this is terrible.”

Vague shapes moved through the darkness, swinging an old tin lantern that dotted the ground with countless tiny lights. Then Huckleberry whispered, shivering:

“It’s the devils, for sure. Three of them! Lord, Tom, we’re doomed! Can you pray?”

“I’ll try, but don’t be scared. They won’t hurt us. ‘Now I lie down to sleep, I—’”

“Shh!”

“What is it, Huck?”

“They’re humans! At least one of them is. One of them sounds like old Muff Potter’s voice.”

“No… it can’t be, can it?”

“I think I recognize it. Don’t move a muscle. He’s not sharp enough to notice us. He’s drunk, just like always—blamed old Rip!”

All right, I'll stay still. Now they're stuck. They can't find it. Here they come again. Now they're hot. Cold again. Hot again. Red hot! They're pointed right this time. Hey, Huck, I recognize another one of those voices; it's Injun Joe.

That's it—the murdering half‑breed! I'd rather they were devils in sight. What are they up to?

The whisper faded completely, because the three men had reached the grave and stood only a few feet from the boys' hiding place.

"Here it is," said the third voice. He lifted the lantern and showed the face of young Doctor Robinson.

Potter and Injun Joe were carrying a handcart with a rope and a couple of shovels on it. They set the load down and started to open the grave. The doctor placed the lantern at the head of the grave, then sat down with his back against an elm tree. He was so close that the boys could have touched him.

"Hurry, men!" he whispered. "The moon could appear at any moment."

They growled a reply and kept digging. For a while there was only the scraping sound of spades moving earth and gravel. It was very monotonous. At last a spade hit the coffin with a dull thud, and a minute or two later the men lifted it onto the ground. They used their shovels to pry off the lid, took the body out and threw it roughly onto the ground. The moon came out from behind the clouds and showed the pale face. The burial mound was prepared, the corpse was placed on it, covered with a blanket, and tied down with rope. Potter took out a large spring‑knife, cut off the hanging end of the rope, and said:

“Now the cursed thing is ready, Doctor, and you’ll get another five, or she’ll stay here.”

“That’s what I hear!” said Indian Joe.

“Listen, what does this mean?” said the doctor. “You wanted your pay in advance, and I paid you.”

“Yes, and you did more than that,” said Indian Joe, moving toward the doctor, who was now standing. “Five years ago you drove me away from your father’s kitchen one night when I came asking for food, and you said I wasn’t welcome. I swore I’d get revenge even if it took a hundred years, and your father had me locked up as a vagrant. Did you think I’d forget? The Indian blood isn’t in me for nothing. And now I have you, and you have to settle, you know!”

He was threatening the doctor, his fist pressed to the doctor’s face.  
The doctor struck suddenly and threw the ruffian to the ground.  
Potter dropped his knife and shouted, “Now, don’t hurt my horse!” He then grabbed the doctor, and the two men wrestled fiercely, kicking the grass and tearing the earth with their heels.  

Injun Joe sprang to his feet, his eyes blazing. He snatched Potter’s knife and moved silently, low and crouching, circling the fighters, waiting for a chance.  

All at once the doctor broke free, grabbed the heavy headboard from Williams’s grave, and slammed it into Potter, knocking him down. At the same moment the half‑breed saw his opening and drove the knife straight into the young man’s chest. Potter staggered, fell partly on top of himself, and blood poured out. At the same time clouds hid the terrible scene, and the two frightened boys ran away into the darkness.  

Later, when the moon came out again, Injun Joe stood over the two bodies, watching them. The doctor whispered unintelligibly, took a long gasp, and fell still. The half‑breed muttered, “That score is settled—damn you.”  

He then stripped the body, placed the murder knife in Potter’s open right hand, and sat on the broken coffin. Three, four, five minutes passed. Then Potter began to stir and moan. His hand clutched the knife; he lifted it, looked at it, and let it drop with a shudder. He sat up, pushed the body away, and looked around, confused. His eyes met Joe’s.  

“Lord, how did this happen, Joe?” he said.

“It’s a dirty business,” Joe said without moving. “Why did you do it?”

“I never did it!”

“Listen! That kind of talk won’t help.”

Potter trembled and turned white.

“I thought I was sober. I didn’t plan to drink tonight. But it’s still in my mind—worse than when we started. I’m confused and can hardly remember anything. Tell me, Joe, honestly, old friend—did I do it? I swear on my soul and honor I never meant to, Joe. Tell me what happened, Joe. It’s terrible, and he was so young and promising.”

“You two were fighting, and he grabbed you by the headboard and you fell flat. Then you got up, reeling and staggering, grabbed a knife, and stabbed him just as he was about to hit you again. Now he lies dead.”

“I didn’t know what I was doing. I’d rather die right now if I’m guilty. It was all because of the whiskey and the excitement, I think. I’ve never used a weapon before, Joe. I’ve fought, but never with weapons. Everybody will say that. Joe, don’t tell anyone! Promise you won’t tell, Joe—that would make you a good man. I’ve always liked you, Joe, and stood up for you, too. Remember? You won’t tell, will you?”

The poor man dropped to his knees before the steady murderer and clasped his pleading hands.

“No, you’ve always been fair with me, Muff Potter, and I won’t betray you. There, that’s as fair as anyone can be.”

"Oh, Joe, you're an angel. I'll thank you for this for as long as I live," Potter said, beginning to sob.

"Come on, that's enough. This isn't the time for crying. Go over there and I'll go this way. Move quickly and don't leave any tracks behind you."

Potter started at a trot that soon turned into a run. The half‑breed watched him go, muttering, "If he's as stunned by the drink and as confused as he looks, he won't think about the knife until he's gone so far that he'll be too scared to come back to a place like this alone—what a coward!"

Two or three minutes later the murdered man, the wrapped body, the open coffin, and the empty grave were left unexamined by anyone but the moon. The silence returned, complete once again.
