At last the sleepy village came to life—the murder trial began in court. It instantly became the main topic of conversation. Tom could not escape it. Every mention of the murder made his heart shudder. His guilty conscience and fears made him think the remarks were being used to test him. He could not see how anyone could suspect him of knowing anything about the murder, but he still felt uncomfortable with all the gossip. It kept him cold and nervous all the time.

He took Huck to a quiet place to talk. He hoped it would be a relief to speak for a little while and share his distress with someone else. He also wanted to be sure that Huck had kept everything secret.

“Huck, have you ever told anyone about that?”

“About what?”

“You know what.”

“Oh—of course I haven’t.”

“Not a single word?”

“Not a single word, I swear. Why are you asking?”

“Well, I was scared.”

“Why, Tom Sawyer, we wouldn’t be alive two days if that got out. You know that.”

Tom felt a little better. After a pause:

“Huck, could anyone force you to talk?”

“Force me to talk? If I wanted that half‑breed devil to drown me, they could make me talk. It’s no different.”

“Well, that’s fine then. I think we’re safe as long as we stay quiet. But let’s swear again, just to be sure.”

“I agree.”

So they swore again with serious dread.

“What’s the talk about, Huck? I’ve heard a lot of it.”

“Talk? It’s just Muff Potter, Muff Potter, Muff Potter all the time. It makes me sweat constantly, so I want to hide somewhere.”

“That’s the way they treat me, too. I think he’s doomed. Don’t you sometimes feel sorry for him?”

“Most of the time—most of the time. He’s no good, but he’s never done anything to hurt anyone. He just fishes a little to get money to get drunk with, and he hangs around a lot; but we all do that—at least most of us—preachers and the like. He’s kind of good, though—he gave me half a fish once when there wasn’t enough for two, and many times he’s helped me when I was out of luck.”

“Well, he’s fixed kites for me, Huck, and tied hooks onto my line. I wish we could get him out of there.”

“We couldn’t get him out, Tom. And even if we did, it wouldn’t help; they’d catch him again.”

“Yes, they would. But I hate hearing them abuse him so badly when he never did that.”

“I feel the same, Tom. I hear them say he looks like the bloodiest villain in the country, and they wonder why he hasn’t been hanged already.”

“Yes, they say that all the time. I’ve heard them say that if he ever got out, they’d lynch him.”

“And they would.”

The boys talked for a long time, but it didn’t comfort them much. As evening fell, they lingered near the small, isolated jail, perhaps hoping something would happen to clear their troubles. Nothing happened; there seemed to be no angels or fairies interested in this unlucky prisoner.

The boys did what they often did— they went to the cell window and gave Potter some tobacco and matches. He was on the ground floor and there were no guards.

His gratitude for their gifts always made them feel guilty, and this time it hurt even more. They felt completely cowardly and treacherous when Potter said:

“You’ve been really good to me, boys—better than anyone else in this town. I never forget that. I often think to myself, ‘I used to fix all the boys’ kites, show them the best fishing spots, and befriend anyone I could. Now they all forget old Muff when I’m in trouble, but Tom doesn’t, and Huck doesn’t—they don’t forget me. And I don’t forget them.’  

Well, boys, I did a terrible thing—drunk and crazy at the time. That’s the only way I can explain it. Now I have to face the punishment, and that’s right. It’s the best thing, I think—at least I hope so.  

We won’t talk about that. I don’t want you to feel bad; you’ve been my friends. What I want to say is: never get drunk—then you won’t end up like me. Stay a little farther west—that’s it. It’s a great comfort to see friendly faces when you’re in a lot of trouble, and there are none here but yours. Good, friendly faces—good, friendly faces. Help each other and let me shake your hands. That’s all. Shake hands—yours will get through the bars, but mine is too big. Small, weak hands have helped Muff Potter a lot, and they would help him even more if they could.”

Tom went home feeling miserable, and his dreams that night were full of horror.  

The next two days he lingered near the courtroom, feeling he couldn’t resist going in, but he kept himself outside. Huck felt the same way. They deliberately avoided each other. Sometimes each of them walked away, but their grim curiosity always pulled them back. Tom listened when people left the courtroom, and he always heard bad news—the pressure was tightening around poor Potter. By the end of the second day, villagers were saying that Injun Joe’s testimony was solid and that there was no doubt about what the jury would decide.  

Tom stayed out late that night and entered his room through the window. He was extremely excited and did not fall asleep for hours. The next morning the whole village went to the courthouse because it was the big day. Men and women filled the crowded hall almost equally. After a long wait, the jury entered and took their seats. Soon after, Potter, looking pale, exhausted, frightened and hopeless, was brought in with chains on him and placed where everyone could see him. Injun Joe was also there, looking as calm as ever. After a short pause, the judge arrived and the sheriff announced that the court was open. Lawyers whispered and gathered their papers. These small delays created a feeling of anticipation that was both impressive and interesting.

A witness was called. He said he saw Muff Potter washing in the brook early on the morning the murder was discovered. He then slipped away quietly. After a few more questions, the prosecutor said, “Call the next witness.” The prisoner looked up for a moment, then looked down again when his own lawyer said, “I have no questions for him.”

The next witness proved that the knife was found near the body. The prosecutor again said, “Call the next witness.” Potter’s lawyer answered, “I have no questions for him.”

A third witness swore that he had often seen the knife in Potter’s possession. The prosecutor said, “Call the next witness.” Potter’s lawyer declined to question him. The audience began to look annoyed. Did the lawyer really intend to waste his client’s chance to defend himself?

Several other witnesses testified about Potter’s guilty behavior when he was brought to the scene of the murder. They were allowed to leave the stand without being cross‑examined.

Every detail of the disturbing events that happened in the graveyard that morning, which everyone present remembered well, was described by reliable witnesses, but none of them were cross‑examined by Potter’s lawyer. The confusion and dissatisfaction in the courtroom turned into murmurs, prompting a warning from the judge.

The prosecutor then said, “By the oaths of honest citizens, whose word we trust, we have proven this terrible crime beyond any doubt against the accused. We rest our case.”

Poor Potter let out a groin and covered his face with his hands. He swayed gently back and forth while a heavy silence filled the courtroom. Many men were moved, and many women showed their compassion with tears. The defense lawyer stood up and said:

“Your honor, at the start of this trial we said we would try to prove that our client acted while under a blind, reckless delirium caused by alcohol. We have changed our mind. We will not use that excuse.” He then turned to the clerk: “Call Thomas Sawyer!”

A look of astonishment spread across every face in the room, including Potter’s. All eyes turned to Tom as he stood and took his place on the stand. The boy looked wild and very scared. The oath was read.

“Thomas Sawyer, where were you on June 17th, around midnight?”

Tom glanced at Injun Joe’s hard face and his tongue failed him. The audience waited in silence, but no words came out. After a few moments, the boy gathered some strength and managed to speak loud enough for part of the room to hear:

“In the graveyard!”

“Speak a little louder, please. Don’t be afraid. You were—”

“In the graveyard.”

A contemptuous smile crossed Injun Joe’s face.

“Were you near Horse Williams’ grave?”

“Yes, sir.”

“Speak up—just a little louder. How close were you?”

“As close as I am to you.”

“Were you hiding?”

“Yes, I was hidden.”

“Where?”

“Behind the elms at the edge of the cemetery.”

Injun Joe flinched slightly.

“Was anyone with you?”

“Yes, sir. I went there with—”

“Hold on. Don’t name your companion now. We’ll bring him in later. Did you bring anything with you?”

Tom hesitated and looked confused.

“Speak up, boy—don’t be shy. Telling the truth is always right. What did you take with you?”

“Just a— a—dead cat.”

A brief laugh spread through the room, but the court shut it down.

“We’ll show the cat’s skeleton. Now, tell us everything that happened. Use your own words. Don’t leave anything out, and don’t be afraid.”

Tom started, unsure at first. As he got into his story, his words came more easily. Soon all other sounds stopped; only his voice was heard. Everyone’s eyes were on him. The listeners leaned forward, breathing quietly, hanging on his words. They ignored the passing of time, captivated by the terrifying details. His emotions built up until he reached the climax:

“…and when the doctor brought the board around and Muff Potter fell, Injun Joe lunged with the knife and—”

Crash! In an instant, the half‑breed rushed toward a window, pushed past everyone trying to stop him, and vanished.
