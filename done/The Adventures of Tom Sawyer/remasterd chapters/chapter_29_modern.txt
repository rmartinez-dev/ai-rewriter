The first thing Tom heard on Friday morning was happy news: Judge Thatcher’s family had returned to town the night before. For a moment, Indian Joe and the treasure were not important at all, and Becky became the main focus of Tom’s attention. He saw her and they spent a long, fun time playing “hispy” and “gully‑keeper” with many of their school friends. The day ended in a very satisfying way: Becky teased her mother into setting the next day for the long‑promised, long‑delayed picnic, and her mother agreed. The child’s joy was endless, and Tom felt the same. They sent out the invitations before sunset, and the village youngsters immediately began preparing with great excitement. Tom was so excited he stayed up very late. He hoped to hear Huck’s “maow” and to have his treasure ready to surprise Becky and the other picnickers the next day, but he was disappointed. No signal came that night.

Morning finally arrived, and by ten or eleven a lively group had gathered at Judge Thatcher’s house, ready to go. It was not usual for older people to attend the picnics. The children were considered safe under the care of a few eighteen‑year‑old girls and some twenty‑three‑year‑old boys. They rented an old steam ferry‑boat for the event, and soon the happy crowd marched down Main Street carrying baskets of food. Sid was sick and had to stay home; Mary stayed to look after him. The last thing Mrs. Thatcher said to Becky was:

"You won't be back until late. Maybe you should spend the night with some of the girls who live near the ferry landing, dear."

"Then I'll stay with Susy Harper, Mom."

"Alright. Take care, behave yourself, and don't cause any trouble."

Soon, while they were walking, Tom said to Becky:

"Listen, I'll tell you what we'll do. Instead of going to Joe Harper's, we'll go up the hill and stop at Widow Douglas's house. She always has ice cream—lots of it. She'll be really happy to see us."

"Oh, that sounds fun!"

Becky thought for a moment and said:

"But what will Mom say?"

"She'll never know."

She turned the idea over in her mind and said reluctantly, "I think it's wrong—but—"

"But come on! Your mother won't find out, so what's the problem? She only wants you to be safe, and I bet she would say it's okay if she had thought of it. I know she would!"

The Widow Douglas’s wonderful hospitality was very tempting. Together with Tom’s urging, it convinced everyone. So they decided not to tell anyone about the plan for the night.

Then Tom thought that Huck might show up that night and give the signal. The idea dampened his excitement a little. But he couldn’t bring himself to miss the fun at the Widow’s house. He told himself, “Why give it up? The signal didn’t come the night before, so it’s no more likely to come tonight.” The certain fun of the evening was more important than the uncertain treasure, and like a child he decided to follow his stronger desire and not think about the money box again that day.

Three miles outside the town, the ferry stopped at the entrance of a wooded hollow and tied up. The group rushed onto shore, and soon the forest valleys and rocky cliffs were filled with shouting and laughter. Everyone got hot and tired, and after a while the travelers wandered back to camp, their stomachs ready for food, and then the feast began.

After the meal they rested and talked in the cool shade of the spreading oak trees. Soon someone shouted, “Who’s ready for the cave?”

Everyone was there. They got bundles of candles and immediately ran up the hill. The cave entrance was on the hillside, shaped like the letter A. Its huge wooden door was open. Inside was a small, very cold chamber with solid limestone walls that felt damp with cold sweat. It felt romantic and mysterious to stand in the deep darkness and look out at the green valley shining in the sun. But the awe faded quickly and the playing started again. As soon as a candle was lit, everyone rushed at the person holding it. A struggle and a brave defence followed, but the candle was soon knocked over or blown out. Then laughter rang out and a new chase began. All things end. Soon the group moved down the steep main passage, the flickering lights barely showing the high rock walls that met about sixty feet above. This main passage was only eight or ten feet wide. Every few steps, narrower side passages branched off on each side. McDougal’s cave was a huge maze of crooked aisles that crossed each other, led out, and went nowhere. People said you could wander there for days and nights without ever finding the end. You could keep going down, down, and still down, into the earth, with one labyrinth after another and no end. No one really knew the cave. That was impossible. Most of the young men only knew part of it and rarely went beyond the part they knew. Tom Sawyer knew as much of the cave as anyone.

The procession walked down the main avenue for about three‑quarters of a mile. Then groups and couples slipped into side streets. They ran through the dark corridors and surprised each other when the corridors met again. For about half an hour the groups managed to avoid one another while staying inside the familiar area.  

Soon, one group after another came back, tired and laughing, to the mouth of the cave. They were covered from head to toe with melted fat, smeared with clay, and completely happy with the day’s success. Then they realized they had not noticed the time and that night was almost here. The bell had been ringing for half an hour. This kind of close‑to‑the‑day adventure felt romantic and was therefore satisfying. When the ferry, loaded with its noisy cargo, entered the stream, no one cared a bit about the wasted time, except the captain.

Huck was already on guard when the ferry’s lights blinked past the pier. He heard no sounds aboard, because the young passengers were quiet and still, as tired people often are. He wondered what kind of boat it was and why it didn’t stop at the pier. Then he pushed the thought aside and focused on his duty.

The night grew cloudy and dark. At ten o’clock the traffic stopped. The street lights began to flicker out, and the last pedestrians disappeared. The village went to sleep, leaving the lone watchman alone with the silence and the shadows. At eleven o’clock the tavern lights were turned off, and darkness covered everything. Huck waited a long, tiring time, but nothing happened. His hope faded. He asked himself whether it was worth it. Was it really worth it? Should he give up and go to bed?

A sound reached his ears. He snapped to attention. The alley door shut softly. He ran to the corner of the brick store. Two men walked by, and one had something under his arm. It must be the box! They were going to take the treasure. Calling Tom now would be useless—the men would get away with the box and never be found. Instead, Huck decided to follow them. He trusted the darkness to keep him hidden. Quietly, he slipped out and moved behind the men, barefoot and careful, staying just far enough behind to stay unseen.

They walked three blocks up River Street and then turned left onto a side street. They kept going straight until they reached the road that went up Cardiff Hill. They took that road. Halfway up the hill they passed the old Welshman’s house without stopping, and they kept climbing. “Good,” Huck thought, “they will bury it in the old quarry.” But they never stopped at the quarry. They kept going up to the top. They slipped into a narrow path between tall sumac bushes and were instantly hidden in the darkness. Huck moved closer and lowered his profile so they could not see him. He trotted for a while, then slowed down because he was moving too quickly. He stopped completely, listened, and heard only the beat of his own heart. An owl hooted from over the hill, an eerie sound. There were no footsteps. He wondered if everything was lost. He was about to run when a man cleared his throat only four feet away. Huck’s heart jumped to his throat, but he swallowed the feeling. He stood shaking as if many fevers had taken over him, feeling so weak that he thought he might fall. He knew where he was. He knew he was only five steps from the gate that led into Widow Douglas’s garden. “Very well,” he thought, “let them bury it there; it won’t be hard to find.”

A very low voice, Injun Joe’s, said, “Damn her, maybe she’s got company—there are lights, even though it’s late.”

“I can’t see any,” someone replied.

This was the voice of the stranger from the haunted house. A cold shiver went through Huck’s heart. This must be the revenge job! He wanted to run away. Then he remembered that Widow Douglas had been kind to him more than once, and maybe these men were planning to kill her. He wished he could warn her, but he knew he was too scared—they might catch him. He thought all this in the short pause between the stranger’s words and Injun Joe’s next line, which was—

“Because the bush is in your way. Now—this way—now you see, don’t you?”

“Yes. Well, there is company there, I guess. Better give it up.”

“Give it up, and I’ll leave this country forever! Give it up and maybe I’ll never get another chance. I’ll tell you again, as I have before, I don’t care about her loot—you can have it. But her husband was hard on me—many times he was hard on me—and he was also the justice of the peace who called me a vagrant. And that’s not all. It’s not even a tiny part of it! He had me whipped!—whipped in front of the jail, like a slave!—with the whole town watching! Whipped!—do you understand? He took advantage of me and died. But I’ll take my revenge on her.”

“Oh, don’t kill her! Don’t do that!”

“Kill? Who said anything about killing? I would kill him if he were here, but not her. When you want revenge on a woman you don’t kill her—nonsense! You attack her looks. You cut her nose—you cut her ears like a pig!”

“By God, that’s—”

“Keep your opinion to yourself! That’s safest for you. I’ll tie her to the bed. If she bleeds to death, is that my fault? I won’t cry if she does. My friend, you’ll help me with this—for my sake. That’s why you’re here; I might not be able to do it alone. If you hesitate, I’ll kill you. Do you understand? And if I have to kill you, I’ll kill her too, and then I think nobody will know who did this.”

“Well, if it has to be done, let’s do it. The sooner the better—I’m shaking.”

“Do it now? And have company there? Look, I’ll become suspicious of you, you know. No—we’ll wait until the lights are out. There’s no hurry.”

Huck felt a silence coming, something worse than all the talk of murder. He held his breath and stepped back carefully. He placed his foot firmly, balancing on one leg in a risky, almost falling way, first to one side then to the other. He took another step back, doing the same careful, risky moves, then another and another, and a twig snapped under his foot. He stopped breathing and listened. There was no sound—the silence was complete. He felt immense relief. He turned around between the sumac bushes, turning as carefully as a ship, then moved quickly but cautiously. When he reached the quarry he felt safe, so he lifted his light shoes and ran. He ran down, down until he reached the Welshman’s house. He knocked on the door, and soon the heads of the old man and his two strong sons appeared in the windows.

“What's the trouble? Who's knocking? What do you want?”

“Let me in—quickly! I’ll tell you everything.”

“Who are you?”

“Huckleberry Finn—let me in right now!”

“Huckleberry Finn, huh? That's not a name that opens many doors, I think. But let him in, boys, and let's see what the problem is.”

“Please don’t ever tell anyone I was the one who told you,” were Huck’s first words when he got inside. “Please don’t—I’d be killed, for sure—but the widow has sometimes been a good friend to me, and I want to speak. I will tell if you promise not to say it was me.”

“By George, he really has something to say, or he wouldn't act like that!” shouted the old man. “Tell us, and nobody here will ever betray you, lad.”

Three minutes later the old man and his sons, all armed, were up the hill, walking quietly on the sumac path with their weapons in hand. Huck did not go any farther. He hid behind a big oak and listened. There was a tense, uneasy silence, and then suddenly there was a burst of gunfire and a shout.

Huck didn’t wait for any details. He ran away, speeding down the hill as fast as his legs could carry him.
