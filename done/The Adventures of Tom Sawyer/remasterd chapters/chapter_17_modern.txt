There was no laughter in the little town that quiet Saturday afternoon. The Harpers and Aunt Polly’s family were grieving, with deep sadness and many tears. The village was unusually quiet, even though it was already a quiet place. The villagers went about their business with a distant look. They spoke little but often sighed. The Saturday holiday felt heavy for the children. They had no enthusiasm for their games and eventually stopped playing.

In the afternoon, Becky Thatcher walked sadly around the empty schoolyard, feeling very sad. She found nothing that could comfort her. She thought to herself,

“Ah, if only I still had that brass knob! But now I have nothing to remind me of him.” She tried not to cry.

Then she stopped and thought,

“It was right here. If I could do it again, I wouldn’t say that—I wouldn’t say it to anyone. But he’s gone now; I will never, ever see him again.”

The thought crushed her, and she walked away, tears rolling down her cheeks.  

A group of boys and girls, friends of Tom and Joe, came over. They stood by the thin fence and spoke in respectful tones about what Tom had done the last time they saw him and about a small comment Joe had made, which now seemed like a terrible warning. Each person pointed to the exact spot where the dead boys had stood. They said things like, “I was right there, just as I am now, as if I were him. I was that close. He smiled like this, and then something awful happened to me. I didn’t understand it then, but I see it now.”  

Then they argued about who had been the last person to see the dead boys while they were alive. Many claimed that title and gave evidence, sometimes altered. When it was finally decided who really had seen them last and spoken their final words, those people felt a special, almost holy importance. Everyone else stared at them and felt jealous.  

One unfortunate boy, who had nothing else to boast about, said with some pride, “Well, Tom Sawyer once beat me up.” His attempt at glory failed. Most of the boys could say the same thing, so the claim lost its value.  

The group lingered, still talking about the lost heroes in awed voices.

When the Sunday‑school hour ended, the next morning the bell began to toll instead of ringing the usual way. It was a very still Sabbath, and the mournful sound matched the quiet hush that lay over nature. The villagers started to gather. They lingered a moment in the lobby, whispering about the sad event. Inside the church there was no whispering—only the soft rustle of dresses as the women took their seats broke the silence. No one could remember the little church ever being so full before.

After a brief pause, Aunt Polly entered, followed by Sid, Mary, and the Harper family, all dressed in deep black. The whole congregation, including the old minister, rose reverently and stood until the mourners were seated in the front pew. Another quiet settled over the room, broken only by occasional muffled sobs. Then the minister spread his hands and prayed. A moving hymn was sung, and the words were: “I am the Resurrection and the Life.”

As the service went on, the minister described the virtues, the good deeds, and the lost promise of the boys. Every person there, thinking they recognized these scenes, felt a sting of remembering that they had always ignored them and only saw faults in the poor boys. The minister told many touching stories about the lives of the departed, showing their sweet and generous nature. Now the people could easily see how noble and beautiful those moments were, and they remembered with sorrow that at the time they had seemed like serious misdeeds, worthy of punishment. The congregation became more and more moved as the sad tale continued, until finally everyone broke down and joined the weeping mourners in a chorus of anguished sobs. The preacher himself could not hold back his feelings and began to cry at the pulpit.

There was a rustle in the gallery that nobody heard. A moment later the church door creaked. The minister lifted his tear‑stained eyes from his handkerchief and froze. One by one, other people looked up, and almost at the same time the whole congregation stood and stared as the three dead boys walked up the aisle—Tom in front, Joe behind him, and Huck, a bundle of drooping rags, shyly at the back. They had been hiding in the empty gallery, listening to their own funeral sermon.

Aunt Polly, Mary, and the Harpers rushed to hug their relatives, covering them with kisses and thanking God. Poor Huck felt embarrassed and uneasy. He didn’t know what to do or where to hide from all the staring eyes. He hesitated and began to slip away, but Tom grabbed him and said:

“​Aunt Polly, this isn’t fair. Someone has to be happy to see Huck.”

“​And they will be. I’m glad to see him, poor mother‑less boy!” Aunt Polly’s kind attention made Huck feel even more uncomfortable than before.

Suddenly the minister shouted at the top of his voice: “Praise God, from whom all blessings come—sing!—and put your hearts into it!”

And they sang. Old Hundred rose with a triumphant burst, shaking the rafters. While it shook the rafters, Tom Sawyer the Pirate looked at the jealous kids around him and felt in his heart that this was the proudest moment of his life.

When the congregation left, they said they would almost be willing to be made ridiculous again just to hear Old Hundred sung like that once more.

Tom received more hugs and kisses that day—depending on Aunt Polly’s changing moods—than he had gotten in a whole year; and he could hardly tell which showed the most thanks to God and love for himself.
