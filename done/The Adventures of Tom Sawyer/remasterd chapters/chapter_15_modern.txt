A few minutes later Tom was in the shallow water of the sandbar, walking toward the Illinois shore. Before the water reached his waist he was already halfway across; the current would not let him keep wading, so he started to swim the remaining hundred yards. He swam a little upstream, but the current still pulled him downstream faster than he expected. He finally reached the shore and walked along until he found a low spot and pulled himself out. He reached into his jacket pocket, found his piece of bark safe, and then ran through the woods along the shore, his clothes soaked. Shortly before ten o’clock he emerged into an open area opposite the village and saw the ferryboat standing in the shadow of the trees and the high bank. Everything was quiet under the blinking stars. He walked down the bank, watching carefully, slipped into the water, swam three or four strokes and climbed into the small boat that was attached to the ferry’s stern. He lay down under the seats and waited, breathing heavily.

Soon the cracked bell rang and a voice ordered “cast off.” A minute or two later the small boat’s bow rose high against the swell of the ferry, and the trip began. Tom felt happy with his success, because he knew it was the boat’s last trip for the night. After a long twelve or fifteen minutes the wheels stopped, and Tom jumped overboard and swam ashore in the dusk, landing fifty yards downstream, away from any possible stragglers.

He ran through the empty alleys and soon reached his aunt’s back fence. He climbed over it, went to the side wing, and looked into the sitting‑room window because a light was on inside.

Aunt Polly, Sid, Mary, and Joe Harper’s mother were sitting together, talking. They were near the bed, and the bed stood between them and the door. Tom went to the door and gently lifted the latch. He pressed the door a little and it opened a crack. He kept pushing carefully, flinching each time it creaked, until he thought he could slip through on his knees. He stuck his head inside and entered warily.

“What makes the candle flicker like that?” Aunt Polly asked. Tom hurried up. “Well, the door is open, I think.” “Of course it is. So many strange things happening now. Go shut it, Sid.”

Tom disappeared under the bed just in time. He lay very still, pretending to breathe calmly, then moved until he could almost touch his aunt’s foot.

“But as I was saying,” Aunt Polly continued, “he wasn’t bad, just mischievous. He was only a little reckless and foolish, you know. He wasn’t any more responsible than a young horse. He never meant any harm, and he was the kindest boy there ever was.” She began to cry.

“It was always like that with my Joe—always getting into mischief, but also as generous and kind as he could be. And may God bless me, I thought I chased him for taking that cream, forgetting that I had thrown it away myself because it was sour. I will never see him again in this world, never, never, never, poor abused boy!” Mrs. Harper sobbed as if her heart might break.

“I hope Tom is better wherever he is,” Sid said, “but if he had been better in some ways—”

“Sid!” Tom felt the old lady’s fierce stare, even though he couldn’t see it. “Don’t say a word against my Tom now that he’s gone! God will take care of him—don’t trouble yourself, sir! Oh, Mrs. Harper, I don’t know how to let him go! I don’t know how to let him go! He was such a comfort to me, even though he tormented my old heart a lot.”

“The Lord gives and the Lord takes away—blessed be the name of the Lord! But it’s so hard—oh, it’s so hard! Only last Saturday my Joe set off a firecracker right under my nose and I knocked him down. I had no idea then how soon… Oh, if I could do it again I would hug him and thank him for it.”

“Yes, I know exactly how you feel, Mrs. Harper. Just yesterday at noon, my Tom gave the cat a dose of painkiller, and I thought the animal would tear the house apart. God forgive me, I hit Tom’s head with my thimble, poor boy, poor dead boy. He is now free from all his troubles. The last words I ever heard him say were a complaint—”

The memory was too much for the old lady, and she broke down completely. Tom was sniffling now, more because he felt sorry for himself than for anyone else. He could hear Mary crying and sometimes hear a kind word about him. He began to think more highly of himself than ever before. Yet he was still moved by his aunt’s grief and wanted to jump out from under the bed and fill her with joy. The dramatic beauty of the idea also appealed to him, but he held back and stayed still.

He kept listening and, piece by piece, learned what had been said. At first people thought the boys had drowned while swimming. Then someone said the small raft was missing. Next a few boys said the missing lads had promised the village would hear something soon. The elders put these clues together and decided the boys had left on the raft and would arrive in the next town downstream soon. But around noon the raft was found stuck on the Missouri shore about five or six miles downstream, and hope died. If they were not drowned, hunger would have forced them back home by nightfall, or even earlier. People believed that looking for the bodies was useless because the drowning must have happened in the middle of the river. The boys could swim well, so they would have reached shore otherwise. It was Wednesday night. If the bodies were still missing by Sunday, all hope would be lost and the funerals would be arranged for that morning. Tom shivered.  

Mrs. Harper cried a sad good‑night and turned to leave. At the same moment the two grieving women ran into each other’s arms, gave a comforting cry, and then pulled apart. Aunt Polly was unusually gentle when she said good‑night to Sid and Mary. Sid sniffed, and Mary walked away crying hard.  

Aunt Polly knelt down and prayed for Tom. Her prayer was heartfelt, full of love, and spoken in a trembling old voice. Tom began to sob again long before she finished.

He had to stay still long after she went to bed because she kept sighing sadly, tossing restlessly, and turning over. Finally she quieted, only moaning a little in her sleep. The boy slipped out, rose slowly by the bedside, blocked the candlelight with his hand, and looked at her. He felt great pity for her. He took out a sheet of paper and placed it by the candle. Then an idea came to him and he paused to think. His face brightened with the solution; he quickly put the paper in his pocket. He leaned down, kissed her faded lips, and then left quietly, closing the door behind him.

He made his way back to the ferry landing and found no one there. Confident, he boarded the boat, knowing the house was empty except for a watchman who always slept soundly. He untied the small boat at the stern, climbed in, and began rowing carefully upstream. After a mile beyond the village, he turned across the river and worked hard. He reached the landing on the other side smoothly; this was something he knew how to do. He thought about taking the boat, arguing that it could be called a ship and therefore a legitimate target for a pirate. But he also knew people would search for it and might discover the truth. So he stepped ashore and walked into the woods.

He sat down and rested for a long time, trying hard not to fall asleep. Then he started carefully toward the end of his journey. The night was almost over. By daylight he reached the island bar. He rested again until the sun rose high and made the great river shine with golden light. Then he jumped into the stream. A short while later he stopped, dripping, at the camp entrance and heard Joe say:

“Tom is loyal, Huck, and he’ll come back. He won’t desert. He knows that would be a disgrace for a pirate, and Tom is too proud for that. He’s up to something. I wonder what it is?”

“Well, the treasure is ours, isn’t it?”  

“Almost, but not yet, Huck. The map says we’ll get it if he’s not back here for breakfast.”  

“That’s him!” Tom shouted dramatically as he stepped proudly into the camp.

A big breakfast of bacon and fish was soon served. While the boys ate, Tom told them (and exaggerated) about his adventures. They acted like proud, bragging heroes when the story ended. Then Tom slipped away to a shady corner to sleep until noon, while the other pirates prepared to fish and explore.
