Tom got home feeling gloomy, and his aunt’s first words showed she was not going to be kind:

“Tom, I could skin you alive!”

“Aunt, what did I do?”

“Well, you’ve done enough. I’m going to Sereny Harper, thinking I’m a softie, hoping she’ll believe all that nonsense about that dream. But then you found out from Joe that you were here and heard everything we talked about that night. Tom, I don’t know what will become of a boy who acts like that. It hurts to think you could let me go to Sereny Harper, make a fool of myself, and never say a word.”

This was a new side of the problem. The cleverness he had shown in the morning had seemed like a good joke to Tom before, very clever. Now it just looked mean and shabby. He hung his head and could not think of anything to say for a moment. Then he said:

“Aunt, I wish I hadn’t done it—but I didn’t think.”

“Oh, child, you never think. You only think about yourself. You could travel all the way here from Jackson’s Island at night just to laugh at our problems, and you could lie to me about a dream, but you never think about feeling sorry for us or helping us.”

“Aunt, I know now it was wrong, but I didn’t mean to be cruel. I swear. And besides, I didn’t come here to laugh at you that night.”

“What did you come here for, then?”

“I’m telling you not to worry about us because we weren’t drowned.”

“Tom, I would be the most grateful person in the world if I could believe you ever thought that, but you know you never did—and I know it, Tom.”

“Yes, I really did, auntie—I wish I could stay still if I hadn’t.”

“Oh, Tom, don’t lie—don’t do it. It only makes things a hundred times worse.”

“It’s not a lie, auntie; it’s the truth. I wanted to keep you from grieving—that’s why I came.”

“I’d give the whole world to believe that—it would hide a lot of sins, Tom. I would almost be glad you ran away and acted badly. But that’s not reasonable; why didn’t you tell me, child?”

“When you started talking about the funeral, I got filled with the idea of us arriving and hiding in the church, and I couldn’t bear to ruin it. So I put the bark back in my pocket and stayed quiet.”

“What bark?”

“The bark I had written to tell you we’d gone pirating. I wish now you had woken up when I kissed you—I really did, honestly.”

Her hard lines softened, and a sudden tenderness appeared in her eyes.

“Did you kiss me, Tom?”

“Yes, I did.”

“Are you sure you did, Tom?”

“Yes, I’m certain, auntie.”

“What did you kiss me for, Tom?”

“Because I loved you, and you were lying there moaning and I felt so sorry.”

The words sounded true. The old lady could not hide a tremor in her voice when she said:

“Kiss me again, Tom!—and then go to school, now, and don’t bother me any more.”

As soon as he left, she ran to a closet and took out the ruined jacket that Tom had taken. She stopped, holding it, and thought to herself:

“No, I can’t. Poor boy, I think he lied about it—but it’s a sweet, sweet lie, and it feels comforting. I hope the Lord—I know the Lord will forgive him, because he meant well when he told it. But I don’t want to find out it’s a lie. I won’t look.”

She put the jacket back and stood there thinking for a minute. She reached for the garment twice and then stopped. She tried again, and this time she reassured herself: “It’s a good lie—it’s a good lie—I won’t let it hurt me.” So she opened the jacket pocket. A moment later she was reading Tom’s piece of bark through falling tears and saying, “I could forgive the boy now, even if he had committed a million sins!”
