Monday morning made Tom Sawyer miserable. Monday mornings always did, because they started another week of slow suffering at school. He usually began the day wishing the holiday hadn’t been in between, because returning to school felt even more terrible.  

Tom lay there thinking. Suddenly he thought, “If I were sick, I could stay home from school.” It was only a vague idea. He checked his body. He found no illness, and he checked again. This time he thought he felt some stomach cramps, and he tried to make them worse, hoping they would turn into a real sickness. But the cramps soon grew weak and disappeared completely.  

He thought more. Then he noticed that one of his upper front teeth was loose. That seemed lucky. He was about to start complaining, which he called a “starter,” when he realized that if he used that excuse with his aunt, she would pull the tooth out, and that would hurt. So he decided to keep the tooth as a backup and look for another reason.  

After a while nothing came to mind, and then he remembered the doctor talking about an illness that could keep someone in bed for two or three weeks and might even cause a finger to be lost. Eagerly, the boy pulled his sore toe out from under the blanket and showed it. He didn’t know exactly what symptoms were needed, but it seemed worth a try, so he started groaning loudly with great effort.  

But Sid stayed asleep, unaware. Tom groaned even louder, pretending that his toe hurt more. Sid still did not react.

Tom was breathing heavily from his effort. He stopped to rest, then puffed out his chest and let out a series of loud groans.  

Sid kept snoring.  

Tom became annoyed. He shouted, “Sid, Sid!” and shook him. It worked, and Tom started groaning again. Sid yawned, stretched, then lifted himself onto his elbow with a snort and stared at Tom. Tom kept groaning. Sid said:  

“Tom! Answer me, Tom!” (No answer.) “Come on, Tom! TOM! What’s wrong with you?” He shook Tom and looked at his face anxiously.  

Tom moaned, “Don’t, Sid. Don’t mess with me.”  

“What’s the problem, Tom? I have to call auntie.”  

“No—never mind. It will be over soon, maybe. Don’t call anyone.”  

“But I have to! Don’t groan like that, Tom, it’s terrible. How long have you been like this?”  

“Hours. Ouch! Oh, don’t move so much, Sid, you’ll kill me.”  

“Tom, why didn’t you wake me earlier? Oh, Tom, stop! It makes my skin crawl hearing you. Tom, what’s happening?”  

“I forgive you for everything, Sid.” (groan) “Everything you’ve ever done to me. When I’m gone—”  

“Oh, Tom, you’re not dying, are you? Don’t, Tom—oh, don’t. Maybe—”  

“I forgive everyone, Sid.” (groan) “Tell them that, Sid. And Sid, give my window sash and my one‑eyed cat to that new girl in town, and tell her—”  

But Sid had taken his clothes and left. Tom was really suffering now, because his imagination was so vivid, and his groans sounded truly genuine.  

Sid ran downstairs and shouted:  

“Oh, Aunt Polly, come quick! Tom’s dying!”  

“Dying!”  

“Yes! Don’t wait—come fast!”  

“Rubbish! I don’t believe it!”

She ran upstairs anyway, with Sid and Mary right behind her.  
Her face turned pale and her lip trembled. When she reached the bedside she gasped:  

“Tom! Tom, what’s wrong with you?”  

“Oh, Auntie, I—”  

“What’s wrong, child? What’s the matter?”  

“My toe hurts badly!”  

The old lady sat down in a chair, laughed a little, then cried a little, then did both at the same time. She felt better and said:  

“Tom, you gave me such a scare. Now stop talking nonsense and get out of here.”  

The groans stopped and the toe stopped hurting. The boy felt a little foolish and said:  

“Aunt Polly, my toe seemed terrible, and the pain made me forget about my tooth.”  

“Your tooth, huh? What’s wrong with it?”  

“One of my teeth is loose and it hurts a lot.”  

“Don’t start moaning again. Open your mouth. Yes, the tooth is loose, but it won’t kill you. Mary, bring me a silk thread and a piece of fire from the kitchen.”  

Tom said:  

“Oh, please, Auntie, don’t pull it out. It doesn’t hurt any more. I wish I could stay away if it does. Please don’t, Auntie. I don’t want to miss school.”

“Oh, you don’t, do you? So all this fuss was because you wanted to stay home from school and go fishing? Tom, Tom, I love you so much, and you keep trying to break my old heart with your wild behavior.” By then the dental tools were ready. The old lady fastened one end of a silk thread to Tom’s tooth with a loop and tied the other end to the bedpost. She then grabbed a piece of fire and thrust it almost into the boy’s face. The tooth now hung from the bedpost.

Every trial has its reward. After breakfast, Tom walked to school. He was the envy of every boy he met because the gap in his upper teeth let him spit in a new, impressive way. A group of boys gathered to watch his display. One boy, who had cut his finger and had been the center of attention until then, suddenly lost his follower and his glory. His heart felt heavy, and he pretended he didn’t care, saying it wasn’t a big deal to spit like Tom Sawyer. Another boy shouted, “Sour grapes!” and walked away, leaving the first boy a broken hero.

Soon Tom met the village’s young outcast, Huckleberry Finn, the son of the town drunk. All the mothers in town strongly disliked and feared Huckleberry. They thought he was lazy, law‑breaking, rude, and mischievous. Yet their children looked up to him, enjoyed being near him, and wished they could be like him.  

Tom was like the other respectable boys. He envied Huckleberry’s bright, outsider status, even though he was told not to play with him. So whenever he had a chance, Tom joined Huckleberry anyway.  

Huckleberry always wore the discarded clothes of grown‑up men. The garments were torn and full of patches. His hat was a large wreck with a wide, broken brim. When he wore a coat, it reached almost to his ankles and had buttons placed low on the back. One suspenders held up his trousers, which hung loosely at the seat and had no pocket. The frayed legs of the trousers dragged in the dirt unless he rolled them up.

Huckleberry came and went whenever he wanted. He slept on doorsteps when the weather was fine and in empty barrels when it was wet. He didn’t have to go to school or church, or call anyone master or obey anyone. He could go fishing or swimming whenever and wherever he liked, and stay as long as he wanted. No one stopped him from fighting. He could stay up as late as he liked. He was always the first boy to go barefoot in spring and the last to put on shoes in fall. He never had to wash or put on clean clothes, and he could swear well. In short, he had everything that makes life good. Every troubled, restricted, respectable boy in St. Petersburg thought the same.

Tom greeted the free‑spirit:

“Hello, Huckleberry!”

“Hello yourself, see how you like that.”

“What do you have there?”

“A dead cat.”

“Let me see it, Huck. Wow, it’s really stiff. Where did you get it?”

“I bought it from a boy.”

“What did you give?”

“I gave a blue ticket and a bladder I got at the slaughterhouse.”

“Where did you get the blue ticket?”

“I bought it from Ben Rogers two weeks ago for a hoop‑stick.”

“So, what’s a dead cat good for, Huck?”

“Good for? It can cure warts.”

“No! Really? I know something better.”

“I bet you don’t. What is it?”

“It’s spunk‑water.”

“Spunk‑water! I wouldn’t give a damn for spunk‑water.”

“You wouldn’t, would you? Have you ever tried it?”

“No, I haven’t. But Bob Tanner did.”

“Who told you that?”

“He told Jeff Thatcher, Jeff told Johnny Baker, Johnny told Jim Hollis, Jim told Ben Rogers, Ben told a black boy, and the black boy told me. That’s it!”

“Well, what about it? They’ll all lie. At least everyone except the black man. I don’t know him. But I have never seen a black man who wouldn’t lie. Shucks! Now tell me how Bob Tanner did it, Huck.”

“Why, he stuck his hand in a rotten stump where rain‑water was collecting.”

“During the day?”

“Sure.”

“With his face close to the stump?”

“Yes, I think so.”

“Did he say anything?”

“I don’t think he did. I don’t know.”

“Aha! Trying to cure warts with spunk‑water that way is a foolish idea! That won’t work. You have to go alone into the middle of the woods, find a spunk‑water stump, and at midnight back up against the stump, jam your hand in and say:

‘Barley‑corn, barley‑corn, injun‑meal shorts,
spunk‑water, spunk‑water, swallow these warts,’

then walk away quickly, eleven steps, with your eyes closed, turn around three times, and walk home without speaking to anyone. Because if you speak, the charm is broken.”

“Well, that sounds like a good method, but it isn’t how Bob Tanner did it.”

“No, sir, you can be sure he didn’t, because he’s the waviest boy in this town; he wouldn’t have a wart if he knew how to use spunk‑water. I’ve removed thousands of warts from my hands that way, Huck. I play with frogs so much that I always have a lot of warts. Sometimes I take them off with a bean.”

“Yes, beans work. I’ve done that before.”

“Did you? What’s your method?”

You take a bean, split it, and cut the wart to draw some blood. Then you put the blood on one half of the bean. Dig a hole and bury it around midnight at a crossroads in the moonlight. Burn the other half of the bean. You will see that the piece with the blood keeps pulling, trying to attract the other piece. That helps the blood pull the wart, and soon the wart comes off.

“Yes, that’s it, Huck—that’s it. When you bury it, say, ‘Down bean; off wart; come no more to bother me!’ It works better. That’s how Joe Harper does it, and he’s been almost everywhere, even near Coonville. But tell me—how do you cure them with dead cats?”

First you take a cat and go to the graveyard about midnight after a wicked person has been buried. At midnight a devil, or maybe two or three, will appear. You can’t see them, only hear a wind‑like sound or voices. When they are taking the corpse away, you send your cat after them and say, “Devil follow the corpse, cat follow the devil, warts follow the cat, I’m done with you!” That will remove any wart.

“Sounds right. Have you ever tried it, Huck?”

“No, but old Mother Hopkins told me.”

“Well, I guess it works then, because they say she’s a witch.”

“Hey, Tom, I know she is. She bewitched my dad. My dad says it himself. He came by one day and saw she was casting a spell on him. He picked up a rock, and if she hadn’t ducked, he would have hit her. That same night he fell off a shed where he had been lying drunk and broke his arm.”

“That’s terrible. How did he know she was bewitching him?”

“Really, my dad can tell quickly. He says when they stare at you straight ahead, they’re trying to cast a spell. Especially if they mumble. Because when they mumble, they’re saying the Lord’s Prayer backwards.”

“So, Hucky, when are you going to try the cat?”

“Tonight. I think they’ll go after old Hoss Williams tonight.”

“But they buried him on Saturday. Didn’t they catch him Saturday night?”

“Right, how can you say that! How could their spells work until midnight—when it’s already Sunday? I don’t think devils are very active on a Sunday.”

“I never thought of that. That’s true. Can I go with you?”

“Sure—if you’re not scared.”

“Scared? Not likely. Will you meow?”

“Yes, and you should meow back if you can. Last time, you kept me meowing until old Hays started throwing rocks at me and shouted ‘Darn that cat!’ So I threw a brick through his window—but don’t tell anyone.”

“I won’t. I couldn’t meow that night because auntie was watching me, but I’ll meow this time. By the way, what’s that?”

“Just a tick.”

“Where did you find it?”

“In the woods.”

“What will you do with it?”

“I don’t know. I don’t want to sell it.”

“Okay. It’s a very small tick, anyway.”

“Oh, anyone can chase a tick that isn’t theirs. I’m fine with it. It’s a good enough tick for me.”

“Sure, there are plenty of ticks. I could have a thousand of them if I wanted.”

“Well, why don’t you? Because you know very well you can’t. This is a fairly early tick, I think. It’s the first one I’ve seen this year.”

“Hey, Huck—I’ll give you my tooth for it.”

“Let’s see it.”

Tom took a piece of paper and carefully unrolled it. Huckleberry looked at it with longing. The temptation was strong. Finally he asked, “Is it genuine?” Tom lifted his lip and showed the empty space.

“Okay,” Huckleberry said, “it’s a trade.” Tom put the tick in the percussion‑cap box that had recently been the bug’s prison, and the boys went their separate ways, each feeling richer than before.

When Tom arrived at the small, isolated school building, he walked in quickly, as if he had been running. He hung his hat on a peg and sat down quickly. The teacher, sitting high in his large wooden armchair, was dozing, lulled by the quiet sounds of study. The noise woke him.

“Thomas Sawyer!” Tom knew that hearing his full name meant trouble.

“Sir!” the teacher shouted. “Come up here. Now, sir, why are you late again, as usual?”

Tom was about to make up a lie, but he saw two long strands of yellow hair hanging down a back he recognized from love, and that was the only empty seat on the girls’ side of the classroom. He immediately said, “I stopped to talk with Huckleberry Finn!”

The teacher’s heart seemed to stop, and he stared helplessly. The quiet of the room stopped. The other students wondered if the boy had gone crazy. The teacher asked, “You—you did what?”

Stopped to talk with Huckleberry Finn.

The words were clear.

“Tom Sawyer, this is the most amazing confession I have ever heard. No simple cane can punish this crime. Take off your jacket.”

The teacher kept striking until his arm got tired and the bundle of switches was almost gone. Then he said:

“Now, sir, go sit with the girls! Let this be a warning to you.”

The giggle that spread through the room seemed to embarrass the boy, but he was really more stunned by his secret admiration for his unknown idol and the nervous excitement of his good luck. He sat down at the end of the pine bench, and the girl turned away from him with a toss of her head. Nudges, winks, and whispers moved around the room, but Tom stayed still, his arms on the low desk in front of him, and appeared to be reading his book.

After a while his attention drifted away, and the usual school murmur rose again in the dull air.  

Soon the boy began stealing quick glances at the girl. She noticed, made a face at him, and turned her head away for a minute.  

When she carefully turned back, a peach lay in front of her. She pushed it away. Tom gently put it back. She pushed it away again, but with less anger. Tom patiently returned it to its place. Then she let it stay.  

Tom wrote on his slate, “Please take it – I have more.” The girl looked at the words but did not answer.  

Now the boy started drawing something on the slate, covering his work with his left hand. For a while the girl pretended not to notice, but her curiosity showed in tiny signs. The boy kept drawing, apparently unaware. The girl tried to look without committing, but the boy did not seem to notice.  

Finally she gave in and whispered hesitantly, “Let me see it.”  

Tom uncovered part of a gloomy picture of a house with two gable ends and a curl of smoke coming from the chimney. The girl’s interest grew and she forgot everything else. When it was finished, she looked for a moment and whispered, “It’s nice – draw a man.”  

The artist added a man in the front yard that looked like a crane. He could have stepped over the house, but the girl was not too critical. She was satisfied with the figure and whispered, “It’s a beautiful man – now draw me coming along.”

Tom drew an hourglass with a full moon and straw sticks attached to it. He gave the spreading fingers a big fan. The girl said:

“It’s so nice—I wish I could draw.”

“It’s easy,” Tom whispered. “I’ll teach you.”

“Oh, really? When?”

“At noon. Are you going home for dinner?”

“I’ll stay if you’ll stay.”

“Great—that’s a surprise. What’s your name?”

“Becky Thatcher. What’s yours? Oh, I know. It’s Thomas Sawyer.”

“That’s the name they call me by. I’m Tom when I’m good. Call me Tom, okay?”

“Yes.”

Tom began to write something on the slate, hiding the words from the girl. But she wasn’t shy this time. She begged to see. Tom said:

“It’s nothing.”

“It is something.”

“No, it isn’t. You don’t want to see.”

“Yes I do. Please let me.”

“You’ll tell.”

“No, I won’t—no matter what.”

“You won’t tell anyone at all? Ever, as long as you live?”

“No, I’ll never tell anyone. Now let me.”

“Oh, you don’t want to see!”

“Now that you treat me like that, I will see.” She placed her small hand on his, and a little struggle followed. Tom pretended to resist, but slowly let his hand move until the words appeared: “I love you.”

“Oh, you naughty!” She gave his hand a sharp tap, looking a little embarrassed but also pleased.

At that moment the boy felt a gentle, strange pressure on his ear and a steady upward pull. In this way he was carried across the house and placed back in his own seat, while the whole school laughed at him. The teacher stood over him for a few uncomfortable seconds, then walked away to his desk without saying anything. Even though Tom’s ear tingled, he felt happy inside.

When the school became quiet, Tom tried to study seriously, but his thoughts were too chaotic. He went to the reading class and did a poor job. Then in geography he mixed up the facts: he called lakes mountains, mountains rivers, and rivers continents, creating more confusion. In spelling class he was rejected by a series of simple words, and finally he dropped the tin medal he had been showing off for months.
