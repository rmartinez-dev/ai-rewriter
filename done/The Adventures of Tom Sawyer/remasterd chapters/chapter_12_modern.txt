One of the reasons Tom’s mind left his secret troubles was that he found a new, serious worry. Becky Thatcher had stopped coming to school. Tom had been proud for a few days and tried to “whistle her down the wind,” but he failed. He started spending nights near her father’s house and felt very sad. She was sick. He feared she might die. The thought kept him distracted. He no longer cared about war or even piracy. Life no longer felt charming; it felt dull. He put away his hoop and his bat because they no longer gave him joy. His aunt noticed and became worried. She began to try every kind of remedy on him. She loved patent medicines and the latest health tricks. She was always testing new ideas. Whenever a new product appeared, she was eager to try it right away. She never tried them on herself because she never got sick; she tried them on anyone who was available. She subscribed to all the “Health” magazines and to phrenology pamphlets. She accepted everything they said as true, even though the advice often contradicted itself from month to month. She believed strongly in the advice about ventilation, how to go to bed, how to get up, what to eat and drink, how much exercise to do, what attitude to keep, and what clothes to wear. She was honest and simple, which made her easy to fool. She collected her questionable magazines and medicines and, armed with them, went about as if she were on a pale horse with “hell following after.” She never realized that she was not a healing angel or a miracle cure for her neighbors.

The new water treatment was now available, and Tom’s poor health seemed like a chance to her. Every morning she took him outside, put him in the woodshed, and poured a lot of cold water over him. She scrubbed him with a towel, then wrapped him in a wet sheet and covered him with blankets until she felt she had washed his soul clean. As Tom later said, “the yellow stains came out through his pores.”

But despite all this, Tom became more sad, paler, and more downcast. She tried hot baths, sitz baths, showers, and plunges, yet he still looked as gloomy as a funeral hearse. She added a thin oatmeal diet and blister plasters. She measured how much he could take like filling a jug, and gave him daily doses of questionable cures.

By now Tom seemed indifferent to the suffering. This worried the old woman a lot. She thought his indifference had to be stopped at any cost. Then she heard about a new pain‑killer. She ordered a large batch right away. She tasted a little and felt grateful; it was like fire in liquid form. She stopped the water treatments and everything else and put all her hope in the pain‑killer. She gave Tom a teaspoonful and watched anxiously for the result.

Her worries vanished instantly and she felt at peace because his indifference seemed broken. The boy could not have become more lively or interested if she had not sparked something in him.

Tom realized it was time to change. His life might have seemed romantic, even with his poor health, but it was becoming too unemotional and full of distractions. He thought about different ways to feel better and finally decided to pretend he liked painkillers. He asked for them so often that he became a nuisance, and his aunt finally told him to take them himself and stop bothering her. If it had been Sid, she wouldn’t have minded sharing her pleasure, but because it was Tom, she began to watch the bottle secretly. She saw that the medicine really helped, but she didn’t realize that Tom was using it to fix a crack in the sitting‑room floor.

One day Tom was trying to fill the crack when his aunt’s yellow cat came over, purring, staring greedily at the teaspoon and begging for a taste. Tom said,

“Don’t ask for it unless you really want it, Peter.”

Peter nodded that he did want it.

“You’d better be sure.”

Peter was sure.

“Now you’ve asked for it, and I’ll give it to you, because I’m not a mean person; but if you don’t like it, you can only blame yourself.”

Peter liked it, so Tom forced his mouth open and gave him the pain‑killer. Peter jumped a few feet into the air, let out a loud war‑cry, and ran around the room. He hit the furniture, knocked over flower‑pots, and made a big mess. Then he stood on his hind legs and danced, laughing loudly, his head tipped back, shouting how happy he was. He rushed around the house again, breaking things as he went. Aunt Polly came in just in time to see him toss a couple of double‑summer sets, give one final loud shout, and fly out the open window, carrying the rest of the flower‑pots with him. The old lady stood frozen with surprise, looking over her glasses; Tom lay on the floor laughing so hard he could barely breathe.  

“Tom, what on earth is wrong with that cat?”  

“I don’t know, Aunt,” the boy gasped.  

“Honestly, I’ve never seen anything like this. What made him act that way?”  

“I really don’t know, Aunt Polly; cats act like this when they’re having fun.”  

“They do, don’t they?” Tom sounded nervous.  

“Yes, I think so.”  

“Yes, really?”  

“Yes.”  

The old lady bent down, and Tom watched her, his anxiety clear. He finally understood what she was planning. The handle of a tell‑tale tea‑spoon could be seen under the bed skirt. Aunt Polly picked it up and held it out. Tom winced and looked away. Aunt Polly grabbed his ear by the usual handle and gave his head a hard tap with her thimble.  

“Now, sir, why did you want to treat that poor helpless animal this way?”  

“I did it out of pity—because he didn’t have an aunt.”

“Didn’t have any aunt!—you fool. What does that have to do with it?”

“Lots. Because if he had one, she would have burned him herself! She would have roasted his insides out of him as if he felt no more than any human does.”

Aunt Polly felt a sudden pang of remorse. This made her see things differently; what was cruelty to a cat could also be cruelty to a boy. She began to soften and felt sorry. Her eyes watered a little, and she put her hand on Tom’s head and said gently:

“I was trying to do what was best, Tom. And, Tom, it really did you good.”

Tom looked up at her with a faint twinkle behind his serious expression.

“I know you meant well, aunty, and I felt the same about Peter. It helped him too. I haven’t seen him around like that since—”

“Oh, go away, Tom, before you irritate me again. Try to be a good boy for once, and you won’t need any more medicine.”

Tom arrived at school early. Everyone had noticed that this strange behavior had been happening every day recently. As usual these days, he lingered at the schoolyard gate instead of playing with his friends. He said he was sick, and he looked the part. He tried to act as if he were looking everywhere, but he was really looking down the road.

Soon Jeff Thatcher came into view, and Tom’s face lit up. He stared for a moment, then turned away sadly. When Jeff arrived, Tom greeted him and, cautiously, tried to find a chance to talk about Becky, but the nervous boy could never find the right opening. Tom watched and watched, hoping that any girl in a pretty dress would appear, and hating the owner as soon as he saw she was not the one he wanted. At last the dresses stopped appearing, and he fell hopelessly into a slump. He went into the empty schoolroom and sat down, feeling miserable.

Then another girl in a dress walked through the gate, and Tom’s heart leapt. The next instant he was out of his seat, running around like a wild Indian, shouting, laughing, chasing other boys, jumping over the fence even though it was risky, doing flips, standing on his head—doing all the heroic things he could think of, while keeping a secret eye on whether Becky Thatcher was noticing. But she seemed unaware of everything; she never looked. Could it be that she didn’t even know he was there?

He carried out his antics right near her; he ran around shouting, grabbed a boy’s cap and threw it onto the school roof, pushed through a group of boys, sending them tumbling in all directions, and fell down right in front of Becky, almost knocking her over. She turned, her nose lifted, and Tom heard her say, “Ugh! Some people think they’re so clever—always showing off!”

Tom’s cheeks felt hot. He collected himself and slipped away, feeling crushed and downhearted.
