Tom had finally made up his mind. He felt gloomy and desperate. He called himself an abandoned, friendless boy. Nobody loved him. He thought that when people saw what they had forced him to do, they might feel sorry. He had tried to behave and get along, but they wouldn’t let him. Since they only wanted to get rid of him, he accepted it. He said they could blame him for what happened—why shouldn’t they? What right did a friendless boy have to complain? Yes, they had finally forced him into a life of crime. He saw no other choice.

He was now far down Meadow Lane, and the school bell rang faintly in his ears. He began to sob, thinking he would never hear that familiar sound again. It was very hard, but he felt forced to accept it. Since he had been pushed out into the cold world, he had to submit—but he forgave them. The sobs came thick and fast.

At that moment he met his close friend, Joe Harper, a hard‑eyed boy who seemed to carry a heavy purpose in his heart. It was clear that they shared the same single thought. Tom wiped his eyes with his sleeve and started to sob, talking about his decision to run away from the harsh treatment and lack of sympathy at home. He said he would travel far away and never come back, and he asked Joe not to forget him.

It turned out that this was a request Joe had just been about to make to Tom, and he had gone to find him for that purpose. His mother had beaten him for drinking some cream he had never tasted and knew nothing about; it was clear she was fed up with him and wanted him to leave. If she felt that way, there was nothing for him to do but give in. He hoped she would be happy and never regret having sent her poor boy out into the harsh world to suffer and die.

As the two boys walked sadly together, they made a new promise to stand by each other like brothers and never be separated until death freed them from their troubles. Then they began to plan. Joe wanted to be a hermit, living on crusts in a remote cave, and eventually die of cold, hunger, and grief. But after listening to Tom, he agreed that a life of crime had some clear advantages, so he agreed to become a pirate.

Three miles downstream from St. Peterburg, where the Mississippi River was just over a mile wide, there was a long, narrow, wooded island with a shallow sandbank at its tip. It was an ideal meeting place. No one lived there; it was far from the opposite shore and next to a thick, almost empty forest. The group chose Jackson’s Island for their rendezvous.

They had not thought about who they would target next. Then they found Huckleberry Finn, and he joined them right away. He didn’t care about the details of the plan. Soon they split up and agreed to meet at a lonely spot on the riverbank, two miles above the village, at their favorite time—midnight. A small log raft was waiting there, which they planned to steal. Each of them would bring hooks, lines, and any food they could pick up in the secret, outlaw‑like way. By the end of the afternoon they had already bragged that the town would soon “hear something.” Anyone who got the vague warning was told to stay silent and wait.

Around midnight Tom arrived with boiled ham and a few small snacks. He stopped in a thick thicket on a small bluff that overlooked the meeting place. The stars were out and everything was very still. The mighty river lay calm like a still ocean. Tom listened for a moment, but no sound broke the quiet. He then gave a low, clear whistle. It was answered from under the bluff. Tom whistled twice more; the same answer came each time. A guarded voice then called out:

“Who’s there?”

“Tom Sawyer, the Black Avenger of the Spanish Main. Say your names.”

“Huck Finn the Red‑Handed and Joe Harper the Terror of the Seas.” Tom gave them those names from his favorite books.  

“Okay. Give the password.”  

Two hoarse whispers said the same terrible word at the same time in the dark night:  

“Blood!”  

Then Tom rolled his ham over the cliff and slid down after it, tearing his skin and clothes a little in the process. There was an easy, comfortable path along the shore below the cliff, but it didn’t have the difficulty and danger a pirate likes.  

The Terror of the Seas had brought a side of bacon and was almost exhausted from carrying it there. Finn the Red‑Handed had stolen a skillet and some half‑cured leaf tobacco, and he also brought a few corn cobs to make pipes. None of the pirates smoked or chewed except him. The Black Avenger of the Spanish Main said they should never start without fire. That was a good idea; matches were rare then.  

They saw a small fire smoldering on a big raft a hundred yards away. They crept toward it and took a piece of wood. They acted like it was a great adventure, saying “Shh!” now and then, stopping suddenly with a finger on their lips, moving their hands as if holding dagger handles, and whispering low: “If the enemy moves, strike him fully, because dead men tell no tales.”  

They knew the people on the raft were all down in the village storing supplies or having a party, but that was not an excuse for their un‑pirate‑like behavior.

They pushed off. Tom was in command, Huck handled the back oar and Joe the front. Tom stood in the middle of the boat, his brow dark and his arms folded, and gave his orders in a low, firm whisper:

“Luff and bring her into the wind!”

“Yes, sir!”

“Steady, steady—”

“Steady, sir!”

“Let her go off a point!”

“Point, sir!”

As the boys slowly and monotonously moved the raft toward the middle of the stream, everyone understood that these orders were only for show and weren’t meant to be taken seriously.

“What sails is she carrying?”

“Courses, topsails and a flying jib, sir.”

“Raise the royal sails! Spread them out up top, half a dozen of you—fore‑top mast lines! Quick now!”

“Yes, sir!”

“Pull out the main halyards! Sheets and braces! Now, my friends!”

“Yes, sir!”

“Hard a port! Be ready to meet her when she comes! Port, port! Now, men! With effort! Steady!”

“Steady, sir!”

The raft drifted past the middle of the river. The boys steered it to the right and then rested on their oars. The water was shallow, so the current was only two or three miles per hour. For the next forty‑five minutes they hardly spoke. The raft moved past a distant town. Two or three faint lights showed where the town slept peacefully, far beyond the dark, star‑filled water, unaware of the great event unfolding.

The Black Avenger stood still with his arms folded, taking a final look at the place of his past joys and later sorrows. He wished she could see him now, out on the wild sea, facing danger and death with a brave heart, heading toward his fate with a grim smile. It was easy for him to imagine Jackson’s Island far beyond the village’s sight, and he took his last look with a broken yet satisfied heart. The other pirates also took their last looks; they stared so long that the current almost carried them away from the island. But they realized the danger in time and turned to avoid it.

Around two o’clock in the morning the raft ran aground on a sandbar about two hundred yards from the island’s tip. They waded back and forth until they unloaded their cargo. Among the few belongings was an old sail, which they spread over a thicket to make a tent for their supplies. They themselves slept outside in good weather, as outlaws do.

They built a fire against a large log about twenty or thirty steps into the dark forest. Then they fried some bacon for dinner and used up half of the cornmeal mush they had brought. It felt like great fun to eat that way in the wild, untouched forest of an unexplored island, far from any people. They said they would never go back to civilization. The fire lit their faces and cast a reddish glow on the tall tree trunks around them and on the glossy leaves and hanging vines.

When the last crisp slice of bacon was gone and they had eaten the remaining cornmeal mush, the boys lay down on the grass, feeling satisfied. They could have moved to a cooler spot, but they didn’t want to give up the romantic feeling of the campfire.

“Isn’t it great?” said Joe.

“It’s crazy!” said Tom. “What would the other boys say if they could see us?”

“They’d love it—hey, Hucky!”

“I think so,” said Huckleberry. “Anyway, I’m happy. I don’t want anything better than this. I usually don’t get enough to eat, and here no one can pick on a guy or bully him.”

“That’s the life for me,” said Tom. “You don’t have to get up early, go to school, wash, and all that nonsense. A pirate on shore doesn’t have to do anything, Joe, but a hermit has to pray a lot and then never has any fun because he’s always alone.”

"Oh yes, that's true," said Joe, "but I haven't thought much about it, you know. I'd rather be a pirate now that I've tried it."

"You see," said Tom, "people don't admire hermits much these days like they used to, but a pirate is always respected. A hermit has to sleep in the hardest place he can find, put sackcloth and ashes on his head, stand out in the rain, and—"

"What does he put sackcloth and ashes on his head for?" asked Huck.

"I don't know. But they have to do it. Hermits always do. You would have to do that if you were a hermit."

"Never if I could," said Huck.

"Well, what would you do?"

"I don't know. But I wouldn't do that."

"Why, Huck, you'd have to. How would you avoid it?"

"I just wouldn't stand it. I'd run away."

"Run away! Then you'd be a lazy old hermit. You'd be a disgrace."

The Red-Handed stayed silent, busy with his work. He had finished carving out a corncob, attached a weed stem to it, filled it with tobacco, and was pressing a coal to the end, blowing out a fragrant cloud. He was completely content. The other pirates envied his comfortable habit and secretly decided they would try it soon. Then Huck asked:

"What do pirates have to do?"

Tom answered:

"They just have a rough time—take ships, burn them, get the treasure and bury it in scary places on their island where ghosts watch over it, and kill everyone on the ships—make them walk the plank."

“And they take the women to the island,” said Joe. “They don’t kill them.”

“No,” Tom agreed. “They don’t kill the women—they’re too noble. And the women are always beautiful, too.”

“And don’t they wear the flashiest clothes! Oh yes! All gold, silver, and diamonds,” Joe said excitedly.

“Who?” Huck asked.

“The pirates,” Joe replied.

Huck looked sadly at his own clothes.

“I don’t think I’m dressed right for a pirate,” he said, sounding regretful. “But these are all I have.”

The other boys told him that good clothes would come soon, after they started their adventures. They explained that his cheap rags were fine for now, even though rich pirates usually begin with fine wardrobes.

Gradually their conversation faded and sleep began to tug at the eyes of the little children. The pipe fell from the Red‑Handed’s fingers, and he fell into a deep, carefree sleep.

The Terror of the Seas and the Black Avenger of the Spanish Main found it much harder to fall asleep. They whispered their prayers silently while lying down, because there was no one there to force them to kneel and say them aloud. In truth, they did not really want to pray, but they were afraid that doing so might bring a sudden punishment from heaven.

They almost reached the edge of sleep, but then an intruder appeared that would not let them rest. It was their conscience. First they felt a vague fear that they had done something wrong by running away. Then they thought of the stolen meat, and the real torment began.

They tried to argue with their conscience, reminding it that they had stolen sweets and apples many times before. Conscience would not be satisfied with such weak excuses. In the end they realized that taking candy was only a small cheat, while stealing bacon, ham and other valuable foods was plain theft – and the Bible forbids that.

So they decided, in their own minds, that as long as they stayed in piracy, they would not add the crime of stealing to their deeds. With that decision, conscience calmed down, and the strangely contradictory pirates fell peacefully asleep.
