Huck said, “Tom, we can slide down if we find a rope. The window isn’t very high.”

“Gee! Why do you want to slide?” Tom asked.

“Well, I’m not used to that kind of crowd. I can’t stand it. I’m not going down there, Tom.”

“Oh, come on! It’s nothing. I don’t mind it at all. I’ll take care of you.”

Sid appeared.

“Tom,” he said, “Auntie has been waiting for you all afternoon. Mary has your Sunday clothes ready, and everyone has been worrying about you. Look—your clothes are covered in grease and mud.”

“Now, Mr. Sid, you just mind your own business. What’s all this fuss about, anyway?”

“It’s one of the widow’s parties she always has. This time it’s for the Welshman and his sons, because they helped her the other night. And I can tell you something, if you want to know.”

“Well, what?”

“Old Mr. Jones is planning to surprise everyone tonight, but I overheard him tell Auntie about it today. He tried to keep it a secret, but it’s not a secret any more. Everybody knows— even the widow, no matter how much she pretends she doesn’t. Mr. Jones needed Huck to be there—he couldn’t do his big secret without Huck, you know!”

“Secret about what, Sid?” Tom asked.

“About Huck tracking the robbers to the widow’s place. I think Mr. Jones wanted to make a big deal out of his surprise, but I bet it will fall flat.”

Sid chuckled, satisfied.

“Sid, was it you who told?” Tom asked.

“Oh, it doesn’t matter who said it. Someone told—that’s enough.”

“Sid, there’s only one person in this town who’s cruel enough to do that, and it’s you. If you had been in Huck’s place you would have slipped down the hill and never told anyone about the robbers. You only do mean things, and you can’t stand to see anyone praised for doing good. ‘No thanks,’ as the widow says,” Tom pinched Sid’s ears and pushed him toward the door with a few kicks. “Now go and tell Auntie if you dare—tomorrow you’ll get it!”

A few minutes later the widow’s guests were sitting at the dinner table. A dozen children were sitting at small side tables in the same room, as was usual in that country at that time. At the right moment Mr. Jones gave his short speech. He thanked the widow for the honor she was giving him and his sons, but he said there was another person whose modesty—

He revealed his secret about Huck’s role in the adventure in the most dramatic way he knew. The surprise it caused was mostly fake and not as loud or enthusiastic as it could have been in better circumstances. Still, the widow acted very surprised. She gave Huck many compliments and thanks, so much that he almost forgot how uncomfortable his new clothes were and how awful it felt to be the center of everyone’s attention and praise.

The widow said she wanted to give Huck a place to live in her house and have him educated. She added that when she could afford it, she would help him start a modest business. Tom saw his chance. He said, “Huck doesn’t need it. Huck is rich.”

Only the group's good manners stopped anyone from laughing at the joke. The silence felt a little awkward, and Tom broke it:

"Huck has money. You might not believe it, but he has a lot. You don’t have to smile—I think I can prove it. Just wait a moment."

Tom ran out the door. The others looked at each other, confused, and then at Huck, who was speechless.

"Sid, what's wrong with Tom?" asked Aunt Polly. "He—well, I never thought that boy would turn out like this. I never—"

Tom came back, struggling with heavy sacks, and Aunt Polly didn't finish her sentence. Tom dumped a pile of yellow coins onto the table and said:

"There—what did I tell you? Half of it is Huck’s and half is mine!"

Everyone gasped. No one said anything at first. Then they all asked for an explanation. Tom said he could provide it, and he did. The story was long but very interesting. No one interrupted, letting the tale flow smoothly. When he finished, Mr. Jones said:

"I thought I had a surprise for this occasion, but it doesn’t compare now. This one makes everything else seem tiny, I admit."

The money was counted. It added up to a little over twelve thousand dollars. No one present had ever seen that much money at one time, even though several people there owned far more in property.
