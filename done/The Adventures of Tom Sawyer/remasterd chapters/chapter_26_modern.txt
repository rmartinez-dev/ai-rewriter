At about noon the next day the boys reached the dead tree. They had come to get their tools. Tom was eager to go to the haunted house, and Huck was just as eager. Then Huck suddenly said:

“Hey Tom, do you know what day it is?”

Tom thought about the days of the week. Then he looked up, surprised.

“Wow! I never thought about that, Huck!”

“I didn’t either, but it just occurred to me that today is Friday.”

“You’re right, we have to be extra careful, Huck. We could get into a terrible mess if we try that on a Friday.”

“We might! Better to say we will. Some days are lucky, maybe, but Friday isn’t one of them.”

“Everyone knows that. I don’t think you were the first to notice, Huck.”

“No, I never claimed to be. And Friday isn’t the only problem. I had a terrible dream last night—about rats.”

“That’s a bad sign. Did they fight?”

“No.”

“Well, that’s good, Huck. When they don’t fight it still means trouble is nearby, you know. All we have to do is stay alert and stay out of it. Let’s leave this for today and have fun. Do you know Robin Hood, Huck?”

“No. Who is Robin Hood?”

“He was one of the greatest men in England—actually the best. He was a thief.”

“Cool, I wish I were. Who did he steal from?”

“Only sheriffs, bishops, rich people and kings—people like that. He never bothered the poor. He liked them. He always shared his loot equally with them.”

“Then he must have been a hero.”

“I bet you he was, Huck. He was the noblest man ever. There are no men like him now, I can tell you. He could beat any man in England even with one hand tied behind his back, and with his yew bow he could hit a ten‑cent piece from a mile and a half away.”

“What’s a yew bow?”

“I don’t know. It’s some kind of bow, of course. If he hit that dime only on the edge, he would put it down, cry, and curse. But let’s play Robin Hood—it’s great fun. I’ll teach you.”

“I agree.”

They played Robin Hood all afternoon. Occasionally they glanced at the haunted house and talked about what might happen the next day. When the sun started to set in the west, they walked home through the long shadows of the trees. Soon they disappeared from view in the woods on Cardiff Hill.

On Saturday, just after noon, the boys returned to the dead tree. They smoked and talked in the shade. Then they dug a little in their last hole. They weren’t very hopeful, but Tom reminded them that many people had stopped digging just six inches from a treasure, and later someone else found it with a single shovel thrust. This time they didn’t find anything. The boys packed up their tools and left, feeling that they hadn’t wasted a chance, but had done everything a treasure hunter should do.

When they reached the haunted house, the dead silence felt strange and frightening under the hot sun. The place also seemed very lonely and empty, which made them hesitate to go inside. They crept to the door and peered in with trembling hands. Inside they saw a weed‑grown, floorless room with bare walls, an old fireplace, empty windows, and a ruined staircase. Ragged, abandoned cobwebs hung everywhere.  

They entered quietly, their hearts beating faster. They whispered to each other, listening for any sound, their muscles ready to run at a moment’s notice.  

After a short time, they became more familiar with the house and their fear lessened. They examined it carefully, feeling proud of their bravery and also amazed by it. Then they wanted to go upstairs. Going up felt like cutting off their way out, but they dared each other anyway. They threw their tools into a corner and climbed the stairs.  

Upstairs they saw the same signs of decay. In one corner they found a closet that seemed mysterious, but it was empty. Their courage grew. They were about to go back down and start working when…  

“Sh!” said Tom.  

“What is it?” whispered Huck, turning pale with fear.  

“Sh… there… did you hear that?”  

“Yes… oh no! Let’s run!”  

“Stay still! Don’t move! They’re coming toward the door.”  

The boys stretched out on the floor, eyes fixed on the cracks in the boards, waiting in terrified silence.  

“They stopped… no… they’re coming… here they are. Don’t say another word, Huck. I wish I could get out of this!”

Two men walked in. Each boy thought to himself, “That’s the old deaf and mute Spaniard who has been in town a couple of times lately. I’ve never seen the other man before.”

The other man was ragged and unkempt, and his face was far from pleasant. The Spaniard was wrapped in a blanket; he had bushy white whiskers, long white hair hanging from under his sombrero, and he wore green goggles. When they entered, the other man was speaking in a low voice. They sat on the floor, facing the door, with their backs against the wall, and he kept talking. As he went on, he became less guarded and his words grew clearer:

“No,” he said, “I have thought it over, and I don’t like it. It’s dangerous.”

“Dangerous!” grunted the deaf‑and‑mute Spaniard, shocking the boys. “Coward!”

The boys gasped and trembled. It was Injun Joe’s voice! Silence fell for a while. Then Joe spoke:

“What’s more dangerous than that job up there—though nothing has happened because of it?”

“That’s different. It’s far up the river, and there’s no other house nearby. No one will ever know we tried, as long as we don’t succeed.”

“Well, what’s more dangerous than coming here in daylight? Anyone who sees us would suspect us.”

“I know that. But there’s no other place as useful after that foolish job. I want to leave this shack. I wanted to leave yesterday, but it’s useless trying to get out while those annoying boys are playing on the hill right in plain sight.”

The boys trembled again at those words and thought how lucky they were to remember that it was Friday, so they decided to wait one more day. In their hearts they wished they could have waited a whole year.

The two men brought out some food and had lunch. After a long, thoughtful silence, Injun Joe said, “Listen, kid—go back up the river where you belong. Wait there until I contact you. I’ll risk going into this town one more time just to look. We’ll do that ‘dangerous’ job after I’ve checked the area and think it’s safe. Then we’ll head for Texas together!”

They agreed. Both men soon started yawning, and Injun Joe said, “I’m exhausted! It’s your turn to keep watch.” He lay down in the grass and soon began snoring. His companion nudged him a couple of times and he stopped. Then the watcher began to nod off; his head fell lower and lower, and both men started snoring.

The boys took a deep, grateful breath. Tom whispered, “Now’s our chance—let’s go!” Huck answered, “I can’t—I would die if they woke up.” Tom tried to push him, but Huck held him back. Finally Tom stood up slowly and quietly, and went alone. The first step he took made the old floorboard scream with a horrible creak, and he froze with fright. He never tried again. The boys lay there, watching the seconds tick by until it felt like time stopped and eternity turned gray; then they were relieved to see the sun finally setting.

One snore stopped. Indian Joe sat up, looked around, and gave a grim smile to his companion, whose head was resting on his knees. He nudged him with his foot and said:

“Hey, you’re the guard, right? It’s all right—nothing’s happened.”

“Did I really fall asleep?”

“Only a little. It’s almost time for us to move, friend. What should we do with the little loot we have left?”

“I’m not sure—maybe we leave it here like we always have. No point taking it until we head south. Six hundred and fifty silver is a lot to carry.”

“Okay, that’s fine. It won’t matter if we come back later.”

“No, but I think we should come back at night like before—it’s safer.”

“Sure, but it might be a long time before we get another chance at a job like this. Accidents could happen. The place isn’t very good, so we’ll just bury it deep and regular­ly.”

“Good idea,” said his companion. He walked across the room, knelt, lifted one of the rear hearth stones, and took out a bag that jingled. He gave himself and Indian Joe twenty or thirty dollars each, then handed the bag to Joe, who was kneeling in the corner, digging with his bowie knife.

The boys instantly forgot their fears and troubles. Their eyes gleamed with excitement. Luck! The treasure was beyond anything they imagined. Six hundred dollars was enough to make six boys rich! They felt like they were on the perfect treasure hunt with no doubt about where to dig. They nudged each other constantly, the nudges meaning, “Aren’t we glad we’re finally here?”

Joe’s knife hit something.

"Hello!" he said.

"What is it?" his companion asked.

"It's a half‑rotten plank—no, I think it's a box. Here, give me a hand and we'll see what's inside. Never mind, I made a hole."

He put his hand in and pulled it out.

"Man, it's money!"

The two men looked at the handful of coins. They were gold. The boys above were as excited and delighted as they were.

Joe's companion said, "We'll get this done quickly. There's an old rusty pick in the weeds on the other side of the fireplace—I saw it a minute ago."

He ran and brought the boys' pick and shovel. Injun Joe took the pick, examined it carefully, shook his head, muttered something, and then started using it. The box was soon dug up. It wasn't very large; it was bound with iron and had been very strong before the years had weakened it. The men admired the treasure for a while in quiet delight.

"Buddy, there's thousands of dollars here," said Injun Joe.

"It was said that Murrel's gang was around here one summer," the stranger observed.

"I know," said Injun Joe. "And this looks like it, I should say."

"Now you won't have to do that job."

The mixed‑blood man frowned and said, "You don't know me. You don't know the whole story. It's not just robbery—it's revenge!" A wicked light flashed in his eyes. "I'll need your help. When it's finished, go back to Texas. Go home to your Nance and kids, and wait until you hear from me."

"Well, if you say so, what should we do with this—bury it again?"

“Yes. [Excitement above.] No! By the great chief, no! [Deep worry above.] I almost forgot. That pickaxe was covered with fresh soil! The boys suddenly felt terrified. Why are there a pickaxe and a shovel here? Why is there fresh soil on them? Who brought them here, and where have they gone? Did anyone hear anything? Did anyone see anyone? What, bury it again and let people see the disturbed ground? Not really. We'll take it to my hideout.”

“Sure, of course! I could have thought of that earlier. You mean the first one?”

“No— the second one, under the cross. The other place is bad, too common.”

“Okay. It's almost dark enough to begin.”

Injun Joe stood up and moved carefully from window to window, peeking out. Then he asked, “Who could have brought those tools here? Do you think they could be upstairs?”

The boys stopped breathing. Injun Joe placed his hand on his knife, hesitated for a moment, then turned toward the stairs. The boys thought about the closet, but they felt weak. The stairs creaked as they went up, and the terrible situation made the boys decide to run for the closet. Suddenly, rotten timbers crashed, and the stairs collapsed. Injun Joe fell to the ground among the broken pieces. He got up, swearing, and his companion said:

“Now what’s the point of all that? If anyone’s up there, let them stay up there—who cares? If they want to jump down now and get into trouble, who objects? It will be dark in fifteen minutes, and then they can follow us if they want. I’m willing. I think whoever brought those things in here saw us and thought we were ghosts or devils or something. I bet they’re still running.”

Joe grumbled for a while, then he agreed with his friend that the remaining daylight should be saved for getting ready to leave.

Soon after, they slipped out of the house as the twilight deepened and walked toward the river with their precious box.

Tom and Huck got up, weak but very relieved, and watched them through the gaps between the logs of the house. Follow? No. They were happy just to reach solid ground without breaking their necks and to take the road toward town over the hill. They didn’t talk much. They were too busy hating themselves—hating the bad luck that had made them use the spade and the pick there. Because of that, Injun Joe would never have suspected. He would have hidden the silver with the gold, waiting there until his “revenge” was satisfied, and then he would have been unlucky enough to find that the money was gone. How unlucky that the tools were ever brought there!

They decided to keep an eye out for that Spaniard when he came to town looking for a chance to carry out his revenge, and to follow him to “Number Two,” wherever that was. Then a terrible thought came to Tom.

“Revenge? What if he means us, Huck!”

“Oh, don’t!” said Huck, nearly fainting.

They discussed it completely. When they reached the town, they agreed that he could be talking about someone else. At the very least, they thought he might be referring only to Tom, because Tom was the only one who had testified.

It gave Tom very little comfort to be in danger alone. He thought having company would be a clear improvement.
