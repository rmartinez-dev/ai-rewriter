Readers can be sure that Tom’s and Huck’s sudden wealth caused a huge stir in the small poor village of St. Petersburg. The amount of cash was almost unbelievable. People talked about it, bragged about it, and praised it until many villagers felt overwhelmed by the excitement. Every “haunted” house in St. Petersburg and the nearby villages was taken apart plank by plank, its foundations dug up, and searched for hidden treasure. The search was done not by boys but by men—some serious, unromantic men. Wherever Tom and Huck went, people courted them, admired them, and stared at them. The boys could not remember that their words had ever mattered before, but now everyone treasured and repeated what they said. Everything they did seemed remarkable. They had lost the ability to do or say ordinary things. Their past was also dug up and shown to be unusually original. The village newspaper printed short biographies of the boys.

The Widow Douglas invested Huck’s money at six percent, and Judge Thatcher did the same with Tom’s money after Aunt Polly asked him. Now each boy had a huge income—about one dollar for every weekday of the year and half a dollar for each Sunday. That was the same amount the minister was supposed to receive, although he often could not collect it. In those simple old days, a dollar and a quarter a week was enough to feed, house, and send a boy to school, and even to buy his clothes and soap.

Judge Thatcher thought very highly of Tom. He said that no ordinary boy could have rescued his daughter from the cave.  

When Becky told her father, in confidence, how Tom had taken her punishment at school, the judge was clearly moved. She begged him to forgive the big lie Tom told to take the punishment from her onto himself. The judge replied, loudly and proudly, that it was a noble, generous, magnanimous lie—one that could stand proudly in history beside George Washington’s famous truth about the hatchet.  

Becky thought her father had never looked so tall and impressive as when he walked across the room, stamped his foot, and said those words. She ran straight to Tom and told him what had happened.  

Judge Thatcher hoped Tom would become a great lawyer or a great soldier someday. He said he wanted Tom to be accepted into the National Military Academy and later to study at the country’s best law school, so he could be ready for either career—or both.

Huck Finn’s new wealth and the fact that he was now under Widow Douglas’s protection forced him into society—really, it shoved him right into it. The suffering it caused was almost more than he could stand. The widow’s servants kept him clean and tidy. They combed his hair, brushed his clothes, and each night they put him to bed on plain sheets that had no spots or stains he could cling to as a reminder of a friend. He now had to eat with a knife and fork. He had to use a napkin, a cup, and a plate. He had to study his book and go to church. He had to speak so properly that his words felt bland in his mouth. Everywhere he looked, the constraints of civilization trapped him and bound him hand and foot.  

He endured these hardships for three weeks. Then one day he disappeared. For two days the widow searched everywhere, very worried. The townspeople were also deeply concerned. They looked high and low and even dragged the river looking for his body. Early on the third morning, Tom Sawyer carefully looked inside some old empty barrels behind the abandoned slaughterhouse. In one of them he found Huck. Huck had been sleeping there. He had just eaten some stolen bits of food and was now lying comfortably with his pipe. He was messy, his hair uncombed, and wearing the same ragged clothes that had once made him look like a picture of freedom and happiness. Tom pushed him out, explained the trouble he had caused, and urged him to go back home. Huck’s calm face changed to a sad expression. He said:

“Don’t talk about it, Tom. I’ve tried it, and it doesn’t work; it doesn’t work, Tom. It’s not for me; I’m not used to it. The woman is good to me and friendly, but I can’t stand her ways. She makes me get up at the same time every morning. She makes me wash, she combs my hair until it hurts. She won’t let me sleep in the woodshed. I have to wear those damned clothes that just smother me, Tom. They don’t let any air get through them. They’re so tightly made that I can’t sit down, lie down, or roll around anywhere. I haven’t slid on a cellar door for—well, it seems like years. I have to go to church and sweat, sweat—I hate those angry sermons! I can’t catch a fly in there, I can’t chew. I have to wear shoes all Sunday. The woman eats when a bell rings; she goes to bed when a bell rings; she gets up when a bell rings—everything is so horribly regular a person can’t stand it.”

“Well, everybody does it that way, Huck.”

Tom, it doesn’t matter. I’m not everybody, and I can’t stand it. It’s terrible to be tied up like that. Food comes too easily—I don’t care about meals like that. I have to ask to go fishing. I have to ask to go swimming—damned if I don’t have to ask for everything.  

I had to talk so nicely that it gave no comfort. I had to go up to the attic and stay there a while every day just to get a taste in my mouth, or I would have died, Tom.  

The widow wouldn’t let me smoke. She wouldn’t let me shout, stare, stretch or scratch in front of people.  

And Dad would fetch it; she prayed all the time! I’ve never seen a woman like her. I had to push, Tom—I just had to.  

Besides, that school is opening, and I had to go there. I couldn’t stand that, Tom.  

Listen, Tom, being rich isn’t as great as people say. It’s only worry after worry, sweat after sweat, and wishing you were dead all the time.  

Now these clothes fit me, and this barber fits me, and I’m never going to shake them off again. Tom, I wouldn’t have gotten into all this trouble if it hadn’t been for that money. Now you just take my share of it along with yours, and sometimes give me ten cents—not often, because I don’t care about anything unless it’s really hard to get—and you go and beg for me with the widow.

"Oh, Huck, you know I can’t do that. It's not fair, and if you keep trying this for a little while longer, you'll end up liking it."

"Like it! Yeah—like I’d like a hot stove if I sat on it long enough. No, Tom, I’m not going to be rich, and I’m not going to live in those damn fancy houses. I love the woods, the river, the barrels, and I’ll stick with them too. Curse it all! We finally got guns, a cave, everything ready for a robbery, and now this stupid mess has to ruin everything!"

Tom saw his chance.

"Look, Huck, being rich won’t stop me from becoming a robber."

"No! Good grief, are you really serious, Tom?"

"As serious as I am sitting here. But Huck, we can’t let you join the gang if you’re not respectable, you know."

Huck’s excitement faded.

"Can’t let me in, Tom? Didn’t you let me be a pirate?"

"Yes, but that’s different. A robber is considered more respectable than a pirate, in general. In most places they’re treated like high‑ranking nobles—dukes and the like."

"Now, Tom, haven’t you always been friendly to me? You wouldn’t throw me out, would you? You wouldn’t do that, right, Tom?"

"Huck, I don’t want to, and I wouldn’t, but think about what people would say. They’d say, ‘Wow! Tom Sawyer’s gang—what low‑life characters!’ They’d be talking about you, Huck. You wouldn’t like that, and I wouldn’t either."

Huck was silent for a while, thinking. Finally he said:"

“Well, I’ll go back to the widow for a month, try it out, and see if I can stand it, if you’ll let me join the gang, Tom.”

“Alright, Huck, that’s great! Come on, old friend, and I’ll ask the widow to cut you some slack, Huck.”

“Will you, Tom—now will you? That’s good. If she eases up on the hardest things, I’ll smoke in private and curse in private, and push through or break. When are you going to start the gang and become robbers?”

“Oh, right away. We’ll get the boys together and have the initiation tonight, maybe.”

“Have the what?”

“Have the initiation.”

“What’s that?”

“It’s a promise to stand by each other and never reveal the gang’s secrets, even if you’re torn to pieces, and to kill anyone and their whole family who hurts a member of the gang.”

“That’s crazy—that’s really crazy, Tom, I tell you.”

“Well, I bet it is. All that swearing has to be done at midnight, in the loneliest, most awful place you can find—a haunted house would be best, but they’re all ruined now.”

“Well, midnight works, anyway, Tom.”

“Yes, it does. And you have to swear on a coffin and sign it with blood.”

“Now, that’s something else! It’s a million times scarier than piracy. I’ll stay with the widow until I rot, Tom; and if I end up a regular thief and everyone talks about it, I think she’ll be proud she got me out of the rain.”

This is the end of the chronicle. Since it has been a history of a boy, it must stop here; the story could not go much further without becoming the history of a man. When someone writes a novel about grown‑up people, they know exactly where to stop—usually with a marriage. But when they write about children, they have to stop as best they can.

Most of the characters in this book are still alive, doing well and happy. One day it might be worthwhile to continue the story of the younger characters and see what kind of adults they become. So it is best not to reveal that part of their lives now.
