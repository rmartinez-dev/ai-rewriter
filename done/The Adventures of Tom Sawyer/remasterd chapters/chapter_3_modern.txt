Tom went to see Aunt Polly, who was sitting by an open window in a pleasant back room that served as bedroom, breakfast room, dining room, and library all at once. The warm summer air, the quiet, the scent of flowers and the low buzz of bees had made her drowsy. She was nodding over her knitting; she had no one with her except a cat that was sleeping in her lap. Her glasses were propped on her gray head for safety. She had thought Tom had left long ago, and she was surprised to see him appear in front of her again so boldly.

He asked, “May I go and play now, Aunt?”

She replied, “Already? How much have you done?”

“It’s all done, Aunt,” he said.

“Tom, don’t lie to me—I can’t stand it.”

“I’m not lying, Aunt; it really is all done.”

Aunt Polly gave him a little trust based on his words. She went to check for herself. She would have been happy to find even a small part of his claim true. When she saw the whole fence white‑washed, freshly coated, and even a stripe painted on the ground, she was almost speechless. She said, “Well, I never! There’s no getting around it. You can work whenever you want, Tom.” Then she added, “But it’s impressive when you do want to, I must say. Go on and play, but make sure you come back sometime within a week, or I’ll punish you.”

She was so impressed by his achievement that she led him into the closet, picked a fine apple, and gave it to him. She also gave him a short talk about how a treat becomes more valuable and tasty when it is earned without guilt through good effort. When she finished with a cheerful biblical quote, he grabbed a doughnut.

Then he left quickly and saw Sid starting up the outside stairs that led to the back rooms on the second floor. Clumps of dirt were everywhere, and the air was suddenly full of them. They swirled around Sid like a hailstorm. Before Aunt Polly could collect her thoughts and rush to help, six or seven clods had hit her, and Tom was already over the fence and gone. There was a gate, but he was too busy to use it. He felt calm now that he had agreed with Sid to point out his black thread and get him into trouble.

Tom walked around the block and entered a muddy alley behind his aunt’s cow barn. He soon got out of reach of capture and punishment, and hurried to the village square, where two “military” groups of boys had arranged to fight. Tom was the leader of one group, and his close friend Joe Harper led the other. The two commanders did not fight personally – that was left to the smaller boys – but they sat together on a hill and directed the battle through their aides. Tom’s side won a big victory after a long, hard fight. Then they counted the dead, exchanged prisoners, agreed on the terms for the next dispute, and set a date for the next battle. After that the groups lined up, marched away, and Tom walked home alone.

As he passed the house where Jeff Thatcher lived, he saw a new girl in the garden – a pretty blue‑eyed girl with yellow hair braided into two long tails, wearing a white summer dress and embroidered knickers. The newly crowned hero fell instantly without firing a shot. A certain Amy Lawrence disappeared from his heart, leaving no memory at all. He had thought he loved her madly; he had called his feeling adoration; but it turned out to be only a brief, fleeting liking. He had spent months trying to win her; she had admitted her feelings only a week ago; he had been the happiest and proudest boy in the world for just seven days, and in an instant she left his heart like a stranger who has finished a visit.

He admired this new girl with secret glances until he saw that she had noticed him. Then he pretended not to see her and started showing off in silly, childish ways to win her attention. He kept up this ridiculous behavior for a while. While he was doing some risky gymnastics, he looked aside and saw the little girl walking toward the house. Tom walked up to the fence and leaned on it, feeling sad and hoping she would stay a bit longer. She stopped for a moment on the steps and then moved toward the door. Tom let out a big sigh as she stepped onto the porch. But his face brightened right away when she tossed a pansy over the fence just before she disappeared.

The boy ran around and stopped a foot or two from the flower. He shaded his eyes with his hand and looked down the street as if he had seen something interesting. Then he picked up a straw and tried to balance it on his nose, tilting his head back. As he swayed from side to side, he moved closer and closer to the pansy. Finally his bare foot landed on it, his toes closed around it, and he hopped away with the treasure, disappearing around the corner. He stayed only a minute, just long enough to push the flower into his jacket, near his heart—or maybe near his stomach, since he wasn’t good at anatomy and didn’t worry about details.

He came back and hung around the fence until night, showing off as before. The girl never appeared again, but Tom tried to console himself by hoping she had been near a window and had noticed his attention. At last he walked home reluctantly, his head full of thoughts.

During dinner his mood was so high that his aunt wondered, “What’s gotten into the boy?” He took a good scolding for teasing Sid and didn’t seem to mind it at all. He tried to steal sugar right under his aunt’s nose and was hit on the knuckles for it. He said:

“Aunt, you don’t hit Sid when he takes it.”

“Well, Sid doesn’t bother anyone the way you do. If I weren’t watching, you’d always be after that sugar.”

She went into the kitchen, and Sid, feeling safe, reached for the sugar bowl. He was showing off in a way that almost annoyed Tom. But Sid’s fingers slipped, the bowl fell, and it broke. Tom was thrilled—so thrilled that he stayed completely quiet. He promised himself he would not say a word, even when his aunt entered, and would sit still until she asked who had caused the mess. Then he would answer, and there would be nothing as wonderful as watching his favourite pet “catch it.”

He was so full of excitement that he could barely keep himself together when the old lady came back. She stood over the broken bowl, her glasses flashing with anger. He thought, “Now it’s going to get serious!” In the next moment, he was lying on the floor. She lifted her hand, ready to strike again, when Tom shouted:

“Wait! Why are you hitting me?—Sid broke it!”

Aunt Polly stopped, confused, and Tom hoped for some kind sympathy. When she got her voice back, she only said:

“Fine! You didn’t get a scratch, I guess. You’ve been up to some other daring mischief while I wasn’t here, that’s for sure.”

Her conscience reminded her that she had done something wrong, and she wanted to say something kind and loving. But she thought that any words would be taken as an admission of guilt, and she was not allowed to admit fault. So she stayed silent and went about her duties with a heavy heart.

Tom sulked in a corner, complaining about his troubles. He knew that, deep down, his aunt felt humbled before him, and that thought gave him a grim satisfaction. He refused to give any signals and ignored everything around him. He could see her longing look at him sometimes, hidden behind tears, but he pretended not to notice.

He imagined himself dying from illness, his aunt leaning over him and begging for a single forgiving word. He would turn his face to the wall and die without saying it. He wondered how she would feel then. He also pictured being pulled from the river, dead, his wet hair and a finally quiet heart. He imagined her throwing herself onto him, her tears falling like rain, praying to God to bring him back, promising never to hurt him again. Yet he would lie there, cold and pale, making no response—a poor, silent sufferer whose sorrows had ended.

He dwelled on these thoughts so intensely that it felt like he was choking, his eyes watering until tears ran down the sides of his nose. He treated his sorrow as a precious treasure and could not stand any cheerful or noisy distraction. It felt too sacred for anything else to touch it. So when his cousin Mary entered, full of joy after a week’s visit to the countryside, she bringing song and sunshine, he rose and slipped away into darkness, leaving her happiness behind.

He wandered far from the usual places where boys hang out and looked for lonely spots that matched his mood. A log raft on the river caught his eye, and he sat on its edge, looking at the bleak, endless water. He wished he could simply drown, all at once and without thinking, instead of going through life's hard routine. Then he thought of his flower. He took it out, crumpled and wilted, and it made his sad happiness even stronger. He wondered if she would feel sorry for him if she knew. Would she cry and want to hug him to comfort him? Or would she turn away coldly like everyone else in this empty world? This picture gave him a painful pleasure that he replayed again and again in his mind, looking at it in different ways until it became worn out. Finally he stood up, sighed, and left into the darkness.

It was around half past nine or ten when he walked down the empty street to the house of the Beloved Stranger. He stopped for a moment. He heard no sound. A candle gave a dim light behind the curtain of a second‑floor window. Was the holy person inside? He climbed over the fence and slipped quietly through the bushes until he stood beneath the window. He stared up at it for a long time, feeling emotional. Then he lay down on the ground beneath it, lying on his back with his hands clasped over his chest, holding his wilted flower.  

He would die there, out in the cold, with no roof over his head, no kind hand to wipe the sweat from his brow, and no caring face to look over him in his great pain. She would see him when she looked out in the bright morning. She might shed a single tear for his lifeless body, or sigh at the sight of a young life so cruelly taken, so early.  

The window opened. A servant’s harsh voice broke the quiet, and a flood of water poured over the lying figure.  

The struggling hero jumped up with a relieved grunt. A whizzing sound like a flying object mixed with a muttered curse, followed by a crack like breaking glass. A small, blurry shape slipped over the fence and disappeared into the darkness.

Soon after, Tom, already in his nightclothes, was looking at his wet clothes by the light of an oil lamp when Sid woke up. He thought about making a remark, but decided not to because Tom’s stare felt threatening.

Tom went to sleep without saying any prayers, and Sid noticed the omission.
