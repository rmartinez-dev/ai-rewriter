Within minutes the news spread. About twelve small boats with men headed for McDougal’s cave. A crowded ferry followed soon after. Tom Sawyer was in the boat that carried Judge Thatcher.

When the cave door opened, a sad scene appeared in the fading light. Indian Joe lay on the ground, dead, his face close to the cracked door. It looked as if his eyes had been fixed on the light and life outside until the very last moment. Tom felt sorrow because he remembered how this villain had suffered. He felt pity, but he also felt a great sense of relief and safety. For the first time he understood how heavy the fear had been on him since he first spoke out against this cruel outcast.

Injun Joe’s bowie knife lay nearby, its blade split in two. He had chipped and hacked at the huge door beam, a slow and tiring job. It was useless work because the natural rock formed a sill outside the beam, and the knife could not cut that hard material; only the knife was damaged. Even if there had been no rock, the work would still have been pointless, because even if the beam were completely cut away, Injun Joe could not squeeze his body under the door, and he knew that. So he only hacked at the beam to have something to do, to pass the long, boring time, to keep his mind occupied.

Usually you could find a few bits of candle stuck in the cracks of this hallway, left by tourists, but there were none now. The prisoner had found them and eaten them. He had also managed to catch a few bats, and he ate them too, leaving only their claws. The poor creatures had starved to death.

Near the ground, a stalagmite had been growing slowly for ages, formed by water dripping from a stalactite above. The captive broke off the stalagmite and placed a stone on the leftover base, carving a shallow hollow to catch a precious drip that fell every three minutes, like the tick of a clock—a spoonful of water every 24 hours. That drop has been falling since the time of the new pyramids, the fall of Troy, the founding of Rome, the crucifixion of Christ, the creation of the British Empire, Columbus’s voyage, and when the Lexington massacre was “news.”

It is falling now, and it will keep falling when history’s afternoon has passed, when tradition fades, and when everything is lost in oblivion.  

Does everything have a purpose? Did this drop wait five thousand years to meet the needs of humans, like a tiny insect? Does it have another important task for the next ten thousand years? It does not matter.  

It has been many years since the unfortunate mixed‑blood person removed the stone to collect the precious drops, but even today visitors spend the most time looking at that sad stone and the slowly falling water when they visit McDougal’s Cave.  

Injun Joe’s cup is the top attraction in the cave; even “Aladdin’s Palace” cannot compare.  

Injun Joe was buried near the cave’s entrance. People came from towns, farms, and villages within seven miles, arriving in boats and wagons. They brought their children and many supplies. They said they felt the funeral was almost as satisfying as a hanging could have been.

The funeral stopped the petition for Injun Joe’s pardon from growing any further. The petition had already been signed by many people. There had been many tearful, passionate meetings. A group of overly emotional women was appointed to mourn loudly, to go around the governor, and to beg him to be merciful and ignore his duty. Injun Joe was believed to have killed five villagers, but that didn’t matter. If he were truly evil, many weak people would still sign a pardon petition and shed fake tears.

The next morning, after the funeral, Tom took Huck to a quiet place for an important talk. By now Huck had heard all about Tom’s adventure from the Welshman and the Widow Douglas. Tom said he thought there was one thing they hadn’t told him, and that was what he wanted to discuss now. Huck’s face looked sad. He said:

“I know what you’re talking about. You went into No. 2 and only found whiskey. No one told me it was you, but I knew it had to be you as soon as I heard about the whiskey. I also knew you didn’t have the money because you must have taken something from me and kept quiet about it, even if you stayed silent to everyone else. Tom, I’ve always felt we’ll never get hold of that loot.”

Tom replied, “Huck, I never told on that tavern keeper. You know his tavern was fine the Saturday I went to the picnic. Don’t you remember you were supposed to watch there that night?”

"Oh yes! It feels like a year ago. It was that night I followed Indian Joe to the widow’s house."

"You followed him?"

"Yes, but you have to keep it quiet. I think Indian Joe has friends who could turn against me and do me harm. If it hadn’t been for me, he’d be in Texas by now."

Then Huck told his whole adventure to Tom, who had only heard the Welshman’s part before.

"Well," Huck said, getting back to the main question, "whoever took the whiskey from No. 2 also took the money, I guess. Anyway, it’s lost for us, Tom."

"Huck, that money was never in No. 2!"

"What!" Huck looked at his friend closely. "Tom, have you found that money again?"

"Huck, it’s in the cave!"

Huck’s eyes lit up.

"Say that again, Tom."

"The money’s in the cave!"

"Tom—honest Indian, now—is it a joke or serious?"

"Serious, Huck—just as serious as I’ve ever been. Will you go in with me and help get it out?"

"I’ll do it! I’ll go if we can find a clear way in and not get lost."

"Huck, we can do that without any trouble at all."

"Great! What makes you think the money—"

"Huck, just wait until we get inside. If we don’t find it, I’ll give you my drum and everything I own. I promise."

"Okay—that’s a deal. When do we go?"

"Right now, if you’re ready. Are you strong enough?"

“Is the cave far away? I’ve been on my pins for only three or four days now, but I can’t walk more than a mile, Tom—at least I don’t think I could.”

“It’s about five miles in if you go the usual way, Huck, but there’s a very short cut that only I know about. I’ll take you straight to it in a small boat. I’ll float the boat down there and pull it back by myself. You won’t have to do any work.”

“Let’s start right away, Tom.”

“All right. We need some bread and meat, our pipes, a bag or two, a couple of kite‑strings, and some of those new‑fangled lucifer matches. I tell you, many times I wished I had some when I was in there before.”

A short time after noon the boys borrowed a small boat from a neighbor who was away, and set off at once. When they were several miles downstream from “Cave Hollow,” Tom said:

“Now you see this bluff looks the same all the way down from the cave hollow—no houses, no yards, just the same bushes. But do you see that white area up there where there’s been a landslide? That’s one of my markers. We’ll go ashore now.”

They landed.

“Now, Huck, where we’re standing you could touch the hole I dug out with a fishing pole. See if you can find it.”

Huck searched the whole area but found nothing. Tom proudly walked into a thick clump of sumac bushes and said:

“Here it is! Look, Huck, it’s the best hidden spot in the country.  
You have to keep it secret. I’ve always wanted to be a robber, but I knew I needed a place like this, and finding one was the hard part. Now we have it, and we’ll keep it quiet. We’ll only let Joe Harper and Ben Rogers join—because a gang is necessary; otherwise it wouldn’t be exciting. Tom Sawyer’s Gang—it sounds great, doesn’t it, Huck?”

“Sure, Tom. Who are we going to rob?”

“Most anyone. We’ll ambush people—that’s the usual way.”

“Do we kill them?”

“Not always. We’ll keep them in the cave until they pay a ransom.”

“What’s a ransom?”

“Money. We make them raise as much as they can, from their friends. If after a year they haven’t paid, we kill them. That’s the usual rule. We don’t kill the women, though. We lock the women up but don’t kill them. They’re always pretty and scared. We take their watches and other things, but we take off our hats and speak politely. No one is as polite as robbers, you’ll see in any book. The women end up loving us, and after a week or two in the cave they stop crying. Then you can’t get them to leave. If you drive them out, they’ll come right back. That’s how it’s written in all the books.”

“That’s really bold, Tom. I think it’s even better than being a pirate.”

“Yes, it’s better in some ways, because it’s close to home and the circus and all that.”

By then everything was ready, and the boys went into the cave, Tom leading. They walked to the far end of the tunnel, then tied their kite‑strings together tightly and kept moving. After a few steps they reached a spring, and Tom felt a shiver run through him. He showed Huck a piece of candle wick stuck to a lump of clay on the wall and told him how he and Becky had watched the flame flicker and go out.

The boys lowered their voices to whispers because the silence and darkness made them feel uneasy. They continued and soon followed Tom’s other passage until they came to the “jumping‑off place.” The candles showed that it was not a cliff but only a steep clay hill about twenty or thirty feet high. Tom whispered, “Now I’ll show you something, Huck.”

He held his candle up and said, “Look around the corner as far as you can. Do you see that? There, on the big rock over there, there is candle smoke.”

“Tom, it’s a cross!” Huck said.

“Now where’s your Number Two? ‘Under the cross,’ right? That’s where I saw Injun Joe light his candle, Huck!”

Huck stared at the strange sign for a moment, then said in a shaky voice, “Tom, let’s get out of here!”

“What! And leave the treasure?”

“Yes—leave it. Injun Joe’s ghost is around there, I’m sure.”

“No, it isn’t, Huck. It would be where he died, at the mouth of the cave, five miles from here.”

“No, Tom, it wouldn’t. It would be near the treasure. I know how ghosts work, and you do too.”

Tom started to worry that Huck might be right. Doubts filled his mind. Then an idea came to him.

“Look here, Huck, what fools we’re making of ourselves! Indian Joe’s ghost isn’t going to come back where there’s a cross!”

Huck understood and it had an effect.

“Tom, I didn’t think of that. But you’re right. The cross is lucky for us. I think we should go down there and look for that box.”

Tom went first, cutting rough steps into the clay hill as he went down. Huck followed. Four tunnels opened from the small cavern where the big rock stood. The boys checked three of them but found nothing. In the tunnel closest to the base of the rock they found a small recess with a blanket spread out, an old suspenders, some bacon rind, and the chewed‑up bones of a few birds. But there was no money box. They searched the area again, but in vain. Tom said:

“He said under the cross. This is the closest spot to being under the cross. It can’t be under the rock itself, because the rock sits solid on the ground.”

They looked everywhere once more, then sat down discouraged. Huck had no ideas. After a while Tom said:

“Look here, Huck, there are footprints and some candle‑wax on the clay on one side of this rock, but not on the other sides. What does that mean? I bet the money is under the rock. I’m going to dig in the clay.”

“That’s a good idea, Tom!” Huck replied eagerly.

Tom’s “real Barlow” went out at once, and after digging only four inches he hit wood.

“Hey, Huck—did you hear that?”

Huck started to dig and scratch right away. Soon they uncovered and removed some boards. The boards had hidden a natural gap that went under the rock. Tom slipped into the gap and held his candle as far under the rock as he could, but he said he couldn’t see the end of the crack. He suggested they explore it. He crouched and went through; the narrow passage gradually went down. He followed its winding path, first to the right, then to the left, with Huck close behind. Tom turned a short curve and suddenly shouted:

“My goodness, Huck, look here!”

It was the treasure chest, just as they had expected, sitting in a snug little cavern. Along with it were an empty powder keg, a couple of guns in leather cases, two or three pairs of old moccasins, a leather belt, and some other junk soaked with water drops.

“Got it at last!” Huck said, scooping the tarnished coins with his hand. “Wow, we’re rich, Tom!”

“Huck, I always thought we’d find it. It’s hard to believe, but we really have it! Let’s not waste time here. Let’s get it out. Let me see if I can lift the box.”

The box weighed about fifty pounds. Tom managed to lift it, though awkwardly, but he couldn’t carry it easily.

“I figured that,” he said. “They carried it like it was heavy that day at the haunted house. I noticed that. I was right to think about getting the small bags.”

They soon put the money into the bags, and the boys carried everything up to the crossing rock.

“Now let’s fetch the guns and the other things,” Huck said.

“No, Huck—leave them there. They’re just the tricks we’ll use when we go robbing. We’ll keep them there all the time, and we’ll have our parties there, too. It’s a very snug place for parties.”

“What parties?”

“I don’t know. But robbers always have parties, and we have to have them, too. Come on, Huck, we’ve been in here a long time. It’s getting late, I think. I’m hungry, too. We’ll eat and smoke when we get to the skiff.”

They soon came out into a clump of sumac bushes, looked around carefully, saw that the coast was clear, and then they ate and smoked in the skiff. As the sun fell toward the horizon they pushed off and began to move. Tom skimmed along the shore in the long twilight, chatting cheerfully with Huck, and they landed shortly after dark.

“Now, Huck,” said Tom, “we’ll hide the money in the loft of the widow’s woodshed. I’ll come up in the morning, we’ll count it and divide it, and then we’ll find a safe place in the woods for it. Just stay quiet here and watch the stuff until I run and hook Benny Taylor’s little wagon. I won’t be gone long.”

He disappeared, then returned with the wagon, put the two small sacks inside, tossed some old rags on top, and set off, dragging his load behind him. When the boys reached the Welshman’s house, they stopped to rest. Just as they were about to move on, the Welshman stepped out and said:

“Hello, who’s that?”

“Huck and Tom Sawyer.”

“Great! Come with me, boys. You’re keeping everyone waiting. Here—hurry up, run ahead—I’ll pull the wagon for you. It’s not as light as it looks. Does it have bricks inside? Or old metal?”

“Old metal,” Tom said.

“I thought so. The boys in this town would rather spend a lot of time hunting for a few pieces of scrap iron to sell to the foundry than work a regular job that would earn them twice as much. That’s just human nature—hurry up, hurry up!”

The boys wanted to know why they had to hurry.

“Don’t worry; you’ll see when we get to Widow Douglas’s house.”

Huck said, a little nervous—he was used to being blamed for things he didn’t do:

“Mr. Jones, we haven’t done anything wrong.”

The Welshman laughed.

“Well, I’m not sure, Huck, my boy. I don’t know about that. Aren’t you and the widow good friends?”

“Yes. She’s been a good friend to me, anyway.”

“Okay then. What are you scared of?”

Huck didn’t finish thinking about the question before he and Tom were pushed into Mrs. Douglas’s drawing room. Mr. Jones left the wagon by the door and followed them inside.

The room was brightly lit, and everyone important in the village was there. The Thatchers, the Harpers, the Rogerses, Aunt Polly, Sid, Mary, the minister, the editor, and many others were all dressed in their finest clothes. The widow welcomed the boys as warmly as anyone could welcome two dirty, greasy-looking boys. They were covered in mud and candle grease. Aunt Polly turned bright red with embarrassment, frowned, and shook her head at Tom. Nobody felt as embarrassed as the two boys did, however. Mr. Jones said:

“Tom wasn’t home yet, so I gave up on him. But I ran into him and Huck right at my door, and I quickly brought them inside.”

“And you did just right,” said the widow. “Come with me, boys.”

She led them to a bedroom and said:

“Now wash up and get dressed. Here are two new sets of clothes—shirts, socks, everything you need. They’re Huck’s—no, no, thanks, Huck—Mr. Jones bought one and I bought the other. They’ll both fit you. Put them on. We’ll wait—come down when you’re clean enough.”

Then she left.
