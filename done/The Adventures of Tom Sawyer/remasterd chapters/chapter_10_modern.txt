The two boys ran on toward the village, silent with terror. From time to time they looked back over their shoulders, worried that something might be following them. Every tree stump that appeared in their way looked like a man, an enemy, and made them gasp for breath. As they hurried past the scattered cottages near the village, the barking of the watchdogs seemed to make their feet move even faster.

“If we can only reach the old tannery before we collapse!” Tom whispered, gasping for air. “I can’t keep going much longer.”

Huckleberry’s hard breathing was his only answer. The boys kept their eyes on the place they hoped to reach and pushed themselves to get there. They got closer and closer, and finally, chest to chest, they burst through the open door and fell, grateful and exhausted, into the cool shadows inside. After a while their hearts slowed, and Tom whispered:

“Huckleberry, what do you think will happen now?”

“If Doctor Robinson dies, I think they’ll hang him.”

“Do you really think that?”

“Yes, I know it, Tom.”

Tom thought a moment, then said:

“Who will tell? Us?”

“What are you talking about? Suppose something happened and Injun Joe didn’t get hanged? He’d still kill us someday, as surely as we’re lying here.”

“That’s exactly what I was thinking, Huck.”

“If anyone should tell, let Muff Potter do it, if he’s foolish enough. He’s usually drunk enough.”

Tom said nothing and kept thinking. After a while he whispered:

“Huck, Muff Potter doesn’t know anything. How could he tell?”

“What makes you think he doesn’t know?”

“Because he’d just taken that blow when Indian Joe did it. Do you think he could see anything? Do you think he knew anything?”

“Honestly, that’s right, Tom!”

“And besides, maybe that blow was for him!”

“No, that’s not likely, Tom. He’d been drinking, I could see that, and he always does. When dad is drunk, you could hit him over the head with a club and it wouldn’t affect him. He says that himself. It’s the same with Muff Potter, of course. But if a man were completely sober, I think that blow might kill him; I don’t know.”

After another moment of silence, Tom said:

“Hucky, are you sure you can stay quiet?”

“Tom, we have to stay quiet. You know that. That Indian devil won’t bother us more than a couple of cats if we don’t tell anyone, and they won’t hang him. Now, Tom, let’s swear to each other to keep quiet. That’s what we have to do.”

“I agree. It’s the best way. Will you hold hands and swear that we—”

“Oh no, that won’t work for this. That’s fine for small everyday matters, especially with girls, because they often change their minds and tell others if they’re upset. But this is a serious matter, it involves blood, and it needs a proper oath.”

Tom fully embraced the idea. It felt deep, dark, and terrible; the time, the situation, and the surroundings all matched it. He lifted a clean pine shingle that lay in the moonlight, took a small piece of “red kelp” from his pocket, used the moonlight on his work, and laboriously wrote these lines. He stressed each slow downward stroke by pressing his tongue against his teeth, and eased the pressure on the upward strokes.

“Huck Finn and Tom Sawyer swear they will stay silent about this, and they wish they could fall dead in their tracks if they ever tell and rot.”

Huckleberry admired Tom’s skill at writing and the greatness of his language. He immediately took a pin from his lapel and was about to prick himself, but Tom said:

“Wait! Don’t do that. The pin is brass. It could have verdigris on it.”

“What’s verdigris?”

“It’s poison. That’s what it is. If you swallow a little, you’ll find out.”

So Tom unwound the thread from one of his needles. Each boy pricked the pad of his thumb and squeezed out a drop of blood. After many squeezes, Tom managed to write his initials, using the tip of his little finger as a pen. He then taught Huckleberry how to draw an H and an F, and the oath was finished. They buried the shingle near the wall, performing simple ceremonies and sayings. They believed the bonds that held their tongues were now locked, and the key was thrown away.

A figure slipped quietly through a gap at the other end of the ruined building, but they did not notice it.

“Tom,” whispered Huckleberry, “does this stop us from ever telling—always?”

“Of course it does. It doesn’t matter what happens, we have to stay silent. We’d die right away—don’t you know that?”

“Yes, I think that’s right.”

They kept whispering for a while. Then a dog let out a long, mournful howl just outside, only about ten feet away. The boys suddenly clutched each other in terror.

“Which one of us is he talking about?” gasped Huckleberry.

“I don’t know—look through the crack. Quickly!”

“No, you, Tom!”

“I can’t—I can’t do it, Huck!”

“Please, Tom. It’s happening again!”

“Oh Lord, thank God!” whispered Tom. “I recognize his voice. It’s Bull Harbison.”

“Oh, that’s good—I tell you, Tom, I was scared to death; I would have guessed it was a stray dog.”

The dog howled again. The boys’ hearts sank once more.

“Oh no! That’s not Bull Harbison!” whispered Huckleberry. “Do it, Tom!”

Tom, shaking with fear, looked through the crack. His whisper was barely audible:

“Oh, Huck, it’s a stray dog!”

“Quick, Tom, quick! Who does he mean?”

“Huck, he must mean both of us—we’re together.”

“Oh, Tom, I think we’re doomed. I’m sure where I’m going. I’ve been so wicked.”

“Dad, get it! This is what happens when you skip school and do everything you’re told not to do. I could have been good, like Sid, if I had tried—but no, I wouldn’t, of course. But if I ever get out of this, I swear I’ll just stay in Sunday‑school!” Tom began to sniffle a little.

"You’re bad!" Huck began to sniffle too. "Damn it, Tom Sawyer, you’re just a kid compared to me. Oh my gosh, I wish I only had half your chance."

Tom stopped and whispered, "Look, Huck, look! He’s got his back to us!"

Huck looked, his heart full of joy.

"Well, he does, for sure! Did he before?"

"Yes, he did. But I, like a fool, never thought about it. This is crazy, you know. Now, who could he be thinking of?"

The howling stopped. Tom listened carefully. "Shh! What’s that?" he whispered.

"It sounds like pigs grunting. No—it’s someone snoring, Tom," Huck said.

"That’s it! Where is it, Huck?" Tom asked.

"I think it’s down at the other end. It sounds like that, anyway. Dad used to sleep there sometimes, together with the pigs, but thank God he only moves things when he snores. Besides, I guess he isn’t coming back to this town any more."

The spirit of adventure rose in the boys’ souls once more.

"Huck, do you dare to go if I lead?" Tom asked.

"I’m not sure. Tom, what if it’s Injun Joe!" Huck replied.

Tom was scared. But soon the urge came back strong, and the boys agreed to try, promising to run away if the snoring stopped. So they crept down, one after another. When they were only five steps from the sleeper, Tom stepped on a stick, and it snapped loudly. The man moaned, twitched a little, and his face was lit by the moonlight. It was Muff Potter. The boys’ hearts stopped, and their hopes faded, but when the man moved they felt relief. They slipped out through the broken weather‑boarding and stopped a short distance away to say a quick goodbye. Then a long, mournful howl rose into the night again! They turned and saw the strange dog standing just a few feet from where Potter lay, its nose pointing upward.  

“Oh, gee, it’s him!” the boys shouted at the same time.  

“Hey, Tom—people say a stray dog was howling around Johnny Miller’s house around midnight, about two weeks ago; and a whip‑poor‑will landed on the banister and sang the same night; and nobody has died there yet.”  

“Well, I know that. And what if they’re wrong? Didn’t Gracie Miller fall into a kitchen fire and get badly burned the next Saturday?”  

“Yes, but she’s not dead. In fact, she’s getting better.”  

“Okay, just wait and see. She’s a goner, as sure as Muff Potter is a goner. That’s what the black folks say, and they know these kinds of things, Huck.”

Then they went their separate ways, thinking. When Tom slipped in through his bedroom window, it was almost morning. He undressed very carefully and fell asleep, feeling proud that no one knew about his adventure. He didn’t realize that the softly snoring Sid was awake and had been for an hour.  

When Tom woke up, Sid was dressed and gone. The light was late, and the atmosphere felt strange. He was startled. Why hadn’t anyone called him—pushed him to get up, as usual? The thought made him uneasy.  

In five minutes he was dressed and downstairs, feeling sore and sleepy. The family was still at the table, but they had finished breakfast. There was no scolding voice, only averted eyes, a quiet, and a solemn mood that sent a chill to the guilty man’s heart. He sat down and tried to act cheerful, but it was difficult. It didn’t bring a smile or a response, and he fell silent, letting his heart sink.

After breakfast his aunt pulled him aside. Tom almost felt hopeful that she would punish him, but she didn’t.  

His aunt began to cry. She asked how he could hurt her so much. Then she told him to keep going, to ruin himself and bring her sorrowful gray hairs until she died, because she could no longer try.  

That felt worse than a thousand beatings, and Tom’s heart hurt more than his body. He cried, begged for forgiveness, and promised to change again and again. He was finally dismissed, feeling that he had received only a partial forgiveness and a weak confidence.  

He left so miserable that he couldn’t even feel angry at Sid, so Sid’s quick escape through the back gate was unnecessary. He walked to school feeling gloomy and sad, and took his punishment with Joe Harper for skipping school the day before, looking like someone whose mind was occupied by serious problems and who didn’t care about small things.  

He sat down, rested his elbows on the desk and his hands on his jaws, and stared at the wall with a hard, exhausted look. His elbow pressed against something hard. After a long time he slowly changed his position and, with a sigh, picked up the object. It was wrapped in paper. He unfolded it. A huge sigh escaped him and his heart seemed to break. It was his brass and iron knob!  

This small thing was the final straw.
