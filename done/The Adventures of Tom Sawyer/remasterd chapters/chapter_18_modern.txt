That was Tom’s big secret – his plan to go back home with his pirate brothers and attend their own funerals. They had rowed to the Missouri shore on a log at dusk on Saturday. They landed five or six miles downstream from the village. They slept in the woods on the edge of town until almost sunrise. Then they slipped through back lanes and alleys and finally fell asleep in the church gallery among a mess of broken benches.  

At breakfast on Monday morning, Aunt Polly and Mary were very kind to Tom and paid close attention to his needs. They talked more than usual. During the conversation Aunt Polly said:  

“​Well, I’m not saying it wasn’t a clever joke, Tom, to keep everyone worried for almost a week while you boys had fun. But it’s a shame you could be so heartless and make me suffer. If you could have come back on that log for your funeral, you could have let me know somehow that you weren’t really dead, just gone.”  

“Yes, you could have done that, Tom,” Mary said. “I think you would have if you had thought of it.”  

“Would you, Tom?” Aunt Polly asked, her face looking hopeful. “Tell me, would you have, if you had thought about it?”  

Tom hesitated. “I… well, I don’t know. It would have ruined everything.”  

“Tom, I hoped you loved me that much,” Aunt Polly said sadly, making Tom uneasy. “It would have meant something if you had cared enough to think about it, even if you didn’t actually do it.”  

“Now, Auntie, that isn’t any harm,” Mary begged. “It’s just Tom’s reckless way—he’s always in such a hurry that he never thinks about anything.”

“It’s such a shame,” Sid would have thought. “And Sid would have come and done it, too. Tom, one day you’ll look back, when it’s too late, and wish you’d cared a little more about me, when it would have cost you so little.”

“Now, auntie, you know I care for you,” Tom said.

“I’d know it better if you acted more like it.”

“I wish I had thought of it earlier,” Tom said with a repentant tone. “But I still dreamed about you. That’s something, isn’t it?”

“It’s not much—a cat does that—but it’s better than nothing. What did you dream?”

“Wednesday night I dreamed you were sitting by the bed, Sid was by the wood box, and Mary was next to him.”

“Well, that’s what we did. We always do. I’m glad your dreams can even think about us.”

“And I dreamed that Mrs. Harper was here.”

“Yes, she was here! Did you have any other dreams?”

“Oh, many. But they’re so vague now.”

“Can you try to remember? Can’t you?”

“It feels like the wind… the wind blew the—”

“Try harder, Tom! The wind did blow something. Come on!”

Tom pressed his fingers to his forehead for a minute, then said, “I’ve got it! It blew out the candle!”

“Thank goodness! Keep going, Tom—keep going!”

“And I think you said, ‘I believe that the door—’”

“Keep going, Tom!”

“Just give me a moment to think—just a moment. Yes, you said you believed the door was open.”

“I’m sitting here, I did! Right, Mary? Go on!”

“Then—you know—I’m not sure, but it seems like you told Sid to—”

“Well? What did I make him do, Tom? What did I make him do?”

“You made him—you— Oh, you made him shut up.”

“Well, for heaven’s sake! I’ve never heard that in all my life! Don’t tell me there’s nothing in dreams any more. Sereny Harper will hear about this before I’m an hour older. I’d like to see her deal with this and her talk about superstition. Go on, Tom!”

“Oh, it’s getting as clear as day now. Earlier you said I wasn’t bad, just mischievous and a little crazy, and not any more responsible than— than—I think it was a colt, or something.”

“And that’s how it was! Goodness gracious! Go on, Tom!”

“And then you began to cry.”

“So I did. It wasn’t the first time. And then—”

“Then Mrs. Harper began to cry and said Joe was the same, and she wished she hadn’t hit him for taking cream when she had thrown it out herself—”

“Tom! The spirit was on you! You were prophesying—that’s what you were doing! Good heavens, go on, Tom!”

“Then Sid said— he said—”

“I don’t think I said anything,” said Sid.

“Yes you did, Sid,” said Mary.

“Shut up and let Tom go on! What did he say, Tom?”

“He said—I think he said he hoped I was better off wherever I had gone, but if I’d been better sometimes—”

“Did you hear that? Those were his exact words!”

“And you stopped him quickly.”

“I swear I did! There must have been an angel there. There was an angel somewhere!”

“And Mrs. Harper told about Joe scaring her with a firecracker, and you told about Peter and the pain‑killer—”

“Just as true as my life!”

“And then we talked a lot about moving the river for us, about having the funeral on Sunday, and then you and old Miss Harper hugged and cried, and she left.”

“It really happened exactly like that! It happened exactly like that, as sure as I am sitting on these tracks. Tom, you couldn't have described it better, as if you had seen it yourself! And then what? Tell me, Tom!”

“Then I thought you prayed for me, and I could see you and hear every word you said. You went to bed, and I felt so sorry that I wrote on a piece of sycamore bark, ‘We aren't dead—we are just off being pirates,’ and placed it on the table by the candle. Then you looked so peaceful, lying there asleep, that I leaned over and kissed you on the lips.”

“Did you, Tom, did you! I forgive you completely for that!” She hugged the boy tightly, making him feel like the most guilty villain.

“It was very kind, even though it was only a dream,” Sid said aloud.

"Be quiet, Sid! A person acts the same in a dream as when they're awake. Here is a big Milum apple I’ve been saving for you, Tom, in case you are ever found again—now go on to school. I’m thankful to God, our Father, that I have you back. He is patient and merciful to those who believe in Him and keep His word, even though I know I’m unworthy of it. If only the worthy received His blessings and His help in the rough places, few would smile here or ever find peace when the long night comes. Go on, Sid, Mary, Tom—leave now—you’ve bothered me long enough."

The children left for school, and the old lady went to call on Mrs. Harper and chase away her realism with Tom’s marvelous dream. Sid gave himself better judgment than to say the thought that was in his mind as he left the house. It was this: “It’s a pretty thin dream, but at least it has no mistakes!”

What a hero Tom has become now! He no longer skips and prances. He walks with a dignified swagger, like a pirate who knows everyone is watching him. And they really are watching; he pretends not to notice the glances or hear the comments as he passes, but they feed him like food and drink. Smaller boys follow him, proud to be seen with him and tolerated by him, as if he were the drummer at the front of a parade or the elephant leading a circus into town. Boys his own age act as if they don’t know he has been away at all, but they still watch him with envy. They would give anything for his tanned skin and his famous reputation, and Tom would not give either up for a circus.

At school the children praised him and Joe so much, and showed such admiration with their eyes, that the two heroes soon became annoyingly “stuck‑up.” They started to tell their adventures to eager listeners – and they had only just begun; with imaginations like theirs, the stories would never end. Finally, when they took out their pipes and calmly puffed away, they reached the highest point of glory.

Tom decided that he could now be independent of Becky Thatcher. Glory was enough for him. He would live for glory. Now that he was distinguished, maybe she would want to “make up.” He let her try—she would see that he could be as indifferent as other people.

Soon Becky arrived. Tom pretended not to see her. He moved away and joined a group of boys and girls and started talking. He noticed that she was hopping happily back and forth, her face flushed and her eyes dancing. She pretended to be busy chasing schoolmates and laughed loudly when she “caught” one. But she always made her catches near him, and she seemed to glance at him on purpose. That pleased his vanity. Instead of winning him over, it only encouraged him more, and he tried harder not to show that he knew what she was doing.

After a while she stopped playing and moved uncertainly, sighing once or twice and looking furtively, wistfully toward Tom. She saw that Tom was now talking more with Amy Lawrence than with anyone else. A sharp pang hit her, and she felt disturbed and uneasy. She tried to leave, but her feet were unsteady and carried her back to the group.

She spoke to a girl standing near Tom, with false cheerfulness:

“Why, Mary Austin! You naughty girl, why didn’t you come to Sunday school?”

“I did come—didn’t you see me?”

“No! Did you? Where did you sit?”

“I was in Miss Peters’ class, where I always go. I saw you.”

“Did you? It’s funny—I didn’t see you. I wanted to tell you about the picnic.”

“Oh, that’s great. Who’s organizing it?”

“My mom is letting me have one.”

“Oh, wonderful; I hope she’ll let me come.”

“She will. The picnic is for me. She’ll let anyone I invite come, and I want you there.”

“That’s very kind. When will it be?”

“Soon. Probably during the holidays.”

“Oh, it’ll be so much fun! Will there be all the girls and boys?”

“Yes, everyone who’s my friend—or wants to be.” She glanced quickly at Tom, but he kept talking with Amy Lawrence about the terrible storm on the island, how lightning smashed the big sycamore tree “to pieces” while he was “standing three feet away.”

“May I come?” asked Grace Miller.

“Yes.”

“And me?” asked Sally Rogers.

“Yes.”

“And me, too?” asked Susy Harper. “And Joe?”

“Yes.”

And so it went, with joyful clapping as everyone begged for invitations except Tom and Amy. Then Tom turned away calmly, still talking, and took Amy with him. Becky’s lips trembled and tears filled her eyes; she tried to hide it with forced cheerfulness and kept chatting, but the excitement left the picnic and everything else. She slipped away as quickly as she could, hid herself, and had what girls call “a good cry.” She sat moody, feeling hurt, until the bell rang. She stood up, a vindictive look in her eye, shook her braided hair, and said she knew what she would do.

During recess Tom kept flirting with Amy, feeling proudly happy. He also wandered around looking for Becky so he could upset her with his act. Finally he saw her, but his mood suddenly dropped. She was sitting comfortably on a small bench behind the school, reading a picture book with Alfred Temple. They were so absorbed, their heads close together over the book, that they seemed unaware of anything else.

Jealousy boiled inside Tom. He began to hate himself for ruining the chance Becky had given him for a reconciliation. He called himself a fool and used every harsh word he could think of. He wanted to cry out of frustration. Amy chatted cheerfully as they walked, her heart singing, but Tom could not speak. He didn’t hear what Amy was saying, and whenever she paused and looked at him, he could only give an awkward, often wrong, answer.

He kept moving to the back of the school again and again, forcing himself to watch the painful scene. He could not stop. It drove him crazy to think that Becky seemed not to notice he was even alive. Yet she did notice, and she knew she was winning her fight too, and she was glad to see him suffer as she had suffered.

Amy’s cheerful chatter became unbearable. Tom tried to hint that he had things to take care of, things that had to be done, and that time was running out. But his hints didn’t work—the girl kept talking happily. Tom thought, “Great, I’m never going to get rid of her.” Finally he had to go to his tasks, and she said simply that she would be “around” when school let out. He hurried away, hating her for it.

Tom’s mind filled with angry thoughts. “Any other boy! Any boy in the whole town except that Saint Louis smart‑aleck who thinks he’s so fancy and aristocratic! Fine, I beat you the first day you came to this town, mister, and I’ll beat you again! Just wait until I catch you. I’ll…"

He acted as if he were fighting an imaginary boy. He punched the air, kicked, and pretended to strike. He shouted, “Oh, you think you can? You shout enough, huh? Let that teach you!” He finished his pretend beating and felt satisfied.

Tom went home at noon. His conscience could no longer stand Amy’s happy gratitude, and his jealousy could not bear the other distress. Becky went back to looking at pictures with Alfred, but as the minutes passed and Tom did not appear, her triumph began to fade. She lost interest, felt serious and absent‑minded, and then became sad. A few times she listened for a footstep, but it was only false hope; no Tom came. At last she became completely miserable and wished she hadn’t taken it so far.

When poor Alfred saw that he was losing her, he didn’t know what to do. He kept shouting, “Oh, here’s a funny one! Look at this!” She finally lost patience and said, “Don’t bother me! I don’t care about them!” She burst into tears, got up, and walked away.

Alfred dropped beside her and tried to comfort her, but she said, “Go away and leave me alone, can’t you! I hate you!” The boy stopped, wondering what he could have done—she had said she would look at pictures all afternoon—and she walked on, crying.

Then Alfred went into the empty schoolhouse. He felt humiliated and angry. He quickly realized the truth: the girl had used him just to vent her spite toward Tom Sawyer. He didn’t hate Tom any less when he thought of this. He wished there was a way to get Tom into trouble without risking himself. Tom’s spelling book lay on the desk. This was his chance. He opened it to the afternoon lesson and poured ink on the page.

Becky looked through the window behind him and saw what happened. She walked away without noticing how she felt. She started heading home, planning to find Tom and tell him. He would be grateful, and their problems would be solved. But before she was halfway home, she changed her mind. The memory of how Tom had treated her when she talked about her picnic came back strongly and made her feel ashamed. She decided to let him be punished for the damaged spelling book and to hate him forever.
