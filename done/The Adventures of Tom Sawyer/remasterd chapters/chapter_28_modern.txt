That night Tom and Huck were ready for their adventure. They hung around the tavern’s neighborhood until after nine. One of them watched the alley from a distance and the other watched the tavern door. No one entered or left the alley, and no one who looked like the Spaniard entered or left the tavern. The night looked clear, so Tom went home with the agreement that if it got very dark, Huck should come and “meow,” and then Tom would slip out and try the keys. But the night stayed bright, and Huck ended his watch and went to bed in an empty sugar barrel around midnight.

On Tuesday the boys had the same bad luck. The same happened on Wednesday. Thursday night seemed better. Tom slipped out early, carrying his aunt’s old tin lantern and a large towel to cover it. He hid the lantern in Huck’s sugar barrel and started the watch. An hour before midnight the tavern closed and its lights—the only ones nearby—were turned off. No Spaniard was seen. No one entered or left the alley. Everything looked promising. Darkness covered everything, and the perfect stillness was broken only by the occasional low rumble of distant thunder.

Tom took his lantern, lit it using the hogshead, and wrapped it tightly in a towel. The two adventurers slipped through the darkness toward the tavern. Huck stood guard while Tom felt his way into the alley.  

Then a long period of anxious waiting weighed on Huck like a heavy mountain. He began to wish he could see a flash from the lantern. It would scare him, but at least it would show that Tom was still alive. It felt like hours since Tom disappeared. Huck wondered if Tom had fainted, maybe died, or if his heart had stopped from fear and excitement.  

Feeling uneasy, Huck kept moving closer to the alley. He feared terrible things and briefly expected a disaster that would take his breath away. He could hardly breathe, taking only tiny breaths, and his heart was beating so fast it seemed it would soon wear out.  

Suddenly a flash of light appeared and Tom rushed past him, shouting, “Run! Run for your life!” He didn’t need to repeat it; one shout was enough. Huck was already running as fast as he could.  

The boys didn’t stop until they reached the shed of an abandoned slaughterhouse at the edge of the village. As soon as they entered the shelter, a storm broke out and rain poured down. As soon as Tom caught his breath, he said:

“Huck, it was terrible! I tried two of the keys as gently as I could, but they made such a huge noise that I could barely breathe. I was so scared. They wouldn't turn in the lock either. Without thinking, I grabbed the knob and the door opened! It wasn’t locked. I hopped in, shook off the towel, and—Great Caesar’s Ghost!

“What!—what did you see, Tom?”

“Huck, I almost stepped on Injun Joe’s hand!”

“No!”

“Yes! He was lying there, fast asleep on the floor, with his old eye patch and his arms spread out.”

“Lord, what did you do? Did he wake up?”

“No, he didn’t move at all. He was probably drunk. I just grabbed the towel and ran!”

“I would never have thought of the towel, I bet!”

“Well, I would. My aunt would get really angry if I lost it.”

“Tell me, Tom, did you see that box?”

“Huck, I didn’t stop to look around. I didn’t see any box, I didn’t see any cross. All I saw were a bottle and a tin cup on the floor near Injun Joe. Yes, I saw two barrels and a lot more bottles in the room. Do you understand now why that room feels haunted?”

“How?”

“Because it’s haunted by whiskey! Maybe all the Temperance Taverns have a haunted room, huh, Huck?”

“Well, maybe that’s true. Who would have thought such a thing? But Tom, now’s a good time to get that box, if Injun Joe is drunk.”

“That’s right! You try it!”

Huck shuddered.

“Well, no—I don’t think so.”

“No, Huck, I don’t think so. One bottle with Injun Joe isn’t enough. If we had three, he’d be drunk enough and I’d do it.”

Tom paused, then said:

“Listen, Huck, don’t try that again until we’re sure Injun Joe isn’t inside. It’s too dangerous. If we keep watch every night, we’ll be sure to see him leave sometime, and then we can grab the box faster than lightning.”

“Well, I agree. I’ll watch all night, and I’ll do it every night if you handle the other part.”

“All right, I will. All you have to do is go up Hooper Street for a block and knock. If I’m asleep, you can throw some gravel at the window to wake me.”

“Agreed, that’s fine!”

“Now, Huck, the storm is over and I’m going home. It will be daylight in a few hours. Will you go back and keep watching?”

“I said I would, Tom, and I will. I’ll stay at that tavern every night for a year! I’ll sleep all day and stand watch all night.”

“That’s fine. So, where are you going to sleep?”

In Ben Rogers’ hayloft, he lets me stay, and his father’s black worker, Uncle Jake, does too. I carry water for Uncle Jake whenever he asks. Whenever I ask him for a little food, he gives me something if he can. He’s a very good man, Tom. He likes me because I never act like I’m better than him. Sometimes I sit down and eat with him. You don’t have to tell anyone. When you’re really hungry, you have to do things you wouldn’t normally want to do.  

If I don’t need you during the day, I’ll let you sleep. I won’t bother you. If you notice something happening at night, just walk around it and meow.
