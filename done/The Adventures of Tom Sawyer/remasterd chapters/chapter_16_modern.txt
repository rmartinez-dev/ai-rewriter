After dinner the whole group went to the sand bar to look for turtle eggs. They stuck sticks into the sand, and when they found a soft place they knelt down and dug with their hands. Sometimes they pulled out fifty or sixty eggs from one hole. The eggs were round and white, a little smaller than an English walnut. That night they had a famous fried‑egg feast, and they ate again on Friday morning.  

After breakfast they ran out onto the bar, shouting and hopping, and chased each other in circles. They took off their clothes as they went, until they were naked, and then they kept playing far out in the shallow water of the bar, against the strong current. The current kept tripping their legs, which made it even more fun. From time to time they gathered in a group and splashed water on each other’s faces with their hands. They moved closer, turning their faces away to avoid the spray, and then grabbed and wrestled. One man ducked under his neighbor, and soon all of them were tangled together, with white arms and legs everywhere. They came up gasping, laughing, coughing, and trying to catch their breath at the same time.  

When they were very tired, they ran back and lay down on the hot, dry sand. They covered themselves with sand, then soon ran back to the water and did the same game again. At the end they realised that their naked skin looked like flesh‑coloured “tights.” They drew a circle in the sand and put on a little circus show, with three clowns, because none of them wanted to give up the best spot to anyone else.

Next they took out their marbles and played “knucks,” “ringtaw,” and “keeps” until the games became boring. Then Joe and Huck went for another swim, but Tom stayed on the shore. He had taken off his trousers and accidentally knocked the string of rattlesnake rattles off his ankle. He wondered why he had never gotten a cramp before without that strange charm. He didn’t go back into the water until he found the rattles, and by then the other boys were already tired and ready to rest.

The boys slowly drifted apart and sat down in the “dumps,” looking longingly across the wide river at the village basking in the sun. Tom began to write “BECKY” in the sand with his big toe. He scratched it out and felt angry at his own weakness. Still, he wrote it again; he couldn’t stop himself. He erased it once more and then stopped tempting himself by gathering the other boys together and joining them.

Joe’s spirits had dropped almost beyond hope. He was so homesick that he could barely stand the sadness. His tears were close to the surface. Huck felt melancholy, too. Tom was downhearted, but he tried hard not to show it. He kept a secret that he wasn’t ready to share yet. If this gloomy mood didn’t end soon, he would have to reveal it. He said, trying to sound cheerful:

“Bet there have been pirates on this island before, boys. We’ll explore it again. They must have hidden treasure somewhere here. How would you like to find a rotten chest full of gold and silver—hey?”

But it only got a weak response, and then it faded away without any reply. Tom tried another one or two ways to persuade them, but those failed as well. It was discouraging work. Joe was poking at the sand with a stick and looked very gloomy. Finally he said:

“Guys, let’s give up. I want to go home. It’s so lonely.”

“No, Joe, you’ll feel better later,” Tom said. “Just think about the fishing we can do here.”

“I don’t care about fishing. I want to go home.”

“But Joe, there isn’t another place to swim anywhere else.”

“Swimming isn’t any good. I don’t feel like swimming when nobody tells me I can’t. I still want to go home.”

“Oh, come on! You want to see your mother, I guess.”

“Yes, I really want to see my mother – and you would too if you had one. I’m not any more a baby than you are.” Joe sniffed a little.

“Well, we’ll let the crybaby go home to his mother, won’t we, Huck? Poor thing – does it want to see its mother? Then it shall. You like it here, don’t you, Huck? We’ll stay, right?”

Huck answered “Yes,” but without any feeling behind it.

“I’ll never talk to you again as long as I live,” Joe said, standing up. “There now!” He walked away moodily and began to dress.

“Who cares!” Tom shouted. “No one wants you to. Go back home and get laughed at. Oh, you’re a nice pirate. Huck and I aren’t crybabies. We’ll stay, right, Huck? Let him go if he wants to. I think we can manage without him, maybe.”

Tom felt uneasy and was worried when he saw Joe quietly keep dressing. It was also uncomfortable to watch Huck stare at Joe’s preparations with a sad look and stay silent. Soon, without saying goodbye, Joe started walking toward the Illinois shore. Tom’s heart sank. He looked at Huck. Huck could not meet his gaze and lowered his eyes. Then he said:

“I want to go, too, Tom. It was getting lonely already, and now it will be worse. Let us go, Tom.”

“I won’t! You can all go if you want. I’m going to stay.”

“Tom, I think I should go.”

“Okay, go ahead—who’s stopping you?”

Huck began picking up his scattered clothes. He said:

“Tom, I wish you would come too. Think about it. We’ll wait for you when we reach the shore.”

“You’ll wait a very long time, that’s all.”

Huck walked away sadly, and Tom stood watching him, feeling a strong urge to put aside his pride and join them. He hoped the boys would stop, but they kept walking slowly forward. Suddenly Tom realized how quiet and lonely it had become. He fought his pride one last time, then ran after his friends, shouting:

“Wait! Wait! I want to tell you something!”

They stopped and turned around. When he reached them, he began to tell his secret. They listened quietly until they finally understood the point he was making. Then they clapped loudly and called it wonderful. They said that if he had told them earlier, they wouldn’t have left. He gave a plausible excuse, but the real reason was that he was afraid the secret wouldn’t keep them with him for very long. He had planned to save it as a last lure.

The boys cheerfully went back and started their games again with energy. They talked all the time about Tom’s brilliant plan and praised how clever it was. After a light dinner of eggs and fish, Tom said he wanted to learn how to smoke now. Joe liked the idea and said he wanted to try, too. So Huck made pipes and filled them. These beginners had never smoked anything before except grapevine cigars, and they bit their tongues, which wasn’t considered manly anyway.

They propped themselves up on their elbows and began to puff carefully, with tentative confidence. The smoke tasted bad, and they gagged a little, but Tom said:

“It’s easy! If I had known this was all there was, I would have learned long ago.”

“So would I,” said Joe. “It’s nothing.”

“Many times I’ve watched people smoking and thought, ‘I wish I could do that,’ but I never thought I could,” said Tom.

“That’s how it is with me, isn’t it, Huck? You’ve heard me say that before, haven’t you, Huck? I’ll leave it to Huck if I haven’t.”

“Yes, many times,” said Huck.

“Well, I have too,” said Tom. “Oh, hundreds of times. Once we were down by the slaughterhouse. Don’t you remember, Huck? Bob Tanner was there, and Johnny Miller, and Jeff Thatcher, when I said it. Don’t you remember me saying that?”

“Yes, that’s right,” said Huck. “That was the day after I lost a white alley. No, it was the day before.”

“There—I told you so,” said Tom. “Huck remembers it.”

“I think I could smoke this pipe all day,” said Joe. “I don’t feel sick.”

“Neither do I,” said Tom. “I could smoke it all day. But I bet Jeff Thatcher couldn’t.”

“Jeff Thatcher! He would collapse after just two puffs. Let him try it once. He’d see!”

“I’m sure he would. And Johnny Miller—I’d love to see Johnny Miller try it once.”

“Oh, I’d love that too!” said Joe. “I bet Johnny Miller couldn’t handle it at all. Just one little draw would knock him down.”

“Exactly, Joe. I wish the boys could see us now.”

“Me too.”

“Boys, don’t say anything about it. Later, when they’re around, I’ll come up to you and say, ‘Joe, got a pipe? I want a smoke.’ And you’ll answer casually, like it’s no big deal, ‘Yes, I have my old pipe and another one, but my tobacco isn’t very good.’ I’ll say, ‘That’s fine, as long as it’s strong enough.’ Then we’ll take out the pipes, light them, and watch them look surprised!”

“Wow, that would be great, Tom! I wish it could happen now!”

“Me too! And when we tell them we learned it while we were out pirating, won’t they wish they had been there?”

"Oh, I don't think so! I'll just bet they will!"

The conversation kept going, but soon it slowed down a little and became disjointed. Long pauses appeared, and the coughing increased a lot. Every pore on the boys' cheeks seemed to turn into a fountain; they could barely keep up with the saliva pouring from their mouths fast enough to stop a flood. Little streams ran down their throats despite all they tried, and sudden retches followed each time. Both boys looked very pale and miserable now. 

Joe's pipe fell from his trembling fingers, and Tom's did too. Both fountains were flowing furiously, and both boys were trying hard to clear them. Joe said weakly:

"I've lost my knife. I think I should go find it."

Tom answered with quivering lips and hesitant words:

"I'll help you. You go over there and I'll look around the spring. No, you don't need to come, Huck—we can find it."

Huck sat down again and waited an hour. Then, feeling lonely, he went to look for his friends. He found them far apart in the woods, both very pale and fast asleep. Something told him that if they had any trouble, they had already gotten rid of it.

That night they didn't talk much at dinner. They looked humble, and when Huck was about to get their pipes ready after the meal, they said they weren't feeling well—something they ate at dinner didn't agree with them.

Around midnight, Joe woke up and called the boys. A heavy, oppressive feeling hung in the air, as if something was about to happen. The boys huddled together and sought the comforting warmth of the fire, though the stale, dead heat of the still atmosphere was suffocating. They sat still, focused and waiting. The solemn silence continued. Beyond the firelight everything was swallowed by darkness. Soon a flickering glow appeared, briefly showing the trees, then disappearing. After a while another light came, a little brighter. Then another. Then a faint moan drifted through the forest branches, and the boys felt a brief breath on their cheeks, shivering as they imagined the spirit of the night had passed. There was a pause. Then a strange flash turned night into day, showing every little blade of grass at their feet, each one separate and clear. It also showed three white, startled faces. A deep roll of thunder echoed down from the sky and faded into distant rumblings. A burst of cold air rushed by, rustling the leaves and scattering the ash from the fire. Another strong flash lit the forest, and an instant crash sounded as if the treetops were ripped apart above the boys. They clung together in terror, surrounded by thick gloom. A few large raindrops fell, pattering on the leaves.

“Quick, boys, go to the tent!” shouted Tom.

They ran away, stumbling over roots and vines in the dark, each taking a different path. A fierce wind roared through the trees, making everything shake. Bright flashes of lightning followed one another, and thunder boomed loudly. Then a heavy rain fell, and the growing storm drove it down in sheets across the ground. The boys shouted to each other, but the wind and thunder drowned their voices. One by one they finally reached the tent and took shelter, cold, frightened, and soaked through. At least they had each other in their misery. They could not talk because the old sail was flapping wildly, even if they had tried. The storm grew stronger, and soon the sail ripped from its ropes and was carried away by the wind. The boys grabbed each other's hands and ran, falling and bruising themselves, to the shelter of a large oak on the riverbank. Now the battle reached its peak. Under the endless flashes of lightning, everything below was clear and sharp: the bending trees, the foaming river, the spray of water, and the faint outlines of the cliffs on the other side, visible through the moving clouds and rain. From time to time a huge tree gave way and crashed through the younger ones. The thunder kept booming in loud, sharp bursts that were terrifying. The storm reached a climax that seemed able to tear the island apart, set it on fire, flood it up to the treetops, blow it away, and deafen every creature at once. It was a wild night for homeless young heads to be out in.

At last the battle was over, and the troops left with weaker complaints and muttering, and peace returned. The boys went back to camp, still a little awed, but they had something to be grateful for: the great sycamore that had sheltered their beds was now a ruin, smashed by lightning, and they had not been underneath it when it fell.

Everything in the camp was soaked, including the campfire, because they were careless young men and had not prepared for rain. This was a cause for worry, as they were drenched and cold. They complained loudly about their discomfort, but then they noticed that the fire had burned down far enough under the big log it was built against (where the log curved upward and lifted off the ground) so that a hand‑wide strip of it stayed dry. They worked patiently, gathering splinters and bark from under the sheltered logs, and managed to get the fire going again. Then they added large dead branches until they had a roaring fire, and their spirits lifted.

They dried their boiled ham and had a feast. After that they sat by the fire, talking about and celebrating their midnight adventure until morning, because there was not a dry place to sleep anywhere around.

As the sun rose, the boys started to feel sleepy. They went out onto the sandbar and lay down to rest. After a while they got sunburned and slowly began to look for breakfast. When they finished eating, they felt stiff, sore, and a little homesick again. Tom noticed how they were feeling and tried his best to cheer the pirates up. They didn’t care about games, circuses, swimming—nothing seemed to interest them. Tom reminded them of their important secret and lifted their spirits for a short time. He then introduced a new idea: they would stop being pirates for a while and pretend to be Indians instead. The boys liked the idea, so soon they covered themselves from head to toe with black mud, looking like striped zebras, each one acting as a chief. Then they rushed into the forest to attack an English settlement.

Later they split into three hostile groups and surprised each other with war cries. They fought, killed, and scalped each other in great numbers. It was a very bloody day, but it also felt very satisfying to them.

At evening they gathered around the camp, hungry and happy. A problem came up: the hostile Indians could not share a meal of hospitality without first making peace, and they could only do that by smoking a peace pipe. They had never known any other way. Two of the men almost wished they had stayed pirates. Since there was no other choice, they put on a brave face, called for the pipe, and each took a puff as it was passed around.

And they were glad they had turned to a wild life, because they had gained something. They found they could now smoke a little without having to go hunting for a missing knife. They didn’t get sick enough to feel really uncomfortable. They were not likely to waste this great opportunity because they didn’t work hard enough. No, they practiced carefully after dinner, and it went quite well, so they spent a happy evening. They felt prouder and happier with their new skill than they would have been cutting off heads or skinning members of the Six Nations. We’ll leave them to smoke, talk, and brag, since we have no more use for them right now.
