The day's adventure haunted Tom's sleep that night. He grabbed the treasure four times, and each time it slipped away as he woke, and reality reminded him of his misfortune. In the early morning, while remembering his great adventure, the events seemed faint and distant, as if they belonged to another world or a long‑ago time. Then he realized the adventure might have been just a dream. One strong reason supported this thought: the amount of money he had seen was far too large to be real. He had never seen fifty dollars all at once, and, like most boys his age, he thought words like “hundreds” and “thousands” were only exaggerated expressions, not real sums. He never imagined that anyone could actually have a hundred dollars in cash. If his idea of hidden treasure were examined, it would turn out to be a few real dimes and a heap of vague, impressive, unattainable dollars.

But as he thought more about the events, they became sharper and clearer. Now he was beginning to doubt that it was only a dream. He decided the doubt had to be cleared. He would grab a quick breakfast and go find Huck.

Huck sat on the side of a flatboat, his feet lazily swinging in the water. He looked very sad. Tom decided to let Huck bring up the topic. If Huck didn’t, the adventure would seem like just a dream.

“Hey, Huck!”

“Hey yourself.”

A minute of silence.

“Tom, if we had left the blame tools at the dead tree, we would have gotten the money. Oh, isn’t that terrible!”

“It isn’t a dream, then, it isn’t a dream! I wish it were. I’d be angry if I don’t, Huck.”

“What isn’t a dream?”

“Oh, that thing from yesterday. I was half‑thinking it was.”

“Dream! If those stairs hadn’t collapsed you would have seen how much of a dream it was! I’ve had enough dreams all night—especially with that one‑eyed Spanish devil chasing me in them—damn him!”

“No, not damn him. Find him! Track the money!”

“Tom, we’ll never find him. A man doesn’t get more than one chance at a fortune—and that chance is gone. I’d feel very nervous if I saw him, anyway.”

“Me too, but I still want to see him—and chase him down—to his Number Two.”

“Number Two—yes, that’s it. I’ve been thinking about that. But I can’t figure it out. What do you think it means?”

“I don’t know. It’s too vague. Say, Huck—maybe it’s a house number!”

“Great! … No, Tom, that’s not it. If it were, it wouldn’t be in this tiny town. There are no numbers here.”

“True. Let me think for a minute. Maybe it’s the number of a room in a tavern, you know!”

“Oh, that could work! There are more than two taverns. We could find out quickly.”

“You wait here, Huck, until I come back.”

Tom left right away. He didn’t want Huck with him in public places. He was gone for half an hour. He found that in the best tavern, room No. 2 had long been taken by a young lawyer, and it was still occupied. In the less fancy house, room No. 2 was a mystery. The tavern keeper’s young son said it was always locked. He never saw anyone go in or out except at night. He didn’t know why it was like that. He was a little curious, but his curiosity was weak. He kept himself busy by thinking the room might be haunted. He also noticed a light in the room the night before.

“That’s what I’ve found out, Huck. I think that’s the room No. 2 we’re looking for.”

“I think so too, Tom. Now what are you going to do?”

“Let me think.”

Tom thought for a long time. Then he said:

“I’ll tell you. The back door of that No. 2 opens onto a small alley between the tavern and the old, rundown brick store. Now you find every key you can, and I’ll take care of Auntie’s stuff. The first dark night we’ll go there and try the keys. And watch out for Injun Joe, because he said he’s coming to town again to look around for a chance to get his revenge. If you see him, just follow him. If he doesn’t go to that No. 2, then it isn’t the right place.”

“Lord, I don’t want to follow him by myself!”

“Yes, it will be night, for sure. He might never see you— and if he does, he probably won’t think anything about it.”  

“Well, if it’s really dark, I think I’ll follow him. I don’t know—I don’t know. I’ll try.”  

“You can count on me to follow him if it’s dark, Huck. He might have found out he can’t get his revenge, and now he’s after that money.”  

“That’s right, Tom, that’s right. I’ll follow him; I will, I promise!”  

“Now you’re talking! Don’t ever give up, Huck, and I won’t either.”
