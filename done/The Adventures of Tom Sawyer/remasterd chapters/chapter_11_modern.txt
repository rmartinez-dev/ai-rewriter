Around noon the whole village was suddenly shocked by terrible news. There was no need for a telegraph, which had not even been imagined yet; the story spread from person to person, from group to group, from house to house, almost as fast as a telegraph. Of course the schoolmaster gave the children a holiday that afternoon; otherwise the town would have thought badly of him.

A bloody knife was found near the murdered man, and someone said it belonged to Muff Potter—so the rumor spread. It was also said that a late‑night passerby saw Potter washing himself in the creek around one or two o’clock in the morning, and that Potter quickly ran away. This seemed suspicious, especially because Potter did not usually wash there. People also said that the whole town had been searched for this “murderer”—the public is quick to look for evidence and make a judgment—but they could not find him. Riders went out on every road, and the sheriff was confident they would catch him before night.

The entire town was moving toward the graveyard. Tom’s sorrow disappeared and he joined the procession, not because he wanted to be there, but because a strange, strong fascination pulled him. When they arrived at the grim place, he squeezed his small body through the crowd and saw the sad sight. It felt like a long time since he had ever been there.

Someone pinched his arm. He turned, and his eyes met Huckleberry’s. Then both looked away quickly, wondering if anyone had noticed their glance. But everyone was talking and focused on the gruesome scene in front of them.

“Poor fellow! Poor young fellow! This should be a lesson to grave robbers! Muff Potter will hang for this if they catch him!” That was the idea behind the comment. The minister added, “It was a judgment; his hand is here.”  

Tom shivered all over when he saw the calm face of Injun Joe. At the same time, the crowd started to move and push, and people shouted, “It’s him! It’s him! He’s coming himself!”  

“Who? Who?” shouted dozens of voices.  

“Muff Potter!”  

“Hey, he’s stopped! Look, he’s turning! Don’t let him get away!”  

People in the trees above Tom said he wasn’t trying to run away; he only looked confused and unsure.  

“What nerve!” said a bystander. “He wanted to come and watch his work in peace, I guess—didn’t expect anyone else.”  

The crowd broke up, and the sheriff pushed his way in, forcefully pulling Potter by the arm. Potter’s face looked tired and his eyes were full of fear. When he stood in front of the dead man, he trembled, covered his face with his hands, and started to cry.  

“I didn’t do it, friends,” he sobbed. “On my word and my honor, I never did it.”  

“Who accused you?” shouted someone.  

The words seemed to hit him hard. Potter lifted his head and looked around with a sad, hopeless look. He saw Injun Joe and said, “Oh, Injun Joe, you promised me you’d never—”  

“Is that your knife?” the sheriff asked, thrusting it forward.  

Potter would have collapsed if they hadn’t caught him and helped him onto the ground. Then he said:

“Something told me I had to come back and get—” He shivered, then waved his numb hand in a defeated gesture and said, “Tell them, Joe, tell them—it’s no good anymore.”

Huckleberry and Tom stood silent, staring. They listened as the cold‑hearted liar read his calm statement. They expected the sky to strike him with lightning at any moment, wondering how long the strike would be delayed. When he finished and was still standing, their urge to break their oath and save the poor, betrayed prisoner faded away. It was clear this villain had sold himself to the devil, and interfering with such a power would be fatal.

“Why didn’t you leave? What did you want to come here for?” someone asked.

“I couldn’t help it— I couldn’t help it,” Potter moaned. “I wanted to run away, but I could only end up here.” He began to sob again.

A few minutes later, under oath at the inquest, Injun Joe repeated his statement just as calmly. The boys saw that no lightning had come, confirming their belief that Joe had sold himself to the devil. To them he had become the most terrifying and fascinating person they had ever seen, and they could not take their eyes off his face.

They decided privately to watch him at night, whenever they could, hoping to catch a glimpse of his dreadful master.

Injun Joe helped lift the dead man’s body and put it in a wagon to be taken away. People in the trembling crowd whispered that the wound was still bleeding a little. The boys hoped this lucky detail would point the blame the right way, but they were disappointed when several villagers said, “It was only three feet from Muff Potter when it happened.”

Tom’s guilty secret and nagging conscience kept him awake for about a week. At breakfast one morning Sid said, “Tom, you toss and turn and mumble in your sleep so much that you keep me up half the night.”

Tom turned red and lowered his eyes.

“It’s a bad sign,” Aunt Polly said seriously. “What are you thinking about, Tom?”

“Nothing. Nothing I know of,” he replied, but his hand shook and he spilled his coffee.

“You keep talking about that stuff,” Sid said. “Last night you kept saying, ‘It’s blood, it’s blood, that’s what it is!’ over and over. And you said, ‘Don’t torture me—I’ll tell!’ Tell what? What are you going to tell?”

Everything seemed confusing to Tom. No one could say what might happen next, but Aunt Polly’s worried look faded, and she smiled at Tom without realizing it. She said, “Wow! That terrible murder haunts me every night. Sometimes I even dream that I’m the one who did it.”

Mary said she had been affected in the same way. Sid seemed satisfied.  

Tom left the room as quickly as he could. After that he complained of a toothache for a week and clenched his jaw every night. He didn’t know that Sid stayed up watching each night. Sid often slipped the bandage off, leaned on his elbow and listened for a while, then put the bandage back on. Tom’s mental distress faded slowly, and the toothache became annoying and went away. If Sid understood anything from Tom’s rambling, he kept it to himself.  

Tom felt that his schoolmates would never finish investigating the dead cats, so the problem stayed on his mind. Sid noticed that Tom never acted as coroner at those inquiries, even though Tom usually took the lead in new projects. He also saw that Tom never served as a witness, which seemed strange. Sid also saw that Tom hated these inquiries and always avoided them when he could. Sid was surprised but said nothing. Eventually the inquiries fell out of fashion and stopped bothering Tom’s conscience.  

Every day or two, while he was sad, Tom looked for a chance and went to the small barred jail window. He slipped small comforts through to the “murderer” whenever he could. The jail was a tiny brick building in a marsh at the edge of the village and had no guards; it was rarely used. These gifts helped Tom feel less guilty.

The villagers really wanted to tar and feather Indian Joe and put him on a rail as punishment for stealing bodies. But his strong character made it hard to find anyone willing to lead the effort, so they dropped the plan. He had carefully started both of his statements to the inquiry with the fight, without admitting the body theft that happened before. Because of this, they thought it was best not to bring the case to court right now.
