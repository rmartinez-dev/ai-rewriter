Tom ran back and forth through the lanes until he was far enough away from the scholars who were coming back. Then he slowed to a steady jog. He crossed a small brook two or three times because of a childish belief that water would stop anyone chasing him. 

Half an hour later he disappeared behind the Douglas house at the top of Cardiff Hill. The school was just a tiny shape in the valley far behind him. He entered a thick forest, found a way without a clear path to its centre, and sat down on a mossy spot under a spreading oak tree. 

There was not even a breeze. The hot midday air had quieted the birds’ songs. Nature seemed to be in a trance, broken only by the occasional distant hammering of a wood‑pecker. This made the silence and the feeling of isolation feel even deeper. 

The boy’s mind was full of melancholy, and his feelings matched the quiet around him. He sat with his elbows on his knees and his chin in his hands, thinking. To him life felt like a trouble at best. He almost envied Jimmy Hodges, who had recently died. He imagined how peaceful it must be to lie forever, dreaming while the wind whispered through the trees and brushed the grass and flowers over the grave, with nothing left to worry about. 

If he only had a clean Sunday‑school record, he could be ready to go and end everything. 

Now about the girl. What had he done? Nothing. He had meant the best, but he was treated like a dog—like a very bad dog. She would be sorry someday—maybe when it was too late. He wished he could die just for a little while.

But a young heart cannot be squeezed into one narrow shape for a long time. Tom began to drift back into the worries of everyday life, almost without noticing it. What if he turned his back now and vanished? What if he left—travel far away to unknown lands across the seas—and never returned? How would she feel then?

The thought of being a clown came back to him, but it made him feel disgusted. Foolish jokes and bright tights seemed offensive when they intruded on a spirit that wanted something more serious and romantic. No, he thought, I will be a soldier and return after many years, scarred by war but honored. 

Better still, he imagined joining the Indians, hunting buffalo, and fighting on the mountain ranges and the endless plains of the West. He saw himself later becoming a great chief, covered in feathers and paint, and marching into Sunday school one sleepy summer morning with a war cry that would make everyone jealous. 

But even that seemed too plain. He would be a pirate! Now his future looked clear and brilliant. He imagined his name ringing around the world, making people shiver with fear. He saw himself sailing the wild seas in a long, low, black‑hulled ship called the Spirit of the Storm, its grim flag flying at the front. 

At the height of his fame, he pictured himself strolling into his old village and walking into church, brown and weather‑worn, wearing a black velvet coat, big boots, a red sash, a belt full of horse pistols, a rusted cutlass at his side, a slouch hat with plumes, and a black flag with a skull and crossbones fluttering behind him. He could hear the excited whispers: “It’s Tom Sawyer the pirate— the Black Avenger of the Spanish Main!”

His decision was final; his plan was set. He would run away from home and start his new life. He would begin the next morning. So now he had to get ready. He gathered his supplies. He walked to a rotten log nearby and started digging under one end with his knife. Soon he hit wood that sounded hollow. He put his hand inside and shouted:

“What hasn’t come here, come! What’s here, stay here!”

He brushed away the dirt and uncovered a pine shingle. He lifted it and found a neat little box with shingles on the bottom and sides. Inside was a marble. Tom was amazed. He scratched his head, looked confused, and said, “Well, that beats anything.”

Then he tossed the marble away pettily and stood thinking. The truth was that a superstition of his had failed here, one that he and all his friends had always believed was infallible. The belief said that if you buried a marble with a certain chant, left it alone for two weeks, and then opened the spot with the same chant, all the marbles you had ever lost would gather there, no matter how far apart they had been. But now that thing had clearly failed. Tom's whole belief system shook to its core. He had heard many times that it worked, but never that it could fail. It never occurred to him that he had tried it many times before and could never find the hiding places afterward. He puzzled over the matter for a while and finally decided that some witch had interfered and broken the charm. He thought that would satisfy him, so he searched until he found a small sandy spot with a little funnel‑shaped dip in it. He lay down, put his mouth close to the dip and called—  

“Doodle‑bug, doodle‑bug, tell me what I want to know! Doodle‑bug, doodle‑bug, tell me what I want to know!”  

The sand began to move, and soon a small black bug appeared for a second, then darted away in fear.  

“He didn’t tell! So it was a witch that did it. I just knew it.”

He knew it was useless to fight the witches, so he gave up feeling discouraged. Then he thought he might as well get back the marble he had just thrown away. He searched carefully, but he could not find it. He returned to his treasure‑room and stood exactly where he had been when he tossed the marble away. He took another marble from his pocket and threw it the same way, saying:

“Brother, go find your brother!”

He watched where it landed, went to that spot, and looked. The marble had either fallen short or gone too far, so he tried again two more times. The third throw succeeded. The two marbles ended up within a foot of each other.

At that moment a faint blast from a toy tin trumpet drifted through the green paths of the forest. Tom ripped off his jacket and trousers, turned a suspenders into a belt, cleared some brush behind a rotten log, and uncovered a crude bow and arrow, a wooden sword, and a tin trumpet. He grabbed them quickly, ran away barefoot with his shirt fluttering, and stopped under a large elm. He blew a response on the trumpet, then began to tiptoe and look cautiously around, looking this way and that. He whispered to an imagined group:

“Hold, my merry men! Stay hidden until I blow.”

Then Joe Harper appeared, dressed as lightly and armed as elaborately as Tom. Tom called out:

“Hold! Who comes into Sherwood Forest without my permission?”

“The man from Guisborne needs no permission. Who are you that—”

“Dares to speak like that,” Tom said, recalling the old words.

“Who are you that dares to speak like that?”

“Yes! I’m Robin Hood, and your miserable body will soon know it.”

“So you’re really that famous outlaw? I’ll gladly fight you in the green forest. Bring it on!”

They picked up their wooden swords, dropped their other weapons on the ground, took a fencing stance, foot to foot, and began a serious, careful fight, moving two steps forward and two steps back. Then Tom said:

“Now, if you can handle it, do it quickly!”

So they fought fast, panting and sweating. After a while Tom shouted:

“Fall! Fall! Why aren’t you falling?”

“I won’t! Why don’t you fall yourself? You’re the one losing.”

“That doesn’t matter. I can’t fall; that’s not how it’s written in the story. The book says, ‘Then with one backhand stroke he killed poor Guy of Gisborne.’ You have to turn around and let me hit you from behind.”

There was no way around the rule, so Joe turned, took the blow, and fell.

“Now,” said Joe, getting up, “you have to let me kill you. That’s fair.”

“Why, I can’t do that, it’s not in the story.”

“Well, that’s just cruel—that’s all.”

“So, Joe, you could be Friar Tuck or Much the Miller’s Son and hit me with a quarter‑staff; or I could be the Sheriff of Nottingham and you could be Robin Hood for a while and kill me.”

It was enough, so they went ahead with the adventures. Tom acted like Robin Hood again, and the deceitful nun let him lose his strength because his wound was ignored. Finally, Joe, who spoke for a whole group of sad outlaws, pulled him out slowly, handed him a bow, and Tom said, “Where this arrow lands, bury poor Robin Hood under the greenwood tree.” He shot the arrow, fell down, and would have died, but he landed on a nettle and got up lively, not like a dead body.

The boys got dressed, hid their gear, and left feeling sad because there were no outlaws left. They wondered what modern civilization could do to replace them. They said they would rather be outlaws for a year in Sherwood Forest than be President of the United States forever.
