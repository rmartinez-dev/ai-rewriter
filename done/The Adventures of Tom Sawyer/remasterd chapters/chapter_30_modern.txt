As dawn began to break on Sunday morning, Huck climbed the hill and knocked gently on the old Welshman's door. The people inside were asleep, but their sleep was uneasy because of the frightening events of the night. A voice called from a window:

“Who’s there?”

Huck answered in a frightened whisper:

“Please let me in! It’s just Huck Finn!”

“That name can open this door any time, boy—welcome!”

These words sounded strange and pleasant to the lonely boy. He had never heard such a welcome before. The door was quickly opened and he went inside. Huck was shown a seat while the old man and his two tall sons got dressed quickly.

“Now, my boy, I hope you’re hungry, because breakfast will be ready as soon as the sun rises. We’ll have a hot one, too—don’t worry about that! The boys and I hoped you would come last night.”

“I was very scared,” Huck said, “and I ran. I left when the guns fired and didn’t stop for three miles. I’ve come now because I want to know what happened, you know; and I came before daylight because I didn’t want to meet those bad men, even if they were dead.”

Well, poor chap, you look like you had a rough night, but there's a bed here for you after breakfast. No, they aren't dead, lad—we're sorry enough for that. By your description we knew exactly where to find them, so we moved quietly on tiptoe until we were within fifteen feet. It was as dark as a cellar on that sumac path, and then I felt a sneeze coming. It was terrible luck! I tried to hold it back, but it was useless—it had to come, and it did. I was leading with my pistol raised, and when the sneeze started the scoundrels began rustling to get out of the way. I shouted, “Fire, boys!” and shot at the place where the rustling was. The boys did the same. The villains ran away quickly, and we chased them through the woods. I think we never hit them. They each fired a shot as they left, but the bullets whizzed past us and didn't hurt us. As soon as we stopped hearing their footsteps we gave up the chase, went back and told the constables. They gathered a posse and went to guard the river bank. When daylight comes, the sheriff and his men will sweep the woods. My boys will join them soon. I wish we had a description of those rascals—it would help a lot. But you couldn't see them in the dark, could you, lad?

"Oh yes; I saw them downtown and followed them."

"Great! Describe them—describe them, my boy!"

"One is the old deaf and mute Spaniard who's been here once or twice, and the other is a mean‑looking, ragged—"

"That's enough, boy, we know the men! They were in the woods behind the widow's house that day and they slipped away. Go, boys, and tell the sheriff—bring back your breakfast tomorrow morning!"

The Welshman's sons left at once. As they were leaving the room, Huck jumped up and shouted:

"Oh, please don't tell anyone that it was me who blew on them! Please!"

"Fine, you can say that, Huck, but you should get credit for what you did."

"Oh no, no! Please don't tell!"

When the young men were gone, the old Welshman said:

"They won't say anything—and I won't either. But why don't you want it known?"

Huck wouldn't explain, except to say that he already knew too much about one of those men and he wouldn't let the man find out that he knew anything against him—he would be killed for knowing it, for sure.

The old man promised secrecy again, and asked:

"How did you end up following those fellows, boy? Did they look suspicious?"

Huck was silent while he thought of a careful answer. Then he said:

“Well, you see, I’m a hard person—at least everyone says so, and I don’t see any reason to disagree. Sometimes I can’t sleep because I keep thinking about it and trying to find a new way to do things. That’s what happened last night. I couldn’t sleep, so I walked up the street around midnight, turning things over in my mind. When I reached that old, shabby brick shop near the Temperance Tavern, I leaned against the wall to think some more. At that moment two men slipped past me, each holding something under their arm, and I thought they had stolen it. One was smoking, the other wanted a light, so they stopped right in front of me. The cigar light lit their faces, and I saw that the taller one was a deaf, mute Spaniard, because of his white whiskers and the patch over his eye, and the other looked like a ragged, rusty‑looking devil.”

“Could you see the rags by the light of the cigars?”

Huck was stunned for a moment. Then he said:

“Well, I don’t know—but somehow it feels as if I did.”

“Then they kept going, and you—”

“I followed them—yes. That’s all. I wanted to know what was happening—they were moving stealthily. I trailed them to the widow’s gate and stood in the dark. I heard the ragged man beg the widow, and the Spaniard swear he would ruin her looks just as I told you and your two—”

“What! The deaf, mute man said all that!”

Huck had made another terrible mistake. He was trying hard to keep the old man from getting any clue about who the Spaniard might be, but his tongue kept getting him into trouble despite his efforts. He tried several times to get out of the situation, but the old man was watching him, and he kept making mistakes.

Then the Welshman said: “Kid, don’t be scared of me. I would never hurt you. I’ll protect you. This Spaniard isn’t deaf or dumb; you let that slip without meaning to, and you can’t hide it now. You know something about that Spaniard that you want to keep secret. Trust me, tell me what it is, and I won’t betray you.”

Huck looked into the old man’s sincere eyes for a moment, then leaned down and whispered in his ear: “It’s not a Spaniard—it's Injun Joe!”

The Welshman almost jumped out of his chair. He then said: “Now it’s clear. When you talked about cutting ears and slitting noses, I thought you were just exaggerating, because white men don’t do that kind of revenge. But an Indian! That’s a completely different story.”

During breakfast the conversation continued. The old man said that the last thing he and his sons had done before going to bed was to take a lantern and check the gate and the area around it for any bloodstains. They found none, but they did find a large bundle of— “What was it?”

If those words had been like lightning, they could not have shot out of Huck’s pale lips any faster. His eyes were wide open, and he held his breath, waiting for an answer. The Welshman froze, stared back for a few seconds—three, five, ten—then said:

“Burglar’s tools. What’s the matter with you?”

Huck sank back, breathing softly but deeply, feeling indescribably grateful. The Welshman looked at him seriously and curiously, then said:

“Yes, burglar’s tools. That seems to make you feel a lot better. But what made you think that? What did you expect us to find?”

Huck felt the pressure of the questioning stare on him. He would have given anything for a good answer, but no idea came to mind. The stare went deeper and deeper. He tried to think of a reply, but there was no time to choose carefully, so he guessed weakly:

“Maybe Sunday‑school books?”

Huck was too upset to smile, but the old man laughed loudly and merrily. He shook his whole body, from head to toe, and said that a laugh was like money in a pocket because it could cut down a doctor’s bill. Then he added:

“Poor fellow, you’re pale and shaky. You’re not feeling well at all, so it’s no wonder you’re a little off balance. You’ll get better. Rest and sleep will help you recover, I hope.”

Huck felt annoyed that he had acted like a fool and shown such nervous excitement. He had dismissed the idea that the package from the tavern was the treasure as soon as he heard the conversation at the widow’s gate. He only thought it wasn’t the treasure because he didn’t know it really wasn’t. The suggestion of a captured bundle overwhelmed his self‑control.

Overall, though, he was glad the incident had happened. Now he knew for sure that the bundle was not the real one, and his mind could finally relax. Everything seemed to be moving in the right direction. The treasure must still be in No. 2, the men would be caught and locked up that day, and he and Tom could take the gold that night without any trouble or fear of being stopped.

Just as breakfast ended, someone knocked on the door. Huck looked for a place to hide because he didn’t want to be linked in any way to the recent events. The Welshman let several ladies and gentlemen inside, including Widow Douglas, and he noticed groups of townspeople climbing the hill to look at the gate. The news had already spread. The Welshman had to tell the visitors what had happened the night before. The widow expressed her gratitude for being saved.

“Please don’t say anything about it, madam. There is another person you owe more thanks to than me and my boys, perhaps, but I’m not allowed to give his name. We wouldn’t have been there if it weren’t for him.”

Naturally this sparked a huge curiosity that almost made the main issue seem small. But the Welshman let this curiosity spread to his visitors and then to the whole town, because he would not reveal his secret. When everything else had been explained, the widow said:

“I was reading in bed and fell asleep. I slept through all the noise. Why didn’t you wake me?”

“We thought it wasn’t worth it,” they replied. “Those men probably won’t come back—they have no tools left to use. What good would it be to wake you and scare you? My three black men guarded your house for the rest of the night. They have just returned.”

More visitors arrived, and the story was told and retold for a few more hours.

There was no Sunday school during the school holidays, but everyone arrived early at church. The exciting event was widely talked about. No sign of the two criminals had been found yet. When the sermon ended, Judge Thatcher’s wife came up next to Mrs. Harper as she walked down the aisle with the crowd and said:

“Is my Becky going to sleep all day? I thought she would be exhausted.”

“Your Becky?”

“Yes,” she said, surprised, “didn’t she stay with you last night?”

“No, she didn’t.”

Mrs. Thatcher turned pale and sat down in a pew just as Aunt Polly, who was chatting quickly with a friend, walked by. Aunt Polly said:

“Good morning, Mrs. Thatcher. Good morning, Mrs. Harper. I have a boy who has gone missing. I think my Tom stayed at your house last night—one of you. Now he is too scared to come to church. I need to talk to him.”

Mrs. Thatcher shook her head weakly and turned even paler.  

“He didn’t stay with us,” said Mrs. Harper, beginning to look uneasy. A clear anxiety appeared on Aunt Polly’s face.  

“Joe Harper, have you seen my Tom this morning?”  

“No, ma’am.”  

“When did you see him last?”  

Joe tried to remember, but he wasn’t sure he could answer. People had stopped leaving the church. Whispered rumors spread, and a frightening uneasiness filled every face. Children were questioned anxiously, and the young teachers too. They all said they had not noticed whether Tom and Becky were on the ferry on the way home; it was dark, and no one thought to ask if anyone was missing. Finally, one young man blurted out that he was afraid they were still in the cave! Mrs. Thatcher fainted. Aunt Polly fell to the ground crying and wringing her hands.  

The alarm spread quickly from person to person, from group to group, from street to street. Within five minutes the bells were ringing wildly and the whole town was awake. The incident on Cardiff Hill became instantly unimportant. The burglars were forgotten, horses were saddled, skiffs were prepared, and the ferry was ordered out. Within half an hour, two hundred men were marching down the high road and along the river toward the cave.

All afternoon the village seemed empty and lifeless. Many women visited Aunt Polly and Mrs. Thatcher and tried to comfort them. They also cried with them, which was better than just saying something. All night the town waited for news. When morning finally came, the only message was, “Send more candles—and send food.” Mrs. Thatcher was almost crazy, and Aunt Polly was too. Judge Thatcher sent hopeful messages from the cave, but they gave no real comfort.

The old Welshman returned home near daylight, covered in candle‑grease, smeared with clay, and nearly exhausted. He found Huck still lying in the bed set up for him, delirious with fever. The doctors were all at the cave, so Widow Douglas came and took care of him. She said she would do her best for him, because whether he was good, bad, or indifferent, he belonged to the Lord, and nothing that belongs to the Lord should be ignored. The Welshman said Huck had good qualities, and the widow added:

“You can be sure of that. That's the Lord’s mark. He never takes it away. He always puts it on every creature he creates.”

Early in the morning, tired groups of men began to wander into the village. The strongest townspeople, however, kept searching. The only news they could find was that remote parts of the cavern—places never visited before—were being ransacked. Every corner and crevice would be examined thoroughly. Wherever someone walked through the maze of passages, they could see lights flickering in the distance, and shouts and gunshots echoed hollowly down the dark corridors. In one area, far from the path usually taken by tourists, the names “BECKY & TOM” were drawn on the rocky wall with candle smoke, and a grease‑stained piece of ribbon lay nearby. Mrs. Thatcher recognized the ribbon and began to weep. She said it was the last thing she would ever have from her child, and no other memory could be as precious because it was the last part of the body to leave before the terrible death. Some people said that, from time to time, a faint spot of light would appear in the cave, followed by a loud shout and a line of men marching down the echoing tunnel. But a sharp disappointment always followed; the children were not there—it was only the light of a searcher.

Three terrible days and nights passed, and the village fell into a hopeless daze. No one felt like doing anything. The accidental discovery that the owner of the Temperance Tavern was keeping liquor on the premises barely stirred the public, even though it was an important fact. In a clear moment, Huck weakly brought up the subject of taverns. He finally asked, fearing the worst, whether anything had been found at the Temperance Tavern since he had been ill.

“Yes,” the widow replied.

Huck sat up in bed, his eyes wide.

“What? What was it?”

“Liquor! And the place has been closed. Lie down, child—you gave me such a scare!”

“Please tell me just one thing—just one—was it Tom Sawyer who found it?”

The widow burst into tears. “Shh, shh, child, shh! I’ve told you before, you must not talk. You are very, very sick!”

Only liquor had been found; if it had been gold, there would have been a big fuss. So the treasure was gone forever. But why was she crying? It seemed odd.

These thoughts drifted through Huck’s mind. Exhausted, he fell asleep. The widow whispered to herself:

“There—he’s asleep, poor wreck. Tom Sawyer found it! It’s a pity, but maybe someone could find Tom Sawyer! Ah, there aren’t many left now who have enough hope or strength to keep looking.”
