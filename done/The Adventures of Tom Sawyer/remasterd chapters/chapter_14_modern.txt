When Tom woke up in the morning, he wondered where he was. He sat up, rubbed his eyes, and looked around. Then he understood. It was the cool gray dawn, and there was a wonderful feeling of rest and peace in the deep calm and silence of the woods. Not a leaf moved; no sound disturbed nature’s quiet. Dewdrops glistened on the leaves and grasses. A white layer of ash covered the fire, and a thin blue wisp of smoke rose straight into the air. Joe and Huck were still sleeping.

Far away in the woods a bird called out, and another answered. Soon the sound of a woodpecker hammering could be heard.

The cool, dim gray of early morning slowly turned lighter. At the same time, more sounds appeared and life began to show itself. The boy watched nature shake off sleep and start its work.

A little green worm crawled over a dewy leaf. It lifted most of its body up, sniffed around, and then moved on. Tom said the worm was measuring the leaf. When the worm came toward him on its own, Tom sat as still as a stone, his hope rising and falling as the worm seemed to decide whether to stay or go elsewhere. Finally the worm lifted its curved body and dropped onto Tom’s leg, beginning to crawl over him. Tom felt delighted, because he imagined it meant he would get a new, bright, pirate‑style uniform.

A line of ants appeared out of nowhere and went about their work. One ant struggled hard, carrying a dead spider that was five times its size, and lifted it up a tree trunk.

A brown spotted ladybug climbed a tall grass blade. Tom bent down and said, “Lady‑bug, lady‑bug, fly home, your house is on fire, your children are alone.” The bug flew away, as if to check the fire. This did not surprise Tom; he knew the insect often believed such stories, and he had played this trick on it before.

A tumblebug rolled a ball forward. Tom touched the bug, and it curled its legs around its body and pretended to be dead.

The birds were very noisy now. A catbird, the northern mimic, perched in a tree above Tom and sang imitations of the other birds with great joy. Then a bright blue jay swooped down like a flash of flame and landed on a twig almost within the boy’s reach. It tilted its head and stared at the newcomer with great curiosity. A gray squirrel and a large fox‑like animal ran past, stopping now and then to look at the boy and make noises. The wild animals had probably never seen a human before and were not sure whether to be scared.

All of nature was wide awake and moving. Long beams of sunlight pierced through the dense foliage, and a few butterflies fluttered into the scene.

Tom woke up the other pirates, and they all shouted and ran off. Within a minute or two they were stripped and chased each other, tumbling over in the shallow, clear water of the white sandbar. They didn’t miss the little village far away beyond the huge stretch of water. A wandering current or a small rise in the river had taken their raft away, but that only pleased them, because losing the raft felt like cutting the link to civilization.

They returned to camp feeling refreshed, happy, and very hungry. Soon the campfire was blazing again. Huck found a spring of clear, cold water nearby, and the boys made cups from big oak or hickory leaves. The water, with its wild‑forest flavor, seemed a good enough replacement for coffee. While Joe was slicing bacon for breakfast, Tom and Huck asked him to wait a moment. They walked to a promising spot on the riverbank and cast their lines. Almost at once they were rewarded. Before Joe could get impatient, they came back with a nice catch: some good bass, a couple of sunfish, and a small catfish—enough food for a whole family. They fried the fish with the bacon and were amazed, because no fish had ever tasted so delicious before. They didn’t know that the faster a fresh‑water fish is put on the fire after being caught, the better it tastes, and they didn’t think much about how the open‑air cooking, the fresh air, the swimming, and their big hunger all added to the flavor.

After breakfast they rested in the shade while Huck smoked a pipe. Then they set off into the woods on an exploring trip.  

They walked cheerfully over old logs and through tangled brush. They passed tall trees that were draped with hanging grape vines.  

Sometimes they found small, cosy spots covered with grass and dotted with flowers.  

They saw many pleasant things, but nothing surprising. They learned that the island was about three miles long and a quarter of a mile wide. The nearest shore was only about two hundred yards away, across a narrow channel.  

They swam roughly every hour, so they returned to camp around mid‑afternoon. They were too hungry to fish, but they ate cold ham quickly and then rested in the shade to talk.  

Soon their conversation slowed and stopped. The quiet, the serious mood of the forest, and the feeling of loneliness began to affect the boys.  

They began to think. An undefined longing appeared. It soon became clear that they were starting to feel homesick. Even Finn the Red‑Handed dreamed of his home and empty barrels.  

But they were embarrassed by their weakness, and none dared to speak the feeling.

For a while the boys had noticed a faint sound far away, like the ticking of a clock you hardly notice. Then the sound grew louder and they had to pay attention. The boys jumped, looked at each other, and each began to listen. A long, deep silence followed. Then a low, heavy boom came from the distance.

“What is that?” whispered Joe.

“I wonder,” Tom said quietly.

“It’s not thunder,” Huckleberry said in awe, “because thunder—”

“Listen!” Tom said. “Don’t talk.”

They waited, which felt like forever, and then the same muffled boom broke the quiet.

“Let’s go and see.”

They jumped up and ran toward the shore near the town. They pushed aside the bushes on the bank and looked out over the water. A small steam ferry was about a mile downstream from the village, moving with the current. Its wide deck was full of people. Many small boats were rowing or drifting near the ferry, but the boys could not see what the men in them were doing. Suddenly a big plume of white smoke shot out from the side of the ferry. As it spread and rose in a lazy cloud, the same dull throb came to their ears again.

“I know now!” Tom shouted. “Someone has drowned!”

"That's it!" Huck said. "They did that last summer when Bill Turner drowned. They fire a cannon over the water, and that pushes him up to the surface. Yes, and they take loaves of bread, put mercury in them, and set them floating. Wherever there is anyone who has drowned, the bread will float right there and stop."

"Yes, I've heard about that," Joe said. "I wonder what makes the bread do that."

"Oh, it's not really the bread," Tom replied. "I think it's mostly what they say over it before they start it."

"But they don't say anything over it," Huck said. "I've seen them and they don't."

"Well, that's funny," Tom said. "Maybe they say it to themselves. Of course they do. Anyone could know that."

The other boys agreed that Tom had a point. An ordinary lump of bread, without a spell, couldn't be expected to work very smartly on such an important job.

"Gee, I wish I were there right now," Joe said.

"Me too," Huck added. "I'd love to know who it is."

The boys kept listening and watching. Then a sudden idea flashed through Tom's mind, and he shouted:

"Boys, I know who's drowned—it’s us!"

They instantly felt like heroes. It was a great victory. People missed them and mourned them. Hearts broke because of them, and tears were being shed. Memories of how unkind they had been to the poor lost boys rose up, and people felt useless regrets and remorse. Most of all, the dead were talked about all over town, and the boys envied the fame. It felt wonderful. Being a pirate seemed worth it after all.

As evening fell, the ferryboat returned to its normal routine and the small boats disappeared. The pirates went back to their camp. They were proud and thrilled about their new fame and the trouble they were causing. They caught fish, cooked dinner, and ate it. Then they started guessing what the village thought and said about them. The stories they imagined about the town’s distress pleased them, from their point of view. When night grew dark, they slowly stopped talking and stared at the fire, their minds drifting elsewhere. The excitement faded. Tom and Joe could not stop thinking of people back home who were not enjoying the adventure as much as they were. Doubt crept in; they felt uneasy and sad; they let out a sigh or two without noticing. Finally, Joe shyly asked a vague question about how the others might react to returning to normal life—not now, but...

Tom mocked him mercilessly! Huck, who hadn’t decided yet, joined Tom, and the hesitant one quickly gave his excuse, happy to get out of the trouble with as little trace of cowardly homesickness on his clothes as possible. The mutiny was temporarily stopped.

As night grew darker, Huck started to nod and soon began to snore. Joe fell asleep next. Tom lay still on his elbow, watching them closely for a while. Finally he got up carefully, on his knees, and looked through the grass and the firelight. He picked up several large half‑cylinders made of thin white sycamore bark and chose two that looked useful. He knelt by the fire and wrote something on each piece with his red pen. He rolled one up and put it in his jacket pocket. He placed the other in Joe’s hat and moved it a short distance away from Joe. He also put several small school‑boy items of great value into the hat: a piece of chalk, a rubber ball, three fishhooks, and a marble called a “sure ’nough crystal.” Then he tiptoed quietly among the trees until he felt he was out of earshot, and then he ran quickly toward the sandbar.
