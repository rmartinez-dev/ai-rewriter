There comes a time in every normal boy’s life when he strongly wants to go somewhere and dig for hidden treasure. That feeling suddenly came to Tom one day. He went out to find Joe Harper, but he didn’t succeed. Then he looked for Ben Rogers; Ben was fishing. Soon he found Huck Finn, who was called the Red‑Handed. Huck agreed to help. Tom took him to a private place and told him the plan quietly. Huck was ready. He always liked to join any fun project that didn’t need money, because he had a lot of free time.

“Where shall we dig?” said Huck.

“Oh, anywhere.”

“Why, is it hidden everywhere?”

“No, not at all. It’s hidden in very specific places, Huck—sometimes on islands, sometimes in rotten chests at the end of a dead tree branch, where the shadow falls at midnight; but mostly under the floor in haunted houses.”

“Who hides it?”

“Robbers, of course—who else would you think? Sunday‑school superintendents?”

“I don’t know. If it were mine I wouldn’t hide it; I’d spend it and have fun.”

“I would too. But robbers don’t act like that. They always hide it and leave it there.”

“Don’t they try to get it back?”

“No, they think they will, but they usually forget the clues or they die. In any case, the treasure stays there a long time and gets rusty. Eventually someone finds an old yellow paper that explains how to find the clues. The paper has to be decoded for about a week because it is mostly symbols and hieroglyphics.”

“Hieroglyphics—what?”

“Hieroglyphics—pictures and symbols that don’t seem to mean anything.”

“Do you have one of those papers, Tom?”

“No.”

“Well then, how are you going to find the marks?”

“I don’t want any marks. They always hide them under a haunted house, on an island, or under a dead tree with one branch sticking out. We’ve looked a little on Jackson’s Island, and we can try again later. There’s also the old haunted house up the Still‑House branch, and there are lots of dead‑limb trees—lots of them.”

“Is it under all of them?”

“How can you say that! No!”

“Then how will you know which one to look at?”

“Search them all!”

“Tom, that will take the whole summer.”

“What does it matter? Imagine you find a brass pot with a hundred dollars in it, all rusty and gray, or a rotten chest full of diamonds. How does that sound?”

Huck’s eyes lit up.

“That’s great. More than enough for me. Just give me the hundred dollars; I don’t need any diamonds.”

“All right. But I bet you I won’t find any diamonds. Some of them are worth twenty dollars each—there aren’t any, maybe just a few that are worth sixty cents or a dollar.”

“No! Is that true?”

“Sure—anyone would tell you that. Haven’t you ever seen one, Huck?”

“Not that I remember.”

“Kings have lots of them.”

“I don’t know any kings, Tom.”

“I guess you don’t. But if you went to Europe you would see a lot of them there.”

“Do they hop?”

“Hopping? Your grandmother! No!”

“So what did you say they do?”

“Sorry, I just meant you would see them—not hopping, of course. What would they hop for? I just mean you would see them scattered around, in a general way. Like that old humpbacked Richard.”

“Richard? What’s his other name?”

“He didn’t have any other name. Kings only have a given name.”

“No?”

“But they don’t.”

“Well, if they like it, Tom, fine; but I don’t want to be a king with just a given name, like a commoner. So, where are we going to dig first?”

“I don’t know. Maybe we should start with that old dead‑limb tree on the hill over by Still‑House Branch?”

“I agree.”

So they took a cracked pick and a shovel and began the three‑mile walk. They arrived hot and out of breath, and dropped down in the shade of a nearby elm to rest and have a smoke.

“I like this,” said Tom.

“So do I.”

“Tell me, Huck, if we find treasure here, what will you do with your share?”

“I’ll have pie and a glass of soda every day, and I’ll go to every circus that comes by. I bet I’ll have a great time.”

“Will you save any of it?”

“Save it? For what?”

“Just so you have something to live on later.”

“That won’t help. Pap will come back to this town someday and grab it if I don’t hurry, and I’m sure he’ll spend it quickly. What are you going to do with yours, Tom?”

“I’m going to buy a new drum, a reliable sword, a red necktie, a bull puppy, and get married.”

“Married!”

“That’s it.”

“Tom, you—why, you’re not thinking clearly.”

“Wait—you’ll see.”

“That’s the most foolish thing you could do. Look at pap and my mother. Fight! They used to fight all the time. I remember it well.”

“That's not anything. The girl I'm going to marry won't fight.”

“Tom, I think they're all the same. They'll all dig a grave. You should think about this for a while. I tell you, you better. What's the girl's name?”

“It isn't a gal at all—it’s a girl.”

“It’s the same, I think; some call her gal, some call her girl—both are fine. Anyway, what's her name, Tom?”

“I'll tell you sometime—not now.”

“Alright, that will do. If you get married, I'll be lonelier than ever.”

“No, you won't. You'll come and live with me. Now get out of this and we'll go digging.”

They worked and sweated for half an hour. Nothing turned up. They kept working another half hour. Still nothing. Huck said:

“Do they always bury it this deep?”

“Sometimes, but not always. I think we aren't in the right place.”

So they chose a new spot and started again. The work was slow, but they made progress. They kept digging in silence for a while. Finally Huck leaned on his shovel, wiped the drops of sweat from his brow with his sleeve, and said:

“Where are we going to dig next, after we finish this one?”

“I think maybe we'll try the old tree over there on Cardiff Hill, behind the widow's house.”

“That sounds good. But won't the widow take it from us, Tom? It's on her land.”

“She might take it! Maybe she wants to try it herself. Whoever finds one of these hidden treasures keeps it. It doesn't matter whose land it's on.”

That was enough. The work continued. After a while Huck said:

“Looks like we're in the wrong place again. What do you think?”

"It’s really strange, Huck. I don’t get it. Sometimes witches interfere. I think maybe that’s the problem now."

"Come on! Witches have no power in daylight."

"Right, I didn’t think of that. Oh, I know what’s going on! We’re such fools! You have to find where the shadow of the branch falls at midnight, and that’s where you dig!"

"Then we’ve wasted all this work for nothing. Now we have to start again at night. It’s a long way. Can you get out?"

"I'm sure I can. We have to do it tonight, too, because if anyone sees these holes they’ll quickly know what’s there and they’ll take it."

"Okay, I’ll come around and meet you tonight."

"All right. Let’s hide the tools in the bushes."

The boys arrived that night at the appointed time. They sat in the shadows waiting. It was a lonely place, and the hour felt solemn because of old traditions. The wind rustled the leaves, sounding like whispers. Ghosts seemed to hide in the dark corners, a distant dog barked, and an owl answered with its hoarse call. The boys felt humbled by the atmosphere and spoke little. After a while they saw that twelve o’clock had arrived; they marked where the shadow fell and began to dig. Their hope grew. Their interest strengthened, and they worked steadily. The hole got deeper and deeper, but each time they hoped to hear the pick hit something, they were disappointed. It was only a stone or a piece of rock. Finally Tom said:

"It’s no use, Huck, we’re wrong again."

“Well, we can’t be wrong. We saw the shadow clearly.”

“I know, but there’s another problem.”

“What’s that?”

“We only guessed at the time. It was either too late or too early.”

Huck dropped his shovel.

“That’s the trouble,” he said. “We have to give this up. We can’t ever tell the right time, and this kind of thing is too scary, especially at night with witches and ghosts flying around. I feel like something is behind me all the time, and I’m afraid to turn around because there might be others waiting in front. I’ve been creeping around ever since I got here.”

“Well, I’ve felt the same, Huck. They always put a dead man’s body near a buried treasure under a tree to guard it.”

“Really?”

“Yes, they do. I’ve always heard that.”

“Tom, I don’t like to mess around where there are dead people. A corpse is bound to cause trouble.”

“I don’t like to stir them up either. Maybe this one was just trying to get attention.”

“Don’t, Tom! It’s frightening.”

“Well, it is. Huck, I feel very uncomfortable.”

“Tom, let’s leave this place and go somewhere else.”

“Okay, I think that’s best.”

“What should we do?”

Tom thought for a moment and then said:

“The haunted house. That’s it!”

“Honestly, I don’t like haunted houses, Tom. They’re a terrible sight—worse than dead people. Dead people might talk, maybe, but they don’t slide around in a shroud, sneaking up on you when you’re not looking, then stare over your shoulder and grind their teeth like a ghost. I couldn’t stand anything like that, Tom—no one could.”

“Yes, but ghosts don’t only appear at night. They won’t stop us from digging there during the day.”

“That’s true. But you know very well that people don’t go near that haunted house, either day or night.”

“That’s mostly because they don’t want to go where a murder happened, anyway. Nothing has ever been seen around that house except at night—just some blue lights moving past the windows, no ordinary ghosts.”

“Well, when you see those blue lights flickering, Tom, you can be sure a ghost is right behind them. It makes sense, because only ghosts use them.”

“Yes, that’s right. But they don’t come around in daylight, so why should we be afraid?”

“Alright. We’ll go into the haunted house if you want, but I think it’s risky.”

They had started down the hill by now. In the middle of the moonlit valley below stood a haunted house, completely isolated. Its fences had been removed long ago, and thick weeds covered the doorsteps. The chimney was ruined, the window frames were empty, and a corner of the roof had collapsed. The boys looked at it for a while, half hoping to see a blue light flash past a window. Then, speaking quietly because of the time and the situation, they turned sharply to the right to give the haunted house a wide berth. They headed home through the woods that lined the back side of Cardiff Hill.
