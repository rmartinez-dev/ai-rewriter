Tom joined the new Temperance Cadet group because he liked their flashy uniform. He promised not to smoke, chew tobacco, or use bad language while he was a member. He soon discovered that promising not to do something often makes you want to do it even more. Tom began to feel a strong urge to drink and swear. The urge grew so intense that only the thought of wearing his red sash kept him from leaving the group. The Fourth of July was coming, but he gave up on it before he had worn his sash for forty‑eight hours. Instead, he placed his hopes on old Judge Frazer, a justice of the peace who seemed to be dying and would have a big public funeral because of his high rank. For three days Tom worried a lot about the judge’s condition and kept looking for news. Sometimes his hopes rose so high that he would take out his uniform and practice in front of the mirror. But the judge’s health kept changing in a discouraging way. Finally the doctor said the judge was improving and then recovering. Tom felt disgusted and also hurt. He handed in his resignation immediately. That night the judge had a relapse and died. Tom decided he would never trust a man like that again.

The funeral was impressive. The cadets marched in a way that would make the deceased jealous.

Tom was free again, in a way. He could now drink and swear, but to his surprise he didn’t want to. Just knowing he could do it removed the desire and the appeal.

Tom soon realized that his long‑awaited vacation was starting to feel like a burden. He tried keeping a diary, but nothing happened for three days, so he gave up.

The first black minstrel show came to town and caused a sensation. Tom and Joe Harper formed a group of performers and were happy for two days.

Even the Fourth of July was a bit of a failure. It rained heavily, so there was no parade. The greatest man in Tom’s eyes, Mr. Benton, a U.S. Senator, was a huge disappointment—he wasn’t twenty‑five feet tall or even close to that.

A circus arrived. The boys pretended to run a circus for three days in tents made of rag carpet. Admission cost three pennies for boys and two for girls. Then they stopped the circus.

A phrenologist and a mesmerist came, then left, making the village feel even duller and gloomier.

There were a few mixed boys‑and‑girls parties. They were so few and so pleasant that they only made the empty gaps feel deeper.

Becky Thatcher had gone to her home in Constantinople to stay with her parents for the vacation, so there was no bright side anywhere.

The terrible secret of the murder caused constant misery. It was like a cancer, bringing endless pain.

Then the measles arrived.

For two weeks Tom stayed in his room, cut off from the world. He was very sick and cared about nothing. When he finally got up and walked weakly into town, everything seemed sad and changed. There had been a revival, and everyone had become religious—not just the adults, but also the boys and girls. Tom walked around, hoping against hope to see just one familiar face, but he was disappointed everywhere. He saw Joe Harper reading the Bible and turned away sadly from the gloomy scene. He looked for Ben Rogers and found him giving out pamphlets to the poor. He found Jim Hollis, who reminded him of the “blessing” of his recent measles as a warning. Every boy he met made him feel more depressed. In desperation he ran to Huckleberry Finn for comfort, but was greeted with a Bible quote. His heart broke. He went home, crawled into bed, and realized that he was the only one in town who was truly lost, forever.

That night a huge storm arrived. The rain poured down, thunder boomed loudly, and lightning flashed everywhere. He pulled the blankets over his head and waited in terrified suspense, feeling certain that the commotion was aimed at him. He thought he had pushed the patience of the powers above to their limit, and that this was the result. It might have seemed a waste of firepower to kill a tiny bug with a whole artillery barrage, but there was nothing odd about a massive thunderstorm being used to knock an insect like him off his feet.

After a while the storm faded away without achieving its goal. The boy’s first reaction was to feel grateful and to want to change. His second reaction was to wait, thinking there might be no more storms.

The next day the doctors returned; Tom had relapsed. The three weeks he spent lying in bed this time felt like an entire lifetime. When he finally got up, he was hardly grateful for being spared. He remembered how lonely his estate had been, how he had been without any companion. He drifted aimlessly down the street and saw Jim Hollis acting as a judge in a juvenile court that was trying a cat for murder, with the victim—a bird—present. He also saw Joe Harper and Huck Finn in an alley, eating a stolen melon. Poor lads! Like Tom, they had suffered a relapse.
